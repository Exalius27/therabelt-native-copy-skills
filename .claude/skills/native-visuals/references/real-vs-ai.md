# Real vs AI — What Makes a Photo Look Real

This file exists so the skill can always reference what REAL candid photos look like vs what AI defaults to. Every creative brief must be written against these rules.

## What the reference photos actually look like

Studied from 30+ real Facebook/social media photos that perform as native ad visuals.

### Lighting: FLAT, not dramatic
- Overhead fluorescent (hospitals, Walmart, kitchens) — flat, even, slightly green or blue tint
- Direct flash — harsh, washes out the foreground, kills all shadows, makes skin look shiny
- Daylight through windows — bright, slightly blown out, no "golden hour" warmth
- Car interior at night — dashboard glow + sodium streetlights, grainy, underexposed
- NO dramatic shadows. NO cinematic mood lighting. NO warm/cool color grading. The light is just whatever was there.

### Composition: BAD, not artful
- Subject is just... in the frame. Not composed. Not framed by doorways or leading lines.
- Often too close or too far. Heads cut off at the top. Feet cut off at the bottom.
- Slightly tilted — 2-5 degrees — because the person is holding the phone with one hand
- Centered OR off-center, but never in a way that looks intentional
- Selfies are arm's length, slightly from above, often with a second person crammed in
- Object shots (wallet, groceries, pill bottle) are held up to the camera or shot straight down at a counter/car seat
- Group photos: everyone lined up against a wall, forced smiles, someone is always blinking or looking away

### Quality: LOW, not crisp
- JPEG compression artifacts visible
- Slightly soft focus — auto-focus on phones is imperfect
- Noise/grain in low light — phones don't handle dark rooms well
- Some are slightly blurry from hand movement
- Colors are whatever the phone sensor captured — not corrected, not graded
- White balance is often wrong (too yellow indoors, too blue in fluorescent)

### People: REAL, not cast
- Overweight, underweight, average. Real body types.
- Glasses, no makeup, bad haircuts, surgical caps, baseball caps
- Scrubs that are wrinkled and actually worn. T-shirts. Flannel. Walmart clothes.
- Skin has texture — pores, redness, age spots, bruises, shine from flash
- Expressions: tired smiles, mid-sentence mouths, squinting, blank stares at the camera
- Posture is bad. Shoulders slumped. Standing awkwardly.
- Nobody looks like they belong in a movie. Everyone looks like they belong in your neighborhood.

### Settings: CLUTTERED, not styled
- Real surfaces: laminate countertops, worn car seats, hospital bed rails, folding tables
- Real clutter: shoes on the floor, stuff on counters, cords, bags, random objects
- Brand names visible: Walmart bags, Hot N Ready delivery bags, specific medication labels, store signs
- Nothing is "set dressed." The background is whatever was behind the person when someone grabbed their phone.

### The "I just snapped this" energy
- These photos exist because someone was IN a moment and took a picture. Not because someone planned a photo.
- The urgency is visible: a bruise someone wanted to document, a dog on their kid's lap, a hospital bed, groceries on a belt
- Many are proof photos — "look at this" photos sent to a friend or posted for a specific reason
- The camera was an afterthought. The moment was the point.

## What AI defaults to (and what we must STOP doing)

### AI gives you CINEMA. You need PHONE.
- AI creates dramatic shadow play → Real photos have flat overhead light
- AI composes subjects in golden-ratio frames → Real photos have bad framing
- AI color-grades everything (warm amber, cool blue) → Real photos have no color grading
- AI makes people beautiful by default → Real people are not beautiful by default
- AI creates "moody" atmosphere → Real life has no mood — it has fluorescent lights
- AI renders skin as smooth porcelain → Real skin has pores, redness, shine
- AI makes backgrounds clean and intentional → Real backgrounds are messy and accidental
- AI uses vignetting and depth of field → Phone photos have everything mostly in focus with flat depth

## Rules for every creative brief

1. **NEVER use the words**: moody, dramatic, cinematic, atmospheric, artful, beautiful, ethereal, golden, warm glow, soft light, tender, intimate framing
2. **ALWAYS specify**: flat overhead lighting OR direct flash OR daylight through windows. Name the specific light source.
3. **ALWAYS include**: at least 3 specific pieces of clutter/mess in the background that are NOT related to the story
4. **ALWAYS specify the phone**: "Shot on an older iPhone, slightly compressed, auto white balance is wrong"
5. **ALWAYS make framing imperfect**: "Subject is slightly too far left and tilted 3 degrees clockwise"
6. **NEVER frame through doorways** or use leading lines — that's cinematography
7. **People must be described as average-looking**: specify real details (glasses, scrubs that are actually wrinkled, no makeup, messy hair in an actual messy way not a "styled messy" way)
8. **Lighting must be ugly**: fluorescent green tint, flash washout, overexposed windows. Real lighting is not flattering.
9. **Colors must be ungraded**: "Colors are straight from the phone sensor with no editing. Whites are slightly yellow from indoor lighting."
10. **Resolution must be low enough**: 720p with visible JPEG compression. Not crisp. Not clean.

## Brief template that produces real-looking images

```
Generate a photo that looks exactly like it was taken on an older iPhone by someone who is not a photographer. This is NOT a professional photo. This is NOT cinematic. This is a low-quality phone photo posted to Facebook.

CRITICAL REQUIREMENTS:
- Flat, unflattering overhead fluorescent lighting OR direct phone flash
- JPEG compression artifacts visible
- Auto white balance is slightly wrong (too yellow or too blue)
- Subject framing is imperfect — slightly off-center, slightly tilted
- Background has random real-world clutter unrelated to the scene
- People look like real everyday people — not models, not actors
- No color grading. No mood lighting. No dramatic shadows.
- No depth of field blur. Everything is mostly in focus because it's a phone camera.
- The overall quality should match a 2018-2022 Facebook post from a regular person.

SCENE: [description here]
SPECIFIC CLUTTER: [list 3-5 random background items]
LIGHTING: [name the exact ugly light source]
FRAMING FLAW: [describe the specific way it's badly framed]
```

## Examples of what to write vs what NOT to write

BAD (produces AI cinema):
"A woman in scrubs sitting on a dark entryway floor. Orange streetlight through the door window. Shot from a distance through a doorway. Raw exhaustion."

GOOD (produces real phone photo):
"A woman in wrinkled blue scrubs sitting on a tile floor next to a front door. Taken with an iPhone from about 8 feet away by someone standing in the hallway. The overhead light is on — flat, yellowish, unflattering. Her work bag is on the floor next to her. A brown dog has his head on her leg. She looks tired, not dramatic. Her hair is frizzy and half-falling out of a bun. There's a shoe rack with mismatched shoes behind her, a jacket hanging crooked on a wall hook, junk mail on the small table by the door. The photo is slightly tilted and has visible JPEG compression. Auto white balance makes everything slightly too warm. This looks like a photo someone took and texted to a friend, not a movie still."
