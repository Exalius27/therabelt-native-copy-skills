# Native Visuals — Candid Photo Style Reference

This guide defines the exact visual style for every image generated by this skill. Every image must pass the "Facebook friend test": would this look normal in a friend's Facebook post? If it looks like an ad, a stock photo, or AI-generated, it fails.

## The Style: "Found Photo" / Candid iPhone

These are photos that look like a real person grabbed their phone and took a picture in the moment. They are not composed. They are not lit. They are not edited. They exist because someone was living their life and thought "I should take a picture of this."

## Camera Characteristics

- **Device**: iPhone 11-13 (good enough to be clear, old enough to have slight compression artifacts)
- **Resolution**: 720p — this is critical. Higher resolution creates the "too clean" AI look
- **Aspect ratio**: 1:1 (square, native to Facebook/Instagram feed)
- **Focus**: Auto-focus — sometimes slightly soft, especially on close-up objects
- **Flash**: Almost never. Natural ambient light only. Exception: a dark room where someone clearly popped the flash (harsh shadows, washed-out foreground)
- **Orientation**: Mostly vertical or square. Horizontal only for wide scenes (parking lots, warehouses, classrooms)

## Framing

- **Off-center**: The subject is almost never perfectly centered. Slightly left, slightly right, slightly too high — how a real person frames a quick photo
- **Tilt**: A very slight tilt (1-3 degrees) is natural. Perfectly level = tripod = not candid
- **Distance**: Varies by scene type:
  - Selfies: arm's length, slightly from above
  - Object close-ups: 6-12 inches away, filling most of the frame
  - Room/scene shots: 4-8 feet back, capturing context
  - Group photos: far enough to fit everyone, often cutting off feet or the top of someone's head

## Lighting

Match the setting. Every setting has a signature light:

- **Hospital**: Overhead fluorescent, slightly blue-white, harsh shadows under the chin
- **Kitchen**: Warm overhead light, sometimes mixed with window daylight
- **Car interior (day)**: Bright through windshield, dashboard in shadow, sometimes sun glare
- **Car interior (night)**: Dashboard glow, streetlight orange through windows, mostly dark
- **Walmart/store**: Bright overhead fluorescent, flat and even, slightly green tint
- **Warehouse**: High industrial lights, dark corners, uneven pools of light
- **Classroom/church**: Mixed fluorescent and window light
- **Outdoor/parking lot**: Flat daylight or sodium-vapor orange at night
- **Bathroom/home**: Warm yellow overhead or vanity mirror lighting

NEVER use: studio lighting, ring lights, golden hour photography, dramatic shadows, backlit silhouettes.

## People

### Who they are
- **Everyday people.** Not models. Not stock-photo-attractive. Real body types, real skin, real hair.
- Ages 30-65 primarily (matches our avatar range)
- Dressed in what real people wear: scrubs, flannel shirts, t-shirts, hoodies, Walmart clothes. Not fashion-forward.
- Glasses, watches, hospital bracelets, lanyards — the accessories real people actually wear

### How they look
- Natural expressions: tired smiles, neutral faces, mid-conversation mouths, squinting
- Not posing (exception: group photos where everyone lines up and smiles)
- Skin has texture — pores, wrinkles, sun spots, redness. NOT smooth/filtered
- Hair is however it actually is — messy, in a bun, hat hair, surgical cap

### What to avoid with people
- Perfect teeth, perfect skin, perfect hair
- Fashion model poses or body language
- Ethnically ambiguous "stock photo diverse" casting
- Everyone looking at the camera in candid shots (in real life, someone is always looking away)
- Babies or small children that look AI-generated (AI is especially bad at realistic babies)

## Objects and Settings

### The "proof photo" category
These are close-up shots of objects that prove the story is real. They are some of the most powerful scroll-stoppers.

Proven examples from reference images:
- **Medication**: Pill bottle held up to camera, prescription box on a counter, pharmacy bag
- **Money/wallet**: Wallet open showing cards (VA card, driver's license), wallet on car seat next to shopping bag
- **Groceries**: Budget groceries on a checkout belt (ramen, bread, bananas, peanut butter)
- **Car interior**: Delivery bags on passenger seat, belongings packed in backseat (living in car), blanket and pillow in backseat
- **Medical**: Hospital wristband, IV in arm, bruise close-up, surgical scrubs, anatomical model
- **Store**: Shopping cart with wallet, Walmart bag on car seat

Key details:
- Objects should have real-world wear — scuffed wallets, wrinkled bags, smudged labels
- Brand names can appear (Walmart, specific medication names) but should be slightly out of focus or at an angle — not perfectly readable
- Surfaces should be real: laminate countertops, worn car seats, hospital bed rails

### Settings that work
From the reference images, these settings appear repeatedly:
- Hospital room / ER / waiting room
- Car interior (front seat, back seat)
- Walmart / grocery store
- Kitchen (home kitchen, restaurant kitchen)
- Church / Sunday school classroom
- Warehouse / stockroom
- Vet clinic hallway
- Parking lot (day and night)

Each setting needs its specific clutter:
- Hospital: monitors, IV poles, call buttons, patterned hospital gowns, white blankets
- Car: cup holders with stuff in them, receipts, sun visors, steering wheel visible
- Kitchen: stuff on counters, appliances, dish towels
- Classroom: colored mats, whiteboards, kid art on walls, small chairs

## Collage / Grid Layouts

Some of the strongest images are 2x2 or 4-panel grids. These work because they tell a compressed visual story.

### When to use collages:
- Medical journey (before → during → after → now)
- "A day in the life" sequence
- Evidence collection (multiple proof photos in one image)
- Showing contrast (what life was vs. what it is now)

### How to compose:
- Equal-sized panels (2x2 is the sweet spot)
- Thin white or black divider between panels
- Each panel should work as a standalone photo
- The sequence should tell a mini-story left→right, top→bottom

## Common Scenes by Copy Type

### Story-Native (identity challenge) copies typically need:
- The inciting incident (vet visit, emergency, health scare)
- The dog in a specific recognizable moment
- The owner's daily life (kitchen, car, store)
- The "noticing" moment (stranger comments, vet reaction)
- A warm domestic scene (couch, backyard, morning routine)

### Evolve-Native (villain architecture) copies typically need:
- The alarming discovery (ingredient label, vet warning, sick dog moment)
- The "research" moment (phone screen, late night at laptop)
- The emotional weight (owner looking at dog, quiet worry)
- The contrast (before/after energy, behavior change)
- The proof objects (kibble bag, ingredient list, vet paperwork)

### Amala-Native (proxy witness) copies typically need:
- The narrator's world (their job, their home, their routine)
- First encounter with the dog owner
- The dog in its environment
- Specific transformation moments witnessed
- The emotional climax scene
- Objects that anchor the timeline (seasonal markers, specific items mentioned in copy)

## Generation Checklist

Before finalizing any image, verify:

- [ ] Looks like it was taken with a phone, not a camera
- [ ] Lighting matches the setting (not studio lit)
- [ ] Framing is slightly imperfect (not perfectly centered)
- [ ] People look like real everyday people (not models)
- [ ] Setting has real-world clutter (not clean/staged)
- [ ] No obvious AI artifacts (wrong fingers, melted text, impossible geometry)
- [ ] No TheraBelt branding or product visible
- [ ] Passes the "Facebook friend test" — would blend into a real Facebook feed
- [ ] Matches the emotional tone of the scene it represents
- [ ] Hits at least one scroll-stop rule (Controversial, Colour, Weird Object, Recognisable)
