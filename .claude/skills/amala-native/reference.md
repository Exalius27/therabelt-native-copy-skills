# Amala-Native — Reference Material

Read this file during execution when writing and reviewing the draft.

## Section 1: Writing Craft Reference

### Structure
- Product (TheraBelt) never appears before 60% through. Target: 60-80% mark.
- The first 2,000 words contain ZERO product signal. Zero health mechanism. Zero hint of what's coming.
- Product enters through a credentialed insider in a trust chain, not a friend's casual mention.
- Benefits shown through the return of named gifts, never listed or claimed.
- Narrator never directly tells the reader to buy. Narrator shares because withholding would be wrong.
- **TheraBelt always stands alone.** Never frame TheraBelt as a complement to supplements or part of a stack.
- The story is 4,000-10,000 words. The length is a structural tool. Every word earns its place.

### The Proxy Architecture

This is the defining structural difference from story-native and evolve-native. The narrator does NOT own the problem.

**Why proxy works:** When the narrator IS the dog owner, the reader's skepticism filter activates at product introduction ("of course she likes it, she's selling me"). When the narrator is a witness, that filter stays off. She has no reason to lie. She saw something and she's telling you about it.

**The three parties:**
- **Victim:** Owns the dog. Carries the identity wound. Has the ONE defining trait. She is the emotional center. The reader empathizes with HER, but projects onto her own life.
- **Credible Insider:** Has earned authority (degree, career, professional experience). Introduces the mechanism and eventually the product. She is NOT an evangelist. She states what she knows calmly. She may be reluctant. That reluctance makes her more believable.
- **Bridge:** Connects narrator to victim. Can be a mutual friend, family member, coworker, neighbor. The bridge creates proximity. Sometimes the bridge IS the credible insider (a vet friend, a researcher neighbor). When the bridge and insider are the same person, the cast stays tighter and the story moves faster.

**Narrator's role:**
- She watches. She notices. She is moved by what she sees.
- She asks questions when the credible insider speaks (same proxy-as-reader technique from evolve-native).
- She has her own emotional reactions to what she witnesses.
- She may have her own dog, but her dog is NOT the one that transforms. Her dog can benefit at the end (in the PS or in a brief aside), but the transformation story belongs to the victim's dog.
- Her final act is telling the reader. That's the whole CTA. "I saw this. You need to know."

### The Identity Wound — One Trait Architecture

The victim has ONE thing. Not three things. Not "she loves her dog and also gardens and also teaches piano." ONE trait that defines who she is to everyone around her.

**How to build the wound:**
1. Show the trait in full bloom in Act 1. Multiple specific scenes of the trait in action.
2. Show how the dog is integral to the trait. The dog isn't just "part of her life." The dog is woven into the SPECIFIC EXPRESSION of the trait.
3. Show the dog's decline removing the trait piece by piece. Not all at once. Gradually. Each loss is a scene.
4. Show the victim without the trait. What she becomes when the thing that defines her is gone. This is the emotional low point.

**The wound must be SHOWN, not told.** Never write "She wasn't herself anymore." Show what she stopped doing. Show the empty space where the trait used to be. Show other people noticing the absence.

### Seed / Take / Return — Named Gift Architecture

This is the single most important structural element unique to Amala-native. It's what makes the transformation FELT instead of claimed.

**Seeding (Act 1):**
- Plant at minimum THREE specific, named, concrete things. Objects, rituals, behaviors.
- Each must be vivid enough that the reader remembers it.
- Each must be connected to the victim's identity and her dog.
- Plant them naturally through scenes. Never list them.

Examples of strong named gifts (for pattern understanding only, never reuse):
- A specific ceramic mug she drinks from on the porch while the dog lies at her feet
- The sound of the dog's nails on the kitchen floor at 5:45am
- A red collar the dog wears only on their Saturday morning walk
- The dog stealing a specific gardening glove every morning
- A bench in a specific park where she sits while the dog sniffs

**Taking (Act 2):**
- Each gift disappears through a specific scene. Not "things changed." SHOW the mug sitting unused. SHOW the morning silence where the nails used to click. SHOW the red collar hanging on the hook, not worn.
- The taking should be gradual. First one gift goes. Then another. The third is the gut punch.
- The victim notices. The narrator notices. The reader notices.

**Returning (Act 3):**
- Each gift comes back through a specific scene after TheraBelt.
- The return order can mirror the taking order or reverse it. Reversing can be powerful: the last thing taken is the first thing to return.
- The return scenes ARE the transformation proof. No claims needed. The named thing is back. The reader knows what that means.
- The returns should be gradual too. Doubt first. "Was that...?" Then confirmation. Then the third gift returning is the undeniable moment.

### The System Villain

The villain in Amala-native is institutional, not corporate. It's not Big Pet Food specifically (that's evolve-native territory). It's the broader medical/veterinary establishment that treats numbers while the patient disappears.

**The key beat:** A well-meaning professional says something that reveals the system's blindness. The vet looks at bloodwork, sees normal numbers, and says "he looks great for his age." But the victim KNOWS he's not great. She can see it. The system can't.

**Rules:**
- Never attack individual professionals. The vet is doing her best.
- The villain is the GAP between what the system measures and what the owner sees.
- The professional's words are the villain's line. "His numbers look good" IS the failure, because the numbers don't capture what the owner lives with every day.
- The reader must recognize this pattern from her own experience. She's heard "the numbers look fine" while knowing something is wrong.
- Frame: the system is designed to catch disease, not decline. By the time the numbers move, years of quality of life have already been lost.

### Tense and Narrative Mode
- **Use deliberate tense shifts.** Past tense for backstory and setup. Present tense for dramatic scenes where the reader needs to feel like they're there.
- Act 1 is mostly past tense (world-building, establishing the "before").
- The wound opening in Act 2 shifts to present tense for key scenes.
- Product discovery and transformation return to present tense so the reader lives it.
- The circular close mirrors the tense of the opening, creating a bookend.

### Reading Level and Tone
- **Third-grade reading level. Always.** Simple words, short sentences. If a ten-year-old wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.
- **Third-grade CLARITY, not just vocabulary.** Every line must be understood instantly on first read.
- First-person narrator. Conversational. Short sentences. Fragments fine.
- Heavy dialogue. Characters talk like real people.
- No marketing language, jargon, or buzzwords.
- No bullet lists or feature dumps inside the story.
- No exclamation marks for emphasis.
- **Never use em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or restructure.
- **Use only common, universally known words.**

### Characters
- Keep the cast small. Narrator + victim + credible insider + bridge (if separate from insider) = 3-4 characters max. Named only.
- **Don't name characters who are only mentioned in passing.** "Her vet" is enough.
- Use simple but original names. Not AI-generic (Sarah, John, Mary).
- The victim gets the most characterization. She must feel like a real person the reader knows.
- The credible insider gets 2-3 sentences of credential establishment. What she did, why she knows, why she's willing to talk.
- The bridge gets minimal characterization. Enough to explain the connection.

### Dialogue Authenticity
- **When a character speaks, the listener responds with actual dialogue.** No silent summaries.
- **Every line of dialogue passes the "would a real person say this?" test.**
- **ZERO dialogue tags. ZERO stage directions.** No "she said," "he asked," "she paused," "she looked at me." The reader knows who's talking from context.
- **No vague pronoun dialogue.** Every "it" and "this" must have a clear referent.
- **Dialogue that exists to move the sales narrative forward instead of sounding like real people is the fastest way to break the story.**
- **Track what each character knows and when they learned it.** Timeline inconsistencies destroy believability.

### Mechanism Delivery in Amala-Native

The mechanism in Amala-native is SIMPLER than in evolve-native. No full AGEs/RAGE/cytochrome c oxidase chain. No catch-22 architecture. The mechanism is delivered through the credible insider in plain language, focused on what the light does.

**The simplified mechanism chain:**
1. Cells make energy. As dogs age, cells make less energy. Less energy = less repair, less recovery, less vitality.
2. Red light (two wavelengths) reaches the mitochondria directly through skin and fur.
3. Cells start making energy again. Not new energy FROM light. The cell's own engine, unjammed.
4. No digestion needed. No supplements to process. Light goes straight to the cell.
5. "It's not giving the cell energy. It's giving the cell its ability to make energy back."

**Delivery rules:**
- Through the credible insider's dialogue, never narration.
- The narrator (or victim) asks questions. The insider explains simply.
- 400-800 words max for the mechanism section. Not 1,000+.
- The insider uses analogies. "Think of it like a car engine that still works but the spark plug is fouled. The engine's fine. It just needs the plug cleared."
- The insider doesn't need to name TheraBelt during the mechanism. She explains the science. The product name comes after, when she says "there's a belt you put on the dog."
- Never cross into aging language. "Slows aging" and "anti-aging" are banned. The mechanism is about cellular energy and repair.

### Transformation Pacing
- Changes are gradual, not instant. Doubt before certainty.
- **HARD BAN: No week or month numbers.** "Week 3" and "Five weeks in" are banned. Time passes through narrative: "Then one morning..." "A Tuesday..." "His next checkup..."
- The first days show nothing. The victim keeps checking. Maybe almost gives up. The doubt period is necessary.
- Transformation order: energy/mood first (subtle, easy to dismiss). Then specific lost behaviors return (the named gifts). Then external signs. Then medical confirmation last.
- **Named gift returns are the transformation proof.** Each return is a scene, not a summary.
- **Restoration is partial, not complete.** "He's still a little slower than he was at four. But that's fine. He's nine." Full reversal breaks belief.

### The Circular Close

The final scene mirrors the opening. Same location. Same characters. Different outcome.

**How to execute:**
- Return to the exact physical setting of the opening.
- Use the same sensory details, transformed. If the opening had "the empty park bench," the close has the bench occupied. If the opening had silence, the close has the sound back.
- The moral is delivered by a secondary character (the victim, the insider, or the bridge), NOT the narrator. One or two lines. Simple. "I almost didn't try it. I almost let him go without ever knowing."
- The narrator's final words circle back to the opening image. The reader feels the bookend.

### PS Architecture

The PS is a second sales letter in story voice. It lives AFTER the circular close. It is still the narrator talking. She never breaks character.

**Structure:**
- 3-5 short paragraphs
- Each handles one objection
- Objections to cover (adapt to concept):
  1. Price ("I know what you're thinking. It's not cheap. But [specific number from the story] is what [victim] was spending on [thing that didn't work] every month.")
  2. Skepticism ("I wouldn't have believed it either. I watched it happen to someone else's dog and I still had to see it myself.")
  3. Time to results ("It didn't happen overnight. [Victim] almost stopped after the first week. I'm glad she didn't.")
  4. "Is this real?" ("I'm not a vet. I'm not selling anything. I'm telling you what I saw.")
  5. One concept-specific objection

**PS rules:**
- Still story voice. Still the narrator. Still first-person.
- No marketing language. No "invest." No "journey."
- No bullet lists. Prose only.
- The PS ends the entire piece. No product name after it. No URL. No sign-off.

### Urgency in Amala-Native

Urgency in Amala-native is different from both story-native and evolve-native.

- **Not scarcity-based** (unlike story-native's "price going up" pattern)
- **Not information-based** (unlike evolve-native's "every day the damage continues" pattern)
- **Moral obligation urgency.** The narrator has witnessed something. She is telling the reader. The urgency is: "I could have been told sooner. [Victim] could have known months earlier. Every month she didn't know was a month her dog didn't have to lose."

The urgency is about TIME LOST, not products running out. The emotional weight is: "How many mornings did [victim] sit with a dog that didn't need to be that way? How many mornings is YOUR dog sitting like that?"

The narrator never says that last sentence. The reader thinks it.

### Patterns to Avoid

All bans from story-native and evolve-native apply. Additionally:

- **No narrator-as-dog-owner structure.** If the narrator's dog is the one transforming, this is the wrong skill. The narrator WITNESSES someone else's dog transform.
- **No identity-challenge beats.** No "what kind of owner would I be" line. No caretaker identity thread. No invisible effort. No recognition moments. Those are story-native architecture.
- **No catch-22 delivery.** No "everything you try runs into the same wall." No condemning obvious solutions as a primary structural beat. That's evolve-native architecture. The elimination path in Amala-native is SHOWN through the victim trying things that fail, not ARGUED through mechanism logic.
- **No "Your move" CTA.** No competitive energy. Moral obligation only.
- **No direct address at any point.** Never "you" until the PS (and even then, sparingly). The narrator talks about what she saw. The reader applies it to herself.
- **No breaking the blind spot.** If you find yourself mentioning health, wellness, supplements, or dog aging before the 2,000-word mark, STOP. Cut the reference. Move it later. The blind spot is non-negotiable.
- **No unnamed gifts.** If you catch yourself writing "they used to walk together" instead of naming the SPECIFIC walk (the creek trail, the loop past the elementary school, the path through the park where the geese are), you're not being specific enough. Name it.
- **No vague wounds.** "She was sad about her dog" is not a wound. "She stopped going to the community garden on Saturday mornings because the dog couldn't make the walk anymore, and she'd gone every Saturday for eleven years" is a wound.
- **No partial Seed/Take/Return.** All three gifts must be seeded. All three must be taken. All three must return. If one gift disappears in the outline, find a third or revisit the concept.

### Hooks and Leads

**Trojan Horse hooks:** The hook has NOTHING to do with dogs or health. It's a human story hook. Pure curiosity, tension, or contrast about a human situation. The reader stops because the HUMAN story demands it.

Example energies (pattern only, never reuse):
- A found wallet. A neighbor's house fire. A retirement party that went sideways. A woman at church who hasn't spoken to her sister in nine years.
- The hook must be specific. Not "Something happened at the party" but "My neighbor threw her husband a retirement party and by the end of it he was sitting in the driveway alone."

**Insider Provocation hooks:** The first line is the insider speaking against her own system. The provocation must be so specific and so self-damaging that the reader cannot look away.

Example energies (pattern only, never reuse):
- "I'm a vet. I've been a vet for sixteen years. And I just spent $4,000 on something my colleagues would laugh at."
- "I spent eleven years making dog food. Then I got a dog."

**Both types:**
- **The hook must be VICIOUS.** A Trojan Horse situation is not automatically a hook. "We drove to college" is a situation. "My sister and I have one rule. Whoever is going through something, the other one drives" is a hook. Apply all 8 vicious hook rules from `wiki/vicious-hooks.md`: relevant in the first line, emotionally charged, open loop, stakes, specificity, doesn't read like an ad, caveman language, consequence first. The Trojan Horse disguise does not excuse a soft opening.
- The hook must require zero cognitive steps to understand.
- The hook must filter for the RIGHT reader (dog owners, specifically senior dog owners).
- Nostalgic hooks are dead. Nobody stops scrolling for warmth.
- Write ONE hook. Do not present options. Commit.

### Emotion

**Emotion is the engine.** An Amala-native ad lives or dies on emotion. The length means the reader must FEEL something on almost every page. A 5,000-word story that's structurally perfect but emotionally flat is a waste of 5,000 words.

**The emotional arc in Amala-native:**

For Trojan Horse:
1. Curiosity/engagement (the human story hook)
2. Warmth/connection (meeting the victim, seeing the trait in bloom)
3. Dread/sadness (the trait cracking, the named gifts disappearing)
4. Frustration (the system failing, "his numbers look fine")
5. Hope (the credible insider, the mechanism)
6. Cautious optimism (the product, the first days of nothing)
7. Quiet joy (the named gifts returning, one by one)
8. Full emotional release (the defining trait restored, the circular close)

For Insider Provocation:
1. Shock/intrigue (the insider's self-indictment)
2. Trust (the insider's credential established)
3. Anger (what the insider knows about the system)
4. Transfer to warmth (meeting the victim through the insider's story)
5. Then steps 3-8 from above

**Use a sensory motif.** One specific sound, sight, or physical detail that threads through the story. The click of nails on tile. The weight of a leash. The sound of a specific bark. Introduce it in bloom. Show it fading. Return it.

## Section 2: The 13-Checkpoint Evaluator

Score every draft against these 13 checkpoints before presenting. Total: /130. Launch threshold: 115+ (88%). No checkpoint below 7/10.

### Checkpoint 0: Entry Mechanism Test (THE GATE — /50, must score 40+)

This is scored out of 50, then converted to /10 for the total. The ad must score 40+/50 to proceed. If it fails the gate, stop. Rebuild.

**Five dimensions, each /10:**

**0A: The Scroll Test (/10)**
Read the first three sentences. Would someone scrolling at 10pm stop for this? Not because the writing is good, but because the SITUATION demands attention.
- 9-10: Impossible to scroll past. The situation itself grabs.
- 7-8: Strong stop. Most readers would pause.
- 5-6: Interesting but not urgent. Some scroll past.
- Below 5: Generic, warm, or vague. Easy to ignore.

**0B: The Comments Test (/10)**
Imagine this as a real post. Write five comments people would leave. Are they engaging with a REAL STORY or spotting an ad?
- 9-10: All five comments engage with the human story. Zero ad suspicion.
- 7-8: Four engage genuinely. One might be suspicious.
- 5-6: Mixed. Some genuine, some "this sounds like an ad."
- Below 5: Multiple comments would call it out as an ad.

**0C: The Brand Removal Test (/10)**
Strip the product out entirely. Does the story still work as a human interest piece?
- 9-10: The story is compelling without ANY product. You'd read it in a magazine.
- 7-8: Mostly stands alone. A few beats feel purposeless without the product.
- 5-6: Significant portions only exist to support the product.
- Below 5: The story collapses without the product.

**0D: The "Did I Write This?" Test (/10)**
Would the victim's archetype read this and feel privately understood? Does the narrator sound like a real person who witnessed this?
- 9-10: The victim recognizes herself. The narrator sounds like a real friend telling a real story.
- 7-8: Mostly authentic. A few lines feel written rather than told.
- 5-6: Clearly constructed. The voice is consistent but not natural.
- Below 5: Reads like a copywriter, not a person.

**0E: Voice Naturalness (/10)**
Read the opening 500 words aloud. Does it sound like a person talking or a writer writing?
- 9-10: Indistinguishable from a real person's post or story.
- 7-8: Natural with occasional writer-voice moments.
- 5-6: Competent but polished. The craft shows.
- Below 5: Clearly written by a professional. Nobody talks like this.

**Gate calculation:** Sum 0A through 0E. If total is below 40/50, STOP. The entry mechanism failed. Rebuild.

Convert to /10: divide by 5.

### Checkpoint 1: Hidden Mirror (/10)

A thematic seed planted in the opening that gains full meaning by the end. The reader doesn't notice it on first read. On reflection, it was always there.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The mirror is invisible on first read but unmistakable in retrospect. It deepens the entire story.
- 7-8: Present and functional. The connection is clear but could be more resonant.
- 5-6: Heavy-handed. The reader can see the setup while reading it.
- Below 5: Missing or forced.

### Checkpoint 2: Witness Structure (/10)

Narrator observes. Never owns the problem.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The proxy structure is invisible. The narrator's witnessing feels completely natural. The reader never questions why the narrator is telling this story instead of the victim.
- 7-8: Solid proxy structure. One or two moments where the narrator's role feels slightly constructed.
- 5-6: The narrator drifts into owning the problem at times.
- Below 5: The narrator is effectively the dog owner. Wrong skill.

### Checkpoint 3: Identity Wound (/10)

One defining trait, systematically destroyed.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The trait is crystal clear, shown through multiple specific scenes, and its destruction is devastating. The reader feels the loss in her body.
- 7-8: The trait is clear and the destruction is felt. Could be more specific in places.
- 5-6: The wound is present but vague. "She was different" instead of showing exactly HOW she was different.
- Below 5: No clear single trait, or the trait is generic ("she loved her dog").

### Checkpoint 4: System Villain (/10)

Institution fails. Professionals do their best inside a broken system.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The system failure is shown through a specific, recognizable scene. The reader has lived her own version of "the numbers look fine." No individual is attacked.
- 7-8: The system villain is present and institutional. The reader recognizes the pattern.
- 5-6: The villain is present but either too vague (generic "system") or too personal (attacking a specific vet).
- Below 5: No villain, or the villain is a person, or conspiracy energy.

### Checkpoint 5: Earned Research (/10)

Research triggered by devastation, not curiosity.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The research moment is emotionally earned. The reader feels the desperation that drives it. The victim searches because she CAN'T not search.
- 7-8: Research feels motivated and natural. The emotional trigger is clear.
- 5-6: Research happens but feels convenient. The emotional weight isn't fully there.
- Below 5: Research happens out of curiosity or is skipped entirely.

### Checkpoint 6: Elimination Path (/10)

Every alternative tried and ruled out with specific reasoning before the product appears.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: Three or more alternatives tried. Each fails for a specific, logical reason the reader can follow. The product becomes the ONLY remaining option. The path feels inevitable, not constructed.
- 7-8: Alternatives tried and eliminated. The logic is sound. Could be more specific.
- 5-6: Some alternatives mentioned but not fully eliminated, or the reasoning is vague.
- Below 5: Product appears without alternatives being tried, or alternatives are dismissed without reason.

### Checkpoint 7: Product Introduction (/10)

Through a credentialed insider, at 60-80% mark, feels like a recommendation.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: Product enters so naturally the reader doesn't register the shift. The insider's credential is established. The timing is right. It feels like a friend telling a friend.
- 7-8: Product introduction is natural. Timing is in range. Credential is clear.
- 5-6: Product introduction feels somewhat constructed. Timing may be off. Credential may be weak.
- Below 5: Product is pitched, not recommended. Or appears too early. Or the introducer lacks credibility.

### Checkpoint 8: Transformation Mirror (/10)

All three named gifts return through scenes.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: All three gifts return. Each return is a specific scene the reader can see. The returns are gradual, doubted, then undeniable. The reader remembers the gifts from Act 1 and feels the circle close.
- 7-8: Gifts return and the scenes are present. Could be more vivid or more connected to Act 1.
- 5-6: Some gifts return, or returns are summarized rather than shown.
- Below 5: Gifts don't return, or the transformation is told rather than shown through the gifts.

### Checkpoint 9: Circular Close (/10)

Story ends where it began. Same place, same characters, different outcome.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: The close mirrors the opening so precisely that the reader feels the bookend physically. Same sensory details, transformed. The moral lands through a secondary character.
- 7-8: The close references the opening clearly. The circular structure is felt.
- 5-6: The close gestures at the opening but doesn't fully mirror it.
- Below 5: No circular structure. The story just ends.

### Checkpoint 10: PS Architecture (/10)

Second sales letter in story voice handling 3-5 objections.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: PS is in full narrator voice. Handles 3-5 real objections. Never breaks character. Addresses price, skepticism, time-to-results, and concept-specific concerns. Feels like the narrator still talking, not a copywriter listing objection handles.
- 7-8: PS is in voice and handles objections. One or two moments feel slightly constructed.
- 5-6: PS is present but breaks voice, or handles too few objections, or feels like a FAQ.
- Below 5: No PS, or PS is clearly an ad appendage.

### Checkpoint 11: Sentence Architecture (/10)

Short beats, heavy dialogue, physical numbers, sensory details. Third-grade reading level.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: Every sentence is instantly clear. Heavy dialogue. Short paragraphs. Physical details ground every scene. Numbers are concrete. Reading level is consistently third-grade.
- 7-8: Mostly clean. Occasional sentences that are too long or complex.
- 5-6: Reading level drifts. Some sections feel written rather than spoken.
- Below 5: The writing is too polished, too complex, or too literary.

### Checkpoint 12: Word Count as Feature (/10)

Trojan Horse: 4,000-8,000 words. Insider Provocation: 5,000-10,000 words.

**Scoring:**
- 9-10: Word count is in range AND every word earns its place. No padding. No sections that exist only for length. The story NEEDS this many words.
- 7-8: In range. Minimal padding. One or two sections could be tighter.
- 5-6: In range but noticeably padded, or outside range.
- Below 5: Significantly outside range, or heavy padding throughout.

## Section 3: Mechanism Chain (TheraBelt-Specific)

The Amala-native mechanism is SIMPLER than evolve-native. No full AGEs/RAGE chain. No catch-22. The credible insider explains what the light does, simply and conversationally.

### The simplified chain for Amala-native:

1. **Every cell has an engine** (mitochondria). That engine makes energy (ATP). Energy is what lets the cell repair itself, fight damage, and do its job.

2. **As dogs age, the engine gets jammed.** Inflammation, oxidative stress, just years of wear. The engine still works. It's just stuck. Like a car engine with a fouled spark plug.

3. **Without energy, cells can't repair.** They can't fight damage. They can't maintain the coat, the joints, the organs. The dog slows down. Not because something is broken. Because the cells don't have the energy to keep things running.

4. **Red light (660nm + 850nm) goes straight to the engine.** Through skin. Through fur. Doesn't need digestion. Doesn't need the cell to already have energy. The light hits the jammed part (cytochrome c oxidase) and clears it.

5. **The cell didn't get energy FROM the light.** It got its ability to make energy BACK. "Unjamming the engine, not giving it gas."

6. **That's why nothing else worked.** Better food, supplements, vet care, they all send MATERIALS to the cell. But the cell needs ENERGY to use them. Without the engine running, materials pile up unused. Light doesn't send materials. It fixes the engine. Then everything else the owner is already doing actually works.

### Delivery rules:
- Through the credible insider's dialogue. Never narration.
- 400-800 words max. This is not the evolve-native deep dive.
- Use analogies. Car engine. Clogged pipe. Dead battery that still holds a charge if you jump it.
- The insider explains SIMPLY. She's talking to a friend (the victim or narrator), not presenting at a conference.
- Never say "red light therapy" until this section. Build the problem first.
- Never cross into aging language. "Slows aging" is banned. "Cells making energy again" is the ceiling.
- The "dogs age faster so it works faster" reframe is optional. Use only if natural.

### Copy Architecture (Four Errors)
1. **Movies vs mirrors** — is the reader seeing her own life reflected through the victim's story, or watching a movie? If the story completes as entertainment, the reader scrolls.
2. **Emotional peak** — is the emotional high point on the transformation (the named gifts returning), or on the human relationship? The peak must be on what the product made possible.
3. **Proxy-to-self transfer** — does the reader move from empathizing with the victim to thinking about HER dog? The proxy structure must transfer by Act 3. If the reader only feels for the victim's dog, projection failed.
4. **Moral urgency** — does the reader feel that NOT acting would be wrong? The narrator's moral obligation must transfer to the reader. "If I knew this and didn't tell you" energy.
