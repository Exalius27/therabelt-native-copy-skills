## What This Skill Does

Generates a long-form first-person narrative ad for TheraBelt (red light therapy belt for dogs, by Lucavel) using the Amala architecture. Output is a single narrative (~4,000-10,000 words) that reads like a genuine human interest story. The reader must not realize they are reading an ad until they are emotionally invested beyond the point of leaving.

**How this differs from `/story-native` and `/evolve-native`:**
- Story-native uses identity-challenge architecture (Luhxe style). ~1,400-1,800 words. Caretaker identity thread. "Your move" or emotional callback close.
- Evolve-native uses villain architecture (V4). ~1,800-3,000 words. Big Pet Food threat + catch-22. Information-as-urgency close.
- Amala-native uses proxy-witness architecture. ~4,500-7,000 words. The narrator WITNESSES someone else's dog transform. Three-act structure with a 2,000-word blind spot. Seed/Take/Return of named gifts. Product introduced through a credentialed insider at 60-80% mark. Moral obligation close.

## Core Principles

These five principles govern everything in this skill. Every decision, outline, scene, dialogue, structure must pass through them. They are not guidelines. They are constraints.

### 1. The 2,000-Word Blind Spot

The reader must not know this is an ad for at least 2,000 words. No product. No health angle. No mechanism. No hint of what's coming. The opening is a HUMAN story that happens to involve a dog. The reader enters because the story demands attention, not because she has a problem.

If a reader could guess "this is an ad for a dog product" before the 2,000-word mark, the entry mechanism has failed. Full stop.

### 2. Proxy Story Structure

The primary emotional content happens to someone OTHER than the narrator. The narrator is a witness. She observes, reports, and is moved by what she sees, but she does not own the problem.

Three parties, always:
- **The Victim** — carries the wound. Her dog has ONE defining trait that is being systematically destroyed. This is not the narrator's dog.
- **The Credible Insider** — a doctor, vet, researcher, ex-industry professional, pharmacist, or specialist. This person introduces the mechanism and eventually the product. They have earned authority through professional experience, not through passion or belief.
- **The Bridge** — connects the narrator to the victim. Often a mutual friend, family member, neighbor, or coworker. The bridge creates the proximity that makes the narrator a witness.

The narrator never seeks the product for herself during the story. She watches. She learns through someone else's crisis. By the end, she is telling the reader what she witnessed because withholding it would be wrong.

### 3. The Identity Wound (One Trait Destroyed)

The victim has ONE defining trait. The thing everyone knows her for. The thing that IS her. And her dog's decline systematically destroys that one thing.

Examples of the architecture (NOT to be reused, just to show the pattern):
- A woman known for her hands (she paints, she gardens, she touches everything with care) whose arthritic dog can no longer walk with her to the garden. The walks WERE the painting ritual. The dog's decline took the garden. The garden took the hands. The hands took who she is.
- A retired teacher known for her morning routine with her dog. The routine IS her identity post-retirement. The dog's decline dismantles the routine piece by piece.

The wound must be SPECIFIC. "She loves her dog" is not a wound. "She is the woman who walks the creek trail at 6am every morning with her Golden, and the whole neighborhood sets their clock by her" is a wound, because when the dog can't walk the trail, the neighborhood notices, and her identity cracks.

### 4. The Healthy Dog (Silent Killer Architecture)

The dog MUST appear completely healthy before the crisis. No visible decline. No mobility issues. No aging signs beyond a white muzzle. The dog walks, eats, wags, plays. The vet says he's fine. Bloodwork is normal. Nobody is worried.

**Warning signs exist but look completely normal.** Grass eating, paw licking, a dog smell. These are NOT presented as concerning. NOT taken to the vet. NOT diagnosed as anything. They are just things dogs do. "Dogs eat grass." "He's a dog. Dogs smell." Nobody thinks twice. The owner may mention them in passing, casually, like weather. The narrator doesn't notice them. The reader doesn't flag them.

**The crisis is SUDDEN.** A collapse. An emergency vet visit. Out of nowhere. "He was fine yesterday. He was eating. He was walking around three days ago. How does a dog go from fine to the emergency room floor in one night?" This shock is what drives the story forward. Not a gradual decline.

**AFTER the crisis, the credible insider reframes everything.** She asks about the "normal" behaviors: Does he eat grass? Does he lick his paws? Does he smell? Then she reveals they were all the same thing — chronic inflammation hiding in plain sight for years. This retroactive reveal is the fear mechanism. The reader's dog probably does the same things. The reader just called them normal.

**This is the kibble-as-silent-killer positioning:** the dog looks healthy while being destroyed from the inside. Bloodwork doesn't catch inflammation until organs start failing. The vet keeps saying everything is fine. Then one night the dog is on the emergency room floor and everyone says "how did this happen so fast?" It didn't happen fast. It happened over years. You just couldn't see it.

### 5. Seed / Take / Return

Plant specific NAMED objects, rituals, or behaviors in Act 1. Take them away in Act 2. Return them in Act 3.

These must be concrete and named. Not "they used to walk together" but "the green leash on the hook by the back door." Not "the dog was energetic" but "he'd steal her gardening gloves every morning and she'd chase him around the yard before coffee."

The reader remembers the named thing from Act 1. When it disappears in Act 2, she feels the loss. When it returns in Act 3, the return IS the proof. No claim needed. The named thing is back. That's the transformation.

Plant at minimum THREE named gifts in Act 1. All three must be taken in Act 2. All three must return in Act 3. The reader tracks them unconsciously.

### 6. Moral Obligation Close

The CTA energy is NOT competitive ("Your move"), NOT information-urgency ("now that you know"), and NOT direct ("if you have a senior dog...").

The CTA is moral obligation. The narrator tells the reader because NOT telling her would be wrong. "I'm telling you this because someone told me, and it changed everything I just described." The narrator is passing along what she witnessed. Withholding it would make her complicit in the reader's ignorance.

The close circles back to the opening scene. The story ends where it began. The moral is delivered by a secondary character, not the narrator. The narrator's final lines return to the opening image, transformed.

## Two Valid Entry Mechanisms

Every Amala-native ad uses one of these two entries. No exceptions.

### Mechanism A: Trojan Horse

The opening has ZERO connection to health, dogs, or wellness. It's a purely human story. A wallet found in a parking lot. A neighbor's house fire. A retirement party that goes wrong. Something that pulls the reader into a HUMAN situation.

The dog enters naturally through the human story. The health angle surfaces even later. The reader is 2,000+ words deep in a story about human relationships before she realizes there's a dog health thread.

**Rules for Trojan Horse:**
- First 500 words: zero dog content. Pure human story.
- Words 500-1,500: dog enters as a background character in the human story.
- Words 1,500-2,500: the health thread begins to surface through the victim's situation.
- Product appears at 60-80% mark.
- Target length: 4,000-8,000 words.

### Mechanism B: Insider Provocation

A credentialed insider speaks against her own system in the opening lines. The provocation is so specific and so contrary to her own self-interest that the reader cannot look away.

Example energy (NOT to be reused): "I worked in pet food for eleven years. I have a dog. I feed him kibble. And every morning I hate myself for it."

The insider's self-indictment creates instant trust because she is sacrificing her own status. The reader thinks: "If someone FROM that world is saying this, it must be real."

**Rules for Insider Provocation:**
- First line is the provocation. Credentialed insider speaking against her own industry/profession.
- The insider becomes a character in the story, not just a voice.
- The proxy story still applies. The insider witnesses someone else's transformation.
- The 2,000-word blind spot applies to the PRODUCT, not the health angle. Health content can surface earlier because the insider's credential earns it.
- Product appears at 60-80% mark.
- Target length: 5,000-10,000 words.

## Three-Act Structure

### Act 1: The Incident (0-35% of word count)

World-building. Zero product signal. The reader bonds with characters through witnessed detail.

Required elements:
- Entry mechanism (Trojan Horse or Insider Provocation) fully executed
- The victim introduced with her ONE defining trait shown in action
- The three named gifts planted through specific scenes
- The narrator's relationship to the victim established (through the bridge or directly)
- The victim's dog shown as integral to the defining trait
- At least two scenes with physical detail, dialogue, and sensory specifics

The reader must WANT to keep reading because the HUMAN story is compelling, not because she senses a health angle coming.

### Act 2: The Discovery (35-70% of word count)

The named gifts have already disappeared (the dog stopped going out, stopped the rituals — but the dog looked HEALTHY the whole time). The identity wound is already open from the dog's disengagement, not from visible sickness. Then the crisis hits. The system fails. The insider reveals what was hiding in plain sight. The product enters.

Required elements:
- The named gifts already gone — the dog stopped going out, stopped the rituals. But the dog looks fine. Walks, eats, wags. The vet says normal. Nobody is alarmed.
- The defining trait cracking because the dog disengaged, not because the dog is visibly sick.
- The SUDDEN CRISIS: the dog collapses out of nowhere. Emergency vet. Organ stress (liver, kidneys). Total shock. "He was fine three days ago."
- The system villain: the vet system that kept saying "everything is normal" while the dog was being destroyed from the inside. Not evil doctors. Doctors who genuinely couldn't see it because bloodwork doesn't catch chronic inflammation until organs fail.
- The credible insider enters AFTER the crisis and reveals the warning signs. She asks about normal dog behaviors (grass eating, paw licking, dog smell) and reframes them as chronic inflammation signals hiding in plain sight. This is the retroactive reveal that makes the reader's stomach drop — her dog probably does the same things.
- The insider delivers the FULL kibble mechanism through dialogue: AGEs from extreme-temp cooking → 122x exposure → coats mitochondria → cells can't make energy → organs fail silently → Mars owns food AND hospitals → vet training funded by kibble companies. The narrator asks questions. The insider explains in plain language.
- The elimination path is handled BY the insider through dialogue, not through the victim trying things. The insider explains why better food and supplements can't work: the cell can't use what it's getting because the engine is clogged. You're sending fuel to a car that won't start.
- Product introduction through the credible insider at the 60-80% mark. Casual. Not a pitch. The insider mentions it because it's the logical conclusion of the mechanism she just explained.

### Act 3: The Transformation (70-100% of word count)

The named gifts return. The transformation is shown, measured, and witnessed. The story circles back.

Required elements:
- Named gifts return one by one through scenes, not summaries
- The transformation is witnessed by multiple independent observers (narrator, neighbors, the vet who failed earlier, strangers)
- The vet's reaction: confusion, then quiet acknowledgment. "I don't know what you changed, but don't stop." The vet never endorses the product by name.
- The defining trait restored. The victim IS herself again, proven through the same specific actions from Act 1.
- Circular close: the final scene mirrors the opening scene, transformed. Same location, same characters, different outcome.
- Moral delivered by a secondary character, not the narrator
- PS Architecture: after the close, 3-5 paragraphs in story voice handling objections. Still in character. Still the narrator talking. Addresses: price, skepticism, "is it real," time to results, and one objection specific to the concept.

## Workflow

### 1. Parse Input

Extract from the user's input:

- **Concept** (required) — the human story situation, the angle
- **Target avatar** (required) — which avatar file to load
- **Entry mechanism** (required) — Trojan Horse or Insider Provocation. If not specified, recommend based on concept and ask for confirmation.

If any are missing, ask before proceeding. Do not assume or default.

### 2. Load Knowledge

Read files in two tiers. **Always load** the core files. **Reference on demand** the rest.

#### Always load (read before writing):
- `wiki/story-craft.md` — what makes the story itself work. Read first.
- `wiki/vicious-hooks.md` — hook framework. **Must be read before writing any hook. No exceptions.**
- `wiki/avatar-{N}.md` — only the avatar file the user specified
- `wiki/direct-response-copywriting.md` — belief chains, story architecture
- `wiki/positioning.md` — mechanism details, positioning chain

#### Auto-search relevant knowledge:
Before writing, read `wiki/index.md` and scan for pages relevant to the concept + avatar. Read the 2-3 most relevant wiki pages.

#### Reference on demand:
- `wiki/company-overview.md` — brand, product name, core benefit
- `wiki/story-native-format.md` — structural rules that still apply
- `wiki/copy-structure.md` — lead/product buildup structure
- `wiki/luhxe-ad-craft.md` — structural principles from reference ads

Confirm to the user which avatar, entry mechanism, and concept you are writing for.

### 3. Validate the Concept

Before outlining or writing anything, test the concept against all five of these:

1. **Does the human story stand alone?** Would someone read 2,000 words of this story even if it never became an ad? If the human situation isn't compelling on its own, stop.
2. **Does it support a proxy structure?** Can the narrator witness someone else's dog transform? If the concept requires the narrator to be the dog owner, it belongs in `/story-native`, not here.
3. **Is there a clear identity wound?** Can you name the victim's ONE defining trait and show how the dog's decline destroys it? If the wound is vague ("she's sad"), stop.
4. **Can you plant three named gifts?** Are there specific, concrete objects/rituals/behaviors that can be seeded, taken, and returned? If the concept only supports vague "they used to do things together," it's not specific enough.
5. **Does the concept support 4,000+ words without padding?** The length is a feature, not a target. If the concept runs out of story at 3,000 words, it belongs in a shorter format.

If the concept doesn't pass all five, stop. Work with the user to find a stronger concept before proceeding.

### 4. Outline the Story

Sketch the three-act outline. For each act, note:

**Act 1:**
- Entry mechanism and opening scene
- The victim's defining trait (shown in action, not told)
- The three named gifts (specific objects, rituals, or behaviors)
- The bridge character and how the narrator connects to the victim
- The dog's role in the defining trait

**Act 2:**
- Named gifts already gone (dog disengaged from rituals, but looks healthy)
- The defining trait cracking because the dog disengaged, not because the dog looks sick
- The sudden crisis (collapse, emergency vet, organ stress)
- The system villain: vet system that said "normal" while organs were failing silently
- The credible insider: who they are, how they enter, what credential they carry
- The warning sign reveal: insider asks about normal dog behaviors, reframes as inflammation
- The full kibble mechanism delivery (through dialogue)
- The elimination path handled by the insider through dialogue (why food/supplements can't work)
- Product introduction moment (when, how, through whom)

**Act 3:**
- How each named gift returns (specific scenes)
- The defining trait restored (shown through the same actions as Act 1)
- Independent witnesses to the transformation
- The circular close (which scene from Act 1 is mirrored)
- The moral and who delivers it
- PS objections to handle

### 5. Check the 13 Required Elements

Before drafting, verify every element has a home in the outline:

1. **Entry mechanism** — Trojan Horse or Insider Provocation, fully executed
2. **Hidden mirror** — a thematic seed in the opening that gains meaning by the end. The reader won't notice it on first read. On reflection, it was there all along.
3. **Witness structure** — narrator observes, never owns the problem
4. **Identity wound** — one defining trait systematically destroyed
5. **System villain** — the vet system that kept saying "normal" while the dog was being destroyed from the inside. Not evil doctors. Doctors who couldn't see it because bloodwork doesn't catch chronic inflammation until organs fail.
6. **Sudden crisis** — the dog collapses out of nowhere. Emergency vet. Organ stress. Total shock. The dog was fine yesterday. This replaces gradual decline.
7. **Retroactive warning sign reveal** — the credible insider asks about normal dog behaviors (grass eating, paw licking, dog smell) AFTER the crisis and reframes them as chronic inflammation signals. The elimination path is handled by the insider through dialogue (why food/supplements can't work), not through the victim trying things.
8. **Product introduction** — through trusted chain (credible insider), at 60-80% mark, feels like a recommendation not a pitch
9. **Transformation mirror** — return of the three named gifts, one by one, through scenes
10. **Circular close** — story ends where it began, transformed
11. **PS architecture** — 3-5 paragraphs in story voice handling objections after the close
12. **Sentence architecture** — short beats, heavy dialogue, physical numbers, sensory details. Third-grade reading level.
13. **Word count as feature** — Trojan Horse: 4,000-8,000. Insider Provocation: 5,000-10,000. The length IS the disguise.

### 6. Write the Draft

**Start by reading `reference.md`.** It contains detailed craft rules for every aspect of the story. You MUST read it before writing any draft.

**The five Core Principles govern the entire draft.** Every scene, every line of dialogue, every beat must pass through them.

**The story must be genuinely interesting without the product.** If you strip TheraBelt out entirely, would someone still want to read this story? Would they share it? If not, the concept isn't strong enough or the execution is flat.

**The whole thing is a story.** There is no point where the story stops and the ad begins. The product, the proof, the mechanism, the urgency, the close, all of it lives inside the narrative. If the reader ever feels the shift from "I'm reading a story" to "I'm being sold something," the spell is broken.

**Length is a structural tool, not padding.** Every paragraph must earn its place. The reader should never feel the story dragging. If a section exists only to hit a word count, cut it and let the story breathe elsewhere. 4,500 well-paced words beats 7,000 padded ones.

**The narrator is telling this story to the reader because she HAS to.** The energy is: "I saw something. I need to tell someone. You need to hear this." Not selling. Witnessing.

### 7. Self-Review

Before presenting, run the full self-review from `reference.md`:

**First: the 13-Checkpoint Evaluator.**
Score the draft against all 13 checkpoints. Total must be 115+/130. No checkpoint below 7/10. If any checkpoint fails, revise before presenting.

**Second: the Amala-specific checks:**
- Is the 2,000-word blind spot maintained? Can the reader guess it's an ad before 2,000 words?
- Is the proxy structure intact? Does the narrator ever own the problem?
- Are all three named gifts seeded in Act 1, taken in Act 2, returned in Act 3?
- Is the identity wound specific? One trait, shown in action?
- Is the system villain institutional, not personal?
- Is the product introduced through a credible insider at 60-80%?
- Does the circular close mirror the opening?
- Does the PS handle 3-5 objections in story voice?
- Is the CTA moral obligation, not competitive or direct?

**Third: the craft checks (shared with other skills):**
- Any em dashes? Remove.
- Any dialogue tags or stage directions? Remove.
- Any AI filler phrases? Remove.
- Any rule-of-three patterns? Break.
- Any poetic or literary lines? Rewrite as plain speech.
- Any marketing language in narrator voice? Remove.
- Third-grade reading level throughout?
- Any timestamped transformation? Remove. Scenes only.

### 8. Present

Output the story as a single block of text. No headers, no metadata inside the ad.

After the story, present:

1. **Metadata:** Avatar used, word count, entry mechanism, concept angle, credible insider type
2. **Named gifts:** List the three planted/taken/returned gifts
3. **13-Checkpoint Scorecard:** Score each checkpoint out of /10. Total out of /130. Flag any below 8.
4. **Confirm** all 13 required elements present.

## Guardrails

### Transferred (from story-native and evolve-native, still apply):
- Never invent testimonials or fake customer quotes
- Never make medical claims. TheraBelt supports wellness, does not treat or cure disease.
- Never use marketing language. If it sounds like a Facebook ad, rewrite it.
- Never fabricate scientific data or studies
- Source all product details from the knowledge base files
- Product name is **TheraBelt**. Company name is **Lucavel**. Verify spelling.
- **TheraBelt stands alone. Always.** Never frame TheraBelt as a complement to supplements or part of a stack.
- No bullet lists or numbered features inside the story. Prose only.
- Urgency must be narrative, not promotional
- **Never label what the product does.** No "slows aging," "anti-aging," "turns back the clock."
- **No rule-of-three patterns.** Two max, never three.
- **No timestamped transformation.** Show transformation through scenes. Time passes through narrative.
- **No marketing language in narrator voice.** "Pitch," "invest," "journey," "game-changer" all banned.
- **No poetic dog interpretation.** Describe what the dog does. Never interpret what the dog thinks or feels.
- **No CTA text after the PS.** The PS ends on the story. No brand name sitting alone.
- **Frame decline as time/ritual disappearing, not physical inability.**
- **ZERO dialogue tags. ZERO stage directions.**
- **No conspiracy language.** Facts only. "They don't want you to know" is banned.

### New (Amala-native specific):
- **The narrator is a WITNESS, not the dog owner.** If the narrator owns the problem dog, this is `/story-native`, not `/amala-native`. The narrator may have her own dog, but it is NOT the dog that transforms.
- **Three named gifts minimum.** If you can only plant two, the concept doesn't support Amala architecture. Push back on the concept.
- **The 2,000-word blind spot is non-negotiable.** No product, no health angle, no mechanism before 2,000 words in a Trojan Horse entry. For Insider Provocation, the product blind spot still holds (health angle can surface earlier through the insider's credential).
- **Product enters at 60-80% mark.** Not before 60%. Not after 80%. If the product enters earlier, the story didn't earn enough emotional investment. If later, there isn't enough room for transformation scenes.
- **The credible insider must have a REAL credential.** Not "she read a lot about it" or "she's really into wellness." She's a vet, a researcher, a pharmacist, a formulation chemist, a nutritional scientist. Earned authority only.
- **The system villain is institutional.** Never attack an individual vet or doctor. The system is the villain. The professionals inside it are doing their best with what they were taught.
- **The circular close is mandatory.** The final scene must mirror the opening. Same place, same characters, different outcome. If the opening is at a park, the close is at the park. If the opening is at a kitchen table, the close is at the kitchen table.
- **The PS is a second sales letter in story voice.** It handles objections. It's still the narrator talking. It never breaks character. 3-5 short paragraphs after the close.
- **Moral obligation CTA only.** Never "Your move." Never "If you have a senior dog..." Never competitive energy. The narrator shares because withholding would be wrong.
- **No identity-challenge architecture.** No "what kind of owner" line. No recognition moments. No caretaker identity thread. No invisible effort threading. These belong in `/story-native`.
- **No catch-22 architecture.** No AGEs/RAGE chain delivered as the primary conversion engine. No "everything you try runs into the same wall." That belongs in `/evolve-native`. The mechanism in Amala-native is delivered more simply through the credible insider, focused on what the light does to cells, not on what kibble does to them.

## Notes

- This skill is conversational. The user may iterate on the draft.
- If the user's concept conflicts with guardrails, flag it and suggest an alternative.
- **When iterating on drafts:** only change what the user specifically called out. If they didn't comment on a line, leave it. If they said they liked something, do not touch it.
- **This skill produces the longest ads in the system.** The length is a feature, not a bug. But every word must earn its place. Padding is the fastest way to kill an Amala-style ad.
- **The 13-checkpoint evaluator is built into the self-review.** Every draft must score 115+/130 before presenting. This is not optional.
