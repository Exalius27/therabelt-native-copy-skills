# Creative Strategist — Reference Material

Read this file during execution when building and evaluating concepts.

## Subreddit Selection Guide

Pick 2-3 based on the sketch:

- Dog owner emotional territory: `dogs`, `OldManDog`, `seniordogs`, `DogAdvice`
- Identity and wellness overlap: `DogCare`, `rawpetfood`, `Pets`
- Life situations with emotional stakes: `TrueOffMyChest`, `offmychest`, `CasualConversation`, `MomForAMinute`
- Aging and loss territory: `OldManDog`, `Petloss`, `GriefSupport`
- Active lifestyle contexts: `RunningWithDogs`, `hiking`, `Agility`
- If the sketch suggests other communities, use them.

## Concept Criteria (Step 8)

**Five things must be true simultaneously for the concept to work:**
1. **The reader lands in a situation already in motion** — something changed and they need to see what happens next.
2. **The concept enables projection, not just empathy.** The reader must think about HER dog, not just feel for the character's. This means the concept must structurally enable three mechanisms:
   - **Comparison triggers** — the concept involves specific ages, breeds, or behaviors that make the reader compare to her own dog. A "10-year-old golden" makes the reader with a 9-year-old lab think about hers.
   - **Universal specific moments** — moments the target avatar has her own version of. Not generic emotions — specific moments she recognizes from her own life.
   - **Real-time discovery** — the concept allows the narrator to discover alongside the reader, not retell a finished story. If the narrator already knows the answer before the story starts, the reader is passive audience to a completed journey.
   A concept where the narrator is ahead of the reader (already bought, already seen results, already knows) can't produce real-time discovery. A concept with no other dogs, no age references, no breed specifics can't produce comparison triggers. Design the concept so these mechanisms are possible.
3. **The story lives in the right territory** — TheraBelt is anti-aging, elevation, quality of life. The woman's investment in keeping her dog active and aging well must be the thing the story is about — what people are fighting over, misunderstanding, or being moved by. Not background. Not one item on a list. The actual subject. A story that works without any reference to the dog's health and what she does to maintain it is in the wrong territory, no matter how compelling it is.
4. **The concept contains the seeds of an observable anomaly.** The dog's condition must be something that can be WITNESSED by multiple independent people — not private, not internal, not only visible to the owner. The concept needs a dog in a context where other people see it, react to it, and independently confirm something that shouldn't be possible. This is the structural setup for undeniable demonstration. If the concept isolates the dog and owner from witnesses, the story can't stack evidence from enough angles.
5. **The owner's identity is at stake.** The dog's condition must reflect who the owner IS — not just how the dog feels. "I'm the owner who does the best for my dog. Now my dog shows it." If the concept works the same whether the owner invested in her dog's health or not, there's no buyer identity. The dog must be evidence of the owner, not just a beneficiary.

**The concept must contain:**
- **Who the character is** — not "a dog mom" but a specific version: her situation, what makes her this person
- **The specific situation** — what is happening or has happened. Something changed. The reader needs to see what comes next.
- **The emotional stakes** — why does the reader care? Not dramatic life-or-death stakes necessarily — "I miss the thing my dog used to do" is enough when it's specific and universal enough that millions of people feel it in their gut.

**The concept must NOT be:**
- A mood ("I love my aging dog")
- A theme ("the guilt of dog ownership")
- A product angle ("woman discovers red light therapy")
- A generic scenario that could apply to anyone
- A character biography or static portrait — describing who someone is and what their routine looks like is not a concept. "A retired woman who does everything for her dog" is a description. Nothing is happening.
- A single moment without forward motion ("dog reveals her routine at book club"). A concept needs trajectory — something changed, something is changing, or something is about to change. There must be a before and an after, or a question the reader needs answered about what happens next.
- A setup for a product pitch — if the concept only exists to create a gap for the product to fill, it's not a concept. It's a sales funnel disguised as a story. The concept must work as a story someone would read and engage with even if the product didn't exist.
- A setup for a claim delivery — if the concept only works because someone eventually explains what the product does, it's not a concept. It's a sales pitch with a story wrapper. The concept must work through what the reader SEES (the dog's condition, people's reactions, the observable anomaly), not what she's TOLD (what the product does, how it works, what the claim is).
- A plot sequence with dialogue. The concept describes the situation and stakes — not what characters say to each other, not scene-by-scene beats, not how the confrontation plays out. If the concept reads like a screenplay or story outline with "she says X, he says Y, then Z happens," it has crossed into story-native's territory. The concept is WHAT is happening and WHY it matters. HOW it unfolds is the writer's job.
- **A compelling story in the wrong territory.** A concept can have real human stakes, real characters, and genuine forward motion — and still fail because the story has nothing to do with TheraBelt. A granddaughter drawing the dog bigger than the grandchildren is an interesting story. A friend accusing her of hiding behind the dog is an interesting story. Neither has anything to do with anti-aging or the woman's investment in her dog's health. Interesting is not enough. The story must be about something TheraBelt belongs to.

**The scroll test:** Before writing the concept paragraph, summarize it in one sentence that follows this structure: "[specific person] [does/discovers/faces something specific] and now [consequence or question]." If you can only describe it as "[person] has [feeling] about [thing]" or "[person] is [description of who they are]," you have a mood or a portrait, not a concept. Rewrite until the sentence has an active verb and a forward-facing consequence.

Example of a mood: "A retired woman's whole day revolves around her aging dog and one morning the dog is at the door."
Why it fails: Nothing is at stake. No one needs to know what happens next. "She watches him" is not forward motion.

Example of a situation: "A retired woman who has been quietly doing everything for her aging dog gets cornered at a family dinner when her sister-in-law asks why she spends more on the dog's supplements than on herself."
Why it works: There's a confrontation. The reader needs to see how she responds. The situation forces something into the open.

## Gate 2 — Concept Quality Questions (Step 9)

Ask yourself:
0. **Does this concept activate the mass desire?** The mass desire is the foundation. If you removed it, would the story collapse? If the story works just as well without the reader wanting [the mass desire], the concept is disconnected. A compelling story that doesn't channel the mass desire will get engagement but zero sales. This is the first check — if it fails, nothing else matters.
1. Describe what HAPPENS using an active verb that isn't "is," "feels," or "watches." If the strongest verb is "notices" or "realizes," you have an observation, not a story.
2. What is the reader waiting to find out? State it in one sentence. If you can't, there's no forward motion. "How does this morning make her feel?" is not a question that demands an answer. "What happens when her daughter confronts her about it?" is.
3. Could I picture a real person this happened to? Is there enough specificity?
4. Would this concept generate comments on Facebook? Would people tag friends? Would they argue about it? If the only response is "that's sweet" or "I relate," it's not interesting enough.
5. Would the target avatar see herself in this? Not just feel for the character, but recognize her own life?
6. Can you identify the SPECIFIC scene or moment where the product enters? "Somewhere in her routine" is not specific enough. Point to the moment. If you can't, the concept doesn't have real product space. Then ask: if you deleted the product from the concept entirely, would the story be different? If the story works without it, the product has no real presence — the concept is in the wrong territory.
7. Does the concept have ONE emotional throughline? If it has a relationship arc AND a product/transformation arc AND both need breathing room, the story will collapse. Secondary elements should be texture, not storylines that need their own resolution.
8. **If this is a behavioral pattern concept** (dog used to do X, stopped, product restores X): did the behavior involve other people — was it a shared ritual between family, guests, or a relationship? If the owner is the only one who noticed it and the only one who notices its return, it's a dead pattern. A dog who stole shoes while a daughter warned every guest at the door is a family ritual. A dog who patrolled the hallway at night while the owner watched is a private observation. Only the former passes.
9. **Mirror or movie?** Will the reader watch someone else's life from the outside, or see her own reflected back? If the concept puts the reader in a passive audience position watching a completed story — narrator already knows the ending, already bought, already saw results — it's a movie. The concept must enable the reader to be an active participant: comparing to her own dog, wondering what happens next, discovering alongside the narrator.
10. **Where is the emotional peak?** Is the most powerful moment in the concept about the relationship/character drama, or about the product anomaly? If the most emotional beat is the narrator hugging her dog, the reunion, the sad backstory — the product is background decoration. The emotional peak must be the moment of undeniable demonstration: the impossible thing that can't be explained away, the reaction from someone who can't believe what they're seeing.
11. **Does the buyer become someone?** Not "learn something" or "feel moved" — become a different person in her own eyes. The concept must enable a transformation where the owner becomes visible proof of who she always believed she was. If the concept ends with the owner feeling grateful or relieved but unchanged in identity, there's no buyer transformation.

All twelve must pass.

## Presentation Templates (Step 11)

**If both gates passed:**

```
## Mass Desire

[The mass desire this concept is built on. One sentence.]

## Concept

[One paragraph: who the character is, the specific situation, the emotional stakes. 3-6 sentences. This is what gets fed into /story-native.]

## Usable Details

[Specific real moments from the research that could live inside this story. Organized by which part of the concept they map to. Each one includes the detail itself and a brief note on what makes it real. These are raw material for the writer — not scene directions, not writing instructions. 5-8 details.]

## Reasoning

[Why you believe this concept works. What you found in research — which posts, which comments, which patterns. What makes the situation compelling, why it connects to this avatar, what makes it a story someone needs to read.]
```

The reasoning must reference specific things found in research. Not generic statements like "this concept works because it's emotional." Articulate the *specific* reason.

**If either gate failed after retry:**

```
## What I Tried

[Honest explanation of the research angles attempted, what subreddits and queries were used, what was found, and why nothing landed as a strong concept. Include anything from the research that might be useful as a starting point for the user to redirect.]
```

## Guardrails

- Never fabricate Reddit quotes or stories. Every research finding must come from actual `/reddit-researcher` results. If research is thin, say so.
- Never make medical claims. Concepts should not position TheraBelt as treating or curing disease. The concept is about the human situation, not the product claim.
- Never search for TheraBelt, Lucavel, or red light therapy on Reddit. The research is about finding human situations, not product discussions.
- One concept per run. No ranked lists, no "here are three options."
- Do not present the concept as a story outline, a hook, or a draft. The concept is the situation — story-native handles everything else. No dialogue, no scene-by-scene beats, no "she says X, he says Y." If it reads like a screenplay, it's not a concept.
- Do not summarize the avatar back to the user. Absorb it and use it.
- If `/reddit-researcher` returns errors or empty results for a subreddit, tell the user rather than inventing findings.
- Quality gates are internal. Do not narrate the gate-checking process unless both retries fail.
- **Maximum 2 subreddits per run, 3-6 deep-reads total.** Targeted research with fewer sources beats broad research with many.
- **Elevation, not problem-solving.** TheraBelt is not a fix. It's what the owner gives because the dog deserves the best. Do not build concepts around problems that need solving, decline that needs reversing, or crises that need intervention. The product elevates — it doesn't rescue.
- **Stay in your lane.** You are a concept builder, not a copywriter. Do not use terms like "scroll-stopper," do not predict what the hook or lead will be, do not describe what the reader will feel. Build the situation. Usable Details are raw material — real moments and details from the research that a writer could pick up and use. They are not scene directions, dialogue suggestions, or instructions for how to write the story. Story-native handles everything after.
- **Do not invent emotional territory that isn't in the research.** If the research shows happy, fulfilled dog owners, do not build a concept around frustration and disappointment. Synthesizing and combining research into something new is expected — inventing emotions that contradict what you found is not.
- **Reality-check every physical detail against the avatar.** You can invent actions and details that make sense for who the avatar is — that's part of building a concept. But before writing any physical action into the concept, ask: could this specific person actually do this? A 60-year-old retired woman is not carrying a dog home. If the detail fails a basic common-sense check for the avatar's age, body, and life, cut it.
- **If the sketch doesn't sharpen after 2-5 messages, stop.** Tell the user the direction isn't producing something research-ready and suggest a different angle. Do not force-build around a weak direction.
- **Never build a concept from the avatar file.** The avatar tells you who the reader is — it does not contain stories or usable details.
- **No portraits, routines, or "day in the life" structures.** See the "must NOT be" list above. If nothing disrupts, reveals, or forces a confrontation, it's not a concept.
- **Product space must be specific, not gestural.** Point to the exact moment where the product enters. "Somewhere in her routine" is a handwave.
- **Wrong territory is a concept failure, not a product problem.** A concept can be interesting, emotionally real, and well-researched — and still be wrong for TheraBelt because the story has nothing to do with anti-aging or the woman's investment in her dog's quality of life. This is not a product placement problem. It's a territory problem. No amount of adding the product into the concept will fix it. The story must be about the right thing from the start.
- **Never frame the concept around a claim.** The concept is a situation where the product's effect is visible, not a setup for explaining what the product does. If the concept's forward motion depends on someone saying or learning what the product does, it's a claim delivery vehicle, not a story concept. The concept must work through observable evidence — a dog that defies expectations, people reacting to what they see — not through anyone explaining, revealing, or announcing what the product did.
