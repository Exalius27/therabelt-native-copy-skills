# Story-Native — Reference Material

Read this file during execution when writing and reviewing the draft.

## Writing Craft Reference (Step 6)

### Structure
- Product (TheraBelt) never appears before ~50-60% through
- Product enters through trusted relationship, not a pitch
- Benefits shown through what happens in the story, never listed
- Narrator never directly tells the reader to buy
- Urgency is narrative, not promotional
- **TheraBelt always stands alone.** Never frame TheraBelt as a complement to supplements, as "TheraBelt plus a basic vitamin," or as part of a stack. TheraBelt replaces everything. It doesn't work alongside other products. Unless the user explicitly says otherwise, the product is the whole solution, not one piece of a regimen.

### Tense and Narrative Mode
- **Use deliberate tense shifts.** Past tense for backstory and setup. Present tense for dramatic scenes where the reader needs to feel like they're there. Study the Luhxe swipe file for how this works.
- The product must reveal itself through scenes the reader experiences in real time, not through summary or retelling. The reader should watch it work, not be told it worked.
- Wrong: "I used it for two months and her coat got better." Right: a scene where someone stops the narrator on a walk and comments on the coat. The reader is in the scene.

### Reading Level and Tone
- **Third-grade reading level. Always.** Every word, every sentence. Simple words, short sentences. If a ten-year-old wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.
- **Third-grade CLARITY, not just vocabulary.** Every line must be understood instantly on first read. No abstract references ("how any of it got that way"), no implied meaning ("she doesn't have to"), no literary metaphor ("through her eyes I can't unsee it"). If the reader has to think about what a sentence means, rewrite it to say what it actually means. This is a repeated failure point.
- First-person. Conversational. Short sentences. Fragments fine.
- Heavy dialogue. Characters talk like real people.
- No marketing language, jargon, or buzzwords. The narrator never uses words like "pitch," "invest," "journey." Real people don't talk like marketers.
- No bullet lists or feature dumps inside the story
- No exclamation marks for emphasis
- **Never use em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence.
- **Use only common, universally known words.** If a word might not be recognized by every reader (e.g., "ottoman"), replace it with something everyone knows. When in doubt, use the simpler word.
- **Every line must be instantly understood on first read.** No vague references ("he doesn't think about how any of it got that way"). Say what you actually mean ("he doesn't notice that I'm the one who did all of it"). If the reader has to interpret what a sentence means, rewrite it plainly. Third-grade clarity means third-grade clarity, not just third-grade vocabulary.

### Characters
- Keep the cast small. 2-3 named characters max besides the narrator. **Don't name characters who are only mentioned in passing.** If a vet, doctor, or other figure appears only inside someone else's dialogue and doesn't appear as a character in the story, they don't get a name. "My vet" is enough. Every name the reader has to track costs attention. Only name characters who earn it by appearing in scenes.
- Use simple but original names. Not AI-generic (Sarah, John, Mary) and not overly complex or unusual. The name should not pull the reader out of the story.
- Every character must serve a clear purpose. If two characters do similar things, merge them.
- **The best supporting characters serve 2-3 story functions at once.** A neighbor who provides social proof (notices the transformation), creates urgency (tries to buy, learns about price increase), and mirrors the reader's situation (has her own aging dog) is doing real work. A character who only does one thing feels purposeless. If a character only serves one function, consider whether that function can be folded into an existing character instead.
- Supporting characters should not say things that are too wise or perfectly timed. Only characters with real authority (a specialist, a vet with decades of experience, someone who has lived it) get profound lines.
- **Validators need established "before" relationships with the dog.** A neighbor reacting to the transformation only works if she saw the decline. Establish the validator's "before" in one or two sentences ("We've walked the same route for three years. She'd seen Bear's slow months.") so their "after" reaction feels earned, not engineered.
- **Give the trusted friend one line of characterization that explains why her recommendation carries weight.** "She's a nurse. She's the kind of person who reads everything about something before she tries it." This doesn't need to be professional credentials — it needs to be the reader understanding why the narrator trusts this person's judgment specifically.

### Antagonist
- The antagonist creates the us-vs-them that makes the reader care. Someone or something to root against.
- A systemic antagonist (an industry, a cultural norm, a gap in what's available) can be more powerful than a single character. But it must make the reader feel something, not just inform them.
- Don't force a character antagonist (dismissive friend, skeptical neighbor) if it doesn't serve the story naturally. A forced antagonist is worse than a subtle one.
- The antagonist should be present early or build naturally from the story, never dropped in halfway.
- **The identity thread does not require an antagonist.** A husband who doesn't notice her effort is not a villain. He's a good man who just doesn't see it. Don't manufacture conflict or frame family members as antagonists. The identity challenge comes from reality — her effort being invisible — not from anyone being against her. Recognition from someone who was never antagonistic is more powerful than validation from someone who was.

### Identity Thread

**This is ONE LAYER woven through the story, not the whole ad.** The identity thread adds emotional depth underneath all other principles (belief architecture, undeniable demonstration, labeling rule, projection). It never replaces them. It never dominates. It runs quietly through the story while everything else does its job.

The buyer's core identity: "I'm the caretaker who does the most. I always have been." That's who she IS. This is deeper than dog ownership. She's spent her life being the one who does the most — for kids, husband, family. The invisibility of her effort (nobody seeing what she does) is the tension she lives with — it is NOT the identity itself. The identity is who she is. The invisibility is what happens to her effort. Keep these separate. The dog is the one relationship where she already feels appreciated.

#### Mandatory (every ad, no exceptions)

**Setup — establish who she is.**
Show the caretaker identity through a small, specific moment early in the story. Not backstory. Not explanation. A moment. Some version of this beat must be in every ad — this is the principle, not an angle. **The identity is "the caretaker who does the most." The invisibility of her effort is the tension, not the identity itself. These are two separate things. Do not define the character AS "the invisible caretaker." She is the caretaker who does the most. The invisibility is what happens to her effort.** First establish who she is, then show that nobody sees it.

Example energy: She's up before anyone. She warms his food because cold food is harder on older stomachs. She checks his water. She brushes him before their walk because his coat tangles faster now. She notices when he's stiff and when his breathing changes. That's what she does. That's what she's always done. Her husband walks through the kitchen, sees the dog fed and brushed and ready, and goes to the garage. He doesn't think about how any of it got that way. He never has.

The moment must be specific enough to feel real, universal enough that the reader has lived her own version of it. No guilt. No attack. Just a Tuesday morning that millions of women have lived.

**CRITICAL: The example above is a PRINCIPLE, not a template.** Never reuse the same setup scene across ads. The kitchen/warming food/garage/"he's a good man" framework has been copy-pasted into every ad and is now a dead pattern. Every ad needs a completely original scene with different specific details, different locations, different moments. The principle is: she does the most, nobody sees it. The execution must be fresh every single time.

**Threading invisibility through the middle.** The setup establishes the pattern. But it shouldn't disappear until the payoff. Add one or two single-line beats throughout the middle of the story — after scenes that are already happening, not as new scenes. One line that reminds the reader nobody sees what she does. Examples: "He wouldn't understand anyway. He's never asked about any of it." or "My husband sits in the same room every night and hasn't noticed." These must feel natural, not forced — they're the narrator's private thought after something just happened, not a deliberate setup. But they function as setup. Each one makes the recognition moment at the end hit harder because the reader has watched the pattern repeat throughout the story, not just once at the beginning.

**The Identity Challenge Line**
At some point, a character (NOT the narrator addressing the reader) says something like: "What kind of owner would I be if I didn't get this for her?" This is in every ad.

This works because:
- It's first-person dialogue from a character, not narration aimed at the reader
- The reader overhears and does the same math on herself
- It puts the character's own identity on trial, and the reader's identity goes on trial alongside it
- It's never guilt. Never attack. The character's own words activate the reader's identity
- **It is NEVER judgmental toward other owners.** "I'm glad I'm not that person" or "what kind of owner watches this happen and does nothing" attacks other owners. The line must be SELF-REFLECTION, not judgment. Right: "What kind of owner would I be if I hadn't done this for her." Wrong: "What kind of owner just watches this happen and doesn't do anything?"
- **The narrator NEVER says this line.** It always comes from another character. The narrator overhears it, same as the reader.

This line works best AFTER the transformation proof is in — the reader needs to have seen the results for "what kind of person would I be" to hit. Placing it before the reader has evidence makes it hollow. **It must come after at least 2-3 transformation proofs are stacked. Never place it during the product discovery conversation — no evidence behind it yet means no weight.** It should feel natural — a character reflecting on her own decision after seeing what the product did, not delivering a message to the reader.

**HARD RULE: The identity challenge line MUST appear after at least 2-3 transformation scenes.** Never during product discovery. Never when the friend first recommends TheraBelt. The line only hits when the reader has already seen the results. Best placement: through a character in the social proof section (e.g., the Facebook group) who has already seen results on her own dog. Placing it during discovery is a repeated failure.

#### Optional (use when the concept supports it)

**Challenge beat — her effort is invisible again.**
Her effort with the dog is shown to make no visible difference. Same breed, same age as another dog whose owner does nothing special. Dogs look the same. All her care, all her attention — no visible proof. Same lifelong pattern, new context. This is one powerful way to challenge the identity mid-story, but don't force it into concepts where it doesn't fit naturally.

**The Recognition Moment**
TheraBelt's version of Luhxe's antagonist validation — but more powerful because it comes from someone whose recognition actually matters.

The person who should have seen her all along (husband, grown daughter, family member) finally does. Not because she asked. Not because she complained. Because the dog showed it. The dog's condition is so undeniable that even someone who never noticed anything before can't ignore it.

**This is the scene that sells the desire of being appreciated. It must be written to make the reader WANT this moment so badly she'll do anything to make it happen.** This is not a quiet, restrained Hallmark moment where everyone sits in silence and nobody says anything. The husband must actually SAY IT. He must connect the dog to her — to everything she's done for him, for the family, for decades. He must put it into words. The reader needs to hear the words she's waited thirty years to hear, because THOSE WORDS are what she's buying. Silence and vague observations ("I never noticed what you do in here") are not the payoff. The payoff is him saying what he's never said.

**Common failure mode:** The scene goes restrained — lots of silence, "I didn't say anything," "we sat there for a long time," vague one-liners from the husband that hint at appreciation without actually delivering it. This kills the payoff. The whole identity thread builds to this moment. If the husband mumbles something half-specific and then everyone goes quiet, all that buildup was wasted. He doesn't have to give a speech. But he has to say the thing. Specifically. Out loud. Connected to her life, not just the kitchen or the dog.

Example energy — the husband must go HERE, not less:
"You did this with the kids too. Didn't you. All those years. Everything just worked and I never thought about it."
"I'm sorry. I should have seen it a long time ago."
He holds her. Not the quick hug on the way out the door. He holds on like he's trying to make up for something.
"You've done so much for us. I love you."

The scene needs: (1) him connecting the dog to her whole life in simple words — not itemizing specifics like "the lunches, the doctor appointments, the permission slips," just "everything just worked and I never thought about it," (2) a real apology, (3) real physical affection — a kiss (not a quick one), holding her face, pulling her in. A hand touch or hug is not enough for ten years of invisible effort. The physical act must match the weight of the moment and contrast with the "quick touch on his way out" pattern, and (4) breathing room after. Let it sit. "For the first time in a very long time I didn't feel invisible." Don't rush past this into the close.

That's what the reader wants to hear. That's what she's buying. Write it like it matters, because it's the most important beat in the entire ad.

Powerful when it fits. Don't force it into concepts where it doesn't belong.

**PREREQUISITE: The recognition moment requires BUILDUP.** One dismissive husband line in the middle + a big speech at the end = forced. The husband must:
- **Have a name.** Not just "my husband" throughout. He's a real character.
- **Appear in at least 3-4 beats through the middle** showing his absence or blindness — naturally, within scenes already happening. Each beat accumulates so the recognition payoff is earned.
- **Have unique dialogue that references THIS story's events** — not the template example energy above. If you can swap the dialogue into a different ad and it still works, it's framework paste.

If the concept doesn't naturally support 3-4 husband beats through the middle, CUT the recognition moment entirely. A forced recognition scene is worse than no recognition scene.

**The P.S. Technique**
After the CTA, one or two casual sentences. "It's been six months since then, and my husband and I..."

This extends the product's value beyond the dog. It's brief. She's almost surprised by it herself. Not a section — a footnote. The power comes from everything the reader already knows about the character by this point. Use when it fits.

**The P.S. ONLY works if the recognition moment does its job first.** If the husband hasn't said the words, hasn't apologized, hasn't connected the dog to her whole life, the P.S. floats in nothing. The P.S. must show a fundamental shift in behavior, not a surface gesture or generic attendance. Generic (weak): "Ray hasn't missed a Sunday." Specific (strong): "Ray sets his alarm for 5:45 now. He's the one holding the leash when I come downstairs." The strong version works because it's a ROLE REVERSAL — he went from not knowing what the walk involved to being the one who initiates it. The P.S. must show the husband DOING something he never did before — taking ownership of a part of what was always invisible.

### Science and Mechanism
- **Mechanism is minimal and casual.** Cooper-level: "two wavelengths, something about cells getting more energy." That's the ceiling. The mechanism should be so brief and so casual that jargon (mitochondria, ATP, collagen synthesis) would be absurd in context.
- **Delivered by a character, not by narration.** The mechanism lives in dialogue — a friend mentioning it over coffee, not the narrator explaining it to the reader. The character doesn't fully understand it herself, and that's what makes it believable. She's passing along what she heard, not teaching a science lesson.
- **The mechanism is about what the light does to cells, not what it does to aging.** Cells getting more energy = factual and believable. Crossing into aging language ("slows aging," "turns back the clock") = labeling what the product does, which breaks the demonstration principle. The reader connects the mechanism to the result herself.
- **The "dogs age faster so it works faster" reframe is not mandatory.** It can appear if the story naturally invites it — a character wondering aloud why results show so fast on dogs. But it should never be forced in as a required beat. If it appears, it should feel like a character's own thought, not a sales point.
- **Mechanism contrast (supplement vs red light) must be earned by the story.** If supplements haven't been discussed or established in the story's context, you can't suddenly compare to them. The contrast only works when the story has naturally set it up. Never force it in.

### Dialogue Authenticity
- **When a character speaks to the narrator, the narrator must respond with actual dialogue.** No silent summaries ("I didn't have a good answer for that"). Real people talk back. The only exception is when silence IS the response and that silence is doing clear emotional work in the scene.
- **Supporting character reactions must pass the "would a real person say this?" test.** A neighbor seeing a healthier dog would NOT say "what are you doing differently?" That's a marketing setup line. A real person says "he looks good" or "wow, Bear's moving around" or just comments on something specific. The response should never conveniently open a door for product discussion.
- **Write what characters SAY, don't leave reactions vague.** When a stranger, vet, or family member reacts to the dog's transformation, put actual dialogue on the page. "She kept looking at him, then back at me, waiting for me to correct myself" is vague — the reader has to guess the reaction. "No. There's no way this dog is 10." is specific — the reader hears it. Don't ask the reader to imagine what the reaction was. Write it.
- **Character consistency.** If you characterize Linda as someone who "reads the studies behind something before she tries it," she cannot then say "I don't really understand the science." Pick one characterization and commit. Track what you've established about each character and don't contradict it.
- **No vague pronoun dialogue.** "I thought it just happened" — what is "it"? Every line of dialogue must have a clear, specific referent. If a character uses "it," "this," or "that" and the reader has to reconstruct what they mean, rewrite with the specific thing. "I walk through this kitchen every morning and her food is out and her leash is ready. I never once thought about who was doing all of it" — that's concrete. "I thought it just happened" — that floats in nothing.
- **Minimize the cast.** Don't introduce a neighbor AND a friend when one character can do both jobs. Every name the reader has to track costs attention. If two characters serve similar functions, merge them.
- **First person possessives.** "My vet," not "his vet" or a named vet. No vet names unless the vet appears in multiple scenes and earns the name.
- **Give husbands names.** The husband is a real character. Name him early, use the name throughout. "My husband" for the entire ad makes him feel like a prop, not a person.
- **Cut unnecessary named characters aggressively.** If a character can be referred to as "Dave's wife" instead of getting her own name, do that. Only name characters who appear in multiple scenes and do unique work.
- **Track what each character knows and when they learned it.** If a friend recommended the product a month ago, she cannot act shocked by the results three weeks later as if she has no idea what's going on. Characters remember what they told people. Timeline inconsistencies between characters destroy believability.
- **THE FRIEND WHO RECOMMENDED THERABELT CANNOT ACT SURPRISED AT RESULTS. This is a repeated failure.** If Diane told the narrator about TheraBelt, Diane cannot say "what happened to her?" or "what is going on with her?" when the dog improves. She KNOWS. Her reaction must reflect knowledge: "told you," a knowing smile, asking how long it's been. Characters remember what they told people. This specific error has occurred in every single draft and must be caught before presenting.
- **When the story has a character conflict, give it real dialogue.** If a husband, friend, or family member creates tension, don't summarize what they said. Put their words on the page. Let them speak. A husband dismissing the dog's care needs real lines: "At what point do we just accept that he's old?" is a scene. "My husband told me we were spending too much" is a summary. The reader needs to hear the words to feel the tension. Confrontation scenes without dialogue are wasted opportunities.
- **Dialogue that exists to move the sales narrative forward instead of sounding like real people talking is the single fastest way to break the story.** Every line of dialogue should pass this test: would this person actually say this in this moment, even if the product didn't exist? If the line only makes sense as a setup for the next sales beat, cut it.

### Story Realism
- Every scene and dialogue exchange must feel like it actually happened. If a beat feels convenient or constructed, it breaks the story.
- **Every character action needs a motivation a real person would actually have.** Nobody pays for a vet visit "to see the numbers." Use real reasons (dog was due for a checkup, routine appointment). If the motivation only exists to create the scene, find a realistic reason or restructure. Convenient motivations break the story's reality.
- If a scene is set in a foreign country, account for language barriers. Characters don't magically speak the narrator's language.
- Dog breeds must be common and relatable (Goldens, Labs, Retrievers). No rare or complex breeds the average reader hasn't encountered.
- When stating a dog's age, be accurate to the breed's real lifespan. Frame exceptional ages against the norm so the reader understands the significance.
- Longevity (dogs living longer) must be shown indirectly through evidence in the story (a dog thriving at an age most of its breed never reach), never stated directly as a product claim.
- **When citing another dog as proof (e.g., a friend's dog, a coworker's dog), give specific visual details.** "People couldn't believe the dog's age" is vague. WHY couldn't they believe it? Coat still thick and shiny, still gets up to greet people at the door, moves like a dog half its age. The reader needs to picture the proof, not just hear the claim.
- **The narrator does normal dog care, not supplement research.** The caretaker identity comes from presence and attention — warming food, brushing the coat, noticing when breathing changes, being the one who remembers things nobody else remembers. NOT from buying turmeric, glucosamine, fish oil, or standing in the supplement aisle reading labels. The supplement-buyer narrator is not realistic for the main avatar. The caretaker is powerful because of WHO SHE IS, not WHAT SHE BUYS.
- **No dramatic unrealistic narrator behavior.** "I'd leave the cart in the aisle and drive home just to check on him" — nobody does that. Worry must be shown through behaviors a real person actually does: watching the dog differently, counting breaths at night, not sleeping well. Every narrator action must pass: "would a real woman actually do this?"
- **No vague emotional lines.** "Something I didn't know I needed to feel" — meaningless. If the narrator is crying, say WHY in plain words. "I cry. Because for the first time in months, I'm not scared." Every emotional line must mean something specific. If you can't explain in plain words what she's feeling, rewrite it.
- **Every detail from research must be earned narratively.** Usable details from Reddit are raw material, not copy-paste elements. If the narrator buys a camera, installs rugs, or does anything specific, the story must set up WHY. If a detail feels dropped in from nowhere ("I bought a little camera for the living room"), it IS from nowhere. Either build the setup or show the same emotion through behavior already established.
- **When a friend's comment is the inciting incident, the narrator asks the devastating question.** The friend observes and comments casually about her own dog. The narrator asks how long. She does it to herself. She regrets it immediately. Wrong: friend volunteers "you have eight months" unprompted — nobody does that. Right: friend says "mine used to do that," narrator asks "how long did you have?" The weight lands harder when the narrator caused it.

### Transformation Pacing
- Changes are gradual, not instant. The narrator should doubt it's working before she's sure.
- The pacing must feel real. If changes happen too fast, the reader won't believe it.
- **HARD BAN: No week or month numbers in transformation.** "Week 3." "Five weeks in." "Two months later." — ALL banned. This is a REPEATED FAILURE across multiple sessions despite being in this file. Use scene-based time: "Then one morning..." "A Tuesday..." "His next checkup..." "For a while, nothing." Time passes through narrative, not calendar entries. Before presenting ANY draft, ctrl+F for "week," "month," and any number attached to a time period in the transformation section. If found, rewrite as a scene.
- **Never write week-by-week or month-by-month transformation timelines.** ("Week 1, nothing. Week 5, coat changed. Month 2, more energy.") This reads like every other product copy. Real people don't journal their dog's progress on a schedule.
- Instead, show transformation through scenes. Pick one or two powerful moments and put the reader inside them. A friend stops the narrator on a walk. A vet tilts her head. Someone doesn't believe the dog's age. These are scenes, not bullet points with timestamps.
- Time passing should be felt through the story's narrative, not stated as a calendar.
- **Bridge transitions between scenes.** When jumping from one emotional beat to another (e.g., a phone call to a vet visit), add a brief bridge line so the pacing doesn't feel instant. Scenes need breathing room between them.
- **Authority scenes need extra breathing room.** When a vet or specialist validates the transformation, don't rush through it. Let them examine the dog. Let them pause. Let them react before they speak. Give the narrator a moment after the scene (sitting in the parking lot, driving home in silence) to let the weight land. These are the most important belief-building scenes in the story. Rushing them wastes the credibility they carry.
- **Restoration must be partial, not complete.** "He's still a little slower on the stairs than he used to be. That's fine. He's nine." Let one "before" behavior stay partially diminished. Full reversal ("moves like he's four again") breaks belief. Partial restoration ("that is not the same dog I was walking with three months ago") is more honest and more convincing. Better, not young.
- **Add a private knowledge moment after the first sign of change.** "I didn't say anything to anyone. I didn't want to jinx it." This creates information asymmetry between the narrator and other characters. The reader holds the private hope with the narrator. It builds investment before external validation arrives.

### Urgency
- **Two layers of urgency.** Product urgency gives the reader a reason to buy now (batches, price, availability). Benefit urgency gives the reader a reason to want the result now — the story must make her feel that waiting costs her dog something. Every day without it is a day her dog doesn't have. Both layers should be present. Product urgency without benefit urgency is just a countdown timer. Benefit urgency without product urgency has no call to action.
- Scarcity and urgency should feel like real constraints, not sales tactics.
- Small batches, material costs, production limits. State facts, don't push.
- **Urgency can be price-based or availability-based, but never imply the reader can't buy right now.** "Sold out," "waitlist," or anything suggesting the product isn't available when they click breaks trust. Availability urgency is fine (batches running low, limited runs) as long as it reads like "get it while it's here," not "you can't get it." Price urgency works too: "price goes up with the next batch." Stated as fact, not pressure.
- **Urgency works best through a character.** A supporting character wants the product after seeing the transformation, learns the price is going up. This is natural because it's a scene, not a sales paragraph.
- **Plant scarcity early, confirm it later.** The trusted friend should mention limited availability or a wait ("her neighbor had to wait a few weeks because they do small runs") when she first recommends the product. A second character confirms it later (price went up, next batch higher). Two scarcity touches across the story, not one clustered at the end.
- **Keep urgency explanations simple.** Don't over-explain WHY the price goes up (supplier details, panel sourcing, manufacturing specifics). "The materials cost more every run" is enough. The reader doesn't need a supply chain breakdown.
- **Never bolt urgency onto the end as a standalone paragraph.** If urgency lives in its own paragraph disconnected from the story, it reads as a sales pitch. It must live inside a scene or dialogue.
- Avoid overtly salesy lines like "if you're seeing this and it's in stock, you're lucky" or "don't sit on it."
- **No standalone product name after the close.** No "TheraBelt." as a sign-off line. The product name is already established in the story through the friend's dialogue. The close ends on the urgency line or the P.S. — never a brand name sitting alone.
- **The Facebook group scene is the preferred social proof + urgency pattern.** The narrator posts in a neighborhood dog group. **The post is visual proof + minimal caption.** Post a video of the dog running at the park, or doing something visually undeniable. Caption is one fact: "He's 10." That's it. Never write vague teaser captions like "Ask me what changed" or "I didn't think I'd see this again" — that's MLM energy. Real people don't write captions designed to generate inquiries. They share something because they're happy and the response happens on its own. Her phone blows up for days. Women message about their senior dogs. She tells them what her friend told her. They start sending updates — specific women, specific dogs, specific results. Each update is one line: "[Name]'s [age]-year-old [breed] was [specific improvement]." Three to four of these, stacked. Then one of them messages later saying the price went up since the narrator bought hers. She ordered anyway. This pattern delivers stacked social proof (multiple dogs, multiple results) and urgency (price increase) through characters, not narration. Use this as the default for social proof and urgency.

### Patterns to Avoid

The following patterns have been corrected multiple times across sessions. Each is a hard ban.

- **No marketing language in narrator voice.** The narrator is a real person, not a copywriter. She would never say "she didn't pitch me anything," "I decided to invest in it," "it was a game-changer," or "my journey." Words like "pitch," "invest," "journey," "game-changer," "hack" belong in marketing decks, not in a woman's story about her dog. If the narrator sounds like she's aware she's in an ad, the spell is broken.
- **No poetic dog interpretation.** Describe what the dog DOES. Never interpret what the dog is thinking, feeling, or "savoring." Wrong: "He lifted his head to the sky. Closed his eyes. Took in the breeze like he was trying to hold onto it. I watched him and thought, he's savoring this. He knows." Right: "He stood there in the sun for a minute with his eyes closed." One simple action. No stacking multiple poetic actions. No narrating the dog's inner state.
- **No forced artificial beats.** (1) No fake dramatic delays — "I don't ask what it's called. Not yet." then calls the friend the next day. Pointless. (2) No fake scripted regret lines — "If I stop now and it could have done something, I will never forgive myself." Nobody thinks in motivational poster sentences. (3) Never force framework beats where they contradict the scene — if a vet WOULD ask what changed (it's her job), don't write "she didn't ask. Nobody ever asks." just to hit the invisible effort beat. Framework serves the story, not the other way around.
- **Frame the dog's decline as time/ritual disappearing, not physical inability.** "Now we barely make it past the driveway" sounds like arthritis. "Last week the whole walk fit between our front door and the mailbox" sounds like time disappearing. Every decline line must pass: "Is this about time vanishing, or about the dog struggling physically?" If the latter, reframe.
- **Never copy framework example dialogue from the SKILL.md or this file into a draft.** The examples in this file and the skill show the emotional WEIGHT needed, not words to reuse. Test: can you swap this dialogue into a completely different story and it still works? If yes, it's framework paste — rewrite from what THIS character observed in THIS story. This applies to ALL framework beats: recognition moments, identity challenge lines, antagonist scenes, friend recommendations — everything.
- **No rule-of-three sentence patterns.** ("He was X. He wasn't Y. He wasn't Z." / "Same bed. Same corner. Same soft snoring." / "Not the food, not the brushing, not the mornings.") This is an AI signature pattern. Before presenting ANY draft, search for triple repetitions and break them. Say it once well. Two max, never three. This is a repeated failure point.
- **No negation-contrast formula.** Never define something by listing what it's NOT before saying what it IS. "Not fast. But smoother." / "Not a supplement. Not a vitamin." / "Not running. Not young. But up." This is an AI signature pattern. Say what it IS directly. The word "not" is fine in normal use. The formula of stacking negation sentences to create contrast is the problem. One instance in 1,500 words is the absolute max. Never stack them.
- No AI-sounding filler ("here's the part that got me," "that's when it hit me," "let me tell you," "I need you to understand").
- No fake emotional beats ("I sat on the floor and cried"). Emotion must be earned.
- **ZERO dialogue tags. ZERO stage directions. No exceptions.** Don't write "She paused." "She looked at me." "Dana kind of smiled." Don't write "I said." "she said." "he asked." "she told me." Don't write "Instead I said" or "That's what I said" or "All I said was." Real Reddit stories never do this. They just write the dialogue. The reader knows who is talking from context. Before presenting ANY draft, ctrl+F for "said," "asked," "told," "paused," "looked," "smiled" attached to dialogue and delete every instance. This is a repeated failure point.
- **Don't introduce product name and company name separately.** "The company was called Lucavel. The product was called TheraBelt" is clunky and confusing. Just introduce TheraBelt. One name. The reader doesn't need to know the company name at the point of discovery.
- **No forced data dumps.** Industry statistics, market size numbers, or systemic arguments must arise naturally from the story's emotional arc. If a stat doesn't have a natural place in the angle, leave it out. A number dropped in without narrative context reads as a pitch, not a story.
- **No DR-style direct address in the close.** Never write "If your dog used to do something that drove you crazy..." or "If you're still buying supplements and hoping for the best..." This is direct-response copy disguised as story. It breaks the spell. The Luhxe swipe file never does this. The close stays in the narrator's world. The reader connects it to her own life without being told to.
- **Use pronouns naturally.** When the narrator is talking about someone close to them (husband, mother, child), don't keep repeating the name. "Tom got sick. And then Tom was gone" sounds like a Wikipedia article. "Tom got sick. And then he was gone" sounds like a wife. A real person uses "he" or "she" when the relationship is obvious. Only repeat the name when clarity requires it or for emotional emphasis.
- **No poetic or literary lines.** "Little kicks like she's sprinting somewhere in her dream." "Through her eyes I can't unsee it." "She doesn't have to." These are writer lines, not storyteller lines. If it sounds like it belongs in a novel, rewrite it as plain speech. Say what happened. Not what it felt like in metaphor. This is a repeated failure point.
- **No long adjustment lists.** When showing the narrator's gradual changes (shorter walks, lower bowls, moved furniture), two examples max. The reader gets the pattern from two. Three or four is padding and reads like AI generating variations.
- **No meta-commentary.** The narrator never comments on the fact she's not selling ("nothing salesy," "I don't pitch anything") or notices her own thematic patterns ("there it was again"). A real person telling her story doesn't flag that she's not selling. Just tell the story.
- **No fake deflections in dialogue.** "What changed?" "I just started paying more attention." Nobody says this. It's a scripted humble line that sounds fake. If the narrator is asked what changed, she tells the truth or the scene focuses on the other person's reaction without needing a clever answer.

### Hooks and Leads
- **The hook and the lead are different things.** The hook is the first line or lines. The lead is the sequence that follows and builds into the story. Feedback on one does not mean changes to the other. Treat them as independent.
- The first three lines must be emotionally compelling and specific. Vague hooks fail.
- **Hooks must be simple.** Not too much happening at once. If the hook has multiple moving pieces, cut it down.
- The hook must make the reader feel something, not just wonder something. Curiosity alone is not enough.
- The hook must answer "why should I read this?" from the reader's perspective, not the narrator's.
- **Never break the story's flow with narrator bio lines** like "My name is X. I'm Y years old." mid-story. If narrator details are needed, weave them into the story naturally so they don't interrupt momentum.
- Test: would a woman scrolling her phone at 10pm stop for this? If not, rewrite.
- **The hook must require zero cognitive steps to understand.** No character names the reader hasn't met. "My dog" not "Rosie." If the reader has to figure out who someone is, piece together that a name refers to a dog, or reconstruct what the sentence means, the hook is dead. Count the cognitive steps before presenting. If more than zero, rewrite.
- **The hook must filter for the buyer, not just stop the scroll.** A hook that attracts broad engagement (marriage drama, relationship conflict, general curiosity) but doesn't specifically call out the target avatar is the wrong hook. The hook's job is to make the RIGHT woman stop — a woman with a senior dog, a woman already deep in the care, a woman who will buy. Broad engagement without buyer intent is vanity metrics. If the hook would work just as well without a dog in the story, it's filtering for the wrong audience.
- **Nostalgic hooks are dead hooks.** "My dog used to outrun every dog in the yard" is a mood, not a scroll-stop. Nobody stops scrolling for warmth or nostalgia. They stop for contrast, pain, or an image they can't ignore. The hook must make someone flinch, not reminisce. If the hook feels gentle or warm, it's not vicious enough.
- **Write the hook. Do not present hook options.** The writer's job is to internalize the vicious hooks principles and write the single best hook possible. Presenting multiple options to the user is asking them to do the writer's job. Commit to one hook. If it's wrong, the user will say so and you iterate. But you never hand over a list of candidates.

### Emotion

**Emotion is the engine of the entire story. Without it, nothing works.** A story that moves through events without making the reader feel something is just a report. Every scene, every moment, every beat should carry emotional weight. The reader should feel something on almost every paragraph — not the same emotion, but something. Love, fear, doubt, hope, frustration, relief, warmth. The emotional current never stops flowing.

**This is the most common failure mode: stories that are structurally correct but emotionally flat.** All the right beats are there — hook, antagonist, product, transformation, close — but the reader doesn't feel anything. That's not a story. That's an outline with sentences. If the draft doesn't make you feel something when you read it back, it's not done.

**The narrator must feel things and the reader must feel them too.** The narrator's love for her dog, her frustration, her hope, her doubt — these aren't background details. They're the substance of the story. The narrator doesn't just observe events. She reacts to them emotionally, and the reader reacts with her.

**The narrator's relationship with her dog must be felt, not just stated.** Facts about the dog (slower mornings, duller coat) are not emotional on their own. The reader needs to feel what those changes mean to the narrator. What it's like to watch your dog slow down. What it's like to notice something you wish you hadn't. What it's like to pretend everything is fine when you know it isn't. That's where the emotion lives.

**Emotion comes from specificity and breathing room.** Don't write "I was heartbroken." Write the moment with enough detail that the reader feels it herself. Slow down in the moments that matter. Let the reader sit in them. A single specific moment with real feeling is worth more than five paragraphs of plot.

**Every reader should think about her own dog.** The emotional specificity should be universal enough that the reader maps the narrator's experience onto her own life. When the narrator watches her dog struggle to get up, the reader should picture her own dog. That's what creates the investment that carries all the way through to the belief and the sale.

**We are writing stories, not ad copy.** A logically clean sequence of events is not a story. If the draft reads like a well-organized report (this happened, then this, then this), it's missing the point. The story exists to make the reader feel something. Everything else — the product, the proof, the CTA — rides on top of that emotion.

**Don't add words for the sake of length.** Emotion is not filler. Every sentence that builds emotional investment earns its place. Cut anything that doesn't make the reader feel more.

**Use a sensory motif to track the story's emotional arc.** Pick one specific sound, sight, or physical detail that represents the dog's presence in the narrator's life: nails tick-tacking on hardwood, the thump of a tail from two rooms away, the sound of paws at the front door. Introduce it as what the narrator loves. Show it fading as the fear. Return it as the transformation proof. A single sensory thread connecting beginning, middle, and end makes the story feel unified and gives the reader something physical to hold onto throughout.

## Self-Review Checklist (Step 7)

**Story quality (from story-craft.md):**
- Is the concept strong enough that someone would stop scrolling for it, even with no writing craft?
- Is the reader with the character the whole time? Or are there moments where the story shifts to summary or outside perspective?
- Are there at least 2-3 real scenes the reader can see — specific moments with time, place, and detail?
- Does the narrator think out loud? Can the reader hear doubt, skepticism, confusion in real time?
- Does the whole thing feel like a story from first word to last? Or does it shift into ad mode at some point?
- Does it feel authentic — like a real person typed this — or does it feel written and polished?
- Would people engage with this as if it were a real post from a real person?

**Ad requirements:**
- **Does the story channel the mass desire?** Every scene should intensify the desire the reader already carries and connect it to the product. If the story would work just as well serving a completely different desire, it's not channeling anything.
- Is the story interesting on its own without the product?
- Does TheraBelt appear before halfway? Push it later if yes.
- All 8 required variables present?
- Any paragraph that reads like a product description? Rewrite as story.
- Does the story have enough dialogue?
- Does it account for weeks/months of TheraBelt use?
- **Can the reader deny what she read?** By the end, has she seen enough — from enough angles, from enough independent sources, with enough specific detail — that she can't deny what happened in this story? If she can shrug it off, the demonstration isn't strong enough.
- **Does the story follow the labeling rule?** Product shown, result shown, but what happened between them is never named. No "slows aging," "anti-aging," "turns back the clock." The reader fills in the gap herself.
- **Is the reader thinking about HER dog, or just feeling for the character's dog?** Empathy without projection is engagement without conversion.
- **Does the reader recognize herself as the caretaker who does the most?** The buyer identity ("I'm the caretaker who does the most. I always have been. The belt just proves it.") should come through naturally without being stated. Mandatory checks: is the setup beat present (she's the caretaker who does the most, and her effort is shown to be invisible — two separate things, identity vs. tension)? Is the identity challenge line present through character dialogue? Optional checks (use when the concept supports it): is there a challenge beat? A recognition moment? A P.S.? Is the identity thread woven through as one layer without dominating?
- Is urgency narrative, not salesy? Does it have both product urgency AND benefit urgency?
- Is this elevation, not problem-solving?

**Craft and tone:**
- Any em dashes? Remove all of them.
- Any technical jargon? Replace with plain language.
- Any AI filler phrases? Remove them.
- Does every story beat feel real? Any convenient jumps or unrealistic dialogue?
- Is the transformation pacing gradual enough? Does the narrator doubt before she's sure?
- Are there any forced characters or scenes?
- Any poetic or literary lines? ("sprinting somewhere in her dream," "the only place she runs anymore") Rewrite as plain speech.
- Any adjustment lists longer than two items? Cut to two.
- Any meta-commentary? ("nothing salesy," "there it was again") Remove.
- Any vague pronoun dialogue? ("I thought it just happened") Make specific.
- Does the friend who recommended TheraBelt act surprised at results she should know about? Fix character consistency.
- Is the identity challenge line placed after transformation proof? If it's during discovery, move it.

**Copy architecture (the four errors that kill conversion):**
1. **Movies vs mirrors** — is the reader seeing her own life reflected, or watching someone else's life like a movie? If the story completes as entertainment, the reader scrolls. If she sees herself, she acts.
2. **Emotional peak** — is the emotional high point on the product/anomaly (the impossible dog, the undeniable result), or on the human relationship (the love, the loss, the bond)? The peak must be on what the product made possible, not on the emotion that already existed.
3. **Buyer transformation** — does the reader become someone (the caretaker who does the most, who always has, and now her dog proves it to the people around her), or does only the dog change? Is her identity challenged and then validated through the recognition moment? If the dog transforms but the buyer's identity isn't challenged and validated, the story generates admiration, not action.
4. **Benefit urgency** — is there urgency to get the RESULT (every day without it is a day the dog doesn't have), not just urgency to buy before the price goes up? Product urgency without benefit urgency is a countdown timer with no emotional weight.
