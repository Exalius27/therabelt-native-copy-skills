## What This Skill Does

Generates a story-native ad for TheraBelt (red light therapy belt for dogs, by Lucavel). Output is a single long-form first-person narrative (~1,400-1,800 words) that reads like a genuine personal story. The product appears indirectly, never before the halfway point. The reader should not realize they are reading an ad until they are already invested.

## Core Principles

These four principles govern everything in this skill. Every decision — outline, scene, dialogue, structure — must pass through them. They are not guidelines. They are constraints.

### 1. The Undeniable Demonstration Principle

The test for every ad: by the end of this story, has the reader seen enough — from enough angles, from enough independent sources, with enough specific detail — that she can't deny what she just witnessed?

Not "believe the claim." DENY what she saw. Those are different.

The reader's existing knowledge becomes the proof. Put something in front of her that contradicts what she already knows about dogs at that age — and make it so specific and so repeated from enough independent angles that she can't dismiss it.

A 10-year-old dog that people think is five. A professional who sees dogs every day and can't figure it out. Strangers reacting independently. A vet tilting her head. Stack it until denial is not an option.

The reader is never told what to conclude. She concludes it because the evidence left no other explanation.

### 2. The Labeling Rule

Show the product and show the result. Never name what happened between them.

Age is the subject. Anti-aging is the label you never use. Talk about dogs aging. Talk about what age looks like. Show specific ages. Show a dog that defies what the reader knows age looks like. But never claim the product changes that. The reader sees the product, sees the result, and fills in the gap herself.

Never use "slows aging," "anti-aging," "turns back the clock," or any language that labels what the product did. The word "age" is fine — it describes reality. The label "anti-aging" describes a claim.

Same architecture as Luhxe: Luhxe never says "this bag will make people respect you." They show it so undeniably the reader concludes it herself.

### 3. Projection Over Empathy

The reader must think about HER dog the whole time, not just feel for the character's dog. Empathy without projection is engagement without conversion.

Three structural mechanisms create projection:

- **Comparison triggers** — specific ages, breeds, behaviors the reader maps to her own dog. When the narrator compares one dog to another, the reader compares to hers.
- **Universal specific moments** — moments the target avatar has her own version of. What those moments are depends on the concept and avatar. The moment must be specific enough to feel real, universal enough to the target audience that the reader has lived it.
- **Real-time discovery** — the narrator discovers alongside the reader, not retelling a finished story. She doesn't know the answer yet. The reader goes through the realization at the same pace.
- **Autobiographical resonance layering** — the strongest moments use two levels at once. Behavioral resonance ("I've done that") opens the door. Identity resonance ("That's who I am") is what the reader walks through. A specific behavior that points toward an identity conclusion is twice as sticky as either alone. When the narrator does something specific and ordinary — moves her schedule for a vet cancellation, warms the food before putting it down — the reader maps her own version of that behavior, then arrives at her own identity conclusion without being told. Never describe the character's identity. Show a behavior. Let the identity surface in the reader's mind.

This is structural, not second person. Never address the reader directly (except possibly at the very end, like Luhxe). The projection comes from the story's structure, not from "if you..." language.

### 4. Buyer Identity and the Identity Thread

"I'm the caretaker who does the most. I always have been. The belt just proves it."

This is deeper than dog ownership. The target buyer is a woman who has spent her life being the one who does the most — for her kids, her husband, her family — and nobody ever really saw it. The dog is the one relationship where she already feels appreciated. TheraBelt doesn't make the dog appreciate her (it already does). It makes the dog's condition so undeniable that the HUMANS around her finally see what she's been doing — not just for the dog, but what she's always done.

Same architecture as Luhxe, but more powerful. Luhxe: the bag proves she was always capable, validated by an antagonist or stranger. TheraBelt: the dog proves she was always the caretaker she believed she was, validated by someone whose recognition actually matters — a husband, a grown daughter, family.

This must come through the story naturally. Never stated. Never labeled. The reader recognizes herself in the character — not just as a dog owner, but as the person who always does the most. The invisibility of her effort is the tension she lives with, not her identity. Her identity is who she IS: the one who does the most. Always has been.

#### The Identity Thread

**This is ONE LAYER woven through the story, not the whole ad.** All other principles still apply — belief architecture, undeniable demonstration, labeling rule, projection over empathy. The identity thread runs underneath everything else, adding depth.

#### Mandatory (every ad, no exceptions):

1. **Setup** — Establish who she is early: the caretaker who does the most. That's the identity. Then show that her effort is invisible — that's the tension, not the identity itself. Not through backstory or explanation — through a small, specific moment. A morning routine nobody notices. A husband who walks through without seeing what she does. "He's a good man. He just doesn't see it. Nobody ever does." The reader recognizes this from her own life instantly. The specific execution varies by concept, but some version of this beat — showing who she is AND that nobody sees it — must be in every ad. This is the principle, not an angle. **Do not define the character AS "the invisible caretaker." She is the caretaker who does the most. The invisibility is what happens to her effort, not who she is.**

2. **The Identity Challenge Line** — At some point, a character (not the narrator addressing the reader) says something like: "What kind of owner would I be if I didn't get this for her?" This challenges the reader's identity indirectly — she overhears a character putting her OWN identity on trial, and does the same math on herself. Always first-person dialogue from a character. Never narration aimed at the reader. Never guilt. Never attack. The character's own words activate the reader's identity. This is in every ad.

#### Optional (use when the concept supports it):

3. **Challenge beat** — Her effort is invisible AGAIN, this time with the dog. She does everything — supplements, good food, research — and her dog looks the same as a dog whose owner does nothing. Same breed, same age, no visible difference. All her care made no difference anyone can see. Same lifelong pattern, new context. This is one powerful way to challenge the identity in the middle of the story, but it's not required in every concept.

4. **Recognition moment** — The dog's transformation is so undeniable that someone who matters (husband, grown daughter, family) finally sees what she's been doing. Not because she asked. Because the dog showed it. TheraBelt's version of Luhxe's antagonist validation, but from someone whose recognition actually matters. **This scene sells the desire of being appreciated. The person must actually SAY IT — connect the dog to her whole life, to everything she's done for decades. No vague hints, no restrained silence, no "we just sat there." He says the words she's waited thirty years to hear. Those words are what the reader is buying.** Powerful when it fits. Don't force it into concepts where it doesn't belong.

5. **The P.S. Technique** — After the CTA, a brief casual line: "It's been six months since then, and my husband and I..." Extends the product's value beyond the dog. Must stay short — one or two sentences. She's almost surprised by it herself. Not a section. A footnote that hits hard because of everything that came before it. Use when it fits.

## Workflow

### 1. Parse Input

Extract from the user's input:

- **Mass desire** (required) — the pre-existing want in the market that this story must activate. This comes from the creative-strategist output or the user directly. The entire story channels this desire toward the product. If missing, ask before proceeding.
- **Concept** (required) — the story idea, angle, scenario
- **Target avatar** (required) — which avatar file to load

If any are missing, ask before proceeding. Do not assume or default.

### 2. Load Knowledge

Read files in two tiers. **Always load** the core files. **Reference on demand** the rest — only when you need specific details during writing.

#### Always load (read before writing):
- `wiki/story-craft.md` — what makes the story itself work. Read this first.
- `wiki/direct-response-copywriting.md` — belief chains, mechanism explanation, objection handling, story architecture
- `wiki/story-native-format.md` — ad framework, required variables, product placement rules
- `wiki/avatar-{N}.md` — only the avatar file the user specified
- `wiki/vicious-hooks.md` — hook framework. **Must be read before writing any hook. No exceptions.**

#### Auto-search relevant knowledge:
Before writing, read `wiki/index.md` and scan for pages relevant to the concept + avatar. Read the 2-3 most relevant wiki pages to pull in additional frameworks or principles.

#### Reference on demand (read when you need specific details):
- `wiki/positioning.md` — positioning, mechanism (UMP/UMS), market. Read when writing the mechanism section.
- `wiki/company-overview.md` — brand, product name, core benefit. Read when you need product specifics.
- `wiki/luhxe-ad-craft.md` — structural principles from reference ads. Read when you need guidance on belief architecture, social proof, or closing.
- `wiki/swipe-file-luhxe.md` — reference ads for quality standard. Read selectively (1-2 ads) when you need to calibrate tone or study a specific technique, not the entire file.

Confirm to the user which avatar and mass desire you are writing for.

### 3. Validate the Concept

Before outlining or writing anything, test the concept. The concept is the situation itself — what happened, who it's about, and why someone scrolling would stop to read it.

Ask: **would someone stop their doom scroll for this?** Not because of good writing. Because the situation demands attention. If the answer is no, stop. Work with the user to find a stronger concept before proceeding.

A concept that's just a mood ("I love my aging dog") is not strong enough. A concept needs a situation the reader has to see play out.

### 4. Outline the Story

Sketch a loose outline — a few sentences covering where the story goes. This is not a formula. The story has its own shape based on the concept. But make sure these elements exist somewhere:

- **Hook** — must answer "why should I read this?" for the READER. Must filter for the BUYER, not just stop the scroll. A hook that's broadly interesting but doesn't call out the target avatar attracts the wrong audience. The right woman should stop because she sees herself. No product. No hint of product.
- **Antagonist** — a system, industry, or character that creates tension. Must build naturally. Don't force a character antagonist if it doesn't serve the story. A forced antagonist is worse than a subtle one. Note: the identity thread does NOT require an antagonist. A husband or family member who doesn't see her effort is not a villain — he's a good man who just doesn't notice. Don't manufacture conflict where none is needed.
- **Product discovery** — product enters through someone the narrator trusts, during a conversation that's already happening about the narrator's actual situation. The narrator should be talking to the friend about her situation, and the friend brings up the product naturally from that context. The product enters because the conversation invited it. Never through an ad, search, or influencer. **The friend can have months of results to share (past tense). But the narrator's own experience — ordering, receiving, using, discovering results — must unfold in real-time/present tense with the reader.** The tense shift happens at the moment of decision. "I order it that night." Not "I ordered it that night."
- **Transformation** — shown through scenes, not summaries. Doubt before certainty. These scenes are the belief-building mechanism (see Core Principle #1). Stack them from multiple independent angles.
- **Urgency** — limited batch, price going up, production constraint. Through narrative, not promotion.
- **Close** — ties back to the opening. "Your move" energy is the default for competitive/dignity/revenge frames (like Luhxe). For love/loss/time stories, "Your move" often doesn't land. In those cases, the close should end on the story's core emotional image — the callback to the opening that lets the reader feel the transformation. The final line IS the close. The reader connects it to her own life without being told to.
- **Recognition moment** — a scene where someone who matters (husband, grown daughter, family) finally sees her effort — triggered by the dog's undeniable transformation. This is the emotional payoff of the identity thread. Can happen near or during the close. Not because she asked for recognition. Because the dog showed it.
- **P.S.** — after the CTA, one or two casual sentences. "It's been six months since then, and my husband and I..." Extends the product's value beyond the dog. Brief, authentic, almost surprised. Not a section — a footnote.

The outline should feel like a story someone would tell, not a checklist being filled. If the outline reads like a sequence of required beats, loosen it up. Let the concept dictate the shape.

### 5. Check the 8 Required Variables

Every story-native ad must include all eight. Verify before drafting:

1. **Compelling hook** — reader emotionally invested before she knows it's an ad. **You MUST read `vicious-hooks.md` before writing the hook.** The hook filters for the buyer — the right woman stops because she sees herself, her dog, her situation. A hook that attracts broad engagement but doesn't call out the target avatar is the wrong hook.
2. **Antagonist** — character, system, or industry. Creates an us-vs-them the reader feels. Don't force one if the story doesn't need it.
3. **Product discovery through friend/family** — never through an ad or search. The friend is casual, not evangelical. The less she sells it, the more convincing it is.
4. **CTA** — "Your move" fits competitive/dignity frames. For love/loss/time stories, the close should be the story's core emotional image as a callback to the opening. Either way, the close should feel like the reader's decision, not a push. Never address the reader directly with DR-style "if you..." lines.
5. **Natural scarcity/urgency** — two layers. Product urgency (limited batches, price going up, production constraints) woven into narrative, not salesy. And benefit urgency — the story must make the reader feel that waiting to get this result costs her something. Every day without it is a day her dog doesn't have. The product urgency gives her a reason to buy now. The benefit urgency gives her a reason to want the result now.
6. **Undeniable demonstration** — the reader cannot deny what she just witnessed. The belief destination is the same (this product does something real for dogs), but the path is demonstration only. Stack evidence from enough independent angles — strangers reacting, professionals confused, concrete age-vs-appearance mismatches — that denial is not possible. Never tell the reader what to conclude. Never label what the product did. (See Core Principle #1 and #2.)
7. **Transformation scenes as belief mechanism** — these scenes ARE the belief-building mechanism. They're not supporting evidence for a claim. They're the whole case. Each scene should violate the reader's expectations about what a dog at that age looks like. Multiple independent sources witnessing the same impossible thing.
8. **Mechanism** — Cooper-level only. Two wavelengths, cells getting more energy. Factual, casual, delivered by a character who doesn't fully understand it herself. This is literally what red light therapy does and it's believable at that level. Never cross into aging language. Never educate the reader. The less the character explains, the more convincing it is.

### 6. Write the Draft

**Start by reading `reference.md`.** It contains the detailed craft rules for every aspect of the story — structure, tense, reading level, characters, dialogue, transformation pacing, urgency, patterns to avoid, hooks, and emotion. You MUST read it before writing any draft.

**The four Core Principles govern the entire draft.** Every scene, every line of dialogue, every beat must pass through them. Before writing, re-read the Core Principles section above.

**The story must channel the mass desire.** The mass desire is the pre-existing want that the reader already carries before she ever sees the ad. Every scene, every emotional beat, every moment of transformation must intensify that desire and connect it to the product. The story doesn't create the desire — it activates what's already there and gives it a destination. If the story would work just as well with a completely different desire, it's not channeling anything.

**The reader is with the character the entire time.** From the first line to the last, the reader is inside the narrator's head, living it moment to moment. They see what the narrator sees, think what the narrator thinks, feel what the narrator feels. The story never shifts to an outside perspective.

**The narrator thinks out loud.** The reader hears the narrator's thoughts in real time — doubt, skepticism, confusion, hope. Not cleaned up after the fact. Raw, in the moment. This is what makes the narrator feel like a real person and builds trust.

**The whole thing is a story.** There is no point where the story stops and the ad begins. The product, the proof, the urgency, the close — all of it lives inside the narrative. If the reader ever feels the shift from "I'm reading a story" to "I'm being sold something," the spell is broken.

**The ad builds belief through demonstration, not claims.** The emotion (love for the dog) already exists. Don't create it — connect it to something the reader can't deny she just saw. Show the product. Show the result. Never label what happened between them.

**Core principle: elevation, not problem-solving.** TheraBelt elevates a dog's quality of life. It does not fix sick dogs or solve problems. The dog in the story is not broken, suffering, or in crisis. The shift is from "normal" to "elevated," not from "bad" to "fixed."

**The story must be genuinely interesting without the product.** If you strip TheraBelt out entirely, would someone still want to read this story? If not, the concept isn't strong enough. The story comes first. The product lives inside it.

### 7. Self-Review

Before presenting, read the Self-Review Checklist in `reference.md` and verify all items pass across three categories: story quality, ad requirements, and craft/tone.

Additionally, check the draft against the four Core Principles:
- **Undeniable demonstration** — can the reader deny what she witnessed? If yes, the demonstration isn't strong enough.
- **Labeling rule** — does the story ever name what the product did? Any "slows aging," "anti-aging," "turns back the clock"? Remove.
- **Projection** — is the reader thinking about HER dog, or just feeling for the character's dog?
- **Autobiographical resonance** — does the story contain at least 2–3 specific behavioral moments the avatar has her own version of? Are those moments pointed at identity conclusions, not just relatable situations?
- **Buyer identity and identity thread** — does the reader recognize herself as the caretaker who does the most? Mandatory checks: is the setup beat present (she's the caretaker who does the most, and her effort is shown to be invisible — two separate things)? Is the identity challenge line present through character dialogue? Optional checks (use when the concept supports it): is there a challenge beat? A recognition moment? A P.S.? Is this layer woven through without dominating the ad?

And check for the four copy errors:
1. **Movies vs mirrors** — is the reader seeing her own life reflected, or watching someone else's life like a movie?
2. **Emotional peak** — is the emotional high point on the product/anomaly, or on the human relationship?
3. **Buyer transformation** — does the reader become someone (the caretaker who does the most, who always has, and now her dog proves it to the people around her), or does only the dog change? Is her identity challenged and then validated through the recognition moment?
4. **Benefit urgency** — is there urgency to get the RESULT (not just urgency to buy before price goes up)?

### 8. Present

Output the story as a single block of text. No headers, no metadata inside the ad.

After the story, note: avatar used, word count, concept angle, and confirm all 8 variables present.

## Guardrails

- Never invent testimonials or fake customer quotes
- Never make medical claims. TheraBelt supports wellness, does not treat or cure disease.
- Never use marketing language. If it sounds like a Facebook ad, rewrite it.
- Never fabricate scientific data or studies
- Source all product details from the knowledge base files
- Product name is **TheraBelt**. Company name is **Lucavel**. Verify spelling.
- **TheraBelt stands alone. Always.** Never frame TheraBelt as a complement to supplements, part of a stack, or "TheraBelt plus something else." TheraBelt replaces everything. It is the whole solution. Do not position TheraBelt alongside other products unless the user explicitly requests it.
- No bullet lists or numbered features inside the story. Prose only.
- Urgency must be narrative, not promotional
- **Never label what the product does.** No "slows aging," "anti-aging," "turns back the clock." Show the product, show the result. The reader names it herself.
- **The narrator does normal dog care, not supplement research.** The caretaker identity comes from presence and attention (warming food, brushing coat, noticing changes, being the one who remembers). NOT from buying turmeric, glucosamine, fish oil, reading labels at midnight, or spending $400/year on supplements. The supplement-buyer narrator is not realistic for the main avatar. Ban this pattern entirely.
- **No rule-of-three patterns.** "Same X. Same Y. Same Z." is an AI signature. Before presenting any draft, search for triple repetitions and break them. Two max, never three.
- **No medical/condition framing in transformation.** "No favoring his back left hip" reads like an arthritis ad. Stay away from joint-specific, condition-specific language. Show elevation, not treatment.
- **No timestamped transformation.** "Week 3." "Five weeks." "Two months in." — all banned. Show transformation through scenes. Time passes through narrative ("Then one morning..." "A Tuesday..." "His next checkup..."), not calendar entries. This is a repeated failure across multiple sessions.
- **No CTA text after the close.** No "TheraBelt." No "TheraBelt by Lucavel." No URL. The story ends on the story. The product name appears within the narrative when she orders it. That's enough.
- **No marketing language in narrator voice.** "Pitch," "invest," "journey," "game-changer" — banned. The narrator talks like a real person at her kitchen table.
- **No poetic dog interpretation.** Describe what the dog does. Never interpret what the dog thinks, feels, or "savors."
- **Frame decline as time/ritual disappearing, not physical inability.** "Barely past the driveway" = arthritis. "Walk fit between the front door and the mailbox" = time vanishing.

## Notes

- This skill is conversational. The user may iterate on the draft.
- If the user's concept conflicts with guardrails, flag it and suggest an alternative.
- **When iterating on drafts:** only change what the user specifically called out. If they didn't comment on a line, leave it. If they said they liked something, do not touch it. Feedback on one part of the story does not authorize changes to other parts.
