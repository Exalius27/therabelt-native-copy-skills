# Evolve-Native — Reference Material

Read this file during execution when writing and reviewing the draft.

## Section 1: Writing Craft Reference

### Structure
- Product (TheraBelt) never appears before ~50-60% through
- Product enters through the credible character, not a random pitch
- Benefits shown through what happens in the story, never listed
- Narrator never directly tells the reader to buy
- Urgency is narrative, not promotional
- **TheraBelt always stands alone.** Never frame TheraBelt as a complement to supplements, as part of a stack, or alongside other products. TheraBelt replaces everything.
- **Don't say "red light therapy" in the first half.** Build the problem and the catch-22 first. Name the solution late. The reader should feel the trap before she hears the escape.

### Tense and Narrative Mode
- **Use deliberate tense shifts.** Past tense for backstory and setup. Present tense for dramatic scenes where the reader needs to feel like they're there.
- The product must reveal itself through scenes the reader experiences in real time, not through summary or retelling.
- Wrong: "I used it for two months and her coat got better." Right: a scene where someone stops the narrator on a walk and comments on the coat. The reader is in the scene.

### Reading Level and Tone
- **Third-grade reading level. Always.** Every word, every sentence. Simple words, short sentences. If a ten-year-old wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.
- **Third-grade CLARITY, not just vocabulary.** Every line must be understood instantly on first read. No abstract references, no implied meaning, no literary metaphor. If the reader has to think about what a sentence means, rewrite it to say what it actually means.
- First-person. Conversational. Short sentences. Fragments fine.
- Heavy dialogue. Characters talk like real people.
- No marketing language, jargon, or buzzwords. The narrator never uses words like "pitch," "invest," "journey." Real people don't talk like marketers.
- No bullet lists or feature dumps inside the story
- No exclamation marks for emphasis
- **Never use em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence.
- **Use only common, universally known words.** If a word might not be recognized by every reader, replace it with something everyone knows.
- **Every line must be instantly understood on first read.** No vague references. Say what you actually mean.

### Characters
- Keep the cast small. 2-3 named characters max besides the narrator. **Don't name characters who are only mentioned in passing.** "My vet" is enough. Every name the reader has to track costs attention.
- Use simple but original names. Not AI-generic (Sarah, John, Mary) and not overly complex.
- Every character must serve a clear purpose. If two characters do similar things, merge them.
- **The best supporting characters serve 2-3 story functions at once.** A character who provides mechanism education, models the emotional journey, AND delivers the product recommendation is doing real work.
- Supporting characters should not say things that are too wise or perfectly timed. Only characters with real authority get profound lines.

#### The Credible Character
The credible character is the most important supporting character in evolve-native. This person carries the mechanism explanation, the villain reveal, and often the product introduction. They must be carefully constructed.

**Types that work for TheraBelt:**
- Ex-formulation chemist who worked for a pet food company
- Researcher who studied AGEs or glycation in animal nutrition
- Vet who left corporate practice after seeing the system from inside
- Nutritional scientist or food scientist
- Lab technician who tested pet food ingredients

**How to establish authority in 2-3 sentences:**
Give them a specific credential AND a reason they're willing to talk. "She spent eleven years formulating kibble for [major brand]. Left two years ago. Wouldn't say why at first." The credential makes the reader trust them. The reluctance makes the reader curious.

**The credible character is NOT:**
- A conspiracy theorist. They state facts calmly. They're not angry. They're tired.
- An evangelist. They don't push. They answer questions when asked.
- Perfect. They can be uncomfortable talking about it. They can hedge. That makes them more believable.

### Dialogue Authenticity
- **When a character speaks to the narrator, the narrator must respond with actual dialogue.** No silent summaries. Real people talk back.
- **Supporting character reactions must pass the "would a real person say this?" test.** If the line only makes sense as a setup for the next sales beat, cut it.
- **Write what characters SAY, don't leave reactions vague.** Put actual dialogue on the page.
- **Character consistency.** Track what you've established about each character and don't contradict it.
- **No vague pronoun dialogue.** Every line of dialogue must have a clear, specific referent. If a character uses "it," "this," or "that" and the reader has to reconstruct what they mean, rewrite with the specific thing.
- **Minimize the cast.** Don't introduce characters who serve the same function. Merge them.
- **First person possessives.** "My vet," not a named vet who only appears once.
- **Cut unnecessary named characters aggressively.** Only name characters who appear in multiple scenes and do unique work.
- **Track what each character knows and when they learned it.** Timeline inconsistencies between characters destroy believability.
- **Dialogue that exists to move the sales narrative forward instead of sounding like real people talking is the single fastest way to break the story.** Every line of dialogue should pass this test: would this person actually say this in this moment, even if the product didn't exist?

#### Narrator-as-Proxy Dialogue
The narrator asks questions throughout the story. These questions are the reader's questions. They pre-handle objections inside the story.

Rules for narrator questions:
- She's uninformed, not stupid. "Wait, so the food is doing this?" is smart-ignorant. "Duh, what's an AGE?" is dumb-dumb. Big difference.
- She resists. She doesn't immediately believe. "That sounds like one of those conspiracy things." The credible character's response to that pushback is what makes it convincing.
- She asks follow-ups. When the credible character explains something, the narrator pushes deeper. "Okay but what about supplements? I give him glucosamine every day." This is an objection the reader has, handled inside the story.
- She has emotional reactions, not just intellectual ones. "I've been feeding him this for eight years." That's not a question. That's the moment the trap closes.
- **Never have the narrator ask a question that only exists to set up the next talking point.** Her questions must feel like a real person processing information she doesn't want to hear.

### Antagonist / Villain System
- The villain is systemic, not personal. Big Pet Food, the corporate structure, the profit cycle.
- Never attack individual vets. Frame: "trained by the people who make the food."
- The villain reveal lands through facts: who owns what, who funds what, who profits from what.
- The villain must be present early or build naturally from the story, never dropped in halfway through a lecture.
- **Don't force conspiracy energy.** The credible character states facts. The narrator reacts. The reader draws conclusions. Nobody needs to say "can you believe this?" The facts speak for themselves.
- **The villain creates the us-vs-them that makes the reader care.** Her and her dog vs. a system that was designed to profit from both the problem and the treatment.

### Science and Mechanism
- **Mechanism goes deeper than Cooper-level in evolve-native.** The full chain is delivered through the credible character: AGEs from processed food, RAGE activation, chronic inflammation, blocked cytochrome c oxidase, cellular energy failure, inability to repair.
- **Delivered by the credible character, not by narration.** The mechanism lives in dialogue. The narrator asks questions. The credible character explains in simpler terms each time.
- **The mechanism must be accurate but accessible.** The science is real. AGEs, RAGE receptors, cytochrome c oxidase are real biochemistry. But the credible character explains it like they're talking to a friend over coffee, not presenting at a conference.
- **Plain-English equivalents for each step:**
  - AGEs = "basically, when you cook protein at high temperatures, it creates these compounds. Every bag of kibble is cooked at 400 degrees."
  - RAGE activation = "your dog's cells have these receptors. When AGEs hit them, they trigger inflammation. Not the kind you can see. The kind that happens inside the cell."
  - Blocked cytochrome c oxidase = "there's an engine inside every cell that makes energy. The inflammation jams it. The engine still works. It's just stuck."
  - Cellular energy failure = "without energy, the cell can't repair itself. Can't fight damage. Can't do its job."
  - The catch-22 = "and here's the thing. Better food, supplements, everything you'd try, they all need the cell to have energy to process them. But the cell doesn't have energy. That's the whole problem."
  - Red light = "light goes through the skin, hits the engine directly, clears the jam. Doesn't need digestion. Doesn't need the cell to do anything. Just hits it."
- **Never cross into aging language.** "Slows aging," "turns back the clock," "anti-aging" are all banned. The mechanism is about cellular energy and repair, not aging.
- **The "dogs age faster so it works faster" reframe is not mandatory.** Can appear if natural. Should never be forced.

### Story Realism
- Every scene and dialogue exchange must feel like it actually happened.
- **Every character action needs a motivation a real person would actually have.** If the motivation only exists to create the scene, find a realistic reason.
- Dog breeds must be common and relatable (Goldens, Labs, Retrievers). No rare breeds.
- When stating a dog's age, be accurate to the breed's real lifespan.
- **When citing another dog as proof, give specific visual details.** WHY couldn't they believe it? Coat still thick, still gets up to greet people, moves like a dog half its age.
- **No dramatic unrealistic narrator behavior.** Every narrator action must pass: "would a real woman actually do this?"
- **No vague emotional lines.** If the narrator is crying, say WHY in plain words.
- **Every detail must be earned narratively.** If a detail feels dropped in from nowhere, it IS from nowhere. Build the setup or cut it.
- **Traumatic or significant events get specific time anchors.** "Last Tuesday" or "It happened on a Tuesday, I got home from work." Never "A Tuesday night" — that vagueness breaks immersion at the moment the reader most needs to feel present. The more traumatic the event, the more specific the time reference must be.
- **Use the dog's name and vary how you refer to them throughout.** Alternate between the name, "my sweet boy," "my lab," "my nine-year-old," "my soul dog" — whatever fits the narrator's voice. Never refer to the dog generically for more than two sentences in a row.

### Lead and Crisis Depth

Before the mechanism, the lead must do three things in order:

1. **Show the dog healthy and present before the crisis.** Specific behaviors that made this dog this dog — running in the yard, making it to the back door first, stealing socks. The reader must feel what was there before it was threatened.

2. **Deepen the crisis.** The emergency visit is not just a plot point. Give it weight: the bill, the hours in the waiting room, the confusion of "he was fine this morning." These details are what make the reader feel the vulnerability — not just of this dog, but of her own.

3. **Earn the symptom reveal before delivering it.** The credible character must ask the narrator "does your dog do X? What about Y? And Z?" — and the narrator answers yes, yes, yes — before the credible character names them as symptoms. This sequence is mandatory. The reader is confirming her own dog's behaviors before she learns what they mean. Never announce "those are symptoms" before the narrator (and reader) has confirmed each one.

### Transformation Pacing
- Changes are gradual, not instant. The narrator should doubt it's working before she's sure.
- **HARD BAN: No week or month numbers in transformation.** Use scene-based time: "Then one morning..." "A Tuesday..." "His next checkup..." Time passes through narrative, not calendar entries.
- **Never write week-by-week or month-by-month transformation timelines.**
- Show transformation through scenes. Pick one or two powerful moments and put the reader inside them.
- **Bridge transitions between scenes.** Add brief bridge lines so pacing doesn't feel instant.
- **Restoration must be partial, not complete.** "He's still a little slower on the stairs. That's fine. He's nine." Full reversal breaks belief. Better, not young.
- **Add a private knowledge moment after the first sign of change.** "I didn't say anything to anyone. I didn't want to jinx it."
- **Transformation must open with no change.** The first days after using TheraBelt, the narrator notices nothing. This is mandatory. It builds believability and creates the gap that the first real change closes. "The first few days, nothing. I wasn't surprised. I told myself to give it time." Never skip this beat.

### Urgency
- **Story-driven urgency, not scarcity.** The urgency is the information itself. Every day the reader doesn't know this, the damage continues. Every bowl of kibble is another dose of AGEs. Every day the cells can't make energy is another day of cumulative damage.
- **One-time purchase framing.** Frame against vet bills ($1,200/day ER, $300-400/month NSAIDs, $11k cancer treatment). "I spend more than that every time I walk into the vet's office."
- **Urgency lives inside scenes, never in standalone paragraphs.** If urgency is disconnected from the story, it reads as a sales pitch.
- **No batch scarcity, no "limited supply," no countdown timers.** The fear of the PROBLEM is the urgency. The cost of NOT acting is measured in damage, not dollars.
- **The close should feel like a decision the reader makes herself.** "Now that you know, what do you do?" Not "buy before it runs out."

### Patterns to Avoid

The following patterns have been corrected multiple times across sessions. Each is a hard ban.

- **No marketing language in narrator voice.** "Pitch," "invest," "journey," "game-changer," "hack" all banned. The narrator talks like a real person.
- **Never use "relay character" chains to introduce the credible character.** "A friend told me about a vet she'd heard of who gave her a number" is two unnecessary degrees of separation. If the credible character is a vet, introduce them directly — through the original vet, a neighbor, a coworker. One bridge maximum, always someone who knew them personally.
- **Never use "whatever you're doing, keep doing it" as vet validation.** It sounds like a marketing line. The vet should validate the dog directly: "He's doing really well for his age. His numbers look great." She comments on the dog, not on the narrator's behavior.
- **Never frame kibble continuation as a confession.** "I still feed him kibble, I know how that sounds" reads as an apology that undermines everything before it. Instead frame it as the payoff: "Here's the best part — you don't have to switch foods. For the first time, the food he's already eating actually works." The catch-22 resolution is not a confession. It's liberation.
- **"A couple days later" for ordering delivery. Never name the exact number of days.** "The box showed up four days later" is oddly specific and reads constructed. "A couple days later" is how real people talk.
- **The last line must be gut-punching.** Any loving dog owner should feel compelled to act after reading it. The strongest closes create the fear of the version where the narrator never made the call — and let the reader project that onto her own dog. "He's nine. There's a version of this where I never made that call. I don't like thinking about that version for very long." Do not settle for a warm, restrained close when a gut-punch is available.
- **No poetic dog interpretation.** Describe what the dog DOES. Never interpret what the dog is thinking, feeling, or "savoring." One simple action. No stacking poetic actions.
- **No forced artificial beats.** No fake dramatic delays ("I don't ask what it's called. Not yet."). No fake scripted regret lines. Never force framework beats where they contradict the scene.
- **Frame decline as time/ritual disappearing, not physical inability.** "Barely past the driveway" = arthritis. "Last week the whole walk fit between our front door and the mailbox" = time disappearing.
- **Never copy framework example dialogue into a draft.** The examples show emotional WEIGHT, not words to reuse. If you can swap the dialogue into a different story and it still works, it's framework paste.
- **No rule-of-three patterns.** Say it once well. Two max, never three.
- **No negation-contrast formula.** Never define something by listing what it's NOT before saying what it IS. One instance in 1,500 words is the absolute max.
- No AI-sounding filler ("here's the part that got me," "that's when it hit me," "let me tell you").
- No fake emotional beats ("I sat on the floor and cried"). Emotion must be earned.
- **ZERO dialogue tags. ZERO stage directions. No exceptions.** No "she said," "he asked," "she paused," "she looked at me." Before presenting ANY draft, search for these and delete every instance.
- **Don't introduce product name and company name separately.** Just introduce TheraBelt. One name.
- **No forced data dumps.** Industry statistics must arise naturally from the story's emotional arc.
- **No DR-style direct address in the close.** Never write "If your dog..." The close stays in the narrator's world.
- **Use pronouns naturally.** Don't keep repeating names when "he" or "she" is obvious from context.
- **No poetic or literary lines.** If it sounds like it belongs in a novel, rewrite it as plain speech.
- **No long adjustment lists.** Two examples max. The reader gets the pattern from two.
- **No meta-commentary.** The narrator never comments on not-selling or notices her own patterns.
- **No fake deflections in dialogue.** "I just started paying more attention" is a scripted humble line. Be real.
- **No conspiracy language.** "They don't want you to know," "wake up," "open your eyes," "the truth they're hiding," "what they're not telling you." All banned. The credible character states facts. The narrator reacts. Nobody needs to sound like a YouTube thumbnail.

### Hooks and Leads
- **The hook and the lead are different things.** Feedback on one does not mean changes to the other.
- The first three lines must be emotionally compelling and specific.
- **Hooks must be simple.** Not too much happening at once.
- The hook must make the reader feel something, not just wonder something.
- The hook must answer "why should I read this?" from the reader's perspective.
- **Never break the story's flow with narrator bio lines.**
- Test: would a woman scrolling her phone at 10pm stop for this? If not, rewrite.
- **The hook must require zero cognitive steps to understand.** No character names the reader hasn't met. "My dog" not "Rosie."
- **The hook must filter for the buyer.** A hook that attracts broad engagement but doesn't call out the target avatar is the wrong hook.
- **Nostalgic hooks are dead hooks.** Nobody stops scrolling for warmth or nostalgia. They stop for contrast, pain, or an image they can't ignore.
- **Write the hook. Do not present hook options.**

#### Hooks for Unaware Audiences
Evolve-native hooks are different from story-native hooks. The reader doesn't know she has a problem yet. She's not looking for a solution. She's scrolling.

The hook must:
- Filter for dog owners (especially senior dog owners) in the first few words
- Create an open loop that demands resolution
- NOT reveal what the ad is about
- NOT mention the product, the villain, the mechanism, or anything that tips the ad

The hook creates curiosity. The lead establishes the credible character. The problem comes after both.

### Emotion

**Emotion is the engine of the entire story. Without it, nothing works.** A story that moves through events without making the reader feel something is just a report.

**The narrator must feel things and the reader must feel them too.** Her love for her dog, her alarm at the mechanism, her anger at the system, her relief when she finds the solution. These aren't background details. They're the substance.

**Emotion comes from specificity and breathing room.** Don't write "I was scared." Write the moment with enough detail that the reader feels it herself.

**We are writing stories, not ad copy.** If the draft reads like a well-organized report, it's missing the point.

**The emotional arc in evolve-native is different from story-native.** In story-native, the arc is: love/invisible effort, identity challenge, transformation, recognition. In evolve-native, the arc is: normalcy, alarm, anger at the system, the catch-22 trap, determination, relief. Both require real emotion. But the emotional engine is fear and determination, not love and validation.

**Use a sensory motif to track the story's emotional arc.** Pick one specific sound, sight, or physical detail that represents the dog's presence. Introduce it as what the narrator loves. Show it fading as the fear. Return it as the transformation proof.

## Section 2: Evolve Strat Architecture Reference

This section expands on each of the 10 architecture points from the SKILL.md outline with TheraBelt-specific guidance.

### Point 1: Hook (Unaware, Filtering, Open Loop)

The reader doesn't know she has a problem. She's scrolling. The hook must stop her without revealing what the ad is about.

**What works for TheraBelt:**
- A narrator situation involving her dog that creates tension or curiosity
- A moment that filters for senior dog owners or dedicated dog caretakers
- An open loop the reader can't close without reading further

**Common failure modes:**
- Hook reveals the topic (pet food, cellular damage, industry corruption) too early
- Hook is broadly interesting but doesn't filter for dog owners
- Hook is nostalgic or warm instead of sharp
- Hook requires cognitive steps to understand

### Point 2: Lead / Credible Character

The credible character must be introduced BEFORE the problem is named. The reader must trust this person before hearing the threat.

**Pacing:** The credible character should enter naturally through the story. A conversation at a barbecue. A coworker's offhand comment. A neighbor who used to work in the industry. The narrator doesn't seek them out. They cross paths.

**The setup:** One or two sentences of characterization. What they did. Why they know. Why they're willing to talk. The reader should think "oh, this person actually knows what they're talking about" before the mechanism starts.

### Point 3: Problem Mechanism Delivery

The full chain delivered through dialogue. This is the hardest section to write well.

**Pacing device:** The narrator's questions. Every time the credible character explains something, the narrator asks a follow-up. This breaks the mechanism into digestible pieces and keeps it feeling like a conversation, not a lecture.

**Common failure modes:**
- The mechanism section reads like a textbook. Fix by adding more narrator reactions and emotional responses between explanations.
- The credible character sounds like a professor. Fix by having them use analogies and plain language. "Think of it like an engine that's jammed."
- The section is too long. The mechanism needs to be complete but concise. Each step in the chain is 2-3 sentences of dialogue max.
- The narrator accepts everything without pushback. Fix by adding resistance. "That sounds like a conspiracy theory" or "My vet never mentioned any of this."

### Point 4: Villain Reveal

Facts delivered as facts. The reader draws conclusions.

**The key fact:** Mars Inc. owns the food companies AND the vet hospitals. This is the "Mars owns the food and the hospital" moment. It's the fact you can't unhear.

**How to deliver it:**
- Through the credible character, calmly
- The narrator asks "wait, who makes [brand]?" and the answer is the reveal
- Or the narrator looks it up herself after the conversation and discovers it
- The revelation should feel like a puzzle piece clicking into place, not like someone shouting at her

**What the villain reveal does structurally:**
- Explains why the system hasn't fixed this (the system profits from the status quo)
- Explains why her vet never told her (vet was trained by the system)
- Creates anger that fuels the urgency to act

### Point 5: Condemn the Obvious Solution

This is where the catch-22 lands. The three things condemned:

1. **Better food** — still needs cellular energy to digest and absorb. The cell's energy production is blocked. Better ingredients going to a cell that can't process them.
2. **Supplements** — same problem. Must be digested, absorbed, transported, then the cell needs energy to USE them. "Sending packages to a house with nobody home."
3. **Vet care** — manages symptoms downstream. NSAIDs for inflammation, joint supplements for mobility. Never touches the cell. Treats what the cell can't do, never fixes why the cell can't do it.

**The emotional beat:** Validate first, then condemn. "You've been doing everything right. That's not the problem. The problem is that none of it can reach a cell that's already running on empty." The reader must NOT feel attacked. She must feel trapped.

### Point 6: Product Through Credible Character

TheraBelt enters as the logical conclusion, not a sales pitch. The credible character explains that only light bypasses digestion. Two wavelengths. Hits the mitochondria directly through skin and fur. The only thing that doesn't need the cell to already be working.

**Product name drops organically.** "She told me about this belt. TheraBelt. You put it on the dog for fifteen minutes." Casual. Not a presentation.

**The narrator's reaction should be skepticism first.** "A belt? That sounded like something from a late-night infomercial." Then the logic chain overcomes it: nothing else bypasses digestion. This does. She's already seen the catch-22. The logic is airtight.

### Point 7: Category Repositioning

The reader never compares TheraBelt to other light therapy. The comparison is always:
- TheraBelt vs. supplements (supplements need digestion; TheraBelt doesn't)
- TheraBelt vs. better food (food needs cellular energy to process; light doesn't)
- TheraBelt vs. vet care (vets treat symptoms; TheraBelt addresses the cell)

The conceptual reframe: "Nutrients vs. energy." Everything else sends nutrients. TheraBelt restores the cell's ability to use them.

### Point 8: Transformation

Shown through scenes. Gradual. Doubted. Then undeniable.

The emotional resolution in evolve-native is different from story-native. It's not just "my dog looks younger." It's "for the first time, I'm doing something that actually reaches the problem." Relief from the trap.

The reader should feel: if SHE does this for HER dog, she'll feel the same relief.

### Point 9: Buying Experience Modeled

The narrator orders. A couple days later it arrives. She puts it on the dog. Mental rehearsal for the reader.

One-time purchase. Frame against vet bills with specific numbers the narrator has actually paid — the emergency visit, the follow-ups, the cost of doing nothing. Never "I spend more than that every time I walk into the vet's office" — that's too clean, too rhetorical. Make her do real math: "The emergency visit alone was more than that." Specific is always stronger than vague.

Never say "The box showed up X days later" with a specific number. Always "A couple days later."

### Point 10: Story-Driven Urgency

The urgency is the information. Every day the reader doesn't act, the damage continues. This is fundamentally different from scarcity urgency.

The close should feel inevitable. The reader has learned what's happening to her dog. She's learned that nothing she's been doing can fix it. She's learned that one thing can. What does she do? The answer is obvious. The story doesn't need to push.

## Section 3: V4 Mechanism Chain (Accurate Science Reference)

This section is reference for the AI writer. The credible character in the story delivers a simplified, conversational version. Never the full chain as written here.

### The chain:
1. **Processed pet food contains AGEs** (Advanced Glycation End-products) at approximately 122x the concentration found in human food. AGEs form when proteins are cooked at high temperatures. Every bag of kibble is extruded at 300-400 degrees.

2. **AGEs activate RAGE** (Receptor for Advanced Glycation End-products) on cell surfaces. RAGE is a pattern recognition receptor. When AGEs bind to it, it triggers an inflammatory signaling cascade.

3. **RAGE activation triggers the NF-kB pathway**, leading to chronic systemic inflammation. Not acute inflammation (redness, swelling). Chronic, low-grade, invisible inflammation at the cellular level.

4. **Chronic inflammation disrupts mitochondrial function**, specifically by increasing nitric oxide production. Excess nitric oxide binds to cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV of the electron transport chain).

5. **Blocked cytochrome c oxidase = reduced ATP production.** The cell's energy factory is jammed. The machinery works. It's just stuck.

6. **Cells without energy can't repair.** Can't fight DNA damage. Can't clear damaged proteins. Can't maintain normal function. Accumulated damage over years leads to the chronic diseases that kill most dogs: cancer (1 in 3), kidney disease, heart disease, liver disease.

### The catch-22:
- Better food: still needs cellular energy to digest, absorb, and process nutrients. The cell doesn't have it.
- Supplements: same problem. Must be digested, absorbed, transported, then the cell needs energy to USE them.
- Vet care: treats symptoms (inflammation, pain, organ failure) downstream of the energy deficit. Never touches the cell itself.
- All three condemned with one logic: everything requires the cell to already have energy. But the cell doesn't have energy. That's the problem.

### The solution:
- Red light (660nm + 850nm wavelengths) photons penetrate skin and fur.
- Photons hit cytochrome c oxidase directly.
- Displaces the nitric oxide that's blocking the enzyme.
- Electron transport chain resumes. Cell makes ATP again.
- Cell didn't get energy FROM light. It got its ability to make energy BACK.
- "Fixing the stove, not giving a meal."
- Even if kibble creates more AGEs tomorrow, unjamming today lets cells repair accumulated damage. Both coexist.
- No digestion, no absorption, no processing needed. Light bypasses everything.

### Key framing:
- "Whatever you feed your dog, his cells are under stress every day. TheraBelt is the only thing that addresses the blockage directly."
- Cells aren't DESTROYED, they're BLOCKED. The machinery works, it's just jammed.
- Don't need reader to stop buying kibble. One product, one behavior change.
- The catch-22 is the conversion engine: the thing she'd try next is already condemned.

## Section 5: Narrator-as-Proxy Craft Rules

The narrator is the reader's stand-in. Everything the narrator experiences, the reader experiences. This is the core structural difference from story-native (where the narrator is a specific character the reader projects onto).

### The Narrator's Emotional Arc
1. **Normalcy** — she feeds her dog, takes him to the vet, does what everyone does. She thinks everything is fine.
2. **Curiosity** — she meets the credible character. Something they say catches her attention.
3. **Alarm** — she learns what's actually happening at the cellular level. The problem is bigger than she thought.
4. **Anger** — she learns about the system. The villain reveal. "They profit from both sides?"
5. **The trap** — the catch-22. She realizes everything she'd try is already condemned. There's no way out through the normal channels.
6. **Determination** — she finds TheraBelt. The one thing that bypasses the system.
7. **Relief** — she uses it. She sees results. For the first time, she's doing something that actually reaches the problem.

This arc IS the reader's arc. The narrator goes through every stage first, and the reader follows.

### Rules for the Narrator
- She starts where the reader starts: uninformed, trusting the system, doing what she's been told
- She asks the questions the reader would ask
- She pushes back when something sounds too extreme
- She has emotional reactions to information, not just intellectual ones
- She's not a conspiracy theorist and she's suspicious of anything that sounds like conspiracy
- She's a regular person processing something she doesn't want to hear
- Her resistance is what makes the eventual belief convincing

### The "Dumb Question" Technique
Every question the narrator asks pre-handles an objection. Examples:
- "But what about organic food? Isn't that better?" → condemns premium food
- "I give him glucosamine every day. That helps, right?" → condemns supplements
- "My vet said his bloodwork looks fine." → addresses vet blind spots
- "That sounds like one of those conspiracy things." → addresses reader's skepticism head-on
- "If this is real, why hasn't anyone done anything about it?" → opens the villain reveal

The question must feel natural. The narrator asks because she genuinely doesn't know and genuinely wants to challenge what she's hearing. Not because the script needs her to.

## Section 6: Self-Review Checklist

### Story Quality
- Is the concept strong enough that someone would stop scrolling for it?
- Is the reader with the narrator the whole time?
- Are there at least 2-3 real scenes with time, place, and detail?
- Does the narrator think out loud? Can the reader hear doubt, skepticism, alarm?
- Does the whole thing feel like a story from first word to last?
- Does it feel authentic, like a real person typed this?
- Would people engage with this as if it were a real post?

### Evolve-Native Ad Requirements (10 variables)
- [ ] Hook: unaware, filtering, open loop, zero product mention?
- [ ] Credible character: introduced before problem, authority established?
- [ ] Villain system: Big Pet Food facts, no conspiracy energy, vets protected?
- [ ] Problem mechanism: full AGEs/RAGE/inflammation/cytochrome c oxidase chain through dialogue?

- [ ] Story-driven urgency: information is the urgency, no batch scarcity?

### Copy Architecture (Four Errors)
1. **Movies vs mirrors** — is the reader seeing her own life reflected, or watching someone else's? If the story completes as entertainment, the reader scrolls. If she sees her dog in every scene, she acts.
2. **Emotional peak** — is the high point on the threat/catch-22 revelation (the trap closing), or on a human relationship? The peak must be on the moment the reader realizes the system is broken and there's only one way out.
3. **Buyer action** — does the reader feel compelled to act because the threat applies to HER dog, or does she just feel informed? Information without personal stakes is a documentary, not an ad.
4. **Benefit urgency** — is there urgency to stop the damage (every day without this, the cells can't repair), not just urgency to buy before a price increase?
