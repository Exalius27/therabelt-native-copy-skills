# The Storytelling Copy Evaluator

A Complete Audit Framework for Narrative-First Branded Storytelling

Version 2.0 — Built from the Luhxe Playbook, Reddit Story Analysis & TheraBelt Calibration

**What this framework is for:** Evaluating long-form branded fiction stories — first-person narratives that read as organic human experience, not advertising. The product appears inside the story, not above it. The reader is persuaded through emotional identification, not logical argument.

**What this framework is NOT for:** Traditional direct response copy, landing pages, VSLs, or any format where the reader knows they are being sold to.

**Two story types, one standard:** Some stories are problem-unaware — the reader doesn't know they need the product, so a human antagonist must create that desire (the Luhxe model). Other stories are problem-aware — the reader already carries the wound, so the problem itself is the engine and structural contrast does the selling (the TheraBelt model). Both types are evaluated against the same persuasion functions. The vehicle is different. The standard is the same.

---

## The Governing Principle

The copy evaluator you're replacing asks: "Does this copy persuade?"

This framework asks a different question: "Does this story breathe?"

A story that breathes is one the reader forgot was an ad. They are inside it. They are the protagonist. By the time the product appears, they don't experience it as a product — they experience it as a solution they just discovered alongside someone they trust.

The moment the reader becomes aware they are being sold to, the story is dead. No amount of technical polish resurrects it. Authenticity is not one checkpoint among many — it is the entire game.

**The single test that overrides all others:** If you posted this story anonymously in a relevant Facebook group or subreddit — no brand name in the post, no CTA, no disclosure — would the comments read "this is an ad" or would they read "I felt this in my soul"?

If the answer is the former, no other checkpoint matters. Stop. Rebuild the story from a real human moment, not a marketing brief.

---

## How to Use This Framework

**Before evaluation:** Read the story once, straight through, without a pen in hand. Let it land the way a reader would. Note where you tensed, where you smiled, where you rolled your eyes. These instincts are data.

**During evaluation:** Work through each checkpoint in order. Do not skip ahead. Checkpoint 0 is a gate — the story does not proceed to technical evaluation until it passes.

**After evaluation:** Your lowest-scoring checkpoints reveal the story's failure mode. The repair instructions in each checkpoint tell you exactly what went wrong and how to fix it.

**Scoring:** Each checkpoint is scored out of 10. Total is out of 110. The passing threshold for a publishable story is 90/110 (82%), with no single checkpoint below 7/10.

## Checkpoint 0: The Organic Content Test (THE GATE)

### No Story Proceeds Until It Passes This

**The Setup:** You are about to paste this story into a Facebook group called "Women Over 45 Who Travel Alone" or a subreddit like r/TrueOffMyChest. No branding. No CTA. No disclosure. Just the story.

**React as a lurker, not a marketer.**

You've seen a thousand posts in this group. You have a sharp nose for sponsored content — you've called it out before. You're scrolling Tuesday night. You're tired. You have zero patience for anything that sounds like someone trying to sell you something.

### The Four Immediate Reactions

**1. The Scroll Test** — Read the first three sentences only. As the lurker:
- Did you keep reading? (PASS)
- Did you recognize a narrative hook that felt personal and specific? (PASS)
- Did you smell a setup? Did anything feel like it was building toward a product? (FAIL)
- Did the opening feel like it could have been posted by someone in your group right now? (PASS)

The most common first-line failures:
- Starting with the protagonist's credentials ("I'm a 54-year-old executive who travels for work...")
- Starting with the conflict too perfectly framed ("My daughter-in-law had decided I was senile...")
- Starting with a question the protagonist is clearly about to answer ("Have you ever been underestimated by someone close to you?")

The Luhxe ads that work best open mid-scene, mid-feeling:
- "After my husband died, my best friends cut me off."
- "My ex-husband's new wife and I ended up on the same flight to Barcelona for my daughter's thirtieth birthday trip. She didn't know I was coming. I did."
- "I got fired after 12 years as a flight attendant."

These are not setups. They are the middle of something. The reader is dropped in. There is no marketing breath before the story begins.

**2. The Comments Section Test** — Imagine the first five comments this post would receive. Write them out.
- Are they "omg I felt this," "this is literally my mother-in-law," "I'm crying at my desk"? → PASS
- Are they "this is an ad," "nice try luhxe," "who writes these, a copywriter?" → FAIL
- Are they polite but distant, like "nice story, thanks for sharing"? → BORDERLINE FAIL (the story didn't land; it was processed, not felt)

**3. The Brand Removal Test** — Remove every mention of the brand name, the bag description, and the product's features. Does the story still work as a standalone piece? Does it still have emotional weight, conflict, resolution?

If the story collapses without the product, the product was doing the story's work instead of the story doing the product's work. **The story should be able to exist without the product. The product should not be able to exist without the story.**

**4. The "Did I Write This" Test** — Identify your protagonist's exact archetype (divorced woman, 54, marketing career, daughter's birthday trip). Find three to five real women who match that description. Would any of them read this story and think "someone got inside my head"?

If you're not sure, you haven't done enough avatar research. The story must feel private — like it was written by someone in the protagonist's exact situation, for people in that exact situation, not broadcast to a general audience.

### Checkpoint 0 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Question | Score |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| The Scroll Test | Does the opening drop the reader mid-scene with no setup? | /10 |
| The Comments Test | Would organic readers respond emotionally, not skeptically? | /10 |
| The Brand Removal Test | Does the story have full weight without the product? | /10 |
| The "Did I Write This" Test | Would the protagonist archetype feel privately understood? | /10 |
| Voice Naturalness | Does the writing sound like a real person, not a copywriter? | /10 |

**Checkpoint 0 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

### Gate Rule

- **40+/50:** PASS. Proceed to Checkpoints 1-10.
- **Below 40/50:** STOP. The story sounds like marketing. Do not polish it — polishing a story that sounds like an ad only makes a more polished-sounding ad. Rebuild from a real human moment. Return to this checkpoint before proceeding.

### The Three Reasons Stories Fail Checkpoint 0

1. **The writer started with the product and built a story around it.** The story must start with the human experience. The product appears inside it, the way a friend's recommendation appears inside a real conversation.
2. **The specificity is generic.** "A woman who had been underestimated" is a type. "A woman whose daughter-in-law had just sent her a laminated address card in case she 'got confused' in Rome" is a person.
3. **The prose has marketing DNA.** Phrases like "life-changing," "exactly what I needed," "game-changer," "I couldn't believe how well it worked" — these are marketing phrases. Real people don't talk this way. Real people say "I cried in my car" and "her face. I'm still thinking about her face."

## Checkpoint 1: Protagonist Specificity

### Is She a Person or a Persona?

The Luhxe stories that work best have protagonists with one quality that makes them impossible to confuse with a marketing avatar: a specific, unnecessary detail that no copywriter would think to invent.

The dad who pretended not to swim (from the Reddit stories) works because of a photograph. One photograph taken decades earlier. The writer didn't explain why the dad lied — they showed a photograph of him treading water in the ocean in his twenties, and let the reader do the work.

That photograph is a Pamplemousse moment. It has no marketing function. It exists only because it happened.

### The Specificity Audit

**A. Demographic Specificity (Not Enough on Its Own)**
- Age: Is it a number, not a range? Not "mid-50s" but 54.
- Occupation: Specific enough to create context? Not "a professional" but "a corporate communications executive," "a flight attendant for 22 years," "a travel agent who books executives."
- Relationship context: Is her situation specific enough to locate her? Not "recently divorced" but "divorced fourteen months ago, after 28 years."

**B. Behavioral Specificity (The Real Test)** — This is where most stories fail. The protagonist needs at least two behaviors that could only belong to her, not to a category of women:
- The 22-year-old with terminal cancer who bought a crab at a fancy ass seafood place. Not "treated herself." A crab. At a specific type of restaurant.
- The woman who packed her daughter's birthday card — the one she made at five years old, crayon on pink construction paper, curled at the corners — in the inside zip pocket.
- The woman who ran five miles that morning, the same morning her daughter-in-law texted that she "must be exhausted from all that standing."

These behavioral details do three things simultaneously:
1. They prove the writer has genuinely inhabited this person's world.
2. They make the protagonist feel like a specific human being, not an archetype.
3. They create the "private" quality — the sense that the story was written by someone who was actually there.

**C. Voice Specificity** — Does the protagonist sound like herself? Not like a marketing voice trying to sound authentic.

Run the Phrase Audit:
- Highlight every sentence in the first person.
- Read them aloud as the protagonist would say them.
- Mark any phrase that sounds like it came from a copywriter rather than a person in this situation.
- Common failures: "I realized I had two choices," "that's when everything changed," "I knew I had to prove them wrong," "I was done apologizing."
- These are real phrases — but they're phrases someone writes when they're narrating their life for an audience. Real people telling real stories use different language. They say "I sat there for an hour after we hung up" and "her voice. That voice she uses. The 'let me explain this to a child' voice."

**D. The One Unnecessary Detail** — Every story needs at least one detail that serves no persuasive function whatsoever. It exists because it happened. Or because it's true-feeling enough to have happened.

If you cannot identify that detail in your story, you have not written a person. You have written a persona.

### Checkpoint 1 Scoring (/40 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Demographic specificity (precise numbers, real occupations) | /10 |
| Behavioral specificity (at least 2 details that belong only to her) | /10 |
| Voice specificity (reads like a person, not a copywriter doing a person) | /10 |
| The unnecessary detail (the Pamplemousse moment) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 1 Total: /40 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 4 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

If behavioral specificity is low: Go back to your protagonist's situation and ask what she would actually do, buy, say, worry about. Not what "a woman in her situation" would do — what this woman, with this specific history, would do. The answer is almost always more specific and more interesting than your first instinct.

If voice specificity is low: Read the story aloud in a monotone and mark every sentence that sounds performed. Rewrite those sentences as if you were texting a close friend about what happened. The texting voice is closer to the real voice than the essay voice.

## Checkpoint 2: The Emotional Wound

### Is the Wound the Right Size?

The emotional wound is the story's center of gravity. Everything orbits it. The wound must be specific enough to sting, proportionate enough that the product can resolve it, and recognizable enough that the reader has felt some version of it.

The wound takes different forms depending on the story's awareness level:

**Problem-unaware stories (dignity wound):** The wound is interpersonal — being underestimated, dismissed, or patronized by someone in the protagonist's orbit. The reader doesn't know she has a problem until the antagonist creates one. The packing guide, the laminated address cards, the wheelchair booking — these are specific, small, domestic moments of condescension that sting precisely because they're wrapped in warmth.

**Problem-aware stories (loss wound):** The wound is a loss the reader already carries. The reader knows the problem. She doesn't need a character to show her — she lives it every day. The wound is the specific thing that stopped, the specific absence that hurts. Not "my dog is getting old" but "my dog stopped stealing shoes." The story's job is to prove "I see exactly what you're going through" — to name the wound so precisely that the reader feels privately understood.

In both cases, the calibration principles are the same:

**Too small:** The reader shrugs. Not enough wound to carry a story.

**Too large:** The stakes have overwhelmed the product's ability to resolve them. A dignity wound that's really about abuse, or a loss wound that's really about terminal illness — no product can carry that weight.

**The sweet spot:** Specific enough to sting, recognizable enough to feel universal, proportionate enough that the resolution feels honest.

#### Benchmarks

**Dignity wound benchmark (problem-unaware):** The packing guide. It required effort (she made a guide), was unsolicited (Claire sent it "in case it's helpful"), was specific to the protagonist's supposed incompetence (international travel), was wrapped in warmth ("I've done Barcelona three times, happy to be a resource"), and the protagonist couldn't object without seeming difficult. The laminated address cards are even more perfect — your daughter-in-law thinks you might get confused about where you're staying. In Rome. On your own.

**Loss wound benchmark (problem-aware):** The shoe stealing stopping. Not "my dog slowed down" — a specific behavior that defined the dog, that the family organized their lives around, that visitors were warned about. Its absence is devastating precisely because it used to be an annoyance. "Nobody needed to be warned about Hank anymore" reframes an irritation as an identity marker — and its loss as a death of personality.

### The Wound Calibration Test

**A. Specificity of the Wound** — Is the wound specific to this protagonist in this situation? Or is it generic?

For dignity wounds: Generic: "She treated me like I couldn't handle things." Specific: "She'd booked me a ground-floor room — elevators can be tricky." The specific version shows the antagonist's thinking and reveals exactly what category of incapability she's projected onto the protagonist.

For loss wounds: Generic: "My dog was getting old." Specific: "My dog stopped stealing shoes off the mat. I didn't train him out of it. He just couldn't get up in time." The specific version identifies the exact behavior that defined the dog and names its absence as the wound.

**B. The Delivery Mechanism** — How is the wound delivered?

For dignity wounds, the best wounds come wrapped in something that makes them impossible to object to:
- Excessive warmth ("I just want to make sure you're okay!")
- Practical helpfulness (the packing guide, the wheelchair assistance booking)
- Concern framed as care ("I'm worried about you being overwhelmed")
- Public performance of concern ("Mom! You must be exhausted from all that standing.")

The wound wrapped in warmth is devastating because it strips the protagonist of the ability to respond.

For loss wounds, the best wounds accumulate gradually and then crystallize in a single moment:
- Gradual escalation: "He'd take a minute to stand up. Then two minutes. Then he'd wait for me to come to him."
- Crystallized moment: the birthday scene where shoes are everywhere and Hank lifts his head, looks at the kids, and puts it back down. The grandmother standing in the doorway watching him sleep through the visit.

The accumulation makes the wound feel inevitable. The crystallized moment makes it feel unbearable.

**C. The Recognition Factor** — Would the reader say "I've felt that"?

"That exact thing" is not required. What's required is that the wound maps to a category of experience the reader has lived. The packing guide maps to every time someone assumed a woman couldn't handle something she'd been handling her whole life. The shoe stealing maps to every dog owner who's watched a behavior disappear and realized what it meant.

**D. The Stakes** — What does the protagonist stand to lose if the wound's premise holds?

For dignity wounds — dignity stakes:
- She'd be the woman who couldn't manage international travel alone.
- She'd be the one who proved her daughter-in-law right.

For loss wounds — love stakes:
- She'd be the woman who watched her dog fade and couldn't do anything about it.
- She'd accept that the best she can do is make him comfortable.
- The thing that made him him would just be gone.

The stakes must be real but proportionate to what the product can resolve. Not survival stakes. Not terminal illness. The reader needs to imagine herself in this situation and understand exactly why it matters.

**E. Proportion Check** — Is the story's resolution proportionate to the wound? If the wound is too small, the resolution seems disproportionate. If the wound is too large, the product can't carry the weight. The product must be able to honestly resolve what was lost — and the story should show partial restoration, not miracle cure.

### Checkpoint 2 Scoring (/40 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Specificity of the wound (not generic, could only happen in this story) | /10 |
| Delivery mechanism (dignity: wrapped in warmth; loss: gradual accumulation with crystallized moment) | /10 |
| Recognition factor (reader maps it to her own experience) | /10 |
| Proportionality (the story's resolution matches the size of the wound) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 2 Total: /40 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 4 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

If the wound is too small: Find the version that the protagonist cannot shrug off. For dignity wounds, escalation works — the packing guide only works because Claire kept going. For loss wounds, accumulation works — stack the small absences until the reader feels the weight.

If the wound is too generic: Find the specific mechanism. For dignity wounds: what exact form did the dismissal take? For loss wounds: what exact behavior stopped? Not "he slowed down" but the specific thing he used to do that defined him.

If the wound is too dramatic: Scale it back. No product can resolve abuse or terminal illness. For dignity wounds, find the domestic version. For loss wounds, find the version where partial restoration is honest — the dog doing something it stopped doing, not a miracle cure.

If the crystallized moment is missing (loss wounds only): The accumulation needs a single scene that makes the reader wince. Not more accumulation — one moment that concentrates everything. The birthday scene where the shoes are everywhere and the dog doesn't move. Find that moment.

## Checkpoint 3: The Contrast Engine

### Is the Story Doing Its Own Selling — Or Is Something Else Doing It?

In traditional direct response copy, the writer does the persuasion: they make claims, present proof, overcome objections. In story-native, something inside the story does all of that work without the writer touching it. The story doesn't argue for the product — it shows contrast that makes the product's value undeniable.

The reader never feels argued at. They feel witnessed.

### The Awareness Routing

Before scoring this checkpoint, determine which path the story takes. This is the decision point that shapes the entire evaluation.

**Problem-Unaware → Antagonist Path:** The reader doesn't know they have a problem. Nobody scrolling Facebook thinks "I need better luggage." A human antagonist must create the desire — someone who embodies the wrong approach, dismisses the protagonist, and through her failure demonstrates what happens without the product. The antagonist IS the engine. Without her, there's nothing driving the story forward.

**Problem-Aware → Structural Contrast Path:** The reader already knows the problem. Every woman watching her senior dog slow down already carries the wound. She doesn't need a character to create it — she lives it. The problem itself is the engine. The story's job is to prove "I see exactly what you're going through" and build belief that something can be done. The contrast comes from failed attempts vs. the product, before vs. after, what was lost vs. what came back.

**How to determine the path:** Ask: "Does the reader arrive already feeling the problem?" If a woman scrolling past the hook already knows the pain being described — she's already living it — the story is problem-aware. If the reader doesn't know she has a problem until the story shows her one through an antagonist's behavior, the story is problem-unaware.

Both paths are held to the same standard: five persuasion functions must happen inside the story without the writer making direct claims. The vehicle is different. The bar is the same.

---

### Path A: Problem-Unaware — The Antagonist Engine

The antagonist does all the selling. The story doesn't argue for the product — it shows the antagonist arguing against the protagonist's capabilities, and then shows the protagonist proving her wrong through the product.

#### The Five Functions the Antagonist Must Perform

**Function 1: Create the Desire** — The antagonist must embody the wrong way to exist in the protagonist's world. Claire's Rimowa luggage becomes a symbol of try-hard fragility. Patricia's $545 luggage shipping service becomes a monument to overcomplicated, effortful, helpless travel. The antagonist's approach must make the reader not want to be her — which means the reader now wants to be the protagonist.

Test: After introducing the antagonist, does the reader immediately understand what the protagonist is trying not to be?

**Function 2: Provide the Proof** — Every time the antagonist struggles — overweight fees, missing luggage, blisters on cobblestones, a destroyed Louis Vuitton box, waiting at baggage claim while the protagonist is already checked in — she's providing a live demonstration of what happens without the product. The writer never has to make a product claim. The antagonist's suffering makes the claim.

Test: List every moment of antagonist difficulty in the story. Do they collectively demonstrate the product's value without the writer ever saying "our product is better"?

**Function 3: Create the Urgency** — At the end of every story, the antagonist asks where to get the product. She's told it's sold out. This isn't a marketing bolt-on — it's the emotional climax. The reader experiences the pleasure of imagining being the protagonist in that moment, which translates into urgency: "I don't want to be the one asking. I want to be the one who already has it."

Test: Is the scarcity element woven into the story as a narrative beat, not appended as a marketing claim?

**Function 4: Overcome the Price Objection** — When the antagonist spends more on worse alternatives — $545 on a luggage service, $255 in baggage fees, a destroyed luxury gift — the product's price becomes a relief, not a cost. The antagonist does the math for the reader. The writer never mentions ROI.

Test: Does the reader do the math themselves based on the antagonist's choices?

**Function 5: Witness the Resolution** — The social proof moment must happen in front of the antagonist. She doesn't just fail while the protagonist succeeds. She watches the protagonist succeed. She sees the validation arrive in real time.

Test: Is the antagonist present when the protagonist is publicly validated?

#### The Antagonist Calibration

**Too villainous:** If the antagonist is cruel, petty, or openly malicious, the reader will feel the story is rigged. The most effective antagonists are people doing something casually dismissive while believing they're being helpful. Claire offering to coordinate the trip because she'd done Barcelona three times.

**Too sympathetic:** If the antagonist has entirely understandable reasons, the reader won't feel the protagonist's victory. There needs to be enough self-righteousness that the reader can enjoy watching it crumble.

**The right calibration:** Someone who has formed a narrative about the protagonist — the widow who can't manage logistics, the retiree who's losing her sharpness — and acts on that narrative sincerely. She is not malicious. She is wrong. And the story is about the moment her wrongness becomes undeniable.

#### Path A Scoring (/60 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Function 1: Does the antagonist's approach create desire for the alternative? | /10 |
| Function 2: Does the antagonist's struggle provide product proof without claims? | /10 |
| Function 3: Does the antagonist's final ask land as a narrative beat, not marketing? | /10 |
| Function 4: Does the antagonist's spending make the product's price feel like relief? | /10 |
| Function 5: Is she present and watching when the protagonist is validated? | /10 |
| Calibration: Is she wrong but not villainous, helpful but patronizing? | /10 |

**Checkpoint 3 Total: /60 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 6 = /10**

#### Path A Repair Instructions

If the antagonist is doing too little: Audit every scene and ask "does this moment demonstrate what happens without the product?" Add one antagonist difficulty moment. It will sharpen the contrast without requiring a single product claim.

If the antagonist is too evil: Give her a sincere motivation. Claire sends the packing guide because she genuinely wants to help. She's not being cruel — she's being presumptuous. That sincerity is what makes the dismissal sting more than open hostility would.

If the scarcity beat is a marketing appendage: Weave it into the story. Make the scarcity something that happens to characters, not something the writer announces.

### Path B: Problem-Aware — The Structural Contrast Engine

When the reader already carries the wound, no human antagonist needs to create it. But the five persuasion functions are still non-negotiable. They happen through structural contrast instead of through a character.

#### The Five Functions Through Structural Contrast

**Function 1: Create Desire Through Failed Alternatives** — The narrator must try the obvious solutions first and fail. Not in summary — in scene. The reader needs to see the narrator at the pet store, or watching the dog not respond to the latest supplement, or throwing away a half-used bottle. These failures create desire for something that actually works. The failed alternatives embody the "wrong way" — the conventional wisdom that doesn't deliver.

Test: Can the reader list at least two specific things the narrator tried before the product? Are they shown failing in scene, not just mentioned?

**Function 2: Provide Proof Through Before/After Contrast** — The before and after must both be shown through specific, observable moments — not summarized. The reader needs to see the dog not getting up, then see the dog standing in one motion. The specific behavioral details are the proof. The writer never says "it worked" — the scenes say it.

Test: List every before moment and its corresponding after moment. Do they pair naturally? Is each pair shown in scene?

**Function 3: Create Urgency Through a Witness Wanting In** — Someone who sees the transformation wants the product for themselves or their own situation. This is the adapted version of "the antagonist asks where to get it." The witness's desire — plus scarcity confirmed through dialogue — creates urgency without a marketing announcement.

Test: Does a character ask about the product after witnessing results? Is scarcity mentioned through character dialogue, not authorial announcement?

**Function 4: Overcome Price Through Wasted Alternatives** — The narrator's spending on things that didn't work makes the product's price feel like relief. Specific dollar amounts are stronger than vague mentions. "I added it up once — over $400 on supplements that year" does more work than "I tried everything."

Test: Does the reader do the math themselves? Is the failed spending specific enough to make the product feel proportionate by contrast?

**Function 5: Witness the Resolution** — Someone who knew the "before" must see the "after." A vet who tracked the decline. A neighbor who saw the dog in January. A family member who remembers what was lost. The witness's reaction — surprise, emotion, disbelief — provides the reader with vicarious satisfaction. This is the emotional climax: someone who knows confirms that the change is real.

Test: Is the witness credible? Did they know the "before" well enough that their reaction to the "after" carries weight? Is their reaction shown in scene?

#### The Contrast Calibration

**Problem too small:** If the loss isn't significant enough, the reader doesn't care about the solution. "My dog seems a bit tired" doesn't carry a story.

**Problem too large:** If the loss is terminal or irreversible, the product can't honestly resolve it. The story will either overclaim or disappoint.

**Failed alternatives are strawmen:** If the narrator dismisses alternatives too easily, the reader doesn't trust the journey. The supplements should be real things that real people try. The narrator should want them to work.

**Resolution is a miracle:** If the product reverses everything completely, the story loses honesty. The best problem-aware stories show partial restoration — the dog carried the shoe three steps, not laps. He's better, not young. That honesty is what makes the reader believe.

**The right calibration:** The problem is real and specific. The failed alternatives are genuine attempts. The resolution is honest — visible improvement, not total reversal. The reader finishes believing "this is possible" rather than "this is too good to be true."

#### Path B Scoring (/60 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Function 1: Do failed alternatives create desire in scene, not summary? | /10 |
| Function 2: Is the before/after contrast shown through specific paired moments? | /10 |
| Function 3: Does a witness want in after seeing results, with scarcity through dialogue? | /10 |
| Function 4: Does wasted spending on alternatives make the product's price feel like relief? | /10 |
| Function 5: Does someone who knew the "before" witness and react to the "after"? | /10 |
| Calibration: Is the problem real but resolvable, with honest (not miracle) results? | /10 |

**Checkpoint 3 Total: /60 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 6 = /10**

#### Path B Repair Instructions

If failed alternatives are summarized, not shown: Find one specific moment of failure and write it as a scene. The narrator at the pet store. The morning she checked and nothing changed. The moment she almost gave up on the latest thing. One scene of visible failure does more than a paragraph of summary.

If the before/after isn't paired: Map each "before" behavior to its "after" return. The dog not getting up → the dog standing in one motion. Not walking to the door → nails clicking on the hardwood. Each pair should feel like a callback, not a coincidence.

If the witness is thin: Give the witness a relationship to the "before." The vet who's tracked the decline since puppyhood. The neighbor who talked about aging dogs in that resigned way. The witness's authority comes from knowing what was lost, not just seeing what's different.

If no specific dollar amounts: Add one. One number — what she spent on supplements that year, what one vet visit cost — makes the failed spending concrete and the product's price feel proportionate.

If the resolution is too perfect: Pull it back. Show the dog doing something it stopped doing — but not everything. Three steps, not laps. Better, not young. The honesty of partial restoration is what makes the reader believe.

## Checkpoint 4: The Trusted Friend Introduction

### Does the Product Enter Like a Recommendation, Not an Ad?

The Luhxe framework identified a structural device that the ads use in every single story: the product is never introduced by the brand. It's always introduced by a credible, competent third party — a trusted friend who already lives the life the protagonist wants.

This device bypasses reactance — the reader's instinct to distrust anything that feels like it's being sold to them. When a trusted friend drops a product recommendation into conversation, it doesn't trigger the ad-detection reflex. It feels like discovery.

### The Trusted Friend Profile

The friend must have three qualities:

**1. Established competence in her own right**
- Janet is a corporate attorney who travels four days a week.
- Monica is a flight attendant of 22 years.
- Elena runs a travel agency that books executives.
- Andrea is a corporate communications executive who travels constantly.
- Margaret has been solo traveling since her divorce five years ago.

The friend's credibility makes the product recommendation feel like insider knowledge, not a sales pitch. When a flight attendant of 22 years tells you about a bag, you listen differently than when a stranger in an ad tells you about the same bag.

**2. She introduces the product mid-conversation, not as a speech** — The product mention is embedded in dialogue, dropped the way you'd mention a restaurant: "Get a Luhxe." "Have you tried the Luhxe bags?" "I've been using mine for two years."

It is not introduced with a pitch. It is not introduced with feature lists. It is introduced with the confidence of someone who doesn't need to convince you because she already knows it works.

**3. She mentions scarcity naturally** — "They do limited drops." "Last batch sold out in five days." "Price goes up when they restock." These aren't marketing urgency tactics — they're what you'd say to a friend when passing along useful information. She mentions it the way she'd mention a restaurant that's hard to get a reservation at.

### The Introduction Timing Test

Where in the story does the product appear?

**Too early (before the wound has fully landed):** The reader hasn't felt the protagonist's problem deeply enough to want a solution. They'll experience the product mention as a setup, and the "trusted friend" will feel like a plant.

**Too late (after the reader has already wondered "so what did she do?"):** You've created desire but delayed satisfaction too long. The reader may have disengaged.

**The right timing:** After the protagonist has experienced the wound fully and reached the moment where she needs a mechanism. For problem-unaware stories, this is after she's decided to prove the antagonist wrong. For problem-aware stories, this is after she's exhausted her options and is at her most desperate. In both cases, the friend appears at the moment of maximum receptivity — when the protagonist (and reader) is most ready to hear about a solution.

### The Introduction Authenticity Test

Read the scene where the trusted friend introduces the product. Replace the brand name with a fictional restaurant. Does the introduction still read as natural conversation?

If yes: The introduction passes. The scene works as a human interaction that happens to contain a product recommendation.

If no: The scene is structured as an ad. The friend is performing a pitch, not having a conversation. Rewrite it so the recommendation is a secondary element in a primary scene (a phone call, a coffee catch-up, a text exchange), not the point of the scene.

### Checkpoint 4 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Friend's credibility (established competence in her own right) | /10 |
| Natural introduction (mid-conversation, not a pitch) | /10 |
| Scarcity as useful information (not marketing urgency) | /10 |
| Timing (appears at the moment of maximum receptivity) | /10 |
| The restaurant test (reads as conversation, not advertisement) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 4 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

If the friend feels like a plant: Give her a life outside the product mention. She calls with something else first. The product comes up sideways, in the middle of another topic. Friends don't call to recommend products — they call for other reasons, and products come up.

If the introduction is a pitch: Cut every sentence that explains a product feature. Features appear later, in the demonstration scene. The friend's job is only to name the product and plant the scarcity. Everything else comes from the protagonist's own experience.

## Checkpoint 5: The Invisible Demonstration

### Are Product Features Shown Through Lived Moments?

This is where almost every imitator of the Luhxe style fails. They understand that the product must appear in the story. They do not understand that the product must be demonstrated through scene, never listed as features.

The difference is the difference between telling and showing — but at a level of specificity that goes beyond that cliche.

### The Demonstration in Scene Principle

"The dresses and blazer went into the garment compartment flat and smooth. Everything else compressed into the body of the bag. Shoes in the side pocket. Zipped it. Four days of Barcelona in something the size of an overnight bag."

That is a complete product demonstration. You have learned:
- The garment compartment keeps clothes wrinkle-free
- The compression system reduces volume significantly
- Multiple compartments organize the contents
- The finished bag looks much smaller than its capacity

You learned all of that through a woman packing for a trip. You never read a single feature bullet. You experienced it.

Compare to: "Features: garment compartment keeps clothes wrinkle-free. Compression panel reduces volume. Multiple pockets for organization. Carry-on compliant."

Same information. No emotion. No belief. Evaluation instead of feeling. You don't want to own it — you're comparing it to other options.

### The Demonstration Scenes Audit

Map every product feature mentioned in the story. For each feature, identify:

**1. Is it shown in action?** The garment compartment is demonstrated when the protagonist unpacks a perfect dress. The compression system is demonstrated when she packs 10 days of clothes into something that looks like a gym bag. The carry-on compliance is demonstrated when the gate agent looks right past it.

If a feature is mentioned but not demonstrated: Either cut the feature mention or write a scene that shows it in action.

**2. Is it demonstrated at a moment of emotional significance?** The best demonstrations happen at moments that would be emotionally charged even without the product. The birthday dinner scene. The airport arrival. The antagonist watching. The product's demonstration is most powerful when it occurs in a moment that already carries weight.

**3. Is it a discovery or a claim?** The protagonist discovers the bag's capacity by actually packing it. She is surprised. The reader is surprised alongside her. This is the difference between the writer making a claim and the reader making a discovery.

The first-time packing scene is essential. It is the moment where the reader discovers what the product can do — through the protagonist's own experience and surprise. If this scene is missing, the reader has been told about the product. They have not experienced it.

### The Feature-to-Scene Conversion Test

List every product attribute mentioned in your story:
- Wrinkle-free garment compartment
- Compression system
- Under-seat fit
- Carry-on size but weekender appearance
- Specific capacity (days of clothes)
- Ease of carry

For each attribute, identify the scene in your story where it is demonstrated. If any attribute lacks a demonstration scene, flag it. Then decide: is this attribute worth including? If yes, write the scene. If no, cut the mention.

### The "Gate Agent Doesn't Look" Test

One of the most effective demonstrations in the Luhxe canon is the gate agent not looking at the bag. It's not the protagonist boasting about her bag's compliance — it's the absence of scrutiny. The gate agent looks past it because it doesn't register as a threat. That invisibility is the product's superpower, demonstrated through a character's non-reaction.

Does your story have a "gate agent doesn't look" moment — a moment where a character's non-reaction to the bag demonstrates its primary benefit?

### Checkpoint 5 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Every key feature is demonstrated in scene, not listed | /10 |
| The first-time packing scene (protagonist's discovery moment) | /10 |
| Demonstrations occur at emotionally significant moments | /10 |
| Features are discovered by the protagonist, not claimed by the writer | /10 |
| The "gate agent doesn't look" moment (non-reaction as demonstration) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 5 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

The most common failure here is compression: the writer knows they need to demonstrate features, so they write one packed packing scene and one arrival scene and consider it done. The Luhxe stories are more distributed than that. Each scene carries a different demonstration, and each demonstration is embedded in a scene that has emotional weight regardless of the product. Find your story's natural dramatic scenes and ask which product feature fits naturally into each one.

## Checkpoint 6: The Social Proof Interruption

### Does Validation Arrive From the Right Person at the Right Moment?

A credible third party — someone whose opinion carries weight — sees the product's results and reacts with visible surprise, desire, or recognition. This device provides proof without the writer making claims. It works differently depending on the story type, but the core mechanics are the same.

### The Five Functions of the Interruption

**1. Third-party credibility without claims** — The brand never says "as recommended by veterinarians" or "as featured in Conde Nast Traveler." Instead, a credible person appears in the story and validates the product through their reaction. Shown, not claimed.

**2. Exclusivity through witnessed scarcity** — When someone with authority or status can't easily get the product, it becomes aspirational rather than pushy. "I've been on the waitlist since January" from a professional. "They only make them in small runs" from someone who tried to order.

**3. The validation lands in front of someone who matters** — For problem-unaware stories: the antagonist watches. The protagonist gets publicly validated in front of the person who underestimated her. For problem-aware stories: someone who knew the "before" witnesses the validation. The neighbor who saw the dog in January. The family member who remembers what was lost. The emotional climax requires an audience that understands the weight of what changed.

**4. Mimetic desire** — The reader sees characters actively wanting the product. This makes it more desirable not because of what it does, but because of how others react to it.

**5. The insider feeling** — The reader, having read to this point, knows about something that professionals and others can't easily get. Clicking the link isn't buying a product — it's joining the group that got there first.

### The Validator Profile Test

Who is your validator? They must pass all three tests:

**Test A: Is their credibility legible?** For problem-unaware stories, this means instantly recognizable social currency: "A writer for Conde Nast Traveler." For problem-aware stories, this means established authority in the relevant domain: "Dr. Reeves has seen Hank since he was a puppy. She's the kind of vet who talks to the dog before she talks to you, and she doesn't say things to make you feel good." The validator's credibility must be established before their reaction lands.

**Test B: Is their validation plausible given who they are?** A travel writer wanting the bag: completely plausible. A vet who's tracked a dog's decline being stunned by its mobility: completely plausible. The validator's relationship to the domain makes their reaction credible.

**Test C: Does the scene confirm scarcity organically?** Through character dialogue or behavior, not marketing announcement. "I've been on the waitlist since January." "The price goes up with the next batch." "She had to wait almost two months."

### The Timing and Placement Test

The interruption must:

**Happen with the right audience present:** For problem-unaware stories: in a public setting with the antagonist present. For problem-aware stories: in front of someone who knew the "before" — a family member, neighbor, or friend who can register the weight of the change.

**Happen after the product has already demonstrated results:** The validation confirms what has already been shown, rather than replacing what hasn't been demonstrated. The interruption is a capstone, not a shortcut.

**Escalate if possible:** The strongest stories have more than one social proof moment. A vet's medical reaction plus a neighbor's witnessed surprise. A Conde Nast writer plus a stranger in the security line. Each additional voice amplifies without repeating.

### Checkpoint 6 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Validator's credibility is established before their reaction | /10 |
| Their validation is plausible given their relationship to the domain | /10 |
| Scarcity is confirmed through character, not marketing announcement | /10 |
| Timing: occurs after results are demonstrated, with the right audience present | /10 |
| Emotional function: protagonist is validated in front of someone who understands the weight | /10 |

**Checkpoint 6 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

If your validator feels planted: Give them a purpose in the scene beyond the product. The vet is there for a checkup. The neighbor came over for a visit. The validation is incidental to their presence, not the reason for it.

If nobody who matters is watching: For problem-unaware stories, restructure so the antagonist is present. For problem-aware stories, ensure someone who knew the "before" is in the room — someone whose reaction carries the weight of "this is not the same as what I saw before."

If validation happens in isolation: Move the scene. The protagonist being told privately that results are impressive is less powerful than being told in front of someone who has context. The audience is what transforms validation into emotional climax.

## Checkpoint 7: The Gap Architecture

### Are There Planted Gaps That Close at Exactly the Right Moment?

The analysis of the Reddit stories identified that the most powerful emotional impact comes from gaps — something the reader is holding open without knowing it, which closes at a moment of maximum leverage.

The Luhxe stories use a specific set of narrative gaps, planted deliberately and closed at precise structural moments.

### The Three Essential Gaps

**Gap 1: The Central Emotional Gap** — This is the gap the reader holds for the entire story. It takes different forms depending on the story type:

For problem-unaware stories (comeuppance gap): Planted the moment the antagonist's dismissiveness is established. The reader wants to know: will she be proved wrong? The gap closes when the antagonist asks where to get the product — and is told it's sold out. The protagonist doesn't gloat. She delivers the information calmly. The coolness of the delivery is what makes it land.

For problem-aware stories (restoration gap): Planted when the reader learns what was lost. The reader wants to know: will it come back? The gap closes when the lost behavior returns. "My dog hasn't stolen a shoe in two years" opens the gap in the first line. The gap closes when Hank picks up the sneaker. The reader has been holding this without knowing it, and the payoff hits physically.

Test: Is the central gap planted early and closed late? Does the closing land with emotional weight — through understated composure (problem-unaware) or through honest, partial restoration (problem-aware)?

**Gap 2: The Scarcity Close** — Scarcity must be planted as something the reader holds, then closed as a narrative beat.

For problem-unaware stories: "By the time she asked, that batch was already gone." The antagonist's too-late request closes the gap as justice.

For problem-aware stories: Scarcity is planted through dialogue — limited runs, price increases, wait times — and confirmed when someone else wants in after witnessing results. The gap is thinner here because there's no antagonist being denied, but it still needs to be planted and resolved through character, not marketing.

Test: Is scarcity confirmed by multiple sources throughout the story before the gap closes? Is it delivered as character dialogue, not authorial announcement?

**Gap 3: The Protagonist's Private Knowledge** — The story works best when someone is holding information the reader tracks.

For problem-unaware stories: The protagonist has information the antagonist doesn't. "She didn't know I was coming. I did." The reader holds the asymmetry, waiting for the reveal.

For problem-aware stories: The protagonist holds something privately — not telling anyone about the improvement, waiting to be sure. "I didn't say anything to June. I didn't want to jinx it." The reader holds the narrator's secret, and when it finally comes out, the release is earned.

Test: Is there a moment where someone is holding information that the reader tracks? Is the eventual reveal satisfying because the reader was in on it?

### Gap Timing

Gaps must be planted before the reader is aware of them. If a gap is planted too obviously ("I knew I would prove her wrong"), it reads as a setup. The best gaps are planted inside emotional moments — the protagonist is focused on something else, and the gap appears in the background without being announced.

The packing scene plants a gap: will the clothes be wrinkled when she arrives? The answer comes when the protagonist unpacks a perfect dress at the hotel. The gap was never announced. It was only recognizable in retrospect.

### Checkpoint 7 Scoring (/40 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Central emotional gap: planted early, closed late, landing with earned weight | /10 |
| The scarcity gap: confirmed throughout, closed as character dialogue | /10 |
| Private knowledge: information asymmetry the reader holds and tracks | /10 |
| Background gaps: small planted moments that close naturally as callbacks | /10 |

**Checkpoint 7 Total: /40 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 4 = /10**

### Repair Instructions

If the central gap closes too loudly: Scale it back. For problem-unaware stories, the protagonist should seem almost indifferent — "They're sold out." Let the antagonist's reaction carry the emotion. For problem-aware stories, the restoration should be honest and partial — three steps, not laps. Let the reader feel the joy without the story insisting on it.

If the scarcity gap never really opens: Plant it earlier and test it harder. Someone should have to wait, or almost miss out, or hear it's hard to get — so that when someone else wants one later, the reader already knows it won't be easy.

If the background gaps are missing: Look for moments the story plants without announcing. The packing scene that pays off with a perfect dress. The behavior that stopped that eventually returns. These gaps are recognizable only in retrospect — and they're what make the second read different from the first.

## Checkpoint 8: The Emotional Resolution

### Does the Story Earn Its Closing Line?

Every story ends with a line that names what happened — what the product meant, what changed, what the protagonist now believes. This line must be honest. It must not overclaim. And it must be earned by everything that came before it.

### Earning the Line

The closing line only works if the story has done three things:

**1. The protagonist must have doubted or despaired at some point.** Not in an obvious, performed way. But there must be a moment — even brief — where the wound landed. Where the protagonist paused. Where she almost gave up. Where she sat on the couch and cried.

For problem-unaware stories: the moment the wound landed and she almost didn't go, almost didn't fight back, almost accepted the narrative someone else built about her. For problem-aware stories: the moment she accepted the loss as permanent, almost stopped trying, almost put the product in the closet.

If the protagonist is entirely confident throughout, the closing feels hollow. The doubt is what earns the resolution.

**2. The product's demonstration must be complete.** By the time the closing line arrives, the reader has experienced everything the product can do. The line only works because the reader has just watched it happen — the dog standing at the door, the dress hanging wrinkle-free, the vet looking at the chart twice. The writer doesn't need to explain. The scenes already did.

**3. Someone must have witnessed enough that their recognition feels earned.** For problem-unaware stories: the antagonist has seen enough — the airport arrival, the validation, the perfect demonstration — that her asking "where did you get it?" feels inevitable. For problem-aware stories: a witness who knew the "before" has seen enough of the "after" that their reaction — surprise, desire, recognition — feels earned, not mechanical.

### The Closing Line Variants

The line should be adapted to the protagonist's specific situation. Not copied verbatim.

For problem-unaware stories, the structure is: [The thing didn't create the quality]. [But it proved the quality was always there.]
- "The bag didn't make me capable. But it proved I never needed anyone to carry my bags."
- "The bag didn't restore my sharpness. But it proved I never lost it."

For problem-aware stories, the structure is: [The thing didn't reverse the problem]. [But it proved the problem isn't the whole story.]
- "The TheraBelt didn't make Hank young. But it proved he wasn't done."
- "I spent a year thinking getting old was just something you watched happen. I don't think that anymore."

Both variants share the same principle: honesty. The product didn't perform a miracle. It created circumstances where something real could happen. The closing line names that honestly, at the exact scale the story demonstrated.

### Checkpoint 8 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Protagonist has a genuine moment of doubt or despair before the resolution | /10 |
| Product demonstration is complete before the closing line arrives | /10 |
| A witness has seen enough that their recognition/desire feels earned | /10 |
| The closing line is adapted to this specific story | /10 |
| The line is honest (doesn't overclaim what the product does) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 8 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

## Checkpoint 9: Closing Restraint

### Does the Story Stop Before It Should?

The close must earn the click — but how it earns the click depends on the story type. Problem-unaware and problem-aware stories end differently because the reader is in a different place by the final line.

### The Awareness Routing

Before scoring this checkpoint, determine which path the story takes. This mirrors the routing in Checkpoint 3.

**Problem-Unaware → Path A (The "Your Move" Close):** The reader has watched an antagonist fail and a protagonist succeed. By the end, the reader is slightly outside the story — they've experienced the comeuppance, the validation has landed, and they're ready for a direct handoff. The explicit CTA structure works because the spell is already transitioning into decision.

**Problem-Aware → Path B (The In-Story Close):** The reader has been inside the narrator's head for 3,000 words. They've lived the wound, the doubt, the gradual restoration. They are still inside the story at the last line. A bolted-on CTA section — contrast recap, direct address, "Your move" — breaks the spell. The close must stay inside the narrative and let the story do the selling.

---

### Path A: Problem-Unaware — The "Your Move" Close

The Luhxe framework's most counterintuitive principle is also its most powerful: the CTA is two words. "Your move." After 3,000+ words of story, the writer stops trusting herself to say more. That restraint is a power move. It says: "I've shown you everything. I don't need to persuade you. You already know what to do."

#### The Restraint Audit

**After the closing line, count the words.** The ideal post-resolution section is under 150 words. It should:

1. Briefly recap the contrast — what was vs. what is now (one sentence each, the antagonist's situation vs. the protagonist's)
2. State the scarcity update (one to two sentences, in character)
3. Address the reader directly with the condition that matches her situation ("If someone has built a story about you that you never agreed to...")
4. Close with "Your move." or equivalent

Everything else is rambling. Every additional explanation after the closing line weakens it. The reader has lived the story. They don't need a summary. They don't need to be told how to feel. They need to be handed the moment.

**The Post-CTA Test** — After "Your move," read the next paragraph (if there is one). Does it add anything the story hasn't already established? Almost certainly not. Cut it.

The P.S. (if used) should be three words to two sentences maximum. A link and one practical note. Not another pitch.

#### The Silence Test

After "Your move," the reader should sit in silence for a moment before clicking.

If the story explains itself after the closing line — if it tells the reader what they should be feeling, why this matters, what will happen if they don't act — the silence is broken before it forms. The reader is pulled out of the emotional space they were in.

Write "Your move." and stop. Trust the 3,000 words you just wrote to do the work.

#### Path A Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Post-closing section is under 150 words | /10 |
| No explanation of the closing line (it stands on its own) | /10 |
| Direct address to the reader matches a specific condition ("if someone...") | /10 |
| CTA is 2-5 words, no more | /10 |
| The story does not summarize itself after the closing line | /10 |

**Path A Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

#### Path A Repair Instructions

The most common failure here is the writer's anxiety. They've just written 3,000 words and they're not sure it all landed, so they explain. The explanation is the tell. It proves the writer doesn't trust the story. If you've done Checkpoints 1-8 well, you've earned the right to stop. Stop.

---

### Path B: Problem-Aware — The In-Story Close

The reader is still inside the narrator's head. The close must keep them there. No contrast recap. No direct address. No "Your move." The story ends on a line that is still the narrator talking, still inside the world — and the reader leaves carrying the emotion, already knowing what to do.

This works because the story has already done the CTA's job. The product has been named. Scarcity has been established through dialogue. Multiple characters have wanted or used the product. The emotional weight of the transformation is the selling. The reader doesn't need to be handed the moment — they're already living in it.

#### The In-Story Close Audit

**1. Does the close stay inside the story?** The last line must still be the narrator's voice, in the narrator's world. Not addressing the reader. Not stepping outside. Not summarizing what just happened. The reader should finish the story and still be in the living room, the kitchen, the parking lot — wherever the narrator is.

**2. Is the product named and searchable?** By the time the close arrives, the product name must have appeared clearly enough that a reader who wants to find it can. The close doesn't need to name it again — but the story must have named it.

**3. Is scarcity established before the close?** Limited runs, price increases, wait times — all confirmed through character dialogue earlier in the story. The close doesn't carry the scarcity. The story already did.

**4. Does the emotional weight do the selling?** The reader finishes moved. They've watched the wound, the doubt, the restoration. They want this for themselves or their own dog or their own situation. The desire to act comes from what they just lived through, not from being told to act.

**5. Does the close end on forward motion?** The best in-story closes don't land on resolution — they land on continuation. The dog is still getting better. The narrator is still watching. Something is still happening. This creates an open loop the reader carries with them — and the only way to close it is to act.

#### Path B Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| The close stays inside the story (narrator's voice, narrator's world) | /10 |
| Product is named and searchable before the close | /10 |
| Scarcity is established through dialogue before the close | /10 |
| Emotional weight does the selling without explicit handoff | /10 |
| The close ends on forward motion, not summary | /10 |

**Path B Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

#### Path B Repair Instructions

If the close steps outside the story: Cut everything after the last line that is still the narrator talking inside her world. "Your move" and direct address belong to Path A. If the story needs a "Your move" to work, the story hasn't done its job — go back to Checkpoints 5-8 and strengthen the demonstration and resolution.

If the product isn't named clearly enough: The product name must appear at least once in clear dialogue or narration before the close. The reader shouldn't have to hunt. But the close itself doesn't name it — the story already did.

If scarcity is missing: Weave it into the story's dialogue earlier. A character mentions limited runs, a wait time, a price increase. This must happen before the close, not at it. The close carries emotion, not logistics.

If the close summarizes: Cut the summary. The reader lived the story. They don't need a recap. End on a single image, a single moment, a single line that is still inside the narrator's experience.

## Checkpoint 10: The Specificity Audit

### The Pamplemousse Pass

This is the final checkpoint and the one most stories fail in subtle ways. Go through the story and mark every specific detail. Then ask of each one: could a copywriter have invented this?

### The Pamplemousse Test

A Pamplemousse moment is a specific detail that:
1. Serves no persuasive function
2. Exists because it happened (or feels like it could only have happened)
3. Would not appear in a marketing brief

Examples from the Luhxe ads:
- "I ran five miles this morning." (The protagonist who was told to sit down and not push herself ran five miles that morning.)
- "The birthday card she made at five — crayon on pink construction paper, curled at the corners." (The first edition book story.)
- "Her voice. The 'let me explain this to a child' voice." (The Barcelona story.)
- "She said it was $95 to check it. Half an inch." (The airline attendant story.)

These details are unnecessary. That's what makes them necessary. They are the proof that a human wrote this.

### The Audit Method

**Step 1:** Highlight every specific detail in the story.

**Step 2:** For each highlighted detail, ask: "Would a copywriter writing from a marketing brief think to include this?"
- If yes: It's a detail that serves a persuasive function. It's fine, but it's not Pamplemousse.
- If no: It's a Pamplemousse moment. Mark it with a star.

**Step 3:** Count your stars. A story needs at minimum three Pamplemousse moments. If you have fewer than three, your story may be technically functional but emotionally hollow.

**Step 4:** If you're below three stars, find them. They come from:
- A secondary character's specific behavior (the antagonist's exact gesture, the daughter's dead-serious warning, the vet who talks to the dog first)
- The protagonist's private life (what she was thinking or doing before the story's events)
- The product's unexpected moment (a detail about using the product that only someone who had done it would know)
- A secondary character's reaction that is more specific than necessary

### The Marketing DNA Scanner

Read the story and mark any phrase that has marketing DNA:
- "Life-changing"
- "I couldn't believe how well it worked"
- "Finally, a solution that actually..."
- "Game-changer"
- "I was blown away"
- "Exactly what I needed"
- "The results speak for themselves"
- "Worth every penny" (allowed only if said by the antagonist as a reluctant admission)
- Any description of the product that uses adjectives the product's own website would use

Every marked phrase must be rewritten in the voice of a person actually living this experience. Not a person performing their experience for an audience — a person telling a friend what happened.

### Checkpoint 10 Scoring (/50 → /10)

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| At least 3 Pamplemousse moments (details no copywriter would invent) | /10 |
| No marketing DNA phrases in the protagonist's voice | /10 |
| Product descriptions use words the protagonist would use, not the brand | /10 |
| Secondary character details are specific, not functional | /10 |
| The story reads differently on second read (new details land) | /10 |

**Checkpoint 10 Total: /50 — Convert to /10 scale: Divide by 5 = /10**

## Priority Repair Order

When the story needs revision, work in this order:

**1. Voice first (Checkpoint 0 and 10)** — If the story sounds like a marketer wrote it, no structural fix will save it. The voice must be right before anything else matters. Go back to the protagonist's reality and find one more Pamplemousse moment. Let that moment pull the language back toward humanity.

**2. The wound (Checkpoint 2)** — If the emotional wound isn't calibrated correctly — too big, too small, too generic — the entire story is misaligned. The wound is the story's emotional center of gravity. Everything orbits it.

**3. The contrast engine (Checkpoint 3)** — The engine must do the selling, not the writer. For problem-unaware stories: if the antagonist isn't performing all five functions, the story is carrying its own persuasion load — audit her and add one more function she's not yet performing. For problem-aware stories: if the structural contrast isn't doing the work — failed alternatives are summarized, before/after isn't paired, witnesses are thin — the writer is making claims instead of showing contrast. Add one more specific scene of failure or one more paired before/after moment.

**4. The social proof interruption (Checkpoint 6)** — If the validation scene doesn't work, the story's emotional climax falls flat. The validator's credentials, the timing, and the right audience (antagonist or "before" witness) are all non-negotiable.

**5. The close (Checkpoints 8 and 9)** — Fix these last. A story with a great close and a weak middle is still a weak story. But a story with a strong middle and a weak close wastes everything that came before.

---

## The Reddit Test — Final Check

Before publication, post the story (stripped of brand name and CTA) to a private document and read it as if you encountered it anonymously on social media.

Ask three questions:

1. Would you share this with someone?
2. Would you feel embarrassed if it turned out to be an ad?
3. Does it feel like it was written by someone who lived this, or someone who was paid to write it?

If the answers are yes, no, and lived it — publish.

If any answer goes the other way, return to the lowest-scoring checkpoint and repair it first.

This framework is built from analysis of the Luhxe ad playbook and the emotional architecture of organic Reddit storytelling. The checkpoints describe what the best stories in this format already do intuitively — and give you a repeatable method for doing it deliberately.

The goal is always the same: a story so native it makes the reader forget, for three thousand words, that they are being sold to. When they remember — when they reach the link — they click because they want to be part of the world the story just showed them.

That world is made of specificity, dignity, and restraint.

Your move.
