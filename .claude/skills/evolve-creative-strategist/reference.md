# Evolve Creative Strategist — Reference Material

Read this file during execution when building and evaluating concepts.

## Concept Criteria (Step 8)

**Five things must be true simultaneously for the concept to work:**

1. **The reader lands in a situation already in motion** — something changed, something was discovered, and the reader needs to see what happens next. A narrator who just found out something about her dog's food. A woman whose vet just dismissed her concern. A neighbor who said something she can't stop thinking about.

2. **The concept enables a credible character to enter naturally** — the situation must create a plausible path for someone with authority to appear. Not forced. Not convenient. The kind of crossing paths that happens in real life. A friend's husband who used to formulate pet food. A coworker who left a vet hospital chain. A neighbor who does nutritional research. The concept must make this encounter feel inevitable, not engineered.

3. **The concept supports the villain reveal landing as fact, not conspiracy** — the narrator must trust the system FIRST so the betrayal lands. If she already distrusts the industry, the reveal has no weight. The concept must show her as someone who did what she was told — fed the recommended food, went to the vet, bought the supplements — so that learning the truth feels like a personal betrayal, not a political argument.

4. **The concept enables the catch-22 to land as a revelation** — the narrator must be doing things for her dog that the catch-22 condemns. Better food. Supplements. Vet visits. The more she invested, the harder the catch-22 hits. "I did everything right and none of it could reach the actual problem." If the narrator isn't actively caring for her dog through the system, there's no trap to spring.

5. **The narrator is a proxy/learner discovering alongside the reader** — she doesn't know the answer. She's at the START of discovery. The concept places her in the moment of "wait, what?" not in the moment of "let me tell you what I learned." If the narrator is ahead of the reader, the reader is passive audience.

**The concept must contain:**
- **Who the character is** — not "a dog mom" but a specific version: her situation, what makes her this person, what she's been doing for her dog
- **The specific situation** — what is happening or has happened. Something tipped. The reader needs to see what comes next.
- **The emotional stakes** — why does the reader care? For evolve-native, the stakes are: the thing she trusted is the thing hurting her dog. That's personal.
- **Where the credible character fits** — not a full character description, but the type and how they enter the story naturally

**The concept must NOT be:**
- A mood ("I'm worried about what's in my dog's food")
- A theme ("the corruption of the pet food industry")
- A product angle ("woman discovers red light therapy bypasses the catch-22")
- A mechanism delivery setup (if the concept only works because someone eventually explains AGEs and RAGE, it's a lecture, not a story)
- A generic scenario that could apply to anyone
- A character biography or static portrait
- A single moment without forward motion
- A setup for a claim delivery — the concept works through what the reader DISCOVERS about the system, not what she's TOLD about the product
- A conspiracy theory setup — the concept should feel like a personal discovery, not an exposé
- A plot sequence with dialogue. The concept describes the situation and stakes — not what characters say to each other.
- **A compelling story in the wrong territory.** The story must be about the systemic threat to dogs and the narrator's discovery of it. A great story about a dog's aging that has nothing to do with the system, the food, or the catch-22 belongs in `/story-creative-strategist`, not here.

**The scroll test:** Summarize in one sentence: "[specific person] [discovers/faces/learns something specific about the system] and now [consequence or question]." If you can only describe it as "[person] has [feeling] about [thing]," you have a mood, not a concept.

Example of a mood: "A woman who feeds her dog premium kibble starts wondering if it's really good enough."
Why it fails: Nothing happened. No discovery. No trigger. No forward motion.

Example of a situation: "A woman who has spent three years buying the most expensive dog food at her vet's office runs into an ex-formulation chemist at a dog park who tells her what's actually in the bag — and that the vet clinic is owned by the same company that makes the food."
Why it works: There's a discovery. The trust is shattered. The reader needs to know what happens next. The credible character is natural. The catch-22 is loaded (she was buying the "best" food through the system that profits from both sides).

## Gate 2 — Concept Quality Questions (Step 9)

Ask yourself:

0. **Does this concept activate the mass desire?** "I want my dog to be healthy and live longer." If the concept doesn't threaten that desire through the systemic villain, it's disconnected. A concept that makes the reader feel uneasy but doesn't make her think about HER dog's health will get engagement but zero sales.

1. Describe what HAPPENS using an active verb that isn't "is," "feels," or "watches." If the strongest verb is "notices" or "realizes," you have an observation, not a story.

2. What is the reader waiting to find out? State it in one sentence. If you can't, there's no forward motion.

3. Could I picture a real person this happened to? Is there enough specificity?

4. Would this concept generate comments on Facebook? Would people tag friends? Would they argue about it?

5. Would the target avatar see herself in this? Not just feel for the character, but recognize her own situation — her own vet, her own dog food, her own routine?

6. **Where does the credible character enter?** Point to the specific moment in the situation where someone with authority crosses paths with the narrator. If you can't point to it, the concept doesn't have credible character space. The entry must feel natural — a conversation already happening, a chance encounter, a connection through someone the narrator already knows.

7. **Where does the villain reveal land?** Point to the moment where the narrator (and reader) learns the systemic fact she can't unhear. If the concept doesn't have a natural place for this, it's in the wrong territory.

8. **Where does the catch-22 close the trap?** Point to the moment where the narrator realizes everything she's been doing runs into the same wall. If the narrator isn't doing things for her dog that the catch-22 condemns, there's no trap. Her effort is what makes it devastating.

9. Does the concept have ONE emotional throughline? If it has multiple storylines that each need resolution, the story will collapse.

10. **Mirror or movie?** Will the reader discover alongside the narrator, or watch the narrator discover from a distance? The narrator must be at the same place as the reader — ignorant, trusting the system, about to learn something she can't unlearn. If the narrator is ahead, it's a movie.

11. **Where is the emotional peak?** The most powerful moment must be the catch-22 revelation — the moment the narrator (and reader) realizes the trap. NOT a relationship moment, NOT the villain reveal alone (that's anger, not despair), NOT the product discovery. The peak is: "I did everything right and none of it mattered because the system is broken at the cellular level." If the emotional peak is elsewhere, the concept won't convert.

All twelve must pass.

## Presentation Templates (Step 11)

**If both gates passed:**

```
## Concept

[One paragraph: who the character is, the specific situation, the emotional stakes, how the credible character fits. 3-6 sentences. This is what gets fed into /evolve-native.]

## Suggested Credible Character

[Type of credible character that fits this concept naturally. One sentence on who they are and why their authority is earned. One sentence on how they enter the story.]

## Catch-22 Setup

[One sentence on what the narrator is doing for her dog that the catch-22 condemns. One sentence on how the trap closes.]

## Usable Details

[Specific real moments from the research that could live inside this story. Organized by which part of the concept they map to. Each one includes the detail itself and a brief note on what makes it real. 5-8 details.]

## Reasoning

[Why you believe this concept works. What you found in research — which posts, which comments, which patterns. What makes the situation compelling, why it connects to this avatar, what makes it a story someone needs to read.]
```

The reasoning must reference specific things found in research. Not generic statements.

**If either gate failed after retry:**

```
## What I Tried

[Honest explanation of the research angles attempted, what subreddits and queries were used, what was found, and why nothing landed as a strong concept. Include anything from the research that might be useful as a starting point for the user to redirect.]
```

## Guardrails

- Never fabricate Reddit quotes or stories. Every research finding must come from actual `/reddit-researcher` results.
- Never make medical claims. Concepts should not position TheraBelt as treating or curing disease.
- Never search for TheraBelt, Lucavel, or red light therapy on Reddit.
- One concept per run. No ranked lists.
- Do not present the concept as a story outline, a hook, or a draft. No dialogue, no scene beats. The concept is the situation — evolve-native handles everything else.
- Do not summarize the avatar back to the user.
- If `/reddit-researcher` returns errors or empty results, tell the user rather than inventing findings.
- Quality gates are internal. Do not narrate unless both retries fail.
- **Maximum 2 subreddits per run, 3-6 deep-reads total.**
- **Problem architecture with villain.** The concept must enable fear of the system, not just love of the dog. The territory is systemic threat and discovery. The narrator's trust in the system is what makes the betrayal land.
- **Stay in your lane.** Concept builder, not copywriter. No "scroll-stopper" predictions, no hook suggestions, no dialogue. Build the situation. Usable Details are raw material for the writer.
- **Do not invent emotional territory that isn't in the research.** Synthesizing is expected. Inventing emotions that contradict what you found is not.
- **Reality-check every physical detail against the avatar.**
- If the sketch doesn't sharpen after 2-5 messages, stop and redirect.
- Never build a concept from the avatar file alone.
- No portraits, routines, or "day in the life" structures.
- **No conspiracy energy.** The concept must enable discovery, not exposé. The narrator is learning, not crusading. If the concept sounds like it belongs in a documentary about corporate evil, it's wrong. It should sound like a woman telling her friend what she just found out.
- **Never frame the concept around a mechanism explanation.** If the concept's forward motion depends on someone explaining AGEs, RAGE, or cytochrome c oxidase, it's a lecture setup, not a story concept. The concept must work through personal discovery and emotional stakes, not through scientific education.
- **The concept must enable the catch-22.** If the narrator isn't actively doing things for her dog (good food, supplements, vet visits), there's no effort for the catch-22 to condemn. Her investment is the trap.
- **Wrong territory is a concept failure.** A concept can be interesting, emotionally real, and well-researched — and still be wrong for evolve-native because it has nothing to do with the systemic threat, the food industry, or the catch-22. Those concepts may belong in `/story-creative-strategist`. The story must be about the right thing from the start.
