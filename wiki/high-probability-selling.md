## Level 1: Core Thesis

Selling causes resistance. The fix isn't better [[persuasion]] — it's to stop persuading entirely. Instead: identify prospects who already want what you sell, build mutual trust before discussing product, and trade commitments instead of closing. When you replace "get them to buy" with "determine if there's a mutually acceptable basis for doing business," resistance disappears because there's nothing to resist.

## Level 2: Principles

### 1. Identification Over Persuasion

Don't try to convert Low Probability Prospects into buyers. Identify the ones who already need, want, and can afford what you sell — and spend all your time there.

**Why:** Aggressive salespeople create defensive prospects. Persistence breeds annoyance. The attempt to persuade creates the very resistance you're trying to overcome. It's called SALES resistance because SELLING causes it.

**Application to copy:** Write for people who already have the problem. Don't try to convince someone they have a problem they don't recognize. The reader who nods in the first two lines is your High Probability Prospect. Everyone else should self-select out.

### 2. Dis-Qualification Is the Engine

Continuously give prospects every opportunity to disqualify themselves. The faster you filter out non-buyers, the more time you have with real ones.

**Why:** The opportunity cost of pursuing Low Probability Prospects is enormous — not just time and resources, but the emotional drain. Every hour spent trying to convert a no is an hour not spent with someone ready to say yes.

**Application to copy:** A well-targeted ad should repel people who aren't in the avatar. This is a feature, not a bug. Specificity in the [[vicious-hooks]] and lead acts as a dis-qualification filter — the right reader stays, everyone else scrolls past.

### 3. Trust and Respect Before Product

Before ANY product discussion, determine if there's mutual trust and respect. This creates competitive advantage no competitor can match.

**Why:** Most people can't evaluate competing products on merit. They buy from the person (or brand, or story) they trust. If you establish trust, you have "power" — a position competitors can't displace by offering better features or lower prices. If you DON'T have trust, you're just another vendor.

**Application to copy:** The [[story-native-format]] already does this — the story builds trust before the product appears. This principle says: that's not a nice technique, it's the FOUNDATION. Product introduced before trust = resistance. Product introduced after trust = welcome.

### 4. The Entire Process Is the Close

Never ask for the order. Instead, negotiate mutual commitments at every stage. Each commitment is a micro-close. By the end, the sale is already done.

**Why:** When you wait until the end to "close," you've invested maximum effort for an uncertain result. When every step involves a commitment, the prospect takes responsibility for each decision. They can't later say "you talked me into it." They feel ownership, not pressure.

**Application to copy:** This maps to belief chains. Each paragraph should earn a micro-commitment — a nod, a "that's me," a "I've tried that too." By the time the product appears, the reader has already agreed with the problem, the failed solutions, and the kind of thing that would actually work. The CTA isn't a "close" — it's the natural next step.

### 5. Resistance Is a Signal, Not an Obstacle

If you encounter resistance, YOU did something wrong. You slipped into persuading. The correct response is to stop, not push harder.

**Why:** Resistance doesn't come from the prospect being difficult — it comes from the prospect feeling manipulated. When you remove the manipulation, resistance disappears. If it shows up, something in your approach triggered the prospect's defense reflex.

**Application to copy:** When a reader feels "sold to," they bounce. The signal isn't that you need a stronger CTA — it's that something upstream broke trust. Check for: too-obvious product placement, benefit stacking that feels like an ad, language that sounds like marketing instead of conversation.

## Level 3: Procedural Frameworks

### Framework 1: The 45-Word Offer (Prospecting)

How to make initial contact without creating resistance.

1. **Language your offer in ≤45 words** including salutation and introduction
2. **Describe what you sell clearly** — concrete features that paint a word picture (not benefits, not hype)
3. **End with a choice:** "Is this something you want?" or "Is this something you want... or not?"
4. **Deliver in neutral, unemotional tone** — no enthusiasm, no convincing, no pitch energy
5. **If yes →** "Do you want to meet to see if what we have is what you want?"
6. **If hesitation →** "It's okay if you don't want to. Just say so." (Giving permission to say no paradoxically increases yes responses)
7. **If yes to meeting →** "If our [product] meets your criteria, what will you do?" (Commitment before investing resources)
8. **If no commitment →** Dis-qualify. Move on.

**Signals you've slipped into selling:** Feeling stress. Being on the call >3-4 minutes. Talking more than the prospect.

**Copy application:** The hook IS the 45-word offer. It should clearly describe what the reader is about to experience (not sell it), then let them decide if they want to keep reading. A hook that tries to trick someone into reading = selling the appointment. A hook that tells you exactly what you're getting = High Probability identification.

### Framework 2: Establishing a Relationship (Trust Before Product)

How to build trust with someone you've just met.

1. **Make an observation** about the person, their environment, or situation. Attach a question.
2. **Ask open-ended follow-ups** based on THEIR answers: "Why?" / "What happened?" / "How did you handle that?"
3. **Follow the thread** — never jump to unrelated topics. When someone reveals something personal, the ONLY valid response is deeper interest in that thing.
4. **Listen without planning your next question.** Thinking comes after listening, not during.
5. **Continue until answers become sincerely personal** — that's when you're "inside." You now have a real sense of who this person is.
6. **Decide: do you trust and respect this person?**
7. **If no →** End the visit politely but firmly. Don't take a shot at selling — it wastes everyone's time.
8. **If yes →** Transition to Discovery/Dis-Qualification.

**Key principle:** Sincerity cannot be faked. People sense the difference between genuine interest and manipulative rapport-building. "Being interested" is the method — not a technique layered on top. If you're not actually interested, the prospect will sense it and disqualify YOU.

**Copy application:** The lead of a [[story-native-format]] ad IS the relationship phase. It's where the reader decides: "Is this person like me? Do I trust this narrator?" If you rush to product, you skip the trust-building. If the lead is generic, you never get "inside." Specific, authentic details build trust — the same way personal answers build trust in a conversation.

### Framework 3: The Commitment Chain

How to close without ever asking for the order.

1. **At every stage, ask for a specific commitment** before proceeding to the next stage
2. **Frame commitments as choices:** "Is that what you want to do?" / "Are you sure?" / "Is there anything else we should discuss?"
3. **If prospect won't commit →** End the meeting. Move on. Don't try to overcome the non-commitment.
4. **Have the prospect restate commitments:** "You said X. Is that still what you want?"
5. **Give prospect every opportunity to back out** — this paradoxically strengthens their commitment (they can't later say they were pressured)
6. **Surface hidden objections proactively:** Keep asking "Are you sure?" until second thoughts come out in the open
7. **By the final stage,** the "close" is just confirming what was already agreed

**Copy application:** Each section of a [[story-native-format]] ad should earn agreement before moving on. The reader should be nodding ("yes, that's me" / "yes, I've tried that" / "yes, that makes sense") at every transition. If you lose their agreement at any point, everything after it is wasted.

### Framework 4: Handling Anger/Resistance (Defuse or Leave)

When facing anger, sarcasm, hostility, or negativity:

1. **"You seem upset."** (flat, unemotional tone — like saying "it looks like it might rain")
2. **"You still seem upset."** (no emphasis on "still")
3. **"Maybe we should discuss this at another time."**
4. **If calm → continue.** If upset again → restart at Step 1.
5. **If all else fails:** "I'm not willing to meet with you now. For us to meet now wouldn't serve you or me. Would you like to schedule another appointment, or not?"

**Key:** Your tone must be flat and neutral throughout. Not judgmental. Not sympathetic. Neutral.

### Framework 5: Non Sequitur Handling

When a prospect gives an answer that doesn't actually answer your question:

1. **Treat the non sequitur as noise** — as if they coughed or cleared their throat
2. **Re-ask the real question** in different words
3. **Continue as long as the prospect keeps answering your actual questions** — the non sequitur noise will often stop on its own
4. **Only dis-qualify when the prospect explicitly says they don't need/want what you sell** — "I'm not interested" alone is NOT a dis-qualification, it's a non sequitur

**Example:** You ask who handles vendor selection. Prospect says "I'm not interested in new vendors." They didn't answer your question. Say: "Does that mean selecting vendors is your responsibility, or does someone else handle that?"

## Level 4: Proven Examples

### Example 1: VP with Ann Kaufman (The Sun Line Deal)

VP visits an existing customer. The buyer is annoyed (different vendor problem). VP acknowledges the emotion first: "You seem upset." / "You still seem upset." / "Perhaps we should come back at another time." — defusing anger before business.

Once calm, VP asks questions. The buyer reveals packaging problems with two other product lines. VP never pitches — only asks: "Do you want our packaging for any of your other lines?" / "Is that something you want us to do?" / "Are you ready to make some commitments?"

When the buyer asks VP to quote without involving the product manager, VP sets a boundary: "That's not how I work. I'm not willing to quote it twice." The buyer respects this.

Result: A deal that made this customer WPC's largest account — without VP ever asking for the order.

**What makes it work:** VP controlled the process through questions and commitments. The buyer felt ownership of every decision. No pressure = no resistance. Having standards ("that's not how I work") increased respect.

### Example 2: Sue's Prospecting Calls

Sue's 34-word offer: "We manufacture self-contained, four-color display packaging, which is shipped flat and is easy to assemble. Is that something you want for your product line?"

- Prospect who won't answer questions openly → Sue refuses to prepare a quote → leaves immediately. Saved hours of estimating and follow-up.
- Prospect who says "send me information" → "Usually when people say that, it's a polite way of saying 'Get lost'. If you want me to 'Get lost', just say so. It's okay."
- Prospect who hedges → "Does that mean you do want self-contained, four-color display packaging, or not?"

**What makes it work:** Clear offer. Neutral tone. Always giving the prospect permission to say no. No time wasted on Low Probability Prospects.

### Example 3: Sue Ending a Meeting

After 10 minutes of questions, a prospect asks for a quote but won't answer Sue's question about the basis for switching vendors. Sue ends the call and leaves.

**Why she was right:** If the prospect won't have a frank, open discussion, the probability of business is low. The quote would require estimating time, review, computer printout, presentation visit, follow-up calls — possibly multiple rounds. All for a Low Probability outcome. That time is better spent with someone who engages honestly.

## Level 5: Nuances and Exceptions

1. **"I'm not interested" is noise, not a no.** It's a non sequitur — the prospect is reflexively defending, not actually answering. Continue asking real questions unless they explicitly say they don't need or want what you sell.

2. **"Interesting" = worthless.** There's no commitment in interest. "That sounds interesting" requires: "Does that mean you want what I'm offering, or not?" Same for "maybe," "I don't know," "I'll consider it."

3. **Low Probability today ≠ Low Probability forever.** Someone who can't buy now may become a High Probability Prospect later. Ask: "Do you want me to check back in [timeframe]?" But don't visit them, build rapport, or invest resources until they're ready.

4. **The "or not?" paradox.** Adding "or not?" to questions sounds harsh in theory but reduces resistance in practice. Giving explicit permission to say no makes prospects more honest — and more likely to say yes if they mean it.

5. **Having standards is attractive, not off-putting.** Telling a prospect "I only do business with people I trust and respect" increases respect. People don't want to work with someone who has no standards.

6. **Comfort is not the goal.** Discomfort is the price of learning something new. If you feel comfortable doing something new, you're probably doing the old thing with new labels. "THE PRIMARY PRICE OF SUCCESS IS DISCOMFORT."

7. **Even experts slip into selling.** The two signals: (a) feeling stress, (b) talking too long. When you catch it, just stop and return to the process. Don't beat yourself up.

8. **First impressions are the window.** "What the prospect puts out at your first meeting is what he wants you to get." A prospect who's short or resistant on first meeting rarely becomes a great customer.

9. **The book only covers Chapters 1-8.** The full methodology includes High Probability Closing (Ch 9), Fine Points (Ch 10), Conditions of Satisfaction (Ch 11), and a Complete Sale walkthrough (Ch 12). These are not captured here.
