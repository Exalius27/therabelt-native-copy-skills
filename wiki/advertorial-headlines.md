# Advertorial Headlines — The 5 Elements + Entry-Point Alignment

> Sources: [[raw/2026-04-08-ecom-masterclass-whop-1-mp4-1.md]], [[raw/2026-04-08-file-1oz8ffczlocwna8dbdqorp.md]]
> Related: [[vicious-hooks]], [[copy-structure]], [[rmbc-ii-advertorials-and-pdps]], [[direct-response-copywriting]], [[collier-letter-book]]
> Last updated: 2026-04-08

Advertorial headlines have one job: stop the prospect and compel them to read the second sentence. Not sell. Not mention the product. Not even mention the main appeal. The headline's physical space is 10-20% of the ad — demanding it do the selling wastes it.

Core philosophy: **formulas kill headlines.** "How [authority] does [result] in [time]" has been seen thousands of times. The brain recognizes the pattern and skips. The solution to being unique is to actually be unique — understand the principles, then let your mind create from them instead of filling in blanks.

Eugene Schwartz: *"The problem defies a formula... The true copywriter must argue with success. He must push on past every time he faces a new product."*

---

## The 5 Elements

Every strong advertorial headline draws from these five elements. Not all five need to appear in every headline, but **relatability** is mandatory. The others amplify.

### 1. Relatability (Mandatory)

The headline must make the reader instantly think: *"This is about me."*

Not because it's clever — because it mirrors their exact life experience, daily habit, frustration, silent fear, or internal monologue. When they read it, they should feel seen.

**How to create relatability:**
- Tap into common behaviors they've tried and failed at
- Call out day-to-day struggles (not abstract problems — lived ones)
- Call out advice they've tried or heard (only if solution-aware)
- Name the emotion they're suppressing — shame, guilt, helplessness, resentment
- Use their exact language, not polished copy — the way they actually talk

**Test:** If your avatar reads the headline and doesn't think "this is for me" or "this is something I'm dealing with," it fails.

### 2. Tension

Create a contradiction, a situation that doesn't make sense — the "what the fuck" moment.

Tension is different from curiosity. Tension is the brain detecting something is OFF. Something doesn't add up. The reader needs to resolve it, which means reading more.

**Forms of tension:**
- Mismatch between what happened and what they were told
- A logical break that needs explaining
- A question they need answered to feel okay

**Example:** *"I lost more than hair. I was losing myself. And a random TikTok girl figured it out before my doctor did."* — How is that possible? A TikTok girl knows more than a doctor? The brain hates this. It must resolve it.

**Not mandatory** — but when tension works, it creates an intense compulsion to read.

### 3. Curiosity

A knowledge gap that hints at an answer but doesn't reveal it. Different from tension: curiosity is "I want to know more." Tension is "something is wrong here."

**Critical rule:** Curiosity must be real and relevant, not clickbait. It should hint at answers without revealing them.

**Training method:** Study news article headlines (especially Buzzfeed-style). Pay attention to the specific words and phrases that spark curiosity. Collect them in a file. This builds vocabulary and instincts for what drives curiosity.

### 4. Humanity

The headline must feel like it came from someone who lives it, not someone trying to sell it. No polish, no catch-phrases, no marketing voice.

**Two approaches depending on advertorial type:**

- **First-person story (hero journey advertorial):** Use the exact words your avatar would say with no one watching. Raw, lived experience. *"No doctor could explain it. Then a girl on TikTok described exactly what I was going through."*
- **Third-person editorial (article-style advertorial):** Even without a personal story, it still needs emotional truth. Frame identity, frustration, or fear — not just facts. Facts don't create curiosity. Emotions do. *"The hiding truth about PCOS — no one talks about the real reasons women feel like they're losing themselves."*

**Test:** Read it aloud. Does it sound like a person talking to a friend at a cafe, or a marketer writing copy?

### 5. Contextual Relevance (Entry-Point Alignment)

**The headline doesn't exist in a vacuum.** It will be read seconds after someone clicks a Facebook ad, a native ad, or a Google result. That ad sets the mental frame the headline must step into.

This is the element most people miss entirely. See the full framework below.

## Entry-Point Alignment Framework

The ad the reader just clicked determines what your headline must do. Headlines don't work in isolation — they're part of an ecosystem: **ad → headline → hero image → sub-headline → body copy.** Everything must create a full, congruent picture.

### Four Entry Points (What Did They Just Click?)

| Entry point | Reader's state | Headline job |
|---|---|---|
| **Raw/ugly image ad** (hair clumps, no text) | Curious but unfiltered — anyone with the broad problem clicked | Headline must FILTER. Anchor the pain specifically. Add the niche qualifier the ad didn't. |
| **Targeted visual with headline** (image + text mentioning the specific condition) | Pre-filtered from the ad — already knows the topic | Headline can lean into EMOTIONAL WEIGHT without repeating surface-level symptoms. |
| **Short video** (3-5 second teaser) | Slightly primed, some context | Headline extends the hook the video started. |
| **Full VSL/UGC** (complete context given) | Fully primed, high awareness | Headline reinforces the core promise or creates next-level curiosity. |

### The Filter Rule

> If your ad doesn't filter, your headline must. If your headline doesn't filter, your sub-headline or hero image must. Somewhere in the above-the-fold ecosystem, the reader must be told: *this is specifically for you.*

If a broad clickbait image attracts all women with hair loss but the advertorial is specifically for PCOS, the headline or surrounding elements MUST introduce PCOS. Otherwise you attract the wrong audience and the funnel leaks.

### The Ecosystem Principle

Above-the-fold is a system: headline + hero image + sub-headline. They work together.

- If the headline is missing context, reinforce it with the hero image
- If the headline is emotionally powerful but too broad, use a sub-headline to filter
- If the ad already did heavy filtering, the headline can go pure emotion

**Never think in isolation.** Always ask: *"What ad did they just click? What are they expecting to see next?"*

## Writing Process

Headlines are written AFTER the advertorial is complete — never before.

1. **Write the full advertorial first** — follow the right level of awareness and market sophistication
2. **Zoom out** — understand the full picture of what the story is about
3. **Remind yourself of the headline's job** — not to sell, but to filter, snap out of autopilot, create curiosity, compel the next line
4. **Consider alignment** — what ads will drive traffic? What mental frame will readers arrive with?
5. **Write many headlines** — back to back, no distractions. Write, edit, write. Keep going until your brain stops copying what it's seen and starts creating what it actually feels
6. **Sanity check each one** against the checklist below

---

## Headline Sanity Checklist

Before shipping any headline:

- [ ] Does it stop the scroll?
- [ ] Does it mirror something they've actually felt?
- [ ] Does it match the reader's level of awareness?
- [ ] Does it open a loop or create a knowledge gap?
- [ ] Does it align with the ad that drove the traffic?
- [ ] Does it feel real — not like copy?
- [ ] If you were the reader, would you feel compelled to read — or scroll past?

**Rule: if even one answer is "no," don't ship. Rework it.**

---

## Anti-Formula Philosophy

Formulas (how X does Y, why Z authority says W) worked when fewer people used them. Today, every prospect sees the same patterns hundreds of times daily. Their brain recognizes the template and dismisses it instantly.

The antidote: understand what makes headlines work (the 5 elements), then create from understanding — not from fill-in-the-blank templates. Study news headlines to build vocabulary for curiosity-driving words. Sit with the work. Allow your brain to get to "the zone" where it stops recycling patterns and starts generating something genuinely new.

This doesn't mean ignore what works. It means: absorb the principles deeply enough that you don't need the formula anymore.
