## Core Thesis

Market selection is more important than copy quality. You can write the best ad in the world and still fail if you're selling to the wrong people. But if you find a genuinely hungry market, even mediocre copy can generate real money. The implication: most marketers are optimizing the wrong variable. They rewrite their copy when they should be rethinking their market.

Halbert's hamburger stand illustration: "I'll give you every advantage you asked for — superior meat, sesame seed buns, best location, lowest prices. I want only one advantage. Give me a starving crowd, and I will whip the pants off all of you."

## Principle 1: The Starving Crowd

The central insight from Halbert's teaching: before you think about copy, before you think about product, before you think about anything else — identify a group of people who have already demonstrated that they want what you're selling.

How do you measure hunger? You don't ask. You observe what they actually buy.

**The survey trap:** People lie in surveys — not deliberately, but aspirationally. They answer what they think they should buy (the Bible, premium beer, live theater) while their wallet shows what they actually buy (the National Enquirer, regular beer, movies). The wallet never lies. The mouth often does.

**Truth is determined by wallets, not words.** Sales data, mailing list size, repeat advertising — these reveal actual desire. Surveys, focus groups, and interviews reveal what people wish they desired.

Application: Before building a product or writing copy, observe what's already selling. Which mailing lists are large and growing? Which ads run month after month, year after year? Which magazines carry dense mail-order advertising in a given category? These signals reveal where real demand lives.

## Principle 2: List Science — The 10 Possibilities

When selecting who to mail, there is a hierarchy of list quality. Each level represents a more targeted, more proven prospect than the one before it. Halbert illustrated this with a 10-step ladder, using a direct mail campaign for a real estate investment book.

**Possibility 1 — Phone book:** Worst. The only thing these people have in common is a phone number. Massive waste circulation.

**Possibility 2 — Phone book from high-income zip codes:** Better, but still not enough. Gardeners and maids live in wealthy areas too.

**Possibility 3 — Known high-income occupations:** Doctors, lawyers, executives. Reasonable — at least they probably have money. But no evidence of interest in your specific category.

**Possibility 4 — Proven mail-order buyers (any category):** A major leap. These people buy things by mail. They're not intimidated by the format. Any mail-order buyer is better than almost any non-buyer.

**Possibility 5 — Buyers of a similar product by mail:** Much better. They have the income, they buy by mail, and they've demonstrated interest in your category by buying something like it.

**Possibility 6 — Repeat buyers of a similar product:** Better still. Repeat purchasing signals genuine sustained desire, not a one-time impulse.

**Possibility 7 — Repeat buyers who paid big money:** The unit of sale matters. Someone who paid $100 for a diet pill is a hotter prospect for high-ticket diet products than someone who paid $10. Higher spending signals stronger desire and greater willingness to pay again.

**Possibility 8 — Recent, repeat, high-ticket buyers:** Add recency to everything above. A buyer who purchased 30 days ago is hotter than one who purchased 18 months ago. The desire is still active.

**Possibility 9 — The above, confirmed working by a trusted broker:** Not all lists perform as their demographics suggest. A good broker knows which lists are actually converting for other mailers right now. This real-world confirmation matters.

**Possibility 10 — Your own customer list (satisfied customers):** The best list of all. These people already bought from you and liked it. The trust is established. The skepticism is gone. Reactivation and upsells to existing satisfied customers consistently outperform cold prospect lists.

### The Three List Quality Metrics

When evaluating any mailing list, apply three filters in order:

**Recency:** The more recently someone purchased, the hotter they are as a prospect. Look for "hotline buyers" — 30- or 90-day buyers. Recency is the most important of the three factors. Get them while they're hot.

**Frequency:** How many times has this person bought products in this category? Repeat buying signals strong sustained desire. A person who has purchased three skin-diving books is a better prospect for a fourth than someone who bought one.

**Unit of sale:** How much did they spend? A person who paid $500 for a diet product is a better prospect than someone who paid $20. Higher unit of sale signals both purchasing power and intensity of desire.

Of the three, Halbert rates recency as most important. Desire fades fast. Catch people while they're still active buyers.

## Principle 3: Market First, Product Second

The single most common mistake in mail-order and direct response: developing a product first and then hunting for a market to sell it to. This is "backasswards."

**Products are a dime a dozen.** They are important but less crucial than finding a hot market. A person with a new product may or may not be able to find a hot market for it. A person who has identified a hot market can almost always find or create a product to fill it.

The correct sequence:

1. Identify a hot market (mailing list, proven buying behavior, demonstrated desire)
2. Find or create a product that serves that market's existing hunger
3. Create the promotion

Not: have a product idea → find buyers for it.

Why it matters: When you start with the product, you're hoping there's a market. When you start with the market, you know there's a market — you've already seen them buy. The risk profile is entirely different.

## Principle 4: A-Pile / B-Pile

A framework for how people sort their mail — and the single most underestimated variable in direct mail response rates.

Every person who receives mail divides it, almost unconsciously, into two piles:

**The A-Pile:** Letters that appear to be personal. From a friend, a relative, a known business associate. These get opened. Every time. Without exception. People always read their personal mail.

**The B-Pile:** Letters that obviously contain a commercial message. The bulk-rate indicia, the teaser copy, the printed label, the clearly commercial return address — all of these signal "sales pitch inside." B-pile letters may get opened when the prospect is bored, or when they look interesting, or if nothing else is competing for attention. Often, they get thrown away without being opened.

**The implication is brutal:** If your outer envelope signals B-pile, only a fraction of your prospects ever even see your copy. The rest of your copywriting investment is wasted. Halbert's rule: get your envelope into the A-pile.

### How to Get into the A-Pile

Make the envelope look like a personal letter:

- Use a live first-class stamp (not a bulk-rate indicia)
- Type or handwrite the address — no Cheshire labels
- Use a simple return address without a company name (just a street address)
- No teaser copy on the outside
- Use clean, personal-looking white envelopes

The recipient sees this envelope and thinks: "This might be from someone I know. I better open it." He doesn't know what's inside, which means he opens it to find out.

The commercially obvious "B-pile" envelope tells the recipient immediately: "This contains a sales pitch." He already knows what's inside without opening it. Why would he?

**Bond Halbert's update (email equivalent):** The A-pile / B-pile maps directly to email. Your primary email address (work, personal) is your A-pile. Your spam address — the one you give to marketers — is your B-pile. Being in the spam folder of a primary email address is better than being in the primary folder of a spam address. Getting into the right inbox matters more than open-rate optimization tricks.

## Principle 5: Attention Grabbers — The Relevance Rule

Halbert was known for attaching physical objects to the top of letters — coins, dollar bills, a plastic baggie of sand or dirt, Mexican pesos, Japanese pennies. These grabbed attention powerfully. But the technique only works when the grabber is relevant to the message.

**The relevance rule:** Your attention grabber must tie in naturally with your copy. If it doesn't, the reader feels deceived, and the attention you earned turns into distrust.

The failure example: "A Submarine That Flies? No, we don't have that — but our pink pills, etc." The reader feels cheated. Don't do it.

The success example: Attaching a plastic baggie of Hawaiian beach sand to a real estate investment letter — then opening with "The sand inside that baggie was taken from a beach on Maui that is now open to private investors." The attention-getter and the offer are one thing.

**How to build a relevant grabber:**

1. Decide what you want to sell
2. Find a physical object that connects naturally to the product or market
3. Write the opener to explain the connection immediately and justify why the object was sent

Halbert's track record: a dollar bill (charitable giving / "I'm dividing my $1,000 donation"), a Japanese penny (something discovered in Japan), a Mexican peso (inflation / devalued currency), Hawaiian beach sand (real estate). Each object tied directly into the copy that followed.

**The fake traffic ticket failure:** Some companies print fake traffic tickets and put them on car windshields. They get attention. But the anger and sense of deception that follows destroys any possibility of a sale. Impact that creates negative emotion is worse than no impact.

## Principle 6: Editorial Look — You Never Get a Second Chance

In most publications, editorial content gets five times as much readership as advertising. The implication: make your ads and DM pieces look like editorial content — specifically, like an exciting news story or a rave review written by an enthusiastic reporter.

**The mental model:** Imagine a reporter who loves your book so much he writes a full-page article in the L.A. Times, explains what makes it extraordinary, and tells readers exactly how to order a copy. That's what your ad should look and feel like.

**Application to DM:** What would that reporter do if he wanted to tell a friend in Hawaii about your book? He'd write a personal letter, include a snapshot, and describe why his friend would love it. That's the DM template.

**The Coat-of-Arms lesson:** Halbert proved this with his family name research business:
- A 70-item full-color catalog: bombed (didn't return mailing costs)
- An 8.5x11 brochure with the three best items: slightly better than break even
- A personal letter with a snapshot of the coat-of-arms plaque: **$40 million in sales**

The more professional and clearly-commercial the piece looked, the worse it performed. The simple personal letter that didn't initially look like a sales pitch outperformed everything by a factor of 20. Moral: "You can do a better selling job when at first it does not appear you are attempting to do a sales job."

## The 7-Step DM Campaign Process

Halbert's operational checklist for building a direct mail campaign from scratch:

1. **Find a hot market** — use the SRDS list book, identify lists where proven buyers exist
2. **Find or create a product** — preferably "paper and ink" (information products with high margins)
3. **Create the DM promotion** — describes the product, the benefits, the offer
4. **Make a test mailing** — 1,000 to 5,000 pieces
5. **Analyze results** — decisive winner, decisive loser, or marginal
6. **If winner, roll out** — 20,000 to 100,000 pieces
7. **If still performing, scale** — finance expansion from cash flow

Key principle: test small, scale only what proves itself. If it loses at 2,000 names, it will lose at 200,000. If it wins at 2,000 names, it can be scaled with confidence.

**On financing:** Halbert's model was to find "bankers" — investors who put up $5,000 to fund a test. If the test won, the banker invested $50,000+ in the rollout and split profits. The key advantage: the copywriter risks no capital while the investor risks no copy quality. Each brings what they have.
