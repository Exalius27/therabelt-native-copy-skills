## What makes a bullet work

Every strong bullet combines multiple elements from this list:
- **Benefit** — what the reader gets
- **Specificity** — exact numbers, names, mechanisms (turns vague claims into believable ones)
- **Curiosity/intrigue** — hide something so the reader needs to keep going
- **Credibility** — reference a source, authority, study, or mechanism that makes the claim believable
- **Emotional precision** — hit the exact desire or fear, not a general one

Weak bullets have one element. Strong bullets stack 3-4 in a single line.

## The Makepeace Makeover (3 steps to fix any flat line)

1. **Add specificity.** "How to profit from stocks" becomes "How to make 307% profit from gold stocks." Numbers and named mechanisms imply research, not guessing.
2. **Reveal the mechanism.** Show the reader HOW they'll get the benefit. A named method, system, or ingredient differentiates your claim from every other promise. "Warren Buffet's Bull Market X-Factor" tells the reader there's a specific, real strategy.
3. **Layer in credibility and intrigue.** Add a timeframe, an authority, or a surprising constraint. "...in the next 60 days" makes it concrete and urgent.

Apply all three to any flat benefit line and it becomes something a reader can't skip.

## 21 bullet formulas (reference list)

| # | Formula | When to use | Core mechanic |
|---|---------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | **How to** | Most versatile. Works anywhere. | Curiosity about method. Tie to deep desires, not surface wants. |
| 2 | **Secret to** | Info not commonly known. Don't overuse or everything feels like a "secret." | Implies exclusive knowledge. |
| 3 | **Why** | Promise to explain a cause. | Reader assumes knowing "why" gives them an edge. |
| 4 | **What** | Specific instructions or hidden info ("What doctors don't tell you..."). | Bumps perceived value while hiding the answer. |
| 5 | **What NEVER** | Alert reader to a mistake they might be making. | Taps fear + curiosity + protection benefit simultaneously. |
| 6 | **PLUS** | End of a bullet list. Greed stacker. | "More, more, MORE" energy. Finishes with momentum. |
| 7 | **Number** | Group multiple ways/secrets/reasons together. | Condenses value. "4 ways to..." feels like a package deal. |
| 8 | **Right... WRONG!** | Reader has a common assumption you can shatter. | Debunking creates an itch — they NEED the right answer. |
| 9 | **WARNING** | Danger ahead. Fear-based. | Works best when you immediately offer the solution too. |
| 10 | **Are you...?** | Reader is already doing/experiencing something. | Gets a "yes" nod, builds momentum toward the sale. |
| 11 | **Gimmick** | You can name a technique or concept from your research. | Combines benefit + specificity + curiosity in a coined name. |
| 12 | **Sneaky** | Conspiracy angle, hidden/guarded info. Use sparingly. | Implies someone doesn't want you to know this. |
| 13 | **Statement of Interest + Benefit** | Lead with a fascinating fact, follow with the payoff. | Two-part structure: intrigue then reward. |
| 14 | **Direct Benefit** | Strong unique claim, lead with action verb. | Fastest bullet — hits immediately, then deepens. |
| 15 | **Specific Question** | Lead with an intriguing question of importance. | Reader wants the answer; you hold it. |
| 16 | **If... Then** | Reader meets a condition, you promise a result. | Self-selection. Reader thinks "that's me" and leans in. |
| 17 | **When** | Benefit tied to a specific time or trigger. | Implies guaranteed result at a specific moment. |
| 18 | **Quickest/Easiest** | Offer the simplest path to a desired result. | Human nature: faster, easier, less effort always pulls. |
| 19 | **Truth** | Controversial or confusing topic. | Promise to settle the debate. People love demystification. |
| 20 | **Better** | Something assumed good, but you have something superior. | Slips under the BS detector by accepting the baseline first. |
| 21 | **Single** | One thing that exceeds all others. Need proof. | "The single most important..." Promises to cut to the best. |

## Production principles

- **Write 7x more than you need, keep the best.** Parris Lampropoulos writes 700 fascinations to keep 100. This guarantees every survivor is exceptional.
- **Vary length, format, and reveal level.** Some blind (hidden info), some open (full benefit revealed). Long and short mixed. Reading bullets should feel like a roller coaster, not a list.
- **One or two bad bullets can kill everything.** Weak bullets cause skimming. Skimming is one step from quitting. No filler.
- **Combine formulas.** The best real-world bullets use 2-3 formulas stacked together. Attack each piece of info from multiple angles, pick the strongest version.
- **Themed bullets hit harder.** Group related bullets around one topic to hit it from every angle. Reader feels you're thorough and comprehensive.
- **Sequential bullets build on each other.** Sometimes bullets work better when each feeds off the previous one, building a connected argument instead of scattered points.
- **Best bullets become headlines.** "How Doctors Stay Well While Treating Sick People All Day" started as a bullet. Always watch for bullets that could promote to headline or subhead.
- **Pure benefit bullets (no mystery to hide):** Make them readable with strong verb choices. Verbs like "rejuvenated, electrified, revitalized" do the heavy lifting when you can't create curiosity.

## How this applies to [[story-native-format]] ads

These aren't just for traditional DR bullet lists. The underlying mechanics — specificity, stacked curiosity, emotional precision, mechanism reveal — are the same ones that make [[vicious-hooks|hooks]], turning points, and product introductions work inside narrative copy. Any line in a story that feels flat is missing the same elements a flat bullet is missing.
