The second half of direct response marketing. Once you've acquired a customer (Stage 1: A→B), the real opportunity is retention, upsells, and repeat revenue (Stage 2: B→C→D→E).

RMBC II covers six critical email sequence types, each serving a different moment in the customer lifecycle.

---

## Master Principles (From 24 Real Sample Sequences)

These principles cut across all sequence types. They emerge from the actual examples, not the theory lessons.

### 1. Product Type Determines Everything

The same sequence type (e.g., abandoned cart) looks completely different depending on what's being sold. The product dictates CTA architecture, discount strategy, and emotional register.

- **Supplements (consumable, low-ticket):** Discount codes, social proof, ingredient science. Every email drives to "complete your order + save X%." Discount is the primary lever.
- **Digital tools (mid-ticket, $497):** Feature/benefit reminders, ROI math, "manual research vs. automation" contrast. Discount still used but paired with transformation framing.
- **Coaching/high-ticket ($5K+):** Never discounts. Uses scarcity (limited spots), objection handling, and "book a free strategy session" CTA. Every email handles a different objection.

This maps to [[rmbc-ii-funnel-structure]] — the 15-factor framework determines not just the funnel shape but also the email copy approach.

### 2. Escalating Pressure Architecture

No sequence starts at maximum intensity. Pressure builds across emails in a predictable pattern:

**Emotional arc:** Helpful → Social proof → Urgency → Scarcity → Future regret
**Discount arc:** No discount → Moderate discount → Maximum discount (never leads with the biggest offer)

Real example from abandoned cart V2: No discount (Days 1-2) → 40% off introduced (Day 3) → Upgraded to 55% off (Day 5) → Final hours at 55% (Day 7). The delay makes the discount feel earned, not desperate.

### 3. Reason-Why for Every Offer

Every promotional email justifies why the discount exists right now. The reason makes the offer believable instead of signaling desperation (connects to [[persuasion]] — reason-why framework and [[collier-letter-book]] — logical excuse for emotion).

- Holiday sales: "Independence Day represents freedom — there's no greater freedom than feeling confident in your body again"
- Flash sales: "I just got off a call with our warehouse manager, and we have excess inventory"
- Winback discounts: "I'm worried about you" / "My team thinks I've lost my mind authorizing this"
- Welcome discounts: Framed as "member appreciation" or "insider access"

### 4. The Four Reorder/Winback Messages

Every retention sequence (reorder + winback) uses exactly four messaging angles, always in this order:

1. **You're making progress** — Affirm their consistency, remind them what's happening inside their body
2. **You'll make MORE progress if you continue** — Month 2 is when breakthroughs happen, you're just around the corner
3. **You'll LOSE progress if you stop** — The "cellular transport breakdown begins within 72 hours," your body goes back to where it started
4. **Here's an incentive** — Discount code, but only after the emotional case is built

This is the retention copy formula. Progress → Promise → Loss → Incentive.

### 5. Multiple Subject Line Options for Every Email

Every sample provides 2-3 subject line options per email. This isn't indecision — it's built for A/B testing. The options typically cover different angles: curiosity-based, urgency-based, and social-proof-based.

## Stage 2: Customer Journey Overview (B → C → D → E)

After checkout (B), there are multiple revenue opportunities:

1. **Upsells at checkout** — Offer complementary product immediately
2. **Abandoned cart recovery** — Win back people who added items but didn't buy
3. **Welcome sequences** — Set expectations, deliver value, prime for future offers
4. **Promotional sequences** — Flash sales, holiday promotions, limited-time discounts
5. **Reorder/repeat sequences** — For subscription or repeat-purchase products, remind when to reorder
6. **Winback sequences** — Recover inactive customers who bought before but haven't returned

## Email Sequence Type 1: Upsells (Immediate Checkout)

### Purpose
Increase order value at the moment of highest buying momentum—right after initial purchase.

### Psychology
- **Frequency principle:** Once someone's bought once, buying a second item is easier
- **Momentum:** They're in "buying mode"
- **Complementarity:** Offering something that pairs with their purchase feels natural

### Structure
**Simple upsell:** "Buyers of X also get Y for $37 today only"
- Usually 1 product, limited time, time-sensitive
- High pressure because momentum is high
- Conversion rate: 10-15% typical; winners hit 20-30%

### Upsell Strategy: Which Frame When

Every upsell introduces one of three problem types, and the product type determines which to use:

| Situation | Problem Type | Frame |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Consumable (supplements, powders) | "What if you run out?" | Continuation (more of the same) |
| Subscription product | New or expanded problem | Complementary product (Frame 2) — selling more of the same undermines the subscription |
| High-ticket device (sauna, hearing aid) | New problem or upgrade | Complementary product OR upgrade version |
| Info product / course | Faster/easier implementation | Done-for-you (Frame 3) — templates, team, coaching |

**What you're selling across all frames:** better results, faster results, easier results, expanded results, or savings. The "new problem" angle (your solution creates a new challenge: weight loss → saggy skin, dating success → keeping the partner) is stronger than "buy more" when the product category allows it.

**Upgrade version upsell** (not a separate frame, but a variant): Cheap entry product → premium version. Example: $9 hearing aid → $149 upgraded model. The initial purchase proves the category works; the upsell sells better performance within the same category.

### Three Upsell Frames (From Real Scripts)

Analysis of five real upsell scripts (Emma, Synocell, Gut Harmony, Sabri Suby, Stem Cell Restore) reveals three distinct framing strategies:

**Frame 1: The Continuation Frame ("More of the Same")**
Used when the upsell IS the same product in larger quantity. The psychological core: stopping too soon wastes everything you already invested. The script walks through a healing/transformation timeline (week 1, month 1, month 2... month 6) showing that real results require sustained use, then reveals that the initial purchase only covers the first phase. Urgency comes from supply chain scarcity ("takes 3-6 months to produce each batch") and the risk of interrupting the restoration process.

Key copy elements:
- Congratulations + reaffirm decision (3-4 paragraphs)
- Explain the healing process and why it takes time
- Show what happens if you stop too soon ("sabotage everything you've worked for")
- Customer regret stories ("they often regret not buying more when they had the chance")
- Supply chain scarcity (ingredient shortages, production delays)
- "Friends and family" or "VIP" pricing frame — positions the discount as exclusive, not a sale
- One-time page-only urgency (structural, not artificial)
- Full money-back guarantee to eliminate risk of stocking up

Tactical sequence from Emma Upsell 1 script ([[raw/2026-04-07-emma-upsell-1-script.md]]):
1. **Authority + Congratulations** — Doctor narrator congratulates, tells them to stay on the page
2. **Reaffirm the problem** — Even decades of problems, Emma will help
3. **Completion education** — Explains the healing process: parasites burrow deep, gut lining needs time to repair, stopping early lets hidden colonies reawaken
4. **Sabotage warning** — "Don't stop halfway through and interrupt the restoration period, or else you could sabotage everything you've worked for"
5. **Customer regret proof** — Others regret not buying more when they had the chance
6. **Supply scarcity (triple-layered)** — Supply chain delays (external), small batch manufacturing (structural), viral demand outpacing capacity (social proof). Three independent scarcity reasons, none artificial
7. **Correct usage frame** — "It's very important to use Emma correctly" repositions the upsell as medical guidance, not selling
8. **Insider pricing origin story** — "Friends and Family Discount" started as employee coupon, now available to VIP customers. Gives the discount a backstory that makes it believable
9. **Reinfection fear loop** — Even after healing, exposure from everyday life (restaurants, handshakes, doorknobs) means constant threat. Product becomes ongoing protection, not one-time fix
10. **Risk reversal as buying logic** — 90-day guarantee positioned not as safety net but as reason to stock up: "Since you can try it risk-free, you have nothing to lose"
11. **Price reframe** — "Just 95 cents a day" — pennies framing for bulk purchase
12. **Hard close** — "Once you leave this page, you'll never see this offer again" repeated twice

Continuation Frame variations from Synocell Upsell 1 script ([[raw/2026-04-07-synocell-upsell-1-script.md]]):
Same skeleton as Emma (congratulations → completion logic → scarcity → exclusive discount → risk reversal) but adapts the frame with four additional moves not in Emma:
- **Research-citation authority** — Instead of mechanism storytelling (Emma's parasites), stacks published studies (Annals of Rheumatic Diseases, Osteoarthritis and Cartilage, Journal of Clinical Rheumatology). Different credibility strategy: narrative authority vs. research authority. Both work for continuation framing
- **Double-dose acceleration** — "Doubling your dose during the first few months gives your body the boost it needs." Reframes bulk purchase as investment in SPEED, not just continuity. Simultaneously justifies buying more AND promises faster results
- **Gift-for-loved-ones expansion** — "Think of the loved ones who may be silently struggling." Expands purchase reason from self-use to altruistic buying. Creates emotional justification beyond personal need. Absent from Emma entirely
- **Cost-of-inaction comparison** — Anchors supplement cost against medical alternatives ($1,500-$4,000/year for treatments, surgery = tens of thousands). Emma used fear of regression; Synocell uses financial comparison. Both drive urgency but through different pain points
- **Rebound effect warning** — "Gaps in anti-inflammatory support can lead to a rebound effect." Stronger than simple regression — implies getting WORSE than baseline if you stop, not just returning to it

Continuation Frame template structure from Gut Harmony sample ([[raw/2026-04-07-sample-upsell-1-script-more-of-the-same.md]]):
This is a teaching template with numbered annotations, making the conscious architecture visible. Reveals additional moves beyond Emma and Synocell:
- **"Second most important decision" reframe** — The initial purchase is framed as "the second most important decision of your journey." The MOST important decision is the upsell on this page. Elevates the upsell above the original purchase
- **6-month healing timeline** — Month-by-month progression (Week 1 → Month 6) showing escalating results. Creates desire for the FULL ARC, not just continued use. Week 1: calming sensation. Month 3: eating fearlessly. Month 6: full digestive freedom. Aspirational completion vs. Emma's medical fear-of-regression
- **Three testimonials before the ask** — Social proof placed BEFORE the upsell pitch, not after. Each testimonial shows a different transformation type (bloating → confidence, constipation → freedom, diet plan → food enjoyment)
- **Double price anchor against professional alternatives** — Before revealing discount: "still about HALF of what you'd pay for a few visits to a specialist" ($894 vs. specialist). After discount: "about 10% of what it would cost for a single consultation" ($174 vs. specialist). The same anchor recalculated at each price point
- **Lifetime guarantee** — Stronger than 90-day: "If you change your mind for ANY reason at ANY point in the future." Followed immediately by "This isn't a hard decision" — the guarantee makes that literally true
- **Price descent: $149/bottle → $29/bottle** (80% discount) — Steeper than any other continuation script. Justified by mission framing: "our mission is to create as many success stories as we possibly can"

**Frame 2: The Complementary Product Frame ("Part II")**
Used when the upsell is a DIFFERENT product that amplifies the first. The mechanic/car analogy: even with the best mechanic, you'd prefer your car never broke down. The script explains what the first product does, then identifies the gap it doesn't cover, then shows how the second product fills that gap. Together they create a "tag-team effect."

Key copy elements:
- "While [Product A] handles X, your body also needs Y"
- Scientific explanation of why both are needed (different mechanisms)
- "Combining them means even MORE [benefit]"
- Studies and research for each ingredient in the complement
- Price anchored against retail, with the bundle discount framed as exclusive to first-time buyers

Tactical sequence from Stem Cell Restore → Genesis upsell ([[raw/2026-04-07-stem-cell-restore-upsell-genesis-docx.md]]):
1. **Congratulations + reaffirm** — Celebrates the purchase, future-paces benefits of Product A
2. **"Part II" positioning** — Explicitly frames upsell as "Part II of my anti-aging program," not a separate product
3. **Gap identification with quantified scale** — Product A handles stem cells, but you have 300 trillion "assigned cells" that aren't stem cells. The gap feels enormous because it's numbered
4. **Mechanic/car analogy** — Even with the best mechanic, you'd prefer your car never broke down. Reframes Product B as prevention, Product A as repair
5. **Mechanism education** — Full telomere education (chains with 8,000 links, losing links = aging). Nobel Prize credibility anchor (Dr. Blackburn). This is the "why both are needed" explained through biology
6. **Per-ingredient study parade** — Each of 8 ingredients gets its own named study with specific results (Telos 95: "reversed biological age by 27 years," Cycloastragenol: "gained 530 telomere links," ALA: Emory heart study, D3/K2: Harvard + London School of Medicine). Research-citation authority applied per ingredient, not just per product
7. **Social proof volume** — "Used by 150,000 people" repeated three times throughout. Volume as credibility
8. **Three-step price descent** — Retail $99/bottle → discounted $74.50/bottle → buy 2 get 1 free → effective $49.66/bottle. Savings anchored at $148 off retail. Maps to [[rmbc-ii-vsl-architecture]] price descent patterns
9. **"Ticking off existing customers" reason-why** — Unique discount justification: the seller is taking a risk by offering this price because 150,000+ people already paid full price. Makes the discount feel genuinely costly to the seller, therefore more believable (connects to [[persuasion]] reason-why framework)
10. **Guilt-trip decline button** — "No thanks. I will pass on this opportunity forever. If I change my mind, I'm okay with paying the extra $50 per bottle." Loss-frames the act of declining
11. **90-day guarantee** — Same risk reversal as Frame 1, but positioned as "I'm taking all of the risk here"

**Frame 3: The Done-For-You Frame ("I'll Do the Work")**
Used for info products where the upsell bridges the gap between knowing and doing. The pitch identifies that most buyers never implement (cites real statistics — "64% of non-fiction books never get completed"), then offers a pre-built system. The copy spends significant time on the PROBLEM of non-implementation before revealing the solution.

Key copy elements:
- Celebrate the purchase extensively (future-pacing all the benefits)
- Dark crossroads: "But what if you don't implement? What if life gets in the way?"
- Research story: "I reached out to buyers who hadn't taken action and asked why"
- The two reasons: no time + too much work
- The solution: done-for-you templates/systems/funnels
- Social proof from people who used the done-for-you version
- Price anchored against hiring an agency ($10K+) or doing it yourself (decade of trial and error)

Tactical sequence from Sabri Suby "Sell Like Crazy" upsell ([[raw/2026-04-07-formatted-sabri-suby-sell-like-crazy-upsell.md]]):
1. **Extended celebration + specific future-pacing** — First ~65 lines are pure celebration. Much longer than supplement upsells because info products are intangible and need more reassurance. Future-pacing is hyper-specific: Range Rover Sport, retiring parents, honeymoon-quality marriage, inbox bursting with leads
2. **Credibility stack** — A decade of research, tens of millions in ad spend, 416 industries, $1.33 billion in sales. Stacked before the dark crossroads to maximize the height of the fall
3. **Dark crossroads with personal attack** — "Maybe your father-in-law was right about you." Brutal emotional whiplash from peak excitement to genuine fear. Not just "what if you don't implement" but "what if your biggest critic was right." Creates visceral need for the solution
4. **The saboteur reveal** — "That thing is you." The enemy isn't the market or the system — it's the buyer's own inaction. Self-directed fear is harder to dismiss than external threats
5. **NYT statistic as proof** — "64% of non-fiction books never get completed." External data makes the implementation problem feel universal, not a personal failure
6. **Research story as discovery narrative** — Reached out to buyers on Instagram, Facebook (1M+ followers), email list. Asked them directly. Found two reasons: no time + too much work. The research makes the problem feel DISCOVERED, not manufactured
7. **"I wished I could just do this for you" origin moment** — The done-for-you solution born from a specific customer conversation. Makes the upsell feel created from genuine care, not revenue optimization
8. **Solution reveal + component walkthrough** — Each bundle component shown with specific results (3,589 leads at $2.46, 2,333 leads at $3.53). Social proof is embedded IN the product description, not separated
9. **Social proof cascade** — Multiple customer messages with specific revenue numbers (73,000 euros from 2,149 spent, $20K in one day, $5,990 deal). Results are attributed to named people with real numbers
10. **Four-step price descent from personal anchor** — $10,000 (his agency's actual price) → $5,000 → $2,500 → $297. Then reframed as 81 cents/day. The anchor is personal (HIS agency) which is more credible than a generic "agencies charge X"
11. **Lifetime value relationship framing** — "Clients happily pay me $50,000-$500,000 every year." Positions the upsell as the START of a long-term relationship. Low price justified by future revenue: "you're going to want to stick with me for a very long time"
12. **30-day test drive guarantee** — Framed as "try it out on me" — the seller is hosting the risk, not just reversing it
13. **Page-exit finality** — "Once you click out, you strike out on this opportunity." Same one-time-page urgency as supplement scripts

### Generation with RMBC II

> "Create a simple upsell offer for customers who just bought [main product]. The upsell product is [complementary product at $XX]. Write copy that: 1) Positions it as the natural next step, 2) Highlights how it magnifies results from main product, 3) Creates urgency (limited time), 4) Includes clear CTA. Keep it to 3-4 sentences. Tone: Confident, not pushy."

**Dr. Clean spray upsell:** Buy cleaning spray, upsell is bulk discount on same product or complementary home care item

## Email Sequence Type 2: Abandoned Cart Recovery

### Purpose
Win back the ~70% of visitors who add items to cart but don't complete checkout.

### Key Insight
These are **ALMOST buyers**. Lower barrier to convince than cold prospects. Don't be aggressive; just address final hesitations.

### Real Sample Structures (Three Variants)

The theory says 3 emails. Real sequences run 7 emails over 7 days. The 3-email model is a minimum — the samples show that longer sequences with escalating pressure and discount upgrades significantly extend the recovery window.

**Variant 1: Discount-Forward (Supplement, 7 emails)**
Leads with the discount from Email 1. Every email includes the code. Discount escalates from 50% to 60% on Day 6. Email sequence: Reminder → Social Proof (6hrs) → Customer Story (Day 2) → Objection FAQ (Day 3) → Science/Credibility (Day 4) → Upgraded Discount (Day 5) → Final Hours (Day 6).

Best for: Price-sensitive audiences, competitive supplement markets, large dropoff at checkout.

**Variant 2: Emotion-First, Delayed Discount (Supplement, 7 emails)**
No discount until Day 3. First two emails are pure emotional storytelling — "mirror moment" identity copy, "your body is fighting you" education. Discount starts at 40%, escalates to 55%. Emotional close on Day 7 uses future-self projection ("the woman in the mirror is waiting to meet you").

Best for: Audiences who need emotional buy-in before price becomes the deciding factor. Higher perceived value positioning.

**Variant 3: No Discount (High-Ticket Coaching, 7 emails)**
Zero discounting. CTA is "book a free strategy session." Each email handles a different objection: Email 1 = curiosity gap, Email 2 = "too good to be true," Email 3 = fear of failure (founder vulnerability story), Email 4 = market timing urgency, Email 5 = limited spots scarcity, Email 6 = social proof + peer pressure, Email 7 = future regret close.

Best for: High-ticket offers where discounting would destroy positioning. The "strategy session" CTA replaces the discount as the conversion lever.

### Structural Pattern Across All Variants

Regardless of discount strategy, the email-by-email emotional arc is consistent:

1. **Reconnect** — Reminder of what they saw, soft re-engagement
2. **Social proof** — Others are succeeding, you're missing out
3. **Story/education** — Deeper emotional connection or mechanism education
4. **Objection handling** — FAQ format or direct objection crushing
5. **Credibility** — Science, credentials, clinical backing (supplements) or personal vulnerability story (coaching)
6. **Escalation** — Upgraded discount or increased scarcity
7. **Final close** — Future regret, crossroads choice, deadline enforcement

### The 3-Email Minimum (For Quick Deployment)

**Email 1 (immediate):** Reminder + soft CTA
**Email 2 (12-24 hours later):** Social proof + FOMO
**Email 3 (48 hours later):** Incentive + hard deadline

### Generation with RMBC II

> "Create a [3/7]-email abandoned cart sequence for [product]. Product type: [supplement/tool/coaching]. Discount strategy: [discount-forward/emotion-first-delayed/no-discount]. Each email should escalate in pressure. Include: subject line options for A/B testing, P.S. with support contact or secondary CTA, clear discount code placement if applicable."

## Email Sequence Type 3: Welcome Sequences

### Purpose — The Four Jobs
Every welcome email should serve at least one of these four purposes (from Georgi's overview):

1. **Reaffirm their decision** — "Great job. That was smart. You're awesome."
2. **Answer vital questions** — Shipping status, what to expect, how to use it
3. **Establish relationship and build trust** — Personal voice, face behind the brand
4. **Convert one-time buyer to repeat buyer** — Cross-sell discounts, subscription push

### Real Sample Structures (Three Variants)

**Variant 1: E-commerce Supplement (15 emails, 8 weeks)**
The most comprehensive model. Mixes relationship-building with escalating discount offers throughout.

Phase 1 — Onboarding (Days 0-3): Welcome celebration → Personal video → Member discount (15%) → Unboxing video → Package arrival check-in. Tone is warm, personal, celebratory. Every email reaffirms the decision.

Phase 2 — Consumption + Cross-sell (Days 5-14): Special member offer (25% off) → 1-week check-in with expected results → 20% discount with 24hr expiry → Last chance on discount. The check-in email lists specific results they should be noticing ("more stable energy, reduced cravings, better sleep") — this encourages continued use.

Phase 3 — Retention + Advocacy (Weeks 2-8): Survey with reward → 25% off restock → Running-low reorder reminder (30% off) → Progress photo request → "Apology" coupon for people who "missed" last sale (35% off) → Anniversary/subscription push (30% off subscribe-and-save).

Key pattern: **Discount escalation through the welcome sequence.** Starts at 15%, climbs to 20%, 25%, 30%, 35% — each positioned as a different "reason" (member perk, restock, apology, anniversary). The discounts never feel like a fire sale because each has a distinct justification.

**Variant 2: Low-Ticket Course → Strategy Session Ascension (12 emails, 15 days)**
The welcome sequence IS the sales funnel for the higher-ticket offer.

Phase 1 — Onboarding (Days 0-1): Welcome + login details → Course roadmap with first assignment → "Fast-track your results" (introduces free strategy session).

Phase 2 — Soft Sell (Days 2-5): Student success stories → First week action plan → "Why 80% of course buyers never succeed" (the pivot to coaching).

Phase 3 — Hard Sell (Days 5-15): Strategy session agenda transparency → Behind-the-scenes detailed success story → Market analysis teaser → Limited spots warning → Final 48 hours → Door closing.

Key pattern: **The consumption-to-ascension bridge.** First 3-4 emails genuinely help them use the course. Then the messaging shifts: "The course gives you knowledge, but the strategy session gives you the roadmap." Every email after Day 3 includes a CTA to book the strategy session, but always alongside genuine course guidance so it never feels like pure selling.

**Variant 3: High-Ticket Coaching (10+ emails, ongoing)**
Pure consumption and implementation focus. No cross-selling because they already bought the top-tier offer.

Emails are structured as: Welcome + future pacing → Portal walkthrough → First assignment with deadline → Community integration → Mindset coaching → Progress check-ins. The entire sequence is about preventing the "bought but never implemented" failure mode.

### Georgi's Warning: "Member" Language

Be careful with "member discount" or "member perks" language if the product is NOT a subscription. Customers may worry they've accidentally signed up for recurring charges. Use "customer appreciation" or "private discount" instead.

### Generation with RMBC II

> "Create a [5/12/15]-email welcome sequence for [product type]. Purpose: [reaffirm + consumption / consumption + ascension / pure consumption]. If ascension model, what's the next offer? [strategy session / subscription / complementary product]. Include: escalating discount schedule if applicable, consumption milestones, subject line options for A/B testing."

## Email Sequence Type 4: Promotional Sequences (Flash Sale, Holiday)

### Purpose
Drive additional revenue through time-limited offers. Georgi's perspective: "This is going to be a fairly fast section. Not because they're not important — we did $273,000 in a single day — but because they're pretty formulaic."

### The Formulaic Structure (From 6 Real Samples)

Six promotional sequences were generated across two product types (supplement line and digital tool). The structural pattern is identical regardless of product. The only difference: supplement promos remind buyers of benefits they already know; tool/software promos must re-educate on features since buyers may not remember what the tool does.

### Flash Sale (3 emails, 24 hours)

**Email 1 (Hour 0):** Launch — Reason-why + discount + product benefits + CTA
**Email 2 (Hour 12):** Midway — Social proof momentum ("response has been incredible"), inventory warnings, testimonials
**Email 3 (Hour 22-23):** Final push — Crossroads close (regret vs. action), countdown, shortest email of the sequence

Key pattern: Flash sales always open with a spontaneous justification ("I wasn't planning this, but we have excess inventory"). This prevents the "another sale" feeling and creates authenticity.

### Holiday Sale (5-6 emails, 3-6 days)

**Email 1 (Launch):** Announce + reason-why tied to holiday meaning + full product/pricing breakdown + code
**Email 2 (Day 2):** Social proof momentum — order volume stats, customer messages, "stocking up" behavior, popular combinations with prices
**Email 3 (Mid-campaign):** Gratitude/emotional angle — tied to holiday sentiment, testimonial story, future-pacing ("what if next [holiday] you're writing ME a thank-you?")
**Email 4 (Day before end):** Inventory scarcity — "just got off the phone with fulfillment team, running low on [top product]"
**Email 5 (Final day AM):** Peak urgency — hours countdown, full product listing with prices, crossroads close
**Email 6 (Final day PM, optional):** "This is it" — shortest email, pure countdown + CTA

### Holiday Emotional Beats (Thanksgiving/Black Friday Pattern)

The Thanksgiving sequences add a unique emotional layer. Thanksgiving Day email is deliberately soft-sell ("Today isn't about sales... it's about gratitude") while still including the code. This creates contrast that makes the Black Friday morning hard-sell feel earned. The gratitude → urgency transition mirrors the escalating pressure principle.

### Multi-SKU vs. Single-SKU

When selling multiple products, promotional emails show "most popular combinations" with combined pricing (e.g., "Complete Health Stack: all 3 for $100.40"). This is the email equivalent of the offer section's "value the component parts" technique from [[offer-design]]. Single-SKU promotions focus on supply quantity ("3-month supply," "6-month supply") instead.

### The Same Structure Works Across Product Types

Georgi explicitly notes that supplement promos and tool/coaching promos use the same structural template. "The copy's really not that different. It's the same model — just not for supplements." The structural elements (reason-why → excitement → social proof → scarcity → countdown) transfer directly. The only adaptation is that non-supplement products need more feature/benefit education since the audience may not fully understand what they're buying.

## Email Sequence Type 5: Reorder/Repeat Sequences

### Purpose
For subscription or repeat-purchase products (supplements, consumables), trigger replenishment.

### The Reorder Strategy

**When to send:** Timed to when customer supply likely runs out
- Supplement (30-day supply): Start sequence 7 days before run-out
- Skincare (2-month supply): Send email on day 55
- Subscription: Send email 3 days before next billing cycle

### Real Sample Structure (Blood Sugar Supplement, 4 emails)

The sample reveals a tighter, more urgent cadence than the theory suggests — 4 emails in the final 7 days, with the discount held until the last email.

**Email 1 (Day -7):** You're making progress
- Affirm their consistency ("you're doing something most people never do")
- Explain what's happening inside their body right now (mechanism reminder)
- Soft reorder CTA — just tell them how to reorder
- Testimonial of someone with great results from consistency

**Email 2 (Day -3):** Don't stop just when it's working
- "The biggest mistake I see patients make isn't starting wrong — it's stopping too soon"
- Explain what happens when you stop: reversal of progress (specific to mechanism)
- "Most patients reorder when they have about 3 days left"
- Additional testimonials showing results came in month 2 or 3

**Email 3 (Day -1):** Urgent — "Cellular Transport Breakdown begins within 72 hours"
- Aggressively frames the consequences of lapse
- Pattern: "patient doing great → life gets busy → forgets to reorder → few days becomes weeks → back where they started"
- Still no discount — pure urgency and loss framing

**Email 4 (Day 0):** Final chance + 20% OFF
- "I don't want cost to be a barrier to your continued health"
- Discount code with 12-hour expiry
- This is the ONLY email with a discount — the first three build enough emotional pressure that the discount is a relief, not a bribe

### Key Insight: Hold the Discount

The reorder sequence holds the discount until the final email. This is the opposite of promotional sequences (which lead with value). Reason: the reorder customer already KNOWS the product works. They don't need a discount to believe in it — they need a reminder of what they'll lose. The discount is a final nudge for the price-sensitive, not the primary lever.

## Email Sequence Type 6: Winback Sequences

### Purpose
Recover inactive customers (bought before, haven't purchased in 60+ days).

### Two Real Variants

**Variant 1: Supplement Winback (4 emails, Days 45-52 after last order)**

Uses the Progress → Promise → Loss → Incentive formula with escalating discounts:

**Email 1 (Day 45, 20% off):** "Noticing some changes?" — Calls out the specific symptoms they're probably experiencing since stopping. Uses mechanism language to explain WHY they feel worse ("GLUT4 transporters have started to weaken again"). Introduces 20% discount. Key angle: your body remembers — restart is faster the second time.

**Email 2 (Day 47, 20% off):** "Month 2 is when breakthroughs happen" — Reframes: you stopped right before the magic happened. Multiple testimonials showing month 2/3 results. Same discount code still active. Georgi's note: "This only really works if they bought a one-month supply."

**Email 3 (Day 50, 40% off):** Major discount escalation — "My team thinks I've lost my mind." Explains why they're giving 40% off (they've seen what happens when patients miss their breakthrough month). More aggressive loss framing.

**Email 4 (Day 52, 40% off):** Final hours — Crossroads close. Midnight deadline. "The biggest regret I hear isn't from patients who tried and failed — it's from patients who stopped right before their breakthrough."

**Variant 2: SaaS/Subscription Winback (4 emails, Days 14-21 after cancellation)**

Different psychological approach — attacks the DECISION to cancel rather than the symptoms of stopping.

**Email 1 (Day 14, 25% off 3 months):** "I'm worried you're falling back into old patterns" — Frames cancellation as regression to "Income Gap thinking." Contrasts manual research time vs. tool automation. Social proof of what other users accomplished in the same 2 weeks.

**Email 2 (Day 16, 25% off):** "The manual research trap is designed to keep you broke" — Attacks the belief that saving $97/month is smart. More social proof. Opportunity cost framing.

**Email 3 (Day 19, 25% off):** "Don't become a 'what if' story" — Calls out the pattern of quitting. Stats on what users accomplished while they were gone. Discount expiring soon.

**Email 4 (Day 21, 50% off + bonus):** "Final intervention" — Maximum discount plus bonus. Dimensionalizes the price ("less than two dinners out, one tank of gas, half a cable bill"). "This is my final attempt" framing.

### Georgi on Winback Pricing

"I might even be more aggressive [with discounts]. I don't want to lose them as a customer. The longer I keep them buying, the more likely they are to stay long term, especially if the product's good." For winbacks, the math favors aggressive discounting because the alternative is losing the customer entirely.

### Supplement vs. SaaS Winback Psychology

The core difference: supplement winbacks attack the BODY ("you're feeling worse"), while SaaS winbacks attack the MINDSET ("you're falling back into old patterns"). Supplement customers need to feel the physical consequences. SaaS/info customers need to feel the identity threat of being someone who quits.

## Email Copy Principles (All Sequences)

### Specificity over vagueness
**Bad:** "Amazing results"
**Good:** "Visible skin improvement by day 14"

### Action-oriented language
**Bad:** "We hope this helps"
**Good:** "Click below and place your order"

### Short paragraphs (3-4 lines max)
Email is skimmed, not read. Every paragraph should fit on mobile screen.

### Clear CTA (repeated)
Don't assume one CTA link is enough. If the email is 200 words, have 2-3 CTA links/buttons. Real samples typically include 2-3 CTA links per email, with the primary CTA appearing both mid-email and at the end.

### Tone consistency
Match the tone of your brand/initial copy. If VSL was conversational, emails are conversational.

### P.S. as second CTA
Every sample email includes a P.S. — usually either a secondary urgency reminder, a customer support contact (for people with unresolved objections), or a testimonial. The P.S. is never wasted space.

### "From" Line as Character
Supplement emails come "From: Sarah at VitalBurn Pro" (founder character). Coaching emails come "From: Marcus 'Mack' Hayes" (personal brand). The "from" line establishes the relationship type: personal guide vs. expert authority.

### SMS Supplement: Refill Promos
For existing customers, SMS can supplement email for refill promotions. Pattern from real sample (GetThin weight loss medication): ALL CAPS emphasis for key terms, dollar savings in first line, "FIRST TIME EVER" / "NEVER happened before" framing, 3-send cadence over 3 days (morning → evening → final afternoon), always includes phone number for immediate call. SMS copy is dramatically shorter — 2-3 sentences max — but uses the same reason-why and urgency principles as email.

## Common Mistakes

**One sequence for all customers.** Welcome sequence for new buyers should be different from reorder email for repeat customers.

**Too many emails too fast.** Respect inbox. 3-4 emails in first week is enough. Space them 2-3 days apart.

**Forgetting CTAs.** Every email should have a clear call-to-action. CTA should appear at least twice.

**Fake urgency in welcome sequences.** "Sale ends tonight" in a thank-you email is jarring. Save urgency for promotional sequences.

**Not personalizing based on customer history.** Winback email shouldn't look like welcome email. Acknowledge that they've been a customer.

**Generic email copy.** Pull details from brief and research. Specific language converts better than template language.
