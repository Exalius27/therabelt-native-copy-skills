## Core Thesis

If you can spend more than your competition to get a customer, you will get more customers than them. Customer Financed Acquisition (CFA) means your customers pay for acquiring new customers — your 30-day gross profit exceeds your cost to acquire them. No outside capital needed. You grow on your own terms, forever.

**The CFA equation:**
```
30 Day Gross Profit > CAC
```

Good CFA: 30 Day GP > CAC (break even in 30 days)
Great CFA: 30 Day GP > 2x CAC (every customer pays for two new ones)

At 2x, you can reinvest half of GP into acquiring more customers, growing the business as fast as it can handle.

## The Three Levers

### Lever 1: Lower CAC (Cost to Acquire a Customer)

CAC = Total costs of advertising + sales + supporting activities / Number of customers acquired

Most entrepreneurs undercount CAC. They report ad spend only, ignoring:
- Sales team salaries and commissions
- Content team payroll
- Software and tools
- Management time spent on acquisition activities

Calculate true CAC monthly, by channel. If you don't know your real CAC, you're flying blind.

**How to lower it:**
- Better ads (higher CTR = more visitors per dollar)
- Better conversion (landing page optimization, friction reduction)
- Better targeting (fewer wasted impressions)
- Recovery channels (abandoned cart, decline recovery — near-zero incremental CAC)

### Lever 2: Increase LTGP (Lifetime Gross Profit)

LTGP = Total revenue from a customer over their lifetime - COGS

The higher a customer's worth, the more you can spend to acquire them. The gap between Gym Launch competitors ($6,000-$8,000 LTGP) and Gym Launch ($45,000+ LTGP) is only 6-8x on paper — but the downstream profits are "breathtakingly different."

**How to increase it:**
- Premium pricing (tested, not assumed — see [[funnel-economics]])
- Continuity/subscription models
- Upsell and cross-sell architecture
- Customer retention and stick rate optimization
- Advanced offer stacking (see below)

### Lever 3: Decrease PPD (Payback Period)

PPD = Time until GP from a customer exceeds CAC

A business that breaks even in 30 days can grow 30x faster than one that breaks even in 3 years — same math, radically different cash flow.

**How to decrease it:**
- Front-load revenue (collect more upfront vs. spread over time)
- Add immediate upsells on day zero
- Use continuity pricing that starts immediately
- Reduce COGS on early deliverables

## Advanced Offer Stacking (The Value Grid)

Beyond single offers, Hormozi lays out a grid of offer types that create layered revenue:

### Front-End (Attraction Offers)

**Free Presentations** — Long-form content (webinars, workshops) that align three core beliefs: the problem is real, the solution exists, and you are the one to deliver it. Higher belief alignment = higher conversion.

**Freemium** — Give away a version so valuable people come to depend on it, then monetize through upgrades. Only works with near-100% incremental margins (software, media). Extremely dangerous without capital — most fail by giving away too much or having costs that exceed paid-user revenue. Not recommended for first-time operators.

**Free Pick Your Price** — Present tiers where $0 gets basic access, higher prices get more support/access. Eliminates the no-show problem and gets a card on file. Works when the upsell is strong enough to make the free tier profitable in aggregate.

**Free With Alternate Revenue Stream** — Give away thing A for free, monetize thing B.
- Paired: "Free agency services as long as you use our software" (monetize the software)
- Independent: "Free book, upsell shipping + course + coaching"

### Back-End (Continuity Offers)

**Lifetime Upgrades** — Continuous improvement to existing products/services as a retention mechanism. Keep adding value so customers never want to leave.

**Lifetime Discounts** — Lock in a lower rate for continuity. The discount is the cost of acquiring guaranteed ongoing revenue.

**Discount + One-Time Fee** — Combine an upfront payment with reduced ongoing pricing. Front-loads cash while maintaining continuity.

## The Performance Diamond (Managing Lead-Getting Employees)

When an employee underperforms at advertising/outreach, diagnose with four variables:

1. **Communication** — They don't know what you want them to do. Fix with clear expectations.
2. **Training** — They don't know how to do it. Fix with patient instruction and fast feedback.
3. **Motivation** — They don't want to do it. Three sub-causes: wrong reward structure, aversion to environment/work/people, or outside life events. Ask questions before assuming.
4. **Circumstances** — Something is literally stopping them ("We're out of beef." / "My internet is too slow."). Often the easiest to fix, but you have to ask.

**The diamond question:** "Over the last [time period] your [task performance] has changed from the norm. What do you think has gotten in the way and how can I help?"

## Maker vs. Manager (Time Management)

**Managers** divide time into 30-60 minute blocks. Empty slots = lost opportunity. Meetings make them productive.

**Makers** need half-day or full-day blocks. A single 30-minute meeting breaks a morning into two unusable chunks. The Zeigarnik effect (remembering open loops) eats attention hours before a scheduled meeting. Meetings destroy maker productivity.

**The cost asymmetry:** A meeting costs a manager one work unit. It costs a maker ten work units.

**Solutions:**
- Managers: understand the 10x cost before requesting a meeting. Respect a maker's "no."
- Makers: batch meetings into designated blocks (afternoons). Schedule back-to-front. Set expectations for slow responses during maker blocks.
- Organizations: mandate "quiet" chunks where teams cannot message or meet.

## Employee ROI Math

Total Payroll / Total Engaged Leads = Cost per engaged lead.

If one in 10 engaged leads becomes a customer, and LTGP is $4,000 with $1,000 CAC, that is a 4:1 return.

If CAC is within 3x industry average, you're doing well — focus on increasing LTGP. If CAC is over 3x, diagnose: are engaged leads qualified (advertising problem) or qualified but not buying (sales problem)? Don't fire your sales team for an advertising problem, or vice versa.
