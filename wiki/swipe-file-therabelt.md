## Ad 1: Batch #17 Relaunch — "Glenn" (Avatar 2)

**Hook:** Opens mid-scene in domestic routine. Narrator describes invisible labor — managing the household, the dog, everything. "He doesn't notice that I'm the one who did all of it. Nobody ever does. Nobody ever has."

**Antagonist:** Glenn (husband). Not cruel. Just absent. Never acknowledged what she does. The antagonism is neglect, not malice. This makes it more universal — most women recognize this dynamic.

**Narrator:** Retired empty nester. Dog (Rosie, 10) is her primary companion. Classic [[avatar-2]] profile.

**Discovery:** Through Peg at the farm stand. Peg's daughter-in-law (a nurse who reads research) found it first. Triple credibility chain: friend > nurse > research.

**Key transformation scenes:**
- Rosie gets up on her own (first sign)
- Nails on the kitchen floor — Rosie standing behind narrator, tail going, ready to go out
- Garden center woman: "There is no way this dog is ten"
- Vet: "I would not have guessed this dog is ten. Her coat, her mobility, her bloodwork."
- Narrator sits in parking lot after — same vet who said "this is normal" now can't explain what she's seeing

**Identity thread:** Invisible caretaker. Does everything. Nobody sees it. The dog's transformation makes her effort visible for the first time.

**Recognition moment (emotional peak):** Glenn sits on the floor next to her while she brushes Rosie. "You do everything around here. You always have. With the kids. With her. And I never once said anything." He holds on. "I'm sorry it took me this long."

**P.S.:** "Glenn gets up before me on Sundays now. First time in forty years he's been up first. He warms Rosie's food and he lets me sleep." Concrete behavioral reversal proving the recognition was real.

**Craft notes:** This ad has the most complete identity-challenge-to-recognition arc. The P.S. delivers the behavioral proof that the recognition wasn't a one-time moment. Glenn's character is built through small beats throughout (the quick arm on the way to the garage, "You're back already?").

## Ad 2: Batch #1 — "Karen Dinner" (Avatar 1)

**Hook:** "My friend humiliated me in front of six women for loving my dog too much. I didn't argue. I didn't explain. I went home that night and made sure that dog became impossible to ignore."

**Antagonist:** Karen. Public humiliation at a dinner party. "Does he have his own Google calendar too?" Gets the laugh. Warm delivery masking cruelty — "the way you say something mean when you want everyone to know you're just joking."

**Narrator:** Family dog mom. Rocky is 7. Kids older but still connect through the dog. Husband good but doesn't notice things. Classic [[avatar-1]].

**Discovery:** Through narrator's sister (competitive agility dog trainer). "Forgotten more about canine performance than most vets know." Single highest-credibility friend character in the collection.

**Key transformation scenes:**
- Daughter home from college: "Mom. What happened to him. He looks incredible. He looks like he did two years ago."
- Husband (who never notices anything) sets coffee down: "Has he been to the vet recently? He looks different."
- Son on the floor with Rocky: "Mom he moves like a puppy."
- Karen grabs Rocky's face at the reunion dinner, pulls hands back, looks at them — "like they'd told her something she wasn't ready to hear"
- Diane at the table: "I have been watching this dog all night"

**Confrontation:** Same dinner. Same table. Same six women. One year later. Karen watches Diane order TheraBelt at the table without looking up. Karen: "Send me the link." Narrator: "They're almost out of this batch. Price goes up when they restock."

**Close:** "Karen figured it out two days after that dinner. The woman who made a table laugh at me for walking my dog and taking him to the vet. Paid full price to do exactly what I had already done."

**Craft notes:** Purest [[luhxe-ad-craft]] proving-wrong-without-speaking architecture. The narrator never argues, never explains, never gloats. The product does all the talking. Karen's own hands tell her the truth. The antagonist ends up paying full price — the price objection is overcome by the antagonist's own urgency.

## Ad 3: Batch #2 — "Vanessa at the Park" (Avatar 1)

**Hook:** "Every morning I walk my dog in the same park at the same time and every morning I run into the same woman. Vanessa. She has a Golden Lab too. About the same age as mine."

**Antagonist:** Vanessa. Not mean — "She just exists in a way that makes you feel like you're somehow behind on something you didn't know you were supposed to be doing." Cold water therapy, integrative vet in Scottsdale, groomer with six-month waitlist. Always delivered with "this little smile."

**Discovery:** Through friend Rae, whose dog showed up looking visibly different. No long explanation. "She said it the way you say something that has just become part of the morning."

**Key transformation scenes:**
- Dog running full sprint across the park field — people on benches watching
- Vanessa's friend asks narrator for advice (status reversal)
- Random vet walks over from a bench: "I'm a vet and I didn't even know you could get that for home use. We have the same thing at the clinic."

**Confrontation:** Vanessa watches her own friend ask narrator for advice. Watches a vet get up from a bench for narrator's dog. Then asks: "So what is it?"

**Close:** "Vanessa ordered hers two weeks after the park. I know because she mentioned it, the way she mentions everything, like she'd found it herself."

**Craft notes:** The vet-from-a-bench scene is a strong third-party validation move — an unsolicited professional endorsement in a public setting. Vanessa's character is built entirely through behavioral description, never through the narrator calling her mean. The close ("like she'd found it herself") is a precise character beat.

## Ad 4: Batch #3 — "Dog Sitter / Cooper" (Outsider Narrator)

**Hook:** "I've been sitting dogs for almost six years. Most weeks I've got four or five going at once."

**Antagonist:** No human antagonist. The antagonist is "the math" — Cooper (10-year-old Aussie) vs. Cal (narrator's 7-year-old Aussie, same breed). The math is wrong and it's irritating the narrator.

**Narrator:** Professional dog sitter (not a dog mom). This is the only ad in the collection with an outsider narrator. She has professional credibility — she sees patterns across hundreds of dogs. "I notice before the owners do. Every time."

**Discovery:** Through Catherine (Cooper's owner). Catherine does red light therapy on herself, thought "why am I not doing this for Cooper too." The belt was sitting in Catherine's kitchen for months — narrator had seen it and never thought to ask.

**Key transformation scenes:**
- Cal gets to the door before narrator, tail going in full circles, "that whole back end moves" — behavior he hadn't done in a long time
- Sister runs hand along Cal's back: "What's going on with him. His coat looks different."

**Close:** "Catherine saw something I didn't. She saw that the way it's always been doesn't have to be the way it is."

**Craft notes:** The outsider narrator is a distinct structural choice. She has no emotional blindness — she sees dogs objectively, professionally. When even she can't explain what she's seeing with Cooper, it's more credible than a loving owner's observation. The "same breed, three years younger, mine looks worse" comparison is a powerful implicit demonstration. No family identity thread — this ad trades the caretaker emotional engine for professional-observer credibility.

## Ad 5: Batch #6 — "Pet Retail Insider / Jennifer" (Industry Exposé)

**Hook:** "I spent 9 years helping the pet industry take money from women like you. Then I watched time run out on my own dog."

**Antagonist:** The supplement industry / "the supplement wall." Floor to ceiling. Every brand. Every formula. $100-150/month. "The dogs weren't getting better." The systemic villain: the whole industry was selling hope while the real solution sat behind clinic doors at $200/session.

**Narrator:** Jennifer, 47, former pet retail worker. 9 years on the inside. Insider credibility — she sold the supplements, she watched the pattern. Now she's out and telling the truth.

**Discovery:** Self-discovered. Late-night searching after lying awake next to Duke. Found videos of 14-15-16 year old dogs running in videos. Then found TheraBelt.

**Key transformation scenes:**
- Duke at the door waiting for the long walk. Two miles. The one he'd stopped asking for.
- Neighbor Linda: "He looks like the Duke from two years ago"
- Vet bloodwork: "These do not look like a 9 year old dog. They look like a 6 year old dog."

**Identity thread (unique):** Not caretaker-identity. Insider-guilt. "Nine years I stood behind that counter. Watching women spend $150 a month on the wall while the thing that was actually changing dogs sat behind clinic doors at $200 a session. I wore the name tag. I stayed silent."

**Close:** "The women in that group are done being silent." Direct pivot from her own silence to collective action.

**Craft notes:** This is the only ad with a systemic antagonist rather than a personal one. The "Paris Hilton's dogs live to 25. It is not genetics. It is not luck. It is that someone told her this exists" line is a powerful class-access reframe. The hook mirrors Luhxe Ad 1 ("I spent 11 years helping airlines take money from women like my mother") — same insider-exposé architecture. The supplement wall is a concrete, visual antagonist that every dog owner has stood in front of.

## Ad 6: Batch #18 — "Patricia" (Social Betrayal)

**Hook:** "I have loved this dog for twelve years and I have never once wondered if I was doing enough. That was before Dorothy called me on a Friday night."

**Antagonist:** Patricia (behind-the-back group chat gossip). Eleven messages over three weeks. "Sometimes the ones who love the most are the last ones to know." Then Dorothy (the messenger) twists the knife: "I mean, look, she might be right though."

**Narrator:** Dog mom, 12 years with Molly. Husband, adult kids. Same invisible-caretaker identity as other ads but the wound is deeper — it's not one comment at dinner, it's systematic group betrayal.

**Discovery:** Through her daughter (veterinary rehabilitation, 9 years). Professional credibility within the family.

**Key transformation scenes:**
- The pause disappearing: "She used to stop before she stood. Every single morning, this small hesitation, her body thinking it over. One morning it was just gone."
- Daughter: "Mom. She looks like she did three years ago." Films Molly, sends to brother. He calls: "What are you doing to that dog."
- Clover Park confrontation: Molly unclipped, running with "that big swinging trot that is completely hers." Ran past Chester (Patricia's dog) without breaking stride. A woman on a bench stood up.

**Unique move — product withheld from antagonist:** Patricia texts asking for the link. Narrator types "sorry, I don't remember what it's called" and puts phone face down. This is the only ad where the narrator actively denies the antagonist access. Every other ad has the antagonist paying full price. This one has the narrator choosing not to share.

**Close:** "Patricia found out four days after she watched Molly run past Chester without breaking stride. Dorothy found out the week after that."

**Craft notes:** The group chat messages are the most visceral antagonist setup in the collection. Reading someone's words about you — with emojis, with "lol," with fake concern — hits differently than a face-to-face insult. The withholding move at the end is emotionally satisfying in a way that paying full price isn't. It's the narrator's one act of agency.

## Structural Patterns Across All Six Ads

### The Demonstration Stack
Every ad uses the same multi-angle proof architecture from [[story-native-format]]:
1. **Dog behavior change** (narrator observes privately)
2. **Casual observer** (neighbor, friend — "what happened to her coat")
3. **Family member** (daughter, husband, son — "she looks like she did X years ago")
4. **Professional authority** (vet bloodwork — "these numbers don't match this age")
5. **Social ripple** (Facebook group, "Superdog moms," 2,000 women, dogs looking impossible)

This stacking order is consistent: private > casual > family > professional > social. Each layer is more credible than the last.

### The "Noted It. Said Nothing." Beat
Five of six ads include the exact phrase "I noted it. Said nothing." This beat appears after family members start commenting on the dog. It marks the narrator's restraint — she has been doing invisible work for years, and now the proof is undeniable, and she chooses not to explain.

### The "Superdog Moms" Social Proof Block
All six ads include a near-identical social proof section: Facebook post > phone doesn't stop > women with senior dogs messaging > updates start coming in (Carol's lab on stairs, Diane's/Patty's golden running, etc.). This is a modular block — same function in every ad, slightly different names, always placed after the personal transformation and before the close.

### Antagonist Taxonomy
1. **Personal humiliation** (Karen, Patricia) — public or behind-the-back social wound
2. **Quiet superiority** (Vanessa) — never says anything mean, just makes you feel behind
3. **Domestic neglect** (Glenn) — not cruel, just absent from the work
4. **Systemic** (supplement industry) — the whole system is the villain
5. **Mathematical** (Cooper vs Cal) — no human antagonist, just the numbers being wrong

### CTA Architecture
All six close with "Your move." Five include batch scarcity ("price goes up when they come back"). The Patricia ad is the only one that closes on the antagonist finding out rather than on scarcity.

### Identity Thread Variations
- **Invisible caretaker** (Batches #17, #1, #2, #18) — she does everything, nobody sees it, the dog's transformation makes it visible
- **Insider guilt** (Batch #6) — she was part of the system, now she's out, telling the truth
- **Professional observer** (Batch #3) — no identity thread; replaced by professional credibility

The invisible caretaker thread is the dominant emotional engine. It appears in 4 of 6 ads. The key line that anchors it: "That was just what I did. That was just mom. That was just how things ran."

### Batch #16: Mass Desires — Five Variations (Part 2)

All five implement the full identity-invisibility-recognition framework (see [[lucavel-ad-craft]]). Each targets a different mass desire. All feature husband recognition scene with physical affection and a P.S. showing concrete behavioral change.

**MD 1 — "I want more time with my dog"**
Hook: "I Googled 'how long do golden retrievers live' and I wish I hadn't."
Bear is 10. Narrator counts: "10 to 12 years. Maybe a thousand mornings." Linda (3 streets over) has been using TheraBelt on her golden Rosie. Results cascade. Husband's recognition: holds her, voice breaks, "You've done so much for us." P.S.: Husband starts writing in the notebook.

**MD 2 — "I don't want to worry about my dog all the time"**
Hook: "My friend lost her dog two years ago. Last week she told me mine has about eight months."
Diane (nurse, lost Penny at 13) tells narrator Bear's shuffle matches Penny's timeline. "Eight months." Narrator starts counting breaths. Diane's neighbor Duke went from "barely getting up off the porch" to strangers thinking he was a puppy. Vet: "If someone walked in here and told me this dog was five, I would believe them." Husband recognition on the couch: "You just take care of everything and you never ask anyone to see it." P.S.: Bill's handwriting appears in the notebook: "Bear walked the full block. Ate everything. Good day."

**MD 3 — "I don't want to regret not spending enough time with them"**
Hook: "Every morning for nine years I've walked Charlie around our neighborhood. Last week the whole walk fit between our front door and the mailbox."
Walk shrinking from 40 minutes to the mailbox. Donna (retired nurse, chocolate lab Max) introduces TheraBelt — Max was the same, now people can't believe he's 10. Charlie walks the route again. Ray asks to join the walk for the first time in nine years: "I want to be here for this." P.S.: Ray sets alarm for 5:45, holding the leash when she comes downstairs.

**MD 4 — "I want my dog to move like she used to"**
Hook: "My dog is ten. Yesterday I watched a three-year-old lab run through my house doing everything she used to do."
Rosie (golden, 10) can't get up to greet Diane's young lab Clementine. Neighbor Pat found TheraBelt. Rosie transforms. Ray walks through kitchen, steps past everything she does. One morning Rosie trots through kitchen "like she's five years old and just outran something." Ray finally sees: holds her face with both hands, kisses her. "Show me what to do. I want to help." P.S.: Ray makes Rosie's breakfast every Sunday. "Doesn't get it quite right. But he's there."

**MD 5 — "I want my dog to be playful again"**
Hook: "My dog used to beat me to the door every time I grabbed my keys. Now she's old and just watches me leave."
Source file cuts off mid-copy. Same structural setup — Rosie (golden, 10), Glenn in kitchen, invisible morning routine.
