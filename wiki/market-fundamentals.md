## Core Thesis

Five forces determine whether an ad succeeds or fails: Desire, Awareness, Sophistication, Identification, and Belief. These are not independent levers — they form a causal chain. Desire determines *what* you say. Awareness determines *how* you say it. Sophistication determines *how different* you must be. Identification determines *how you show it visually*. Belief determines *how you develop and prove it*. Master all five, and you can diagnose any underperforming ad. This is Evolve's operational application of Schwartz's Breakthrough Advertising framework to e-commerce advertising.

---

## 1. Desire — The Force You Cannot Create

You cannot create desire. It exists in the market. Your only job is to find it and channel it. The strength of the desire you tap into determines the scalability of your business.

### Two types of desire in the market

**Permanent desires:** Health, wealth, relationships. These never disappear. The strongest businesses tap into all three (e.g., the iPhone touches all three, which is why Apple is a trillion-dollar company).

**Trending desires:** Crypto, AI, Valentine's Day. These come and go. They drive short-term gains but are unreliable for long-term scaling.

### Desire strength = intensity x scope

- **Intensity:** Minor headache vs. severe migraine. The more urgent the desire, the more powerful your tailwind.
- **Scope:** How many people share this desire. "Toned calves" vs. "healthy physique" — the broader desire gives you a bigger market.

Strong desire is a tailwind — your ad goes further with less effort. Weak or no desire is a headwind — your ad stalls no matter how good it is.

### The desire spectrum in practice

A real example: a client selling skate cut-resistant gear was doing $13-15K/day. When a hockey player died from a skate cut, desire exploded — the client did $200K+ in a single day with *zero changes to their ads*. The only thing that changed was the intensity and scope of desire in the market. This is the raw power of desire.

### Physical vs. functional product

Every product has two versions:
- **Physical:** Carbon fiber, wires, motherboard — the tangible components. These justify quality and provide evidence.
- **Functional:** Shiny, smooth, celebrity-style hair — the outcome the customer actually buys.

Most customers buy the functional version. Your messaging should lead with functional benefits. Physical features support and prove the functional promise.

### Finding mass desire

- Research: Look for "I was looking for X" and "I needed Y" statements.
- Test desires in ad creatives — different desires produce dramatically different results. Example: "Find your style" ad = total failure. "I want to look wealthy" ad = $20K+ spend winner. Same product, different desire.

**Connection:** Desire selection is the first variable in the [[desire-testing]] framework. It is also the starting input for [[angles-belief-shifts]] — the angle is downstream of the desire you choose.

## 2. Market Awareness — The Five Stages

Market awareness is the level of understanding your customer has about their problem, the solutions, and your product. It determines how you structure your ad — what you can assume they know and what you must educate them on.

### The five stages (cold to hot)

| Stage | Definition | Goal | Action |
|-------|-----------|------|--------|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have a problem. Mild discomfort. | Educate that a problem exists | Lead with symptoms; never mention the product |
| **Problem-aware** | Knows they have a problem, doesn't know what to do about it | Name the problem, build urgency | Lead with the problem, agitate it, hint at solution category |
| **Solution-aware** | Knows solutions exist, hasn't chosen one | Show why your *solution type* is best | Compare solution categories (slideboard vs. Peloton), not products |
| **Product-aware** | Knows your product exists, isn't convinced | Show why *your product* specifically is best | Call out benefits, features, future-pacing, social proof |
| **Most-aware** | Knows your product and what it does, just hasn't bought | Make the offer irresistible | Reduce risk, make it easy, great offer, get out of the way |

### Critical distinction: solution-aware vs. product-aware

Solution-aware ads compare *solution types* (our slideboard vs. a Peloton). Product-aware ads compare *products within the same type* (our humidifier vs. their humidifier). Changing what you compare against lets you climb or descend awareness levels strategically.

### Facebook knows awareness levels

Facebook's algorithm predicts who is ready to buy and who isn't. Depending on your full-funnel activity, different awareness-level ads get served to the appropriate segments automatically.

**Connection:** Awareness levels map directly to the [[meta-ads-strategy]] "sell the click vs. sell the solution" framework. Lower awareness = longer education path = longer destination asset.

## 3. Market Sophistication — How Saturated Is the Conversation

Market sophistication is how many products, solutions, and claims the market has already been exposed to. It determines how different you need to be. The longer a market exists and the more competitors enter, the higher the sophistication and the more skepticism your prospect carries.

### The five stages

| Stage | State of Market | Strategy | Example |
|-------|----------------|----------|---------|
| **Stage 1** | Brand new category, no competition | Simple, direct claim — just state what it does | BlackBerry: "A two-way pager that can receive email" |
| **Stage 2** | Early competition, few similar products | Enlarge the claim — make it bigger and bolder | Nokia: "Connecting people" |
| **Stage 3** | Market has heard the claims before | Introduce a new mechanism — same promise, new way to deliver it | Apple iPhone: touchscreen = new mechanism for connecting |
| **Stage 4** | Market knows the mechanism | Enlarge the mechanism — make it faster, easier, better | iPhone 3G: "Twice as fast, half the price" |
| **Stage 5** | Market has heard it all, doesn't believe claims | Identification marketing — sell the persona, not the product | Nike "Find Your Greatness," Rolex Everest commercials |

### The cycle repeats

At Stage 5, the best brands loop back to Stage 3 by introducing another new mechanism. Or they go all the way back to Stage 1 with an entirely new product.

### How to determine your sophistication level

1. Analyze your product's unique mechanisms and the problems it solves
2. Identify all competitors selling similar products AND all other solutions to the same problem
3. Create a Foreplay/Atria board of all competitor ads — visually see what the customer sees
4. Find which claims are overdone (the "same t-shirt" problem) and where you can be the "black t-shirt" that stands out

Real example: A hair dryer client's messaging was "fast hair drying without heat damage." Research revealed seven competitors saying the exact same thing. That's why the ads failed — the messaging was Stage 2 (enlarge the claim) in a Stage 4 market.

**Connection:** Market sophistication directly informs the [[insight-vectors]] process — you need a new mechanism (Stage 3) or enlarged mechanism (Stage 4) to break through. Also connects to [[unique-mechanisms]] for how to generate those mechanisms.

## 4. Identification — Selling the Role, Not the Object

Identity marketing is about showcasing who your product helps the customer *become*. People don't just buy objects — they buy roles. The identity the customer wishes to embody determines the *visuals* of your ad (environment, model, styling).

### Core principle: unspoken communication

The key word is "unspokenly." A woman buys low-calorie food not just for health — she's projecting an image of attractiveness and youthfulness. A man buys a sports car not for transportation — he's projecting prestige and success. Neither would say this out loud. Your ad must communicate the identity without stating it directly.

### Four techniques for building identification

1. **The Yes Train:** Ask a series of questions the avatar would answer "yes" to. Each "yes" puts them deeper in the bucket of "this is for me." By the time you introduce the product, your recommendation carries weight because you've proven you understand them.

2. **Symptoms / Shared Experience:** Go into granular detail about the specific pain points and daily experiences of your customer. Not "our socks don't rip" but "every time I'd put on my sock, I debated whether or not to even wear them — that's if they didn't stink out the entire locker room." When the detail is precise enough, the reader thinks "this person gets it."

3. **Authority / Belief Challenge:** Call out an existing belief directly and explain why it's false — but only if you have strong evidence and genuine authority. Acknowledge the current belief first ("Forget everything you've ever heard about what age must do to your appearance"), then present overwhelming proof. Without authority, this backfires.

4. **Logic Chain:** Build an airtight logical sequence where each statement flows inevitably into the next. "A hair dryer works by blowing heated air. Hotter air = faster drying. But excessive heat damages hair molecules. Therefore, you need the right temperature. Our product auto-cycles temperature based on hundreds of thousands of tests." Each step is undeniable, so the conclusion feels inevitable.

**Connection:** Identification is the foundation of [[avatar-system]] — avatar profiles define who the customer wants to be. It also connects to the "undeniable demonstration" principle in applied learning — showing the identity transformation rather than claiming it.

## 5. Belief — The Filter Everything Must Pass Through

Belief is how your customers see reality. Every prospect has a mental picture of the world, and your product must fit through that filter. If it violates their established beliefs, no amount of desire, awareness strategy, or identification will save the ad.

### The three belief rules

**Rule 1: You cannot challenge a person's core beliefs.** If you violate your ideal customer's established beliefs in the slightest, nothing you promise can save your ad. The Kepler-452b test: if someone told you that you were born on another planet, you'd scroll past no matter what evidence they offered. That's what happens when ads challenge beliefs.

**Rule 2: Too many claims destroy belief.** A supplement that promises to help with sleep, focus, joint pain, back pain, energy, anxiety, gut health, wrinkles, immune support, mood, stress, and memory — even if all are true — is unbelievable. One justified promise, built with depth, is infinitely more powerful than twelve unsupported claims. (See [[persuasion]] on single-belief architecture.)

**Rule 3: If there is zero belief, there are zero sales.** A newspaper ad offering $100 to anyone who mailed in a clipping produced zero responses — it was too good to be true. Channeling belief can be more powerful than the strongest desire.

### How the five forces work together

- **Desire** determines the *content* of your ad (what you talk about)
- **Identification** determines the *visuals* of your ad (who you show, what environment)
- **Belief** determines the *development* of your ad (how you build and prove your case)

The customer must have conviction that the desire, identity, and outcome you're promising will actually come true through your product. Without belief, nothing converts.

### Building belief: the four techniques

1. **Yes Train** — Show the customer you're talking about THEM (agreement)
2. **Symptoms** — Prove you've been there yourself with granular detail (shared experience)
3. **Authority** — Challenge false beliefs with overwhelming evidence (credibility)
4. **Logic Chain** — Prove your product works through inevitable logical reasoning (irrefutable argument)

**Connection:** Belief architecture connects directly to [[persuasion]] (reason-why framework, pre-commitment), [[angles-belief-shifts]] (angle = belief-shift mechanism), and [[direct-response-copywriting]] (one belief + 10 questions method from Albuquerque).

## Diagnostic Application

When an ad fails, diagnose using this sequence:

1. **Is there enough desire?** If the market doesn't want what you're selling, nothing else matters.
2. **Are you at the right awareness level?** A product-aware ad shown to problem-aware prospects will miss. A most-aware ad shown to unaware prospects will confuse.
3. **Is the market too sophisticated for your approach?** If seven competitors say the same claim, you need a new mechanism, not a louder claim.
4. **Does the visual sell the right identity?** If the identity in the ad doesn't match who the customer wants to become, they scroll.
5. **Does the customer believe you?** If your claims exceed your proof, or you violate their existing beliefs, they won't buy no matter how badly they want the outcome.

This diagnostic chain maps directly to the [[desire-testing]] troubleshooting SOP — check the desire first, then escalate through the other forces.
