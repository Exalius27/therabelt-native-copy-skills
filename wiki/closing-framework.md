## Core Thesis

Closing is not about getting the prospect to buy. Closing gives the prospect enough power to overcome decision avoidance. People on the fence lack decision-making power — they waffle. The goal is to return that power to them so they can make a genuine decision, yes or no. Both outcomes are wins. Letting someone stay on the avoidance treadmill is the only loss.

Advertising splits audiences into three buckets: YES (laydowns who buy no matter what), NO (unqualified), and MAYBE (on the fence). Most businesses only capture bucket one. Closing captures bucket three — the massive middle. Sales teams routinely double close rates with effective closing training.

**"Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust."** Belief and trust are continuums, not binaries.

## The Onion of Blame

People avoid decisions by blaming things that look reasonable but don't hold up. All objections fall into three layers:

**Layer 1 — External Circumstances:** "I must get what I want without obstacles, or else the circumstances are to blame."
- Time: "I'm too busy" / "I don't have time"
- Money: "Too expensive" / "Can't afford it"
- Fit: "Not right for me" / "I need something different"

**Layer 2 — Other People:** "I must be treated fairly, or else other people are to blame."
- Authority: "I need to check with my spouse/partner"
- Trust: "I've been burned before" / "Let me think about it"

**Layer 3 — Self:** "I must be perfect, or my character is to blame."
- Fear: "It might work for them but not me"
- Avoidance: "I need to think about it" / "Send me a brochure"

People often present multiple layers. Peel one back, another appears. This is expected and means you are closing correctly. Keep going until they can make a genuine decision.

## 28 Rules of Closing

The most important operating principles:

1. **When in doubt, repeat back what they said.** Buys time, builds rapport.
2. **Acknowledge or agree, never disagree.** The moment you disagree, hostility rises. "Totally understand" / "That's a great point" / "You're totally right, I forgot to mention..."
3. **Before getting real, get permission.** "Can I be a coach instead of a friend for a second?"
4. **Stack closes.** If one works, two work better. Hit it from multiple angles.
5. **Understand the logic, don't memorize scripts.** When you understand the reasoning, you can use them in your own words.
6. **You only need 2-3 closes per obstacle.** Not an encyclopedia. Master a few.
7. **Use nudges on the edge.** One-liners that tip deals over.
8. **Yellows are golds.** Practice on unqualified leads — bigger challenge, better practice.
9. **Volume negates luck.** Do so many sales it's unreasonable you aren't world class.
10. **Don't assume objections mean they won't buy.** "It's expensive" is often an observation, not an objection. Let them process out loud.
11. **Never change price on the spot.** Ethically questionable, invites bullying.
12. **If you're always in a battle, fix what happens before the call.**
13. **Only ask for the sale when you think you've got them.**
14. **People want to believe you.** Help their brains justify the decision they already want to make.
15. **Selling happens before the ask. Closing happens after.**
16. **Handle problems before you ask for money** — easier than after.
17. **Expect and plan for no.**
18. **Price shock isn't a no.** Unless they say there's a problem, they're processing.
19. **The person who cares the most about the prospect wins the sale.**
20. **Seek to understand, not to argue.**
21. **Closers ask hard questions because they genuinely care.** Kind, not nice.
22. **Say offensive things about someone like them, not about them.**
23. **Once they say yes, shut up.** Don't sell yourself out of the sale.
24. **Record all sales and review them.** Game tape.

## All-Purpose Closes (The 80/20)

These work on any objection that is not yes:

**"What's Your Main Concern?"** — Surface the real obstacle. If they give fluff: "What are you afraid of happening?" If still stuck, spell out the absurd worst case.

**"The Reason" Close** — "The reason you're telling yourself not to do this is the reason you should do it." The chain holding you back is the chain you need to break. Works for money ("How long do you want 'I can't afford this' to be the headline of your life?"), time, authority, avoidance.

**"The Hypothetical" Close** — "If this were perfect, would you do it? Then what's the difference between perfect and what we've got?" If they can't name anything: "It sounds like it's nothing to do with the program — it's something else. What are you afraid of?"

**"The Zoom Out" Close** — "Let's zoom out. You want X. We sell X. Do you think overanalyzing details might be the reason you haven't made it happen yet?"

**"1 to 10" Close** — "On a scale of 1 to 10, where are you?" If 9 or less: "What would it take to get you to a 10?"

**"Best Case Worst Case" Close** — Three variants. All contrast risk-free options where only walking away guarantees not getting the result.

## Closes by Blame Type

### When They Blame Time
- **"Better to start when busy"** — If you can't make it work during busy seasons, it won't last. What better time than when you need it most?
- **"You're gonna get busy again"** — Even if you wait, you'll stop again the moment life gets busy. Learn to make it work now = permanent.
- **"Priorities, not timing"** — Either your goal is more important than the interruptions, or it's not.
- **"The When/Then"** — "I'll do it when I have time" = "I'll go to the doctor when I'm healthy." It flips the order.

### When They Blame Money
Four flavors: not enough value, can't afford it, competitors cheaper, haggling.
- **Not enough value** — Translate "price too high" into "value too low." Put painful present next to awesome future.
- **Can't afford it** — Appeal to resourcefulness. Having goals just beyond reach is an advantage.
- **Competitors cheaper** — "Why didn't you buy from them already?" Highlight delivery differences.
- **Haggling** — Offer to charge more. Ends haggling, forces a decision.

### When They Blame Other People
- **Decision maker** — Rely on past times the decision maker supported them. "Has your spouse ever supported a decision you felt strongly about?"
- **Trust/burned before** — "What makes us different is [specific proof]." Separate past experience from present opportunity.

### When They Blame Self
- **Fear** — "It might not work for me." Address with matching proof (people identical to them who succeeded).
- **Avoidance** — "I need to think about it." Use main concern close to surface the real obstacle.

## Connection to [[high-probability-selling]]

HPS says: if you encounter resistance, stop selling. Hormozi says: closing starts after they say no.

These are not contradictory. HPS operates on prospecting and qualification — filtering for High Probability Prospects who already want what you sell. Hormozi's closing operates on the MAYBE bucket — qualified prospects who want the result but have decision-avoidance patterns. HPS dis-qualifies the NOs. Closing empowers the MAYBEs.

The Commitment Chain (HPS) and the Onion of Blame (Hormozi) are complementary frameworks for different stages of the same sales process.
