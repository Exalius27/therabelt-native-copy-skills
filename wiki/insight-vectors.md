## Core Thesis

People walk around with mental models — incomplete maps of how the world works. These maps have gaps: assumptions about causality, patterns, and control points that are unconscious and often wrong. An insight vector identifies one of these gaps and introduces new understanding into it. When it lands, the prospect experiences a genuine epiphany — an update to their worldview that sticks for days or weeks and drives action. This is the underlying structure of what makes mechanisms, hooks, and ads powerful. Reverse-engineer the structure, and you can generate insights on demand for any market.

Critical distinction: Mental models are NOT static beliefs — they're working **systems** that represent the world. This matters because it lets you analyze assumptions through systems thinking grammar (causality, control points, patterns, cycles) rather than just looking for "wrong beliefs."

**Connection:** This file answers HOW to generate the core insight that becomes a mechanism. [[direct-response-copywriting]] covers how mechanisms work in copy. [[persuasion]] covers why people buy. This sits between them — the idea generation layer.

## Principles

### 1. Mental Models Are Systems With Gaps

Every prospect carries a working system-model of how their problem, body, dog, health, etc. works. These models are always incomplete — places where they haven't been fully mapped, borders that aren't clearly marked, assumptions that have never been tested. Good marketing identifies and communicates these gaps. When you insert new understanding into a gap, you gain mindshare.

Why it matters: You're not convincing people of something new from scratch. You're updating something they already believe. That's far easier and far stickier.

### 2. Systems Thinking Reveals the Grammar of Assumptions

Because mental models are working systems, people's assumptions follow a finite grammar:
- **Causality** — what causes what, in which direction, with how many inputs
- **Patterns** — what always goes together, what predicts what
- **Cascades** — what compounds upward or downward
- **Control Points** — what's the real bottleneck, what system are they optimizing
- **Model Dynamics** — what variables are missing, where does the model break down

These are the categories where unconscious assumptions live. Once you understand this grammar, you can systematically look for weaknesses in any market's model.

Why it matters: This turns insight generation from art ("I need a brilliant idea") into process ("let me check each assumption category").

### 3. Insight Vectors Have a Finite Taxonomy

There are specific, repeatable types of mental model updates. Each exploits a different kind of assumption weakness:

| Category | Vector Type | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Causality** | Reverse Causation | Flips cause-effect: "A doesn't cause B — B causes A" |
| **Causality** | Multiple Causes | Shows the problem has more inputs than assumed: "It's A+B+C together" |
| **Patterns** | System Archetypes | Maps the problem to a classic pattern: "This follows pattern X" |
| **Patterns** | Leading Indicators | Points to an early signal nobody watches: "X always precedes Y" |
| **Cascades** | Virtuous Cycles | Shows how the solution compounds upward |
| **Cascades** | Vicious Cycles | Shows how the problem compounds downward |
| **Control Points** | Hidden Constraints | Reveals the real bottleneck: "The real limiting factor is X" |
| **Control Points** | System Boundaries | Shows they're optimizing the wrong system entirely |
| **Model Dynamics** | Missing Variables | Introduces a factor they never considered |
| **Model Dynamics** | Model Limitations | Shows their model only works under certain conditions |
| **Model Dynamics** | False Assumptions | Directly challenges a core belief they hold |

Why it matters: You don't need to wait for a flash of genius. Run any market's assumptions through this taxonomy and you'll generate candidate vectors systematically.

### 4. The Power Is in the Structure, Not the Content

The same vector types work across every market — health, wealth, relationships, pet care, B2B. The market changes. The assumption grammar doesn't.

Why it matters: You can transfer insight vector thinking from one product to another.

### 5. A Single Strong Insight Vector Can Carry an Entire Sale

Source example: Two sentences closed a multi-thousand-dollar coaching deal:
- "20-30% of your energy is being spent on SUPPRESSION. If you release those things you'll free up a huge untapped reservoir of energy." (missing variable)
- "You're a copywriter, which means your greatest gift is to persuade and influence people. However, you're weaponizing those skills against yourself to hold yourself back." (false assumption / hidden exception)

The buyer repeated these lines verbatim weeks later. You don't need 10 insights. You need 1-2 that are genuinely strong.

### 6. Raw Vectors Need the 8-Fold Structure to Reach Full Power

A raw insight vector is a seed. The 8-Fold Structure elaborates that seed into a complete [[persuasion]] sequence. Most "good" copywriters hit these beats by accident. Knowing them explicitly gives you:
1. A much stronger mechanism
2. Infinite ammo for [[vicious-hooks]] and attention devices

Why it matters: The vector alone creates interest. The 8-Fold Structure creates conviction, urgency, and action.

## Frameworks

### Insight Vector Generation Framework

Derived from: Principles 2 + 3 (systems grammar + taxonomy)

Step 1: **Map the prospect's current mental model.** Write out what your market currently believes about the problem, the cause, and the solution. Use their language (from [[avatar-2]] research, Reddit, reviews). What do they think causes it? What do they think controls it? What patterns do they assume?

Step 2: **List the assumptions.** Pull out every assumption embedded in that model. "X causes Y." "A and B always go together." "The bottleneck is Z." "If I just do more of W, the problem goes away." Write them as explicit statements.

Step 3: **Run each assumption through the taxonomy.** For each assumption, ask:
- Can I reverse the causation?
- Can I add multiple causes they don't see?
- Can I create a type or archetype?
- Can I point to a leading indicator?
- Can I show a virtuous or vicious cycle?
- Can I reveal a hidden constraint?
- Can I show they're optimizing the wrong system?
- Can I introduce a missing variable?
- Can I show their model only works in certain conditions?
- Can I challenge this as a false assumption?

Generate as many candidate vectors as possible. Quantity first, quality second.

Step 4: **Test each candidate.** Does this create a genuine "oh shit" reaction? Would the prospect repeat this to a friend? Does it change how they see the problem — not just add information? If it just sounds smart but doesn't shift anything, discard it.

Step 5: **Select the strongest 1-2 vectors.** Build your mechanism, hook, or ad around them. One strong vector is better than five mediocre ones.

When to use: Before writing any mechanism, hook, or ad. This is the idea generation step that feeds everything downstream.

### The 8-Fold Structure (Insight DNA)

Derived from: Principle 6. Once you have a raw insight vector, elaborate it through these 8 beats to build full persuasion power.

**Beat 1: Paradoxical Question**
Purpose: Opens a question in the prospect's mind almost against their will. Creates engagement quickly while laying the seed for the insight vector.

Three subtypes:
- **Known Fact + Why:** Takes something they already know and asks a question that exposes missing information.
  - "Why is it so hard for an adult to learn a new language, when young children have no problem learning multiple languages naturally?"
- **New Fact + Why:** Introduces a surprising fact, then asks the question.
  - "Why are these 2,500-year-old teeth perfectly preserved after millennia lying in dirt, while our teeth are practically rotting in our heads with modern dentistry?"
- **The Invisible Difference:** Two identical situations with drastically different outcomes — all variables the same except one.
  - "These two dogs are the same age, eat the same food, exercise the same, have the same genetics — yet one has grey fur and can barely hobble up the stairs, while the other plays like a puppy. What makes the difference?"

**Beat 2: UMP/UMS Trigger**
Purpose: Answers "why haven't I heard this before?" Introduces the inciting incident that led to the discovery.

- **UMP (Unique Mechanism Proof):** Uses scientific evidence, news, historical examples, or research to establish authority.
  - "A landmark study from Columbia University uncovered a psychological trigger that makes women feel an uncontrollable pull of attraction. Dr. Hoffman calls it 'The Lovemap Loophole.'"
- **UMS (Unique Mechanism Story):** Uses personal story, analogy, or counterintuitive wisdom.
  - "After a devastating breakup, I stumbled upon an ancient Tibetan manuscript that revealed the secrets of 'Emotional Resonance.' This little-known practice, used by monks for centuries, creates an unbreakable bond between two people."

**Beat 3: Testable Proof**
Purpose: Provides intuitive proof the prospect generates INTERNALLY — making it frictionless. The prospect proves it to themselves.

- "Just gently press your gums with your finger like this for three seconds. Do you feel a tender or sore spot? That's the Tooth Rat gnawing at your gums."
- "If your feet swell, or you find yourself going to the bathroom after drinking water, it's because it's not getting into your cells."
- The wrist lick test, the gum line bleeding test, the lightbulb electricity test.

**Beat 4: Intensifiers & Elaboration**
Purpose: Cleans up objections ("well, why isn't this true for everyone?") and creates urgency that makes accepting the idea easier.

- Show charts, data, escalating consequences
- Explain that at a certain point, the problem becomes catastrophic
- Build the case that this is getting worse, not better

**Beat 5: Myths & Mistakes**
Purpose: Weakens the prospect's current mental model, making room for the new one.

Two subtypes:
- **Prevailing Myths:** Debunks widely believed myths.
  - "One of the biggest myths about building wealth is that you need a high-paying job or inherit money. The truth is, anyone can become financially independent with the right mindset and strategies."
- **Costly Mistakes:** Warns against common mistakes people make when trying to solve the problem.
  - "A costly mistake many women make in their dating lives is trying to change themselves to fit what they think men want."

**Beat 6: Cause (Surprising Culprit / Adversaries / Weakness)**
Purpose: Explains why this insight wasn't discovered previously. Gives the prospect someone or something to blame.

Three angles:
- **Surprising Culprit:** "Modern air conditioning. Our studies showed that constant exposure to artificial air actually pulls moisture from deep within collagen fibers."
- **Adversaries:** "The $50 billion moisturizer industry doesn't want you to know that surface creams can't reach these deep collagen fibers."
- **Weakness:** An inherent vulnerability the prospect didn't know they had.

**Beat 7: UMP Quiz**
Purpose: Gives the prospect confidence that this problem actually applies to THEM. Self-qualification.

Format: 5-6 yes/no questions, each expanding on a symptom they recognize. Then: "If you answered yes to even one of these questions, there's a chance [mechanism] is the culprit. If you answered yes to several..." Creates personal urgency.

Example: The "Hidden Constipation" quiz — bloating? unsatisfied bowel movements? certain foods trigger fatigue? belly distention? digestion worse with age? regular poops but still feel off?

**Beat 8: How-Tos**
Purpose: Creates rapport and reciprocity by "giving" them something. Lowers sales resistance. Then transitions to the product as the easier/better version.

Pattern: Give a real, actionable tip they can do at home. Then show why it's impractical at scale. Then introduce the product as the workaround.

- "Peel the inner membrane from boiled eggs and toss them in your dog's bowl. Unfortunately, this means boiling and peeling a dozen eggs... Luckily, scientists have a clever way to EXTRACT the membrane directly."

When to use: After selecting your strongest insight vector. Run it through all 8 beats to build the full persuasion sequence. Not every ad needs all 8 — but knowing them means you can deploy any beat as a standalone hook, lead, or section.

### Market Mental Model Mapping

Derived from: Principle 1 (mental models are systems with gaps)

Step 1: **Gather raw language.** Pull from avatar research, Reddit threads, Amazon reviews, support emails — anywhere the market talks about the problem in their own words.

Step 2: **Extract the causal chain.** What does the market believe causes the problem? Write it as: "[They believe] A causes B, and B leads to C." Follow the chain as far as it goes.

Step 3: **Extract the control beliefs.** What does the market believe they can and can't control? "I can control my diet but I can't control my genetics."

Step 4: **Extract the pattern assumptions.** What does the market assume always goes together? "Old age = decline." "More exercise = more energy."

Step 5: **Write the model as a simple diagram.** Causes on the left, outcomes on the right, control points in the middle. This is the map you're going to update with insight vectors.

When to use: Before running the Insight Vector Generation Framework. You need to know what the market believes before you can find the gaps.

### Vector Strength Test

Derived from: Principle 5 (one vector can carry a sale)

Step 1: **The verbatim test.** Would the prospect repeat this line to a friend, unprompted, days later? If the language is forgettable, the vector is weak.

Step 2: **The model update test.** Does this actually change how the prospect sees the problem? Or does it just add a fact? Facts inform. Vectors transform.

Step 3: **The "oh shit" test.** Read the vector cold, as if you're the prospect hearing it for the first time. Does it produce genuine surprise or recognition?

Step 4: **The action test.** Does this vector make the prospect more likely to act? Not just more informed — more compelled.

When to use: After generating candidate vectors. Kill weak vectors early.

## Proven Examples

### Vector Examples (by type)

**Reverse Causation:**
"Most think overeating causes weight gain. But groundbreaking research shows the opposite — metabolic damage causes overeating. Your body isn't making you fat because you eat too much... you're eating too much because your cells are desperately trying to compensate for metabolic damage."

**Multiple Causes:**
"Weight gain isn't just about calories — it's actually a 'Perfect Storm' of disrupted sleep cycles, hormonal interference, and cellular inflammation all working together. That's why fixing just one piece rarely works."

**System Archetype:**
"Your metabolism follows the classic 'Shifting the Burden' pattern — where quick-fix diets provide temporary relief but make the underlying metabolic damage worse, creating dependency on more extreme measures."

**Leading Indicator:**
"Your morning temperature is the crystal ball of metabolism — a 0.2 degree drop reliably predicts weight gain 3-4 weeks before it shows on the scale. Elite performers track this number religiously."

**Virtuous Cycle:**
"Each time you eat in harmony with your natural rhythms, your cells become more efficient at burning fat — leading to better energy — making it easier to stay in rhythm — creating even more metabolic efficiency."

**Vicious Cycle:**
"Every crash diet triggers a protective response — slowing metabolism — leading to quick regain — causing more extreme dieting — creating worse metabolic damage — until your body fights to store every calorie."

**Hidden Constraint:**
"The real limit isn't willpower or knowledge — it's your cellular inflammation level. Until you reduce this invisible blocker, even perfect diet and exercise won't create lasting change."

**System Boundaries:**
"You're optimizing the wrong system — focusing on calories when you should be focusing on circadian rhythm alignment. The timing system controls the calorie system."

**False Assumption:**
"The core belief that 'discipline equals results' is fundamentally wrong. Your body's natural rhythm determines results far more than willpower."

**Missing Variable:**
"Everyone obsesses over macros while ignoring 'metabolic flexibility' — your body's ability to switch between burning fat and sugar. This missing factor explains why some people get stuck despite perfect diets."

### Compound Examples (Vectors + 8-Fold Structure)

**The LEGO Limitation Principle** (Structure Revelation + Model Limitations)
"Ever notice how kids with fewer LEGO pieces often build more creative structures than kids with massive collections? That's because constraints force systematic thinking. Copywriting works the same way. Writers with 'unlimited vocabulary' and 'creative freedom' actually STRUGGLE more because they're drowning in options. But when you reduce ALL persuasion down to just 5 blocks? Suddenly your brain stops searching and starts STACKING. It's not a limitation — it's a liberation."

Why it works: Familiar experience (LEGO) + counterintuitive claim (fewer = better) + explains why talent underperforms + makes the system feel like an advantage.

**The Commitment Evaporation Effect** (Delayed Causation + Threshold Causation + Model Limitations)
"Studies show you're 42% more likely to achieve goals when you write them down. But here's what they don't tell you — that effect DISAPPEARS within 72 hours unless the goal stays visible. It's called 'Commitment Evaporation.' Your brain treats written goals like wet ink: powerful when fresh, invisible when dry. That's why January gym memberships vanish by February. The goal didn't change. Your brain just... forgot it existed."

Why it works: Builds on their existing belief (42%) + reveals hidden condition + explains universal experience + creates clear mechanism.

**The Midnight Factory Shutdown** (Hidden Constraint + Leverage Point)
"Ever wonder why night shift workers gain more weight even when they eat the same calories? Scientists discovered your metabolism has a 'night shift' too — and most people accidentally fire their best workers. Between 10pm and 2am, your body runs its most powerful fat-burning crew. But the moment you eat that late-night snack, your body immediately reassigns those workers to digestion instead. It's like sending your night security team to sort mail."

Why it works: Relatable metaphor (night shift) + specific timing window + shows why snacking sabotages more than calories + makes solution obvious.

### Sale-Closing Examples

**"20-30% of your energy is being spent on SUPPRESSION"**
Source: Coaching sales call | Result: Multi-thousand-dollar sale, repeated verbatim weeks later

Old model: More effort = more output.
New model: Output - suppression (hidden drain) = more output. Subtraction, not addition.
Pattern: "X% of your [resource] is being spent on [hidden drain they never considered]."

**"Your greatest gift — persuasion — is being weaponized against yourself"**
Source: Same call | Result: Same sale, also repeated weeks later

Old model: "My skill X is always good."
New model: "My skill X is mostly good, except in this context where it works against me."
Pattern: "Your greatest strength — [X] — is actually [doing the opposite] in [this specific context]."

## Nuances and Exceptions

### Vectors are seeds, not finished mechanisms
An insight vector is the core idea. It still needs to be wrapped in story, evidence, and [[copy-structure]] to function in an ad. The 8-Fold Structure is one way to amplify it. A vector alone won't sell anything — but without a strong vector, no amount of copy craft will create real belief.

### Not all vectors work for all products
A reversed causation vector requires your product to actually address the reversed cause. A leading indicator vector requires your product to control that indicator. The vector must be authentically connected to what the product does.

### Vectors must survive the "so what" test in context
A vector that's intellectually interesting but doesn't connect to the prospect's lived experience will fail. "Morning temperature predicts weight gain" only works if the prospect has EXPERIENCED unexplained weight gain. The best vectors name something the prospect has already observed but couldn't explain.

### The 8-Fold beats work as standalone tools
Not every ad needs all 8 beats. A paradoxical question alone can be a [[vicious-hooks]]. A testable proof alone can be a lead section. A how-to alone can be a goodwill builder. Know them so you can deploy any beat independently.

### How-tos create the product bridge
The how-to beat has a specific architecture: give real value -> show it's impractical -> introduce product as the workaround. This is NOT "bait and switch." The tip must be genuinely useful. The product is genuinely more practical.

### Application to TheraBelt / pet wellness
The taxonomy applies but the mental model mapping needs to come from dog owner language, not human health language. Dog owners have their own assumption set: "old dogs just slow down" (false assumption), "joint supplements are the only option" (missing variable), "decline is one-way" (vicious cycle that could be reversed). The Invisible Difference paradoxical question subtype is especially strong for TheraBelt: same breed, same age, drastically different vitality.

## The Two-Part Mechanism Framework (Georgi — RMBC Method)

A mechanism becomes dramatically more powerful when split into two parts. This is not the same as having two mechanisms. It's one mechanism told in two stages.

**Part 1: Mechanism of the Problem**
Why the prospect failed to get results in the past. The real reason — not their reason, and not what they're usually told. This must:
- Take blame off the prospect's shoulders (they didn't fail because they were weak or lazy)
- Indict the solutions they tried (not specifically — categorically)
- Point to a specific, previously-unidentified cause that those solutions never addressed

This is a False Assumption vector or Hidden Constraint vector applied to the prospect's HISTORY of failure. It rewrites their past into a story where they were right to try and the solutions were systematically wrong.

**Part 2: Mechanism of the Solution**
How your product addresses the exact cause identified in Part 1. The solution mechanism must:
- Be a direct and logical answer to the problem mechanism
- Explain specifically HOW the product addresses that cause (not just that it does)
- Feel exclusive — the product must be the only thing that addresses the real cause in this specific way

The sequence: "Everything you've tried didn't work because [problem mechanism]. [Product] works because it [solution mechanism] — which directly addresses the real cause for the first time."

**Why two parts are more powerful than one:**
- The problem mechanism validates the prospect's past experience (empathy + explanation)
- The solution mechanism shows why THIS is different (differentiation + hope)
- Together they create a paradigm shift — the prospect moves from "jaded and skeptical" to "maybe this really is different"
- Without the problem mechanism, the solution mechanism sounds like every other claim. Without the solution mechanism, the problem mechanism is just depressing.

**Applied to TheraBelt:** Problem mechanism — existing dog supplements address symptoms (joint inflammation, pain) but not the underlying cause (reduced circulation and cellular efficiency that drives visible decline). Solution mechanism — TheraBelt's specific approach directly addresses circulation at the source, which is why dogs using it recover functional vitality rather than just experiencing reduced discomfort.

**Connection:** The two-part mechanism maps directly to the [[direct-response-copywriting]] RMBC outline sections 3 and 4. It also directly answers Q4 (What's holding me back) and Q8 (How does it work) in the 16-Word framework. Research from [[copy-research]] feeds the problem mechanism — what people say failed and why in forums and reviews is exactly the evidence needed to make Part 1 credible.

## The Tier Hierarchy of Mechanism Information Power

Not all new information is equally persuasive. The type of information your mechanism delivers determines how much belief-shifting it can actually do. This hierarchy predicts mechanism strength before you test it.

| Tier | Type | What it does | Strength |
|------|------|-------------|----------|
| **1** | Reframe an observable problem | Takes something the reader already sees/experiences/knows and gives it a completely new meaning. Doesn't require them to believe anything new — reframes what they already know is true. | Strongest |
| **2** | Explain why previous solutions failed | Shows why things they already tried didn't work. Validates their experience, removes guilt of failure, reveals a gap only your product fills. | Strong |
| **3** | Introduce a new mechanism for a familiar problem | They know the problem and want it solved. You introduce a completely new way of understanding how to solve it. Requires more work because the information is entirely new. | Moderate |
| **4** | Create awareness of a problem they didn't know existed | Starting from zero. Must convince them the problem exists before selling the solution. Highest resistance, most heavy lifting. Most failed health products operate here. | Weakest |

**The strongest mechanisms combine Tier 1 + Tier 2.** Reframe what they're experiencing AND explain why everything they tried didn't work. That combination makes the product feel like the only logical answer.

**Tier examples (cat water fountain niche):**
- **Tier 1:** "Your cat ignores the water bowl. You think she's picky. She's not. Cats are biologically wired to reject still water because in the wild, still water harbored bacteria. It's not a preference. It's a survival instinct." (Reframes observable behavior they already see.)
- **Tier 2:** "You switched to wet food thinking it would fix the hydration problem. But wet food only covers 50-60% of daily hydration needs. Your cat still got kidney disease because wet food was never going to be enough." (Explains why their past solution failed.)
- **Tier 3:** "Stagnant water loses its dissolved oxygen within hours. Low oxygen water triggers a biological avoidance response in cats. It's not about freshness. It's about oxygen content." (New mechanism — requires them to accept new information.)
- **Tier 4:** Trying to convince cat owners that whisker fatigue from narrow bowls is causing their cat stress. (Problem they don't know exists — maximum resistance.)

**Diagnostic use:** If your mechanism isn't converting, check what tier you're operating at. If Tier 3 or 4, try moving up to Tier 1 or 2. Reframe something they can already see. Explain why what they already tried didn't work. That's where belief shifts happen.

**Connection to vector taxonomy:** Tier 1 maps to False Assumption vectors and Reverse Causation vectors. Tier 2 maps to Hidden Constraint vectors. Tier 3 maps to Missing Variable vectors. Tier 4 maps to situations where no existing mental model gap exists and you're building from scratch. The tier hierarchy is a quick diagnostic for the [[desire-testing]] troubleshooting framework — when mechanism isn't converting, identify your tier and move up.

### Reality-Reinforced Mechanisms vs. Pure Logic Mechanisms

> Source: GTE Copywriting Sauce — [[raw/2026-04-08-11-copywriting-sauce-06-part-6.md]]

The practical test for whether a mechanism will convert: does it rely on the avatar's lived experience as proof, or does it require them to trust your logic?

**Pure logic mechanism (weak):** "Most people try to lose weight by cutting calories, but single nutrients can't target belly fat on their own. You need a specific combination of ingredients working together to activate fat loss in the midsection."

This is an argument. The reader asks: "Why should I believe you?" You have no answer except "trust me." The mechanism sounds good to a marketer but is dog shit from the avatar's perspective.

**Reality-reinforced mechanism (strong):** "Most people try getting abs by cutting calories. But if you lose weight without muscle, your abs won't show. That's why the scale keeps going down, but your midsection stays soft. You don't have a calorie problem. You have a muscle activation problem."

Every claim is reinforced by something the reader has already experienced. The scale HAS been going down. The midsection IS still soft. The new information (muscle activation) explains their reality. They convince themselves.

**Four mechanism failure modes:**
1. **Pure logic** — Explaining without experiential proof. Reader has no reason to believe.
2. **Argument** — Directly contradicting what the reader already believes, from the jump. Triggers resistance.
3. **Explanation without reframe** — Telling them how it works without changing how they see their problem. Information, not transformation.
4. **Jargon** — Using unfamiliar terms that create friction. If they have to pause to figure out what you mean, you've lost them.

**The diagnostic:** A strong mechanism makes the avatar say "Oh, THAT'S why nothing I tried worked." If they don't have that light-bulb moment, you have a bad mechanism. The new information must explain an experience they have already observed in their own life.

**Connection:** This is the practical application of Tier 1 + Tier 2 in the hierarchy above. Reality-reinforced = reframe observable (Tier 1) + explain why previous solutions failed (Tier 2). Pure logic = Tier 3 or lower, requiring belief without experiential evidence. See also [[angles-belief-shifts]] — the revelation dynamic operates on the same principle: the prospect convinces themselves through self-evident proof.
