# CUB Critique — Copy Review Framework

> Sources: [[raw/2026-04-07-agora-s-big-black-book-1-07-part-7.md]], [[raw/2026-04-08-11-copywriting-sauce-03-part-3.md]] (GTE Copy Evaluation Prompt v2.0)
> Related: [[four-legged-stool]], [[copy-structure]], [[direct-response-copywriting]], [[rule-of-one]], [[copy-research]], [[avatar-system]]
> Last updated: 2026-04-08

Invented by Mike Palmer at Stansberry & Associates (Agora). A fast review system for improving the body of a sales letter — not just the lead. While the Peer Review focuses on leads, CUB makes it possible to review and improve an entire letter in hours rather than days.

CUB stands for **Confusing**, **Unbelievable**, **Boring**. Readers go through copy and mark every section that triggers one of these three reactions. Then you fix them.

---

## C — Confusing

Any section where the reader has to re-read or stops to figure out what's being said.

**Common causes:**
- Too many details packed into one passage
- Industry jargon without explanation
- Complex ideas not broken down
- Unclear antecedents or vague pronouns

**The fix:** Simplify. Cut excess detail. Explain jargon in plain language. One idea per paragraph. If a reader has to think about your WRITING instead of your MESSAGE, the copy is confusing.

**Example fix — Pharmacy Benefit Managers:**

Before (confusing): A paragraph naming six PBM companies, explaining ownership structures, listing subsidiaries — too much detail, too many names, reader loses the point.

After (clear): "Did you know that every time you buy a drug, there's a middleman called a Pharmacy Benefit Manager that makes a huge profit? Even when you buy from big discounters like Walgreens or Rite Aid... the PBM middleman gets a huge cut."

Same point. A fraction of the words. Reader gets it instantly.

---

## U — Unbelievable

Any claim, promise, or assertion the reader's gut rejects.

**Common causes:**
- Claims without proof
- Exaggerated benefits
- Missing context that would make the claim believable
- Insufficient credibility for the spokesperson

**The fix:** Add specifics. Add proof. Add a credible source. Or tone down the claim to something defensible. If you can't prove it, don't say it — or reframe it as something provable.

**Connection:** Unbelievable sections are often where a leg of the [[four-legged-stool]] is missing — usually Proof or Credibility.

---

## B — Boring

Any section where the reader's eyes glaze over or they start skimming.

**Three deadly boring patterns:**

### 1. Telling the reader what they already know

If you're writing to someone who has cancer, you don't need to tell them how bad cancer is. If you're writing to someone who needs a job, you don't need to tell them how bad unemployment is. Remind them of the problem briefly. Then focus on the opportunity and the solution.

### 2. Spending too much time on a boring subject

Tax regulations, healthcare system mechanics, legal principles — even if your promotion involves these, you must filter out the complexity. Act as an "excitement filter." The reader doesn't need the full picture, just the part that matters to them.

### 3. Wandering — taking too long to get to the point

Copy that meanders through unnecessary setup, explains which service something would be recommended in, describes the writer's research process, or tells the reader things they don't care about.

**The fix for all three:** Cut. Ruthlessly. The reader is competing for your reader's attention with everything else in their feed. You can't let the copy drag. Get to the point, fast.

**Example fix — wandering copy:**

Before: A full paragraph about going to Vancouver, being tipped off by someone, how this would normally go in a different service, how you can get the report, why the tipping person compares it to another company...

After: "I recently went to Vancouver (the gold and silver mining capital of the world) to do research. I was tipped off to this opportunity by one of the richest men I know — a man personally worth several hundred million dollars. Let me explain exactly what I'm talking about..."

Same destination. Straight line instead of a winding road.

## How to Run a CUB Review

1. Print the copy or display it where multiple reviewers can read it
2. Each reviewer reads through and marks every passage C, U, or B
3. Collect all marks — patterns emerge quickly (multiple people marking the same sections)
4. Fix each marked section:
   - **C** → simplify and clarify
   - **U** → add proof or reduce the claim
   - **B** → cut, condense, or replace with something more engaging
5. Read the revised copy through once more to verify the fixes didn't create new CUB issues

**When to apply:** After the lead is solid (via Peer Review or similar). CUB is for the body — everything after the lead. Then apply the [[four-legged-stool]] test to check structural balance.

## The Authenticity Gate (Checkpoint 0)

> Source: GTE Copywriting Sauce — Universal Copy Evaluation Prompt v2.0

CUB evaluates technical execution. But technical excellence on an inauthentic foundation is worthless. Before running any technical evaluation, the copy must pass the **Avatar Mirror Test**: would the target avatar react emotionally ("how did they know?") or analytically ("nice ad")?

If the avatar's gut response is "this sounds like a marketer wrote it" or "another ad," no amount of structural perfection will save the copy. Voice authenticity is the gate — pass it first, then evaluate technically.

**The test:** Imagine the avatar encountering this copy at 11pm on a Tuesday after a long day. Does she:
- Feel seen and understood? (pass)
- Feel marketed to? (fail)

**Red flags:** Avatar gives marketing feedback instead of personal reaction. Avatar stays analytical instead of emotional. Multiple phrases sound like "marketer voice" instead of customer voice.

**The fix when it fails:** Go back to real avatar research (Reddit threads, reviews, forums). Collect actual language patterns and phrases. Rewrite using ONLY the language found in research. The problem is almost always insufficient avatar research, not insufficient writing skill.

**Connection:** Extends CUB's principle — CUB catches technical problems (confusing, unbelievable, boring). The Authenticity Gate catches the deeper problem that makes all three irrelevant: the avatar never started reading because the voice wasn't theirs.
