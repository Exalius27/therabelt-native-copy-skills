## Core Thesis

Research is not preparation for the real work — it IS the real work. The Evolve research system moves platform-by-platform through the internet the way a prospect moves through it, building understanding of desires, awareness levels, sophistication, beliefs, and objections as it goes. Everything lives in a centralized research document that feeds directly into ad creation. The system is designed for e-commerce brands advertising on Meta, but the principles apply to any paid acquisition channel.

This page covers the *operational* research process. For research *principles* (forum mining, Amazon review analysis, 22 questions, etc.), see [[copy-research]]. For the market psychology frameworks that research feeds, see [[market-fundamentals]].

## The Research Document

All research flows into one centralized document (Google Docs or ClickUp). Sections include:

**Quick links:** Research doc, brand guidelines, onboarding docs, Foreplay/Atria board
**Brain dump:** Headline ideas, visual ad ideas, overall ideas — capture anything that sparks during research
**Product section:** Company info, products, features, benefits, competitive advantages, use cases, reviews
**Core research:** Problems and solutions (every problem with all known solutions), good/bad competitor reviews, buzzwords with frequency counts
**Avatar section:** Name, age, gender, occupation, income, core challenges, goals, values, objections, failed solutions
**Phrase bank:** Positive frames (words for the main claim being delivered), negative frames (words for relatable issues), visual ideas
**Competitor research:** Landing pages, ad libraries, offers, pricing, positioning
**Beliefs & objections:** What beliefs need to be handled, what objections arise, problems to solve in marketing
**FAQs:** Questions that show up repeatedly in research — update on website AND consider as ad ideas

## The Research Sequence

### Step 1: Understand Your Product

Before researching the market, understand what you're selling. Go through your website in incognito mode as a first-time visitor. Can you figure out what the product does and why you should buy it?

**Capture:** Features, benefits, competitive advantages, claims, use cases. Separate physical (components, materials, tech) from functional (the outcome the customer buys). The functional side drives dominant desire.

**Website audit:** Is the site actually communicating the value you think it is? If features/benefits exist in your head but not on the site, fix that immediately.

### Step 2: Google Research

**Setup:** Use VPN to target market country. Use incognito mode for unbiased results.

**What to look for:**
- What competitors dominate search? Big trusted brands (Dyson, Shark, Walmart, JCPenney) create skepticism for unknown brands — note this for belief-building
- What claims are being made? Track buzzwords with frequency counts (e.g., "no heat damage" x7, "fast drying" x12) — overdone buzzwords signal high market sophistication
- What market segments emerge? (Budget, mid-range, luxury, professional) — each segment has different desires, objections, and price sensitivity
- What articles rank? Read review roundups to see how products are positioned and compared
- Search your own brand — see what prospects find when they search you

**Ad ideas from Google:** Seeing that every competitor claims the same thing can spark differentiation ideas. If "fast drying without heat damage" is said by 7 brands, that specific claim is dead — find a new angle.

### Step 3: Amazon Research (with Shoelix)

Amazon is a goldmine for understanding what real buyers value and what frustrates them. Shoelix summarizes reviews at scale.

**What to extract:**
- **Top pros by frequency:** What do buyers praise most? (lightweight, fast drying, price) — these are the benefits your market values
- **Top cons by frequency:** What do buyers complain about? (overheats, breaks easily, smells like burning) — these are ad angles and objection-handling opportunities
- **Purchase motivation:** Why did they buy? (price, gift, replacement) — this informs offer positioning
- **Use case scenarios:** When/where do they use it? (after shower, at home, traveling) — this informs filming environments and visual strategy
- **Questions customers ask:** What information is missing? (voltage, weight, RPMs, cold shot button) — this feeds CRO and FAQ content
- **Market segments by price:** Research budget ($10-50), mid-range ($75-200), and premium ($300+) separately. Each has different pros, cons, and purchase motivations

**The sophistication signal:** If customer questions are heavily feature/specification-focused, the market is sophisticated — buyers understand the outcome and are comparing mechanisms. Lead with mechanism, not with claim.

### Step 4: Reddit Research

Reddit provides unfiltered, unperformed conversation between real people. No one is selling anything.

**What to extract:**
- Beliefs that must be overcome ("There's no difference between luxury and cheap hair dryers — they all do the same shit")
- Segment-specific desires ("What type of hair do you have? How do you like to wear it?" — this opens avatar-level targeting)
- Investment framing from loyal users ("The best investment I've ever made" — this reframes price objection)
- Price sensitivity data ("Buy a $100 hair dryer or a $20 one? Looking for longevity")
- Professional opinions that challenge/confirm assumptions

**Turning beliefs into ads:** A strong belief like "all hair dryers do the same thing" becomes the opening of an ad: "I used to think all hair dryers were the same. Here's what changed my mind." Address the belief head-on.

**Connection:** Reddit research methodology extends [[copy-research]] forum research principles to e-commerce specifically. Also feeds directly into [[desire-testing]] desire identification.

### Step 5: YouTube Research

YouTube reveals what content resonates, what headlines work, and what desires drive engagement.

**What to extract:**
- Headlines that get views (these are tested hooks — "How to eliminate frizzy hair overnight" = hook + time constraint)
- Content formats that perform (tutorials, ASMR, comparisons, reviews, before/after)
- Creator discovery for UGC partnerships — note creators in the niche for potential collaboration
- Comment sections — the most valuable part. Real reactions, real questions, real desires
- Adjacent content ideas (hair dryer ASMR = 26M views = ad idea for calm, relaxing content)

**The desire chain:** YouTube helps you trace from surface wants → deeper desires. "Tutorials on blow drying" → "want healthier hair" → "want to feel beautiful and confident." Product feature → surface benefit → deep desire.

### Step 6: Competitor Research (Facebook Ad Library)

The Facebook Ad Library lets you see every active ad from any brand. This reveals market sophistication in real time.

**What to capture:**
- Landing page URLs used in ads (link to research doc)
- Product price ranges across competitors
- Active offers (try before you buy, free shipping, warranties, split payments)
- How long ads have been running (longer = likely performing well)
- Claims and positioning — what's everyone saying? What's overdone?
- Visual style and production quality

**Market sophistication visual test:** Create a Foreplay/Atria board of all competitor ads. View them as a mosaic. Which ones stand out? Which blend together? Your job is to be the "black t-shirt" — visually and conceptually distinct from the rest.

**Bad competitor reviews:** Read 1-star reviews of direct competitors. Look for patterns of product failures, customer service issues, quality problems. These become your competitive edge: "If anything goes wrong, we'll send you a brand new one, no questions asked."

### Step 7: Creative Research (Atria)

Atria ($39/month) replaces Foreplay ($49/month) and also replaces Motion (which costs $2-3K/month at scale). It provides:
- Ad library swipe file functionality (save ads, organize by client)
- Creative analytics (performance reporting by ad)
- Cheaper than alternatives at all spend levels

Use Atria to maintain an ongoing board of competitor ads and inspiring creative across the market.

### Step 8: TikTok Research

TikTok reveals what *content* resonates organically — not ads, but native content in your niche.

**What to look for:**
- Search desires and problems (e.g., "thin hair," "how to stop frizz") — see what gets millions of views
- Content formats: tutorials dominate most niches. Tutorial-style ads perform well because they match native content
- Creator identification: find creators in your niche for UGC partnerships. Check their other platforms (Instagram, YouTube) for cross-platform collaboration
- Comments: "I need your hair care routine" = evidence of desire. Comments are where real desire surfaces

### Step 9: Understanding Other Desires & Solutions

Research every alternative solution to the same problem your product solves. Each alternative solution reveals:
- What the prospect has already tried (and possibly failed with) — this becomes ad content
- Visual ad ideas (show the ridiculous complexity of alternatives vs. the simplicity of your solution)
- Cross-solution skepticism (if they tried solution X and it failed, they're now skeptical of all solutions)

Example: Hair dryer alternatives include microfiber towels, hair masks, expensive shampoos/conditioners, air drying, clip-and-section techniques. Ad idea: "You spend hundreds on premium shampoo... then dry it with a $20 hair dryer that's frying your hair."

### Step 10: Beliefs & Objections

After all research, crystallize the beliefs and objections that must be addressed:
- "Too good to be true" beliefs (same features as Dyson for 1/3 the price — how?)
- Category skepticism ("All hair dryers are the same")
- Price anchoring (when budget competitors are $13-20, a $130 product feels luxury)
- Fatigued messaging (every competitor says "fast drying without heat damage")

For each objection, brainstorm solutions and analogies. Example: "We don't make customers pay for branding — that's why we cost less." Or: "Buying a quality hair dryer isn't luxury — it's like choosing to eat healthier instead of fast food."

### Step 11: Clean Up the Research Doc

After completing all platform research, organize and polish:
- Remove duplicates, combine similar entries
- Add frequency counts to buzzwords
- Fill in any gaps (if a section is empty, go back and research it)
- The document should be ready to feed directly into ad creation, landing page copy, and the creative roadmap

## The Ongoing Nature of Research

Research is never "done." Every ad performance result, every new competitor ad, every customer review adds to the research document. The document compounds over time. Brands that do ongoing research consistently outperform brands that research once and then create from memory.

**Connection:** The research doc directly feeds [[desire-testing]] (which desires to test first), [[creative-testing]] (hypothesis formation for new ads), and [[market-fundamentals]] (identifying which of the five forces is weak).
