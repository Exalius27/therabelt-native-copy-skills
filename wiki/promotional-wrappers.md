## Core Thesis

Promotional wrappers are the packaging around your Grand Slam Offer. The core offer stays the same — the wrapper makes it more attractive to cold audiences. There are three wrappers: Premium (full price, no incentive), Free (something for nothing), and Discount (massive reduction). Each has tradeoffs. The right choice depends on your market position, cash reserves, and operational capacity.

**The sequence:** Get flow first. Monetize flow second. Then add friction.

---

## Premium Offers

The Grand Slam Offer presented at full price with no discounts or incentives.

**Pros:**
1. Simplest math — one product, one price, one metric.
2. Only quality customers — premium pricing skims the cream. Fewer, better clients.
3. High lead quality — transparent intentions on both sides. Conversion depends on perceived value.

**Cons:**
1. Most expensive cost per lead — requires cash to drive volume. Less room for error.
2. Requires strong copywriting and deep avatar knowledge — specificity is what gives copy its edge.
3. Must be good at sales — a good old-fashioned sale with no crutches.
4. Least efficient for capturing a marketplace — low volume, high value per unit.
5. Must have a truly irresistible result — packed with bonuses, creative guarantees, premium support.

**When to use:** When you have proven results, deep avatar knowledge, a trained sales team, and capital to absorb higher CPAs. Premium is the endpoint — most businesses should start free/discount and graduate to premium.

## Free Offers

The most powerful promotional wrapper. "If it's free, it's for me."

**The penny gap (Dr. Dan Ariely):** 9x more people take a free Hershey Kiss than one sold for a penny. Going from $0.01 to $0 can multiply leads by 9x.

**Pros:**
1. Highest lead volume — nothing beats free for raw volume.
2. Lowest lead cost — more raised hands per dollar spent.
3. Massive companies are built on free — Facebook, YouTube, Uber, Netflix, Dropbox, Claire's.

**Cons:**
1. Volume is a double-edged sword — may attract more prospects than you can handle.
2. Some people have no intention of buying — the math still usually works (500 leads with 50% qualified beats 200 leads with 80% qualified).

**The "free brings broke people" myth:** In four independent split tests across ten markets, closing percentage was identical between free-offer respondents and non-free respondents. Same close rate, same average ticket. The only difference: free produced 5x cheaper leads.

**When to use:** When entering a new market, when you lack conviction, when you need testimonials fast, when volume matters more than immediate revenue. Bottom line: "If I only had one offer to convert or my family would be killed, it would be a free offer."

## Friction: The Volume Dial for Free Offers

Free generates too much volume? Add friction to increase lead quality without switching wrappers:

1. **Increased qualifications** — "Must be over 25, employed, and a homeowner." Repeat everywhere.
2. **Increased information requirements** — 20-question application vs. first-name-only. Type of info matters: cell phone number > first name. Open-ended > multiple choice.
3. **Increased steps** — One-step opt-in > five-step funnel. Each step drops unqualified leads.
4. **Forced consumption** — 40-minute video before CTA appears. Only pre-indoctrinated prospects proceed. Best for large cheap audiences.
5. **Advertisement length** — 2-hour video vs. 30-second clip. Fewer clicks, but each click is worth more.

The sweet spot: enough friction to weed out tire-kickers, not so much you lose lazy whales.

## Discount Offers

Massive discounts (50%+) — not marginal 5-25% reductions, which just cut margins without driving behavior.

**Four ways to display discounts:**
1. Dollar amount off
2. Percentage off (use [[offer-design]] Rule of 100: % for under $100, $ for over $100)
3. Reduced price shown directly
4. Bundled savings framing

**Pros:**
1. High volume within legal constraints — some jurisdictions restrict "free" marketing.
2. Collect some money upfront — helps liquidate acquisition costs.
3. Prospects expect to spend — less friction in the upsell conversation.
4. **Two-step sale (biggest benefit)** — card on file eliminates "forgot my wallet" and enables frictionless upsells. Amazon one-click purchasing, Disney wristbands — removing the wallet is removing friction.
5. Eliminates no-shows — people show up for things they paid for (85-90%+).

**Cons:**
1. Risk training customers to buy only at discounted times — never discount your core offer repeatedly. Splinter the offer instead: give a small piece at a discount, upsell the full thing.
2. Still some non-buyers — but better qualified than free.

**When to use:** When you want the volume benefits of free but need a card on file, when no-shows are killing your business, when you want a two-step selling process.

## Strategic Layering

The three wrappers can be stacked:

**Free front-end → Discount middle → Premium back-end**

Example: Free 6-Week Challenge (gets volume) → $19 consultation upsell (gets card on file, eliminates no-shows) → $2,100 program sale (premium, demonstrated value).

The progression: demonstrate value at each step, earn trust, then sell the premium offer to someone who has already experienced your quality.

**Key principle:** The point of a promotion is to enhance your Grand Slam Offer, not change it. Think of wrappers as gift wrapping — same gift inside, but a Chanel bag with matte black paper beats recycled newspaper.
