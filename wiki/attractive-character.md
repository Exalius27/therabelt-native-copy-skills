# Attractive Character — Persona That Builds Following and Drives Sales

## Core Thesis

An Attractive Character is the persona that draws people to you, builds trust, and makes them want to buy. It's not about being good-looking — it's about being relatable, interesting, and polarizing enough that people follow you. The AC is the difference between a business that makes $1,000/month and one that makes $100,000/month. Without an AC, your emails don't get opened, your content doesn't get shared, and your list stays cold. Every successful brand has one — Subway had Jared, movies have their lead actors, and your business needs its own.

## Principles

### 1. The Backstory Is the Hook

Every AC needs a backstory that the audience can relate to. Without the backstory, the character is just a stranger. With it, the audience sees themselves in the character's past — and wants to follow them to their present.

The backstory must relate to the product being sold. Weight loss product → weight loss backstory. Business coaching → business struggle backstory. Dog wellness → personal journey with a dog.

Why it matters: The backstory creates identification. If the audience can see where you came from (and it mirrors their current situation), they'll trust that you know the path to where they want to go. Without it, you're untouchable — successful but unrelatable.

**Critical:** The AC doesn't have to be YOU. It can be a customer, a case study, a character. Subway didn't put its CEO on TV — it put Jared, the customer who lost 400 pounds eating subs. Your success stories ARE potential Attractive Characters.

**Connection:** Directly maps to [[story-native-format]] structure. Every story-native ad IS an Attractive Character story — the protagonist has a backstory, faces a struggle, and finds a transformation. The principles below apply both to brand-level AC and to individual ad characters.

### 2. Parables Teach and Stick

Parables are short stories from the AC's life that illustrate a point. They're more powerful than facts because they're memorable, emotional, and easy to retell.

Why it matters: Facts inform. Parables transform. When you teach through stories, the lessons stick because the brain encodes them as experiences, not information. Every core principle you want your audience to believe should have a parable attached to it.

**The Coach Schultz parable (from source):** Brunson's wrestling coach took the money from his wallet after giving him a training video. "If I gave you that tape for free, you'd never watch it. But because you've paid for it, you'll learn from it." Brunson uses this parable every time he asks someone to invest. It teaches the principle of investment through story — far more effective than saying "you should invest in yourself."

### 3. Flaws Make the Character Real

Perfect characters are unrelatable. Every believable AC has visible flaws — struggles, mistakes, vulnerabilities. Sharing flaws creates empathy. The audience thinks "they're like me" instead of "they're better than me."

Why it matters: Most people try to present a perfect facade to their audience. This alienates real people. Superman is boring until you introduce Kryptonite. Your flaws are your Kryptonite — they make you three-dimensional and worth following.

**Connection:** Maps to [[story-craft]]'s authenticity principles. The "real people don't give perfect speeches" rule is the same principle applied to ad characters. Flawed characters in [[story-native-format]] ads are more believable than heroes who do everything right.

### 4. Polarity Creates Fans (and Haters — That's the Point)

Neutral characters attract no one. Polarizing characters split the audience into three groups: those who agree, those who disagree, and those who are neutral. The agreers become diehard fans. The disagreers talk about you (free attention). The neutrals convert to fans or haters over time.

Why it matters: The temptation is to avoid offending anyone by staying neutral on everything. But neutrality is invisible. Taking a stand — having real opinions, drawing lines — is what turns "fair weather fans" into people who buy everything you sell and share everything you post.

**The Howard Stern principle:** People who hate him listen as much as people who love him. Both groups drive ratings. If he were neutral, no one would know his name.

## Frameworks

### Attractive Character Construction

Derived from: All 4 principles

Step 1: **Choose or identify the AC.** Is it you? A customer success story? A character that represents your brand? The AC must have a real (or real-feeling) backstory that your dream customer can identify with.
Step 2: **Write the backstory.** Where did the AC start? What was their situation that mirrors where the audience is now? What struggle did they face? The backstory must connect to what you're selling — if you sell dog wellness, the backstory involves a dog.
Step 3: **Collect parables.** List 5-10 short stories from the AC's life (or the life of real customers) that illustrate core beliefs. Each parable should teach one principle and end with a clear takeaway. These become the building blocks of all content.
Step 4: **Identify and share flaws.** What mistakes has the AC made? What do they struggle with? What are they honest about that most people hide? Pick 2-3 flaws that make the AC human without undermining credibility.
Step 5: **Take polarizing positions.** What does the AC believe strongly enough to say publicly, even if some people disagree? What line in the sand can the AC draw? Write 3-5 strong opinions related to the product's territory.
Step 6: **Choose the identity type** (see Identity Types below). This shapes the tone and approach of all communication.

When to use: When building a brand presence. When creating email sequences. When developing the "voice" of ad campaigns. Before writing any ongoing content series.

### Parable Development Process

Derived from: Principle 2 (Parables Teach and Stick)

Step 1: Identify the core principle you want your audience to believe (e.g., "investing in your dog's health now prevents suffering later").
Step 2: Search for a real moment — in the AC's life, in a customer's life, in a story you've encountered — where that principle was demonstrated through action, not explanation.
Step 3: Write the moment as a scene: who was there, what happened, what was said, what the AC realized. Keep it under 200 words.
Step 4: End with the lesson, stated simply. Not preachy — just the honest takeaway. "That's when I realized..." or "That changed how I thought about..."
Step 5: Test it: if you removed the lesson at the end, would the story still imply it? If yes, the parable is strong. If no, the story needs to be more specific.

When to use: Email sequences, social content, ad copy, landing pages. Any time you need to teach a principle, reach for a parable instead of a fact.

### Polarity Deployment

Derived from: Principle 4 (Polarity Creates Fans)

Step 1: Identify a belief in your market where most voices stay neutral or hedge. (e.g., "supplements for dogs" is a topic where most brands are vague and cautious.)
Step 2: Decide your stance. Not a controversial hot take for its own sake — a genuine belief backed by your experience or evidence.
Step 3: State it directly. No hedging, no "some people think." Own it. "Most dog supplements are garbage designed for the label, not the dog. We don't make those."
Step 4: Let the audience self-sort. Those who agree will respect the directness. Those who disagree will leave — and that's good, because they weren't going to buy anyway.
Step 5: Reinforce consistently. One polarizing statement isn't enough. The position must show up in content, emails, ads, and brand voice repeatedly to crystallize the tribe.

When to use: Brand [[positioning]]. Content strategy. When the brand voice feels generic or interchangeable with competitors. When email engagement is low (often a sign of being too neutral).

## Identity Types

The AC adopts one primary identity. Each shapes communication style:

### The Leader
Already achieved the result the audience wants. Leads them along the same path. Most common for coaches, consultants, experts.
- Tone: Authoritative but empathetic. "I've been where you are."
- Risk: Can feel distant if backstory isn't strong enough.

### The Adventurer / Crusader
Curious explorer who goes on journeys and brings back treasures (insights, frameworks, discoveries). Doesn't claim to have all the answers — shares what they find.
- Tone: Enthusiastic, discovery-oriented. "I went and found this for you."
- Risk: Can feel unfocused without a clear through-line.

### The Reporter / Evangelist
Doesn't have personal experience yet, so interviews experts and shares their wisdom. Builds credibility through association with high-status sources.
- Tone: Curated, interview-based. "I talked to 50 experts and here's what they all agreed on."
- Risk: Can feel secondhand. Works best as a starting identity that evolves into Leader or Adventurer.
- **Best entry point** for brands new to a market — you build authority by proximity to authorities.

### The Reluctant Hero
Didn't want the spotlight but was compelled by circumstance. Shares knowledge because they feel obligated, not because they sought attention.
- Tone: Humble, reluctant. "I never planned to share this, but..."
- Risk: Can feel performative if overplayed. Must be genuine.

## Proven Examples

### Jared (Subway)
Source: *DotCom Secrets* Ch. 6 | Result: 15+ year brand transformation

A 425-pound man lost weight eating Subway twice a day. Subway made him the AC. The fast food chain transformed from "another restaurant" into a "weight loss plan." Jared's backstory (morbidly obese → healthy) created instant identification for anyone trying to lose weight.

What it shows: The AC doesn't have to be the founder or CEO. A customer success story can BE the Attractive Character. The backstory is what creates the connection — without it, Jared is just a guy eating subs.

Pattern: [Customer with dramatic before/after] + [simple, repeatable method] + [consistent visibility] = brand differentiation through character, not product.

### Brunson's Audience Shift
Source: *DotCom Secrets* Ch. 6 | Result: Observed audience transformation at live events

When Brunson told only wrestling stories from stage, only male athletes bought. When he shared his fertility struggle and family story, wives and mothers started buying too. When he told a female student's success story (Joy Anderson), the audience went 50/50 male/female. Each backstory element attracted a different segment.

What it shows: The elements of your AC that you share directly determine WHO is attracted to you. Share athletic stories → attract athletes. Share family stories → attract parents. Share a woman's success → attract women. This is controllable and predictable.

Pattern: [Share backstory element from segment X's experience] → [Segment X identifies with AC] → [Segment X buys].

## Nuances and Exceptions

### Polarity has limits in health/wellness
Situation: Making strong polarizing claims about health products for pets.
Why the rule bends: Unlike business coaching or lifestyle brands, health claims carry regulatory risk. Being polarizing about "the supplement industry is garbage" is different from making polarizing medical claims.
What to do instead: Be polarizing about VALUES and APPROACH, not about health outcomes. "We believe most pet supplements are designed for the label, not the animal" is a values position. "Our product cures arthritis" is a medical claim. Polarity lives in the philosophy, not the efficacy claims.

### AC in story-native ads vs. brand AC
Situation: Each [[story-native-format]] ad has its own protagonist, but the brand also needs an Attractive Character.
Why the distinction matters: The ad character and the brand AC serve different functions. The ad character creates identification in a single reading. The brand AC builds following over time across all touchpoints.
What to do instead: Treat ad characters as INSTANCES of the brand AC's values. The brand AC's beliefs, tone, and polarizing positions should show up through the characters in the ads — even if the characters are different people with different stories.

### The Reporter identity can stall growth
Situation: Starting as a Reporter (interviewing experts) and never transitioning.
Why the rule bends: The Reporter builds initial credibility through association, but long-term followers want a Leader or Adventurer who has their own perspective. Staying in Reporter mode makes you a middleman, not a destination.
What to do instead: Use Reporter as a launchpad. Interview experts, synthesize their knowledge, develop your own perspective, then transition to Leader or Adventurer. Brunson did exactly this — started as a reporter, evolved into a leader.
