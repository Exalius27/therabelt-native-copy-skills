## Core Thesis

Show rate is the hidden multiplier between advertising and revenue. At 20% margins, a 20–40% improvement in show rate produces 2–3x profit — in year one alone. Most businesses ignore it because they think they can't control it. They're wrong. Four variables predict virtually all show rate variance: Availability, Speed, Personalization, and Volume.

**Three terms:**
- **Schedule rate:** % of engaged leads who schedule an appointment
- **Show rate:** % of scheduled leads who actually show up
- **Throughput:** total % of engaged leads who show up (schedule rate × show rate)

This framework covers medium-term lead nurture — the window after advertising, before sales.

## Principles

### 1. Availability Is the Biggest Single Variable

More open time slots → more schedules → more shows. Not just directionally true — Hormozi's ALAN data showed "an almost perfect correlation with throughput." On an absolute basis, businesses with the most time slots had the most schedules, shows, and purchases.

**Why it matters:** If you have no availability when leads have motivation, they either don't schedule at all, or schedule at a bad time and ghost. A bad-time appointment also steals a slot from someone who would've shown and bought.

**Key levers:**
- Take appointments 7 days per week (5 days = 40% of the week unavailable)
- Extend hours (6am–6pm PST covers the highest % of US conversions)
- Give 4 scheduling options per hour (every 15 min) — same appointment length, more start-time flexibility
- Offer all three booking methods: inbound calls, outbound calls, self-scheduling online

**On self-scheduling:** Make available times visible immediately on page load. Remove every unnecessary step. If info was already collected earlier, don't ask again.

**When to add friction:** If you're getting too many unqualified appointments, add a qualifying step (video to watch, sales letter to read, price displayed). The goal is not maximum volume — it's maximum qualified volume.

### 2. Speed Compounds

Contact speed has a dramatic, nonlinear effect:
- 391% more conversions when contacted within 60 seconds (Velocify)
- 7× more likely to qualify leads when contacted within 1 hour vs. even slightly later (Harvard Business Review)
- 60× more likely vs. waiting 24 hours (Harvard Business Review)
- 78% of customers buy from whoever responds first (Lead Connect)

The average business takes 42 hours to respond. This is the gap to exploit.

**Three speed levers:**

**Speed to first contact:** Gold standard is under 5 minutes. Under 60 seconds gets you compliments that close sales. A secondary benefit: faster first contact means fewer total follow-ups needed. The longer you wait, the colder the lead, and the more touches you need before they buy.

**Speed to first appointment:** Schedule within 72 hours. Show rates for same-day appointments are higher than future-day; show rates for "right now while we're talking" are 100%. Always try to pull appointments forward. If someone schedules 5+ days out, call them to offer an earlier slot.

**Speed of response:** After scheduling, leads often make buying decisions before the appointment itself. Whoever responds fastest to their questions in that window wins. Slow response signals disorganization and indifference — both kill show rates.

### 3. Personalization Creates a Reason to Show Up

Core mechanism: figure out what a specific lead wants → get that thing ready for them → tell them they can have it when they show up. This lowers the perceived risk of the appointment ("won't waste my time") and raises the cost of missing it ("if I skip, I lose something I want").

**Six tactics:**

**1. Use preferred communication channel.** Contact leads everywhere initially (text, call, DM, email). Then continue where they actually respond. Switch to richer medium later. Don't reach out once on one platform — that's how you miss people.

**2. Qualify leads.** Use application questions to filter out poor fits before the appointment. Remove low-probability leads from your calendar. This is not losing sales — it's freeing time for real buyers. One weight loss company that actively canceled bad appointments made more money than competitors who kept them all.

**3. Route best leads to best closers.** Score leads 1–5 or red/yellow/green based on how closely they match your best customers. Then route high scores to your best closers. A timeshare company that implemented this went from $200M to $1B in annual sales over five years.

**4. Segment messaging.** Easy version: research each lead before reaching out (profile, website, social). Know one specific thing about them. Hard version: build different follow-up sequences for each lead category. Hubspot improved email marketing ROI 7× after segmenting.

**5. Incentivize showing up.**
- *Push incentive:* Give something before they arrive. Gift card, free product, surprise. Reciprocity still works even when people know you're doing it.
- *Pull incentive (A/B):* Give a binary choice that assumes attendance. "Do you prefer black or pink?" — as soon as they answer, they've committed. Must meet three conditions: lead wants the thing, clear connection to your offer, you incurred real cost preparing it.

**6. Show matching proof.** Show case studies and testimonials that match the lead's demographics. Older white male professional → show results from older white male professionals. Between scheduling and the appointment. Between first and second call. And if they ghost: every 90 days, show them results from people who started when they dropped off. Creates feeling of being left behind.

### 4. Volume Overcomes Inertia

Most communication attempts fail for reasons that have nothing to do with interest: bad timing, distracted, missed notification, wrong moment. Reaching out again is not harassment — if they opted in, they asked to be contacted.

44% of salespeople give up after the first attempt. Average attempts before giving up: 1.3. This is the gap.

**Scheduling cadence:**
- Day 1: Call → double-dial immediately → voicemail → text → repeat 2 more times (few hours apart)
- Days 2–3: Call twice (morning + evening), text after second call each day
- Days 4–7: One call + one text per day
- After day 7: Transition to long-term nurture (free value + soft CTA until they re-engage, then restart)

Front-load the outreach. The more days that pass, the less likely they schedule.

**Automated reminders:** immediately after scheduling (confirm time, date, caller's number/area code, person they're meeting), then 24 hours before, 12 hours before, 3 hours before. Always include the area code you'll call from — doubles pickup rates. Always use their local time zone.

**Manual reminders (from a real phone, separate number):**
- Night before (~6pm)
- Morning of (~8am)
- 60 minutes before

Send multiple short messages in a row — how people actually text. Open with a question to gather more info. Compliment their answer. Ask the next question or offer to pull the appointment forward.

## Framework: BAMFAM (Book A Meeting From A Meeting)

Source: Sharran Srivatsaa, via Hormozi

**The rule:** Never end a call or appointment with "I'll follow up" or "let me send you some times." You're both there right now. Pull up the calendar. Pick a time.

**Why it works:** The probability of scheduling an appointment is highest when both parties are present. Once the call ends, the lead puts you in their rearview mirror. The gap between meetings is where leads fall through the cracks permanently.

**Application:** Treat reluctance to schedule the next meeting as an objection. Handle it. "Someone being dodgy about their next call is an objection."

Insurance company case: implementing BAMFAM as standard practice skyrocketed second-call show rates and increased sales significantly.

**Connection to [[high-probability-selling]]:** BAMFAM is the show-rate implementation of the Commitment Chain — every interaction ends with a concrete next commitment. No limbo.

## Framework: Lead Scoring and Routing

Derived from: the "best leads to best closers" principle

Step 1: Define your best customers — what actions did they take before buying? What demographics? What application answers?

Step 2: Embed those qualifying questions in opt-ins and pre-appointment applications.

Step 3: Score every inbound lead:
- Green / 4–5: matches best customer profile closely
- Yellow / 2–3: partial match, worth pursuing
- Red / 1: poor fit, remove or downsell

Step 4: Route greens to best closers first. Greens should never waste time with junior reps.

Step 5: "Yellows are the new gold" — don't discard yellows. Use them for practice, have junior reps work them, sometimes they close. The best closers typically practiced on the most volume, including yellow leads.

## Nuances and Exceptions

**More availability can occasionally hurt quality.** If you're getting too many unqualified people booking time, the solution isn't less availability — it's more friction before booking (qualifying video, application, price disclosure). Maximize qualified throughput, not raw throughput.

**Speed has diminishing returns past a threshold.** Under 5 minutes is dramatically better than 1 hour. Under 60 seconds is better than 5 minutes. Under 30 seconds vs. 60 seconds is negligible. The big gains are in eliminating the hour-plus delays, not in optimizing already-fast responses.

**Yellow leads need different handling than red leads.** Yellows are lower probability but not zero probability. Red leads genuinely don't fit — pursuing them costs more than they return. The distinction matters: yellows get worked, reds get removed.

**BAMFAM only works if the appointment is worth keeping.** If your sales conversation isn't valuable, booking the next meeting just delays an inevitable no. Nurture and the sales process reinforce each other — [[high-probability-selling]] for what to do in the meeting.
