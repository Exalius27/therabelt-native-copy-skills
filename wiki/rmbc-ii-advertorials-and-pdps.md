# RMBC II: Advertorials & Product Pages

Two critical pre-checkout pages in modern funnels. Advertorials and PDPs serve different purposes and audiences, but both follow systematic structures that can be generated and optimized using RMBC II methodology.

## Advertorials: Editorial Sales Copy

### What is an Advertorial?

A long-form sales piece disguised as editorial/journalistic content. Maintains the tone and format of an article or news story while making a sales argument.

**Why they work:** Users expect editorial content. Advertorials meet that expectation while slowly building a case for the product.

**Context:** Used in both native advertising (Native ad → Advertorial → PDP) and Meta (Video/static ad → Advertorial → PDP). The format adapts based on the ad that precedes it.

### Why Advertorials Exist: The Economic Case

Sending cold ad traffic directly to a standard PDP results in:
- **CPA too high** — prospects aren't educated or convinced enough to buy, so most bounce
- **AOV too low** — without pre-selling, buyers purchase the minimum (one unit instead of bundles)
- **Net result:** You lose money on every sale and can never scale

The advertorial fixes both problems. It pre-educates, builds belief, amplifies the pain point, and sells the value of buying more. After a good advertorial, the PDP just needs to facilitate the checkout — the selling is already done.

**The "dirty secret" of Shopify:** Basic product pages don't convert cold traffic. Shopify's success is built on the pre-sell pages (advertorials, listicles, interstitials) that go in front of them. The PDP is a checkout facilitator, not a sales tool.

**Simplifies testing:** With this model, you primarily test ads and advertorials. The PDP/checkout stays relatively stable. Testing lots of advertorial variants that all funnel to the same PDP is easier than constantly redesigning product pages.

### The Ad Determines the Advertorial Format (Meta)

On Meta, the same brand often runs two advertorial formats simultaneously — matched to the ad type:

| Ad Type | What the Ad Does | Advertorial Format |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Static/short ad | Hooks interest, doesn't reveal product | **Long story-based advertorial** — full emotional story, problem amplification, education, UMP reveal, product discovery, social proof |
| 1-2 minute video ad | Reveals the product, amplifies the problem, shows how it works | **Shorter product-focused advertorial** — more graphically heavy, features/benefits/social proof emphasis, less story needed |

The principle: the more pre-selling the ad does, the less the advertorial needs to do. When a video ad already educates on the problem and reveals the product, the advertorial can skip the story arc and go straight to proof and purchase justification. Both formats still lower CPA and increase AOV compared to going direct to PDP.

## Advertorial Lengths & Strategies

### Short Advertorial (200-500 words)

**Purpose:** Teaser. Builds enough curiosity/belief to justify clicking to next page (VSL or product page).

**Structure:**
1. **Headline** (news-style) — Hook that looks like interesting news
2. **Lead paragraph** — Problem statement in journalistic style
3. **Body 1** — Expand on problem, build tension
4. **Body 2** — Introduce mechanism (without full explanation)
5. **CTA** — "Watch the video" or "See results" linking to VSL/PDP

**Example headline:**
"Scientists Discover the Real Reason Your Energy Crashes—And It's Not What You Think"

**Example structure:**
> "For decades, we've blamed aging for afternoon energy crashes. But new research from Harvard suggests a different culprit: broken recovery cycles. And there's a solution."

Then: problem explanation, mechanism teaser, CTA.

### Long Advertorial (1500-3000 words)

**Purpose:** Full sales argument disguised as article. Can lead directly to checkout without VSL.

**Structure:**
1. **Headline** (news-style hook)
2. **Lead** (hook, relatability, problem statement)
3. **Problem deep-dive** (2-3 sections explaining root causes)
4. **Research/science section** (credibility, studies backing the angle)
5. **Mechanism explanation** (the solution, how it works)
6. **Case studies/testimonials** (social proof through customer stories)
7. **Objection handling** (address doubts, build certainty)
8. **Offer section** (pricing, options, guarantee)
9. **CTA** (clear button to checkout)

## Background Story Structure (Hero-Story Advertorials)

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-ecom-masterclass-whop-02-mp4.md

The background story is the emotional engine of hero-story advertorials. It's not just a story — it's a reflection of the reader's own experience, told through someone they trust. The story earns permission to introduce new beliefs, mechanisms, and eventually the product.

**Why story, not science:** Story bypasses resistance, disarms the brain, lowers the guard. When psychology techniques (loss aversion, future pacing, regret anticipation) are embedded in story, they don't feel manipulative. They feel like someone telling their experience.

**POV principle:** Describe struggles from the narrator's POV, not the reader's. "I tried minoxidil and it didn't work" instead of "You've tried minoxidil and it didn't work." First-person doesn't trigger resistance because it's not confrontation — it's identification. The reader thinks "me too" without feeling sold to.

### The 7-Step Sequence

1. **Establish the narrator.** Who is telling this story? Hero (same person as avatar), expert (sees it firsthand), caregiver (witness to someone's suffering).

2. **Emotional setup.** Surface the pain the reader lives with but hasn't said out loud. Mirror their internal struggle — confusion, fear, guilt, frustration. Goal: "This person gets it. They've seen what I'm living through."

3. **Surface emotional weight.** Intensify the pain through specific scenes. Shower breakdowns, avoiding mirrors, hiding under hoodies. Use avatar research language directly — stolen from their journals, not written by a brand.

4. **Walk through "tried everything."** Show failed solutions they've already tried (minoxidil, rosemary oil, scalp massagers). Don't explain WHY they failed yet — just show the growing frustration. Each attempt makes things feel worse, not better. Establishes the "nothing works" loop.

5. **Introduce the breaking point.** The crisis moment where ignoring the problem was no longer an option. Something shifted — a photo, a wedding coming up, a scalp showing through. This creates the emotional-practical bridge to searching for answers.

6. **Spark the search for truth.** The narrator shifts from passive pain to active pursuit. Not looking for another band-aid — asking deeper questions. Reading, researching, connecting dots. This is the bridge into the root cause section.

7. **Transition to discovery.** Something clicks — a pattern, a question, a post, a phrase — that shifts how the narrator sees the problem. You don't reveal the full answer yet, but the tone changes. What comes next is the root cause and mechanism explanation.

### Story as Awareness Ladder

The background story moves the reader through awareness levels smoothly:
- **Solution-aware** (they've tried things) → story mirrors their failed attempts
- **Root cause revealed** → story explains WHY nothing worked (not their fault — wrong approach)
- **Mechanism-aware** → story introduces the new mechanism through the narrator's discovery
- **Product-aware** → story transitions to the product as the logical solution

Each transition feels like narrative momentum, not education or selling.

## Root Cause Explanation — The Yes-Yes-Yes Sequence

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-ecom-masterclass-whop-03-mp4.md

The root cause explanation follows the background story. When done correctly, 50% of the selling is already done before the product appears.

**The belief chain:** Root cause belief → mechanism belief → product belief. Each "yes" depends on the prior one. If the reader doesn't believe the root cause, nothing downstream converts. If she believes the root cause AND the mechanism that fixes it, the product with that mechanism becomes the obvious choice.

**Three scenarios for root cause positioning:**

1. **Audience never heard it.** Your root cause is genuinely new to them. Most powerful position — you're the first to explain what's really going on.
2. **They've heard it but don't fully understand it.** They have a vague sense (e.g., "something hormonal") but never had it explained clearly. Your job: make it click.
3. **They never realized it applied to them.** They know the concept exists but didn't connect it to their specific problem. Your job: bridge the gap.

**4 psychology principles for root cause sections:**

1. **System 1 first.** The explanation must be processed automatically — no mental effort required. If the reader has to stop and think, you've lost the flow state. Emotions and gut-level understanding, not logic puzzles. Logic rides underneath, just enough to make it click.
2. **Processing fluency.** Short lines, no jargon, visual flow. The brain equates "easy to read" with "must be true." If it's hard to understand, they bounce.
3. **Villain externalization.** Give them something outside themselves to blame — a broken system, bad advice, a hiding mechanism. Shift from "I am the problem" to "now I know what's really wrong." Builds relief, urgency, and belief that it can be fixed. (See also [[landing-page-architecture]] Section 4: Root Cause + Villain Framing.)
4. **KISS.** Should feel like a real expert finally explaining it in plain English for the first time. Not a science lecture, not casual — clear, simple, believable.

**Analogies as the most powerful root cause tool:**

- Turn invisible problems into something visual the reader can picture
- Search Reddit, YouTube comments, and niche forums for analogies the audience already uses (e.g., sciatica = "jelly donut" for disc herniation)
- If no popular analogy exists, create one that's visual, simple, and emotionally accurate
- Pair analogies with actual visuals (animations, graphics, arrows) — today's audiences are screen-trained and struggle to visualize from words alone

**Connection to perceived value:** The perceived value of the product starts building HERE — not at the product section. Root cause → mechanism → product is the chain that makes the price feel justified before it appears. See [[copy-structure]] Product Buildup Section.

## Consequences Section + Unique Mechanism Solution

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-ecom-masterclass-whop-04-mp4.md

### The Full Advertorial Sequence (Refined)

Root cause → **Consequences of inaction** → Unique mechanism → Product reveal

The consequences section sits BETWEEN root cause and mechanism. After the reader understands what's causing their problem, show what happens if they DON'T fix it. This creates maximum urgency right before you present the solution.

**How to write consequences without being dramatic:**
- Stay in the narrator's POV: "I found that PCOS hair loss accelerates 2x faster than normal hair loss"
- Use social proof: "I found girls on Reddit sharing their journeys — from slight thinning to near-complete loss in months"
- Let the narrator's fear be the reader's fear: "I saw my future heading that way. I needed a solution now."
- Never lecture or threaten directly — the story carries the weight

### Unique Mechanism Solution: Making the Fix Feel Obvious

The mechanism explanation comes after consequences have maximized the urgency. Its job: show the reader there IS a real, fixable path forward — and it directly addresses the root cause they now believe in.

**The 80% threshold:** When the reader believes the root cause AND believes the mechanism can fix it, 80% of selling is done. The product just needs to contain that mechanism.

**Non-negotiables for mechanism sections:**

1. **Directly fixes the stated root cause.** If root cause = hormonal inflammation, the mechanism must address hormonal inflammation. Any gap between root cause and mechanism breaks the belief chain.

2. **Extends the same analogy.** If you used "jelly donut" for the root cause, use the same analogy for the mechanism: "remove the pressure → jelly dries and shrinks → no more nerve contact." Don't introduce a new visual system — build on what they already understand.

3. **Feels obvious in hindsight.** The best mechanism explanation makes the reader think: "Wait, why has no one explained it like this before? This just makes so much sense." Not flashy, not complex — obvious.

4. **Same psychology as root cause.** System 1, processing fluency, KISS. Someone half-asleep on their phone should get it. Simple = understandable = believable = actionable.

5. **Named mechanism.** Give your mechanism a proprietary name. Creates perceived value, innovation, and premium positioning. But the name should be memorable, not jargon.

**Connection:** The mechanism section feeds directly into [[copy-structure]] Product Buildup and Product Reveal sections. When mechanism belief is strong, the product reveal feels like a natural conclusion, not a pitch.

## Native Advertising: Congruence and Destination Matching

Native ads appear on news sites, weather sites, and content platforms where users are in article-reading mode. This creates specific requirements beyond the Meta advertorial model.

### The Congruence Problem (Native-Specific)

On Meta, the primary problem without an advertorial is economic (CPA too high, AOV too low). On native, there is an additional problem: **congruence**. Users are reading articles. Clicking a native ad that looks like an article link and landing on a product page breaks the experience. The reader thinks: "What the hell? Where am I?"

The advertorial solves this by maintaining the content consumption mode: read article → click interesting link → read another article (the advertorial) → arrive at product page already pre-sold. Each transition feels natural, not jarring.

### Advertorial Length Matches Destination

| Destination | Advertorial Length | Why |
|-------------|-------------------|-----|
| **VSL** | Short (under 500 words) | Just enough to maintain congruence. The VSL does the heavy education. Example: "Breathing issues? Groundbreaking discovery for clear breathing. Watch now." |
| **PDP** | Long (1500-3000+ words) | The advertorial does ALL the pre-selling. The PDP is just a checkout facilitator. Full story + education + UMP + social proof. |

### Pain-Point-Specific Advertorials to Generic PDP

A powerful native strategy: write many advertorials targeting specific pain points that all funnel to the same product page. Example: an all-purpose cleaner runs advertorials about greasy ovens, bathroom stains, and carpet spills. Each advertorial matches a specific native ad angle. The PDP stays generic ("all-in-one cleaning solution") and never changes.

This works because the advertorial bridges the gap. By the time readers reach the PDP, they already know the product handles their specific problem AND other problems. The general product page no longer feels disconnected.

### Native vs. Meta Compliance

Native ad networks tend to have lower compliance standards than Meta. More aggressive claims, health scares, and authority figure positioning appear on native. This doesn't mean it's advisable — it means you'll see it when researching swipe files, and you should separate "what works on native" from "what's compliant."

### Native Advertorial Benchmarks and Campaign Tactics

**Landing page CTR targets (from advertorial to next page):**
- Short jump pages (to VSL): 40-50% CTR
- Medium/long advertorials (to PDP): 25-30% CTR
- Don't place CTAs too early on medium/long pages — pre-qualify the traffic first

**Ad-level on native:** Curiosity-driven images outperform product images. Weird household item images (baking soda, kitchen ingredients) imply an at-home solution and drive high CTR. Compensate for high CPMs with high CTR to achieve low CPCs.

**Keyword patterns:** Certain words consistently convert on native (e.g., "bionic" for knee sleeves, "itchy" for skincare). Regular ad spying trains pattern recognition for these trigger words. Use tools like Anstrex — filter by "gravity" for current momentum, "ad strength" for historical winners.

**Campaign setup for native (Taboola, Outbrain):**
- Block mail traffic (Yahoo Mail, AT&T Mail), push, and gaming publishers at launch — these are different ad formats with different user behavior
- Start with a 100-150 publisher whitelist from your rep
- Get vertical-specific benchmarks (CPM, CTR, CPC, CPA) from your rep before launching
- Run dedicated campaigns for mail traffic separately if testing it later

## Two Advertorial Frameworks: Personal Story vs. Authority Figure

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course)

Two distinct advertorial archetypes, each with different narrator positioning:

1. **Nightmare Personal Story Advertorial** — Narrator is the avatar (or someone like them). Tells a first-person story of suffering, searching, and finding the solution. Works through identification and emotional transportation. This is the framework detailed in the Background Story Structure section above.

2. **Authority Figure-Based Advertorial** — Narrator is an expert or authority figure (doctor, researcher, specialist). Tells a discovery story from the expert's perspective. Works through credibility and mechanism revelation. Example: "Harvard Child Development Expert Exposes Why Smart Kids Are Falling Behind by Age Seven."

**When to use which:** Authority works when the market responds to expertise and credentials (health, finance, education). Personal story works when the market responds to relatability and shared experience (beauty, lifestyle, pet care). Both follow the same underlying structure (problem → root cause → mechanism → product) but with different narrator trust dynamics.

**Practitioner result:** Authority-based advertorial took one store from $1K/day to $7K/day. The framework produced usable output across niches (including children's toys) without product-specific research, suggesting the structure itself carries significant persuasion weight.

## Advertorial Tone & Style

**Key principle:** Maintains editorial tone throughout, even in the offer section.

**What NOT to sound like:**
- Sales pitch ("Buy now!")
- Hype ("This will change your life!")
- Marketing-speak ("Transform your results")

**What TO sound like:**
- Journalist explaining a discovery
- Expert sharing knowledge
- Friend sharing something they learned

**Voice techniques:**
- Use "researchers found..." vs. "we discovered..."
- Use data/statistics to build credibility
- Use case stories, not just testimonials
- Maintain curiosity-driven pacing (don't reveal everything at once)

## Advertorial Self-Audit Tools

### The Sell-Sentence Diagnostic

Read your advertorial back. Mark every sentence where you stopped telling a story and started selling. Every one of those marks is where someone closed the tab. If you find clusters of sell-sentences, the product isn't woven into the story — it's interrupting it. The product must live inside the narrative as the vehicle for transformation, never as a pitch breaking through the surface.

### Write Transformation First, Then Backwards

Before writing the advertorial, answer: "What does their life look like when this problem is completely solved?" Write that end state first. Then write the advertorial backwards from there — the before state, the agitation, the mechanism discovery, the product, all leading to the transformation you already wrote. Spend more time in the before state than the after. If they finish the advertorial feeling different (with hope) than when they started, the transformation worked.

### Sticky CTA Timing Rule

Never show a sticky CTA button before the reader is product-aware. The sticky button should appear only AFTER the product has been introduced. Showing it during the problem/agitation/mirroring phase breaks the story and triggers ad-guard before the reader is ready to act.

## Advertorial Common Mistakes

**Tone breaks.** Switching from editorial to salesy at the offer section. Maintain tone throughout.

**Buried mechanism.** Putting the solution reveal at the end. Move it to middle (around 1000 words) where it can be explained fully.

**Too much jargon.** If a lay person can't understand it, rewrite.

**Weak problem setup.** Spend 2-3 sections building problem; this creates tension for mechanism reveal.

**Fake urgency/scarcity.** In advertorials, fake scarcity feels wrong. Use real scarcity or none at all.

## Product Pages (PDPs): The Checkout Facilitator

### What is a Product Page?

A sales page focused on selling one specific product. Ranges from minimal (product image, price, checkout button) to comprehensive (full sales argument).

### The Core Principle: Selling Must Happen Somewhere

With very few exceptions, every product requires education and persuasion before someone buys. The PDP length is determined by how much selling happened before it:

- **Static/short ad + short advertorial → LONG PDP** (the PDP must do the heavy selling)
- **Long video ad + long advertorial → SHORT PDP** (just facilitate the checkout — selling already done)
- **15-min video + long advertorial → still long PDP** (rare exception, tested by Nooro — unusual but working for some brands)

The only exception: products with massive pre-existing demand (GLP-1 medications, trend-hijacked products) where everyone already knows it works and wants it. Then the PDP just needs trust signals and a checkout button.

### PDP Generation: Template Extraction Workflow

The fastest way to create new PDPs in 2025:

1. **Find a PDP you like** (working, well-designed, similar vertical). Happy Mammoth, Emma Relief, Primal Queen are strong models.
2. **Extract the template:** Give Claude the PDP URL or copy and say: "Review this product page, then create a templatized outline that captures the structure and all common elements. This should be something someone could use for other products and brands to produce similar pages."
3. **Generate new PDP:** Give Claude the template + a brief (or product URL) and say: "Using this structure, create a new PDP for [product]. Here is the brief with all product information."
4. **Iterate:** Adjust length, fix claims, verify congruence with the funnel preceding it.

You don't need a dedicated Claude project for PDPs. Just find the structure you want, extract it, feed new information, and generate. The structure is what you're swiping, not the copy. Because you're feeding it a brief with unique product data, the output won't be a direct swipe.

**Mindset:** There is no single perfect PDP structure. Multiple structures will work. "Hit doubles, not home runs" — accept that any solid structure can convert, test multiple, and don't chase perfection.

**Why two types:** Different markets need different persuasion levels.

## Short Product Pages (Minimal Pre-sale)

**When:** Solution-aware or product-aware markets. Buyers are ready to buy; you just need to show the product and remove friction.

**Structure:**
1. **Hero section** — Product image, benefit headline
2. **Key benefits** (3-5 bullets) — Quick reasons why
3. **Pricing options** — Clear, visual pricing table
4. **FAQ section** (5-8 questions) — Objection handling
5. **Guarantee** — Risk reversal
6. **Checkout button** — Prominent, repeated

**Example structure:**
- Image of product
- "Feel 40% more energetic by day 3"
- Bullets: "Natural ingredients," "FDA-tested," "Ships in 24 hours"
- Pricing: $47 one-time OR $39/month subscription
- FAQ: Is it safe? Will it work for me? How long until results?
- 180-day guarantee
- "Add to Cart" button

**Key principle:** Assume visitor is ready. Get out of the way. Remove friction.

### Long Product Pages (Full Sales Letter)

**When:** Problem-aware markets. Buyers know the problem but need convincing on YOUR solution.

**Structure:**
1. **Hero section** — Hook headline, product image
2. **Problem deep-dive** (2-3 sections) — Build tension, establish stakes
3. **Solution introduction** — Introduce product naturally
4. **Mechanism explanation** — Why it works (detailed)
5. **Benefits section** — What they'll feel/see/become
6. **Social proof section** — Testimonials, results, social validation
7. **Objection handling** — FAQ-style but persuasive
8. **Offer section** — Pricing, options, comparison table
9. **Guarantee section** — Money-back promise, risk removal
10. **Final CTA** — Prominent checkout button

**Example structure:**
- "You Know That Feeling When Your Energy Crashes at 3 PM?"
- Problem deep-dive (2 sections on energy physiology)
- "Here's What Actually Works" (introduce product as solution)
- How it works (mechanism explanation)
- "What You'll Feel" (results section)
- 5-10 testimonials from customers
- FAQ handling objections
- Pricing table with options
- 180-day guarantee with clear terms
- "Add to Cart" button (prominent, repeated)

## PDP Decisions Based on Market Awareness

**Solution-aware buyer** (shopping between options)
- Go SHORT
- Lead with benefits, not education
- Emphasize differentiation (why this one)
- Showcase pricing competitiveness
- Prominent guarantee to remove risk

**Problem-aware buyer** (knows problem, exploring solutions)
- Go MEDIUM-LONG
- Explain mechanism clearly
- Show social proof (others like them succeeded)
- Address specific objections (safety, results timeframe, cost)
- Guarantee is crucial (they're uncertain)

**Unaware buyer** (if directing to PDP instead of VSL/advertorial)
- Go LONG
- Spend sections on problem education
- Use case stories extensively
- Detailed mechanism explanation
- Extra reassurance throughout

## The 6 Questions Every PDP Must Answer

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course)

A high-converting PDP must clearly address these 6 customer questions. Answer them repeatedly throughout the page — in headlines, copy, visuals, and FAQs. Not once each — woven throughout.

1. **What is the product?** Show it in action. Simple headline, clear images/videos. Product title should highlight what it DOES, not just a generic name. ("Obvi Collagen Fat Burner — Boosts Metabolism & Supports Weight Loss" not just "Obvi Collagen.")
2. **Why does it exist?** Explain the problem it solves or the need it fulfills. Story works best here.
3. **How does it benefit me?** Translate features into personal benefits and outcomes.
4. **Why is this the best option on the market?** Highlight differentiators: unique mechanism, social proof, guarantees, awards.
5. **If I order now, how soon do I get it?** Be transparent with shipping times, fulfillment speed, delivery expectations.
6. **What happens if I don't like it?** Clear risk-reversal: refund policy, satisfaction guarantee, easy returns.

**Connection:** These 6 questions map directly to the [[pdp-image-strategy]] 7 essential images — product images should answer most of these visually for visitors who never read the copy.

## PDP Congruency — Tailoring Pages to Ad Desire

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course)

**The principle:** When an ad targets a specific desire and gets high CTR but low CVR, the problem is often congruency — the PDP speaks to every desire equally instead of matching the specific desire the visitor clicked on.

**The fix:** Tailor benefit bullets and reviews to the specific desire being tested in the ad.

- Person clicks ad about specific desire → lands on PDP tailored 100% to that desire = highest CVR
- Person clicks ad about specific desire → lands on generic PDP that spreads across all desires = lower CVR

**Result from practitioner:** CVR went from 3.2% to 6.5% with ~15 minutes of effort per variant. Changes as simple as swapping benefit bullets and selecting reviews that handle objections for that ONE desire.

**Secondary benefit:** Lower CPM. Fewer people bounce immediately from the LP, which signals quality to the ad platform.

**Connection:** This is the PDP equivalent of the congruence principle in [[rmbc-ii-funnel-structure]] — each step in the funnel must feel like a continuation, not a pivot. If the ad sells desire X, the page must deliver on desire X immediately.

## PDP Objection-Handling Review Prompt

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course community)

An audit prompt for running any PDP through multiple AI models (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) to surface the biggest customer objections the page fails to overcome. When multiple models flag the same objection, it's almost certainly real.

**Role:** Direct-Response Copywriting & CRO Expert.

**Mission:**
1. Analyze the product page and identify up to 10 biggest, realistic objections customers would still have after reading the page.
2. Perform a separate, detailed audit of the Hero section (top viewport — images + copy only).
3. Prioritize the top 3-5 objections the Hero must address, and provide Hero overlay copy (headline + two ultra-short bullets) that directly neutralizes them.

**Non-Negotiable Rules:**
- Objections must be real and important — no generic, nitpicky, or unrealistic items.
- Write objections in raw customer language (how they'd say it on a forum, in reviews, or to friends: short, emotional, unpolished).
- Ground everything in research docs provided (customer avatar, forum analysis, demographics). If unsupported by research, exclude it.
- Provide fewer than 10 if fewer than 10 meaningful objections exist.
- Do not rate or critique the offer — only analyze what's covered or uncovered in messaging.
- Hero overlay bullets: 5 words max, benefit/"why different" focused.

**Required Inputs:**
- Product page (URL or PDF)
- Hero screenshot(s) (top viewport)
- Customer research docs (forum analysis, avatar profiles, demographics)
- If any input is missing, pause and ask. Do not infer or hallucinate.

**Output Format (exact section headers):**

1. **HERO AUDIT — Images & Copy Only:** What images imply (1-3 bullets), what copy claims (1-3 bullets), what Hero clearly covers (2-5 bullets), what Hero fails to cover (2-5 bullets, customer-impact focus).

2. **TOP OBJECTIONS (UP TO 10) — Customer Language Only:** Numbered list, one sentence each in raw customer voice, evidenced by research. Example: "I've already spent money on similar products — why would this be any different?"

3. **PRIORITY FOR HERO (3-5 Biggest):** Highest-impact objections the Hero must address first. One line each, customer voice.

4. **HERO OVERLAY COPY (3 Variations):** Three testable overlay sets, each = headline (solution-focused) + 2 bullets (5 words max each — root-cause, mechanism, proof, credibility, or risk reversal).

5. **COVERAGE MAP (Table):** Objection (customer language) | Covered on page? (Yes/No) | Where it's (not) covered | Evidence from research (phrase or theme).

6. **RISKY GAPS (One-Liners):** 3-5 concise lines on biggest conversion risks if these objections remain unanswered above the fold.

**Connection:** This prompt operationalizes the 6 PDP questions framework above — it tests whether the page actually answers them from the customer's perspective, not the marketer's. Run across multiple AI models for triangulation.

## PDP Common Mistakes

**One-size-fits-all page.** Short for solution-aware, long for problem-aware. Know your audience first.

**Weak problem setup.** If page is long, spend time building problem. Creates tension for solution reveal.

**Vague benefits.** "Amazing results" is filler. "Visible skin improvement in 30 days" is selling.

**Overwhelming options.** Too many pricing tiers confuse buyers. Keep to 2-3 clear options.

**FAQ that doesn't address real objections.** Mine objections from research and testimonials, not guesses.

**Guarantee that feels risky.** "90 days" feels shorter than "90 days, no questions asked, full refund." Be explicit.

## Advertorial vs. PDP: When to Use Each

### Use Advertorial When:
- Target is unaware or problem-aware
- You need to build belief gradually
- You have space to tell a story
- Competitor funnel shows advertorials working
- Market expects editorial tone

**Typical flow:** Native ad → Advertorial → VSL → Checkout

### Use PDP When:
- Target is solution-aware or product-aware
- You need speed to checkout
- Budget is limited (shorter copy)
- Platform is Google Shopping or direct e-comm
- Audience is ready to buy

**Typical flow:** Ad → PDP → Checkout

### Use Both When:
- Very high-ticket offer (coach + education before checkout)
- Complex product (full education, then detailed PDP for final confirmation)
- Testing (advertorial vs. PDP A/B test)

**Typical flow:** Ad → Advertorial → PDP → Checkout

## Optimization & Testing

**Short advertorials:** Test headlines, hook angles, and mechanism presentation.

**Long advertorials:** Test problem depth, mechanism metaphors, and social proof order.

**Short PDPs:** Test headline, benefit bullets, and CTA button copy.

**Long PDPs:** Test problem deep-dive length, mechanism metaphors, objection handling, and pricing presentation.

## Swipe File Patterns: Real Advertorials in the Wild (RMBC II Reference)

Patterns extracted from 14 real native ads (6 short, 4 medium, 4 long) in the RMBC II swipe file collection. These confirm and extend the theoretical framework above.

### Editorial Disguise Techniques (How Real Ads Suppress Ad-Guard)

Every single swipe file ad uses at least 3 of these techniques simultaneously:

1. **Fake publication branding.** "Health Headlines," "Beauty Love Laugh," "Rising Health Trends," "Urology Research Group News." None are real publications. All use real-publication design patterns (masthead, byline, date, view count).
2. **Byline with writer name.** "By Sarah McKinsey," "Linda Collins - Health & Beauty," "By Dr. John Martin." Always a person, never a brand.
3. **Recency signals.** "Posted - 20 minutes ago," "3 HOURS AGO," "Monday, March 31, 2025." Creates urgency and editorial freshness.
4. **View/engagement counts.** "185,622 views," "241,006 views," "12,325 views." Social proof that this is content people are consuming, not an ad they're seeing.
5. **Category labels.** "U.S. | MEN'S HEALTH," "Health & Beauty | Advertorial," "TECHNOLOGY." Positions content within editorial taxonomy.

**Key principle confirmed:** The RMBC II theory says advertorials "maintain editorial tone." In practice, they go further — they actively fabricate the signals of editorial publishing. The disguise is structural, not just tonal.

### The Mechanism Reveal Gradient

How mechanism depth scales with ad length (confirmed across all 14 ads):

| Length | Mechanism Treatment | Example |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| Short | Named but unexplained | "Island loophole," "failing fibroblasts" |
| Medium | Named, root cause explained, solution teased | "Inflammation causes all ear problems. This spray targets it." |
| Long | Full explanation with how/why + comparison to alternatives | "Nanocoil fibers let head sink in, then lift neck into alignment. Unlike foam (flattens), polyester (no support)..." |

**Principle:** The mechanism reveal IS the trust-builder. How much mechanism you reveal determines how much belief you can generate — and therefore how close to checkout you can drive from that page alone.

### CTA Patterns by Length

- **Short:** Always to video. Never to product. "Watch Now," "Click Here To Watch The Video."
- **Medium:** To video OR product page. "Click on your state below to check if you qualify." Some use interactive elements (state selection, quiz).
- **Long:** Direct to checkout with pricing. "GET 60% OFF NOW," "APPLY DISCOUNT & CHECK AVAILABILITY." Full offer stack on the same page.

**Principle confirmed:** Ad length determines how many funnel steps the page can absorb. A long advertorial IS the funnel — it does the work of ad + VSL + product page in one asset.
