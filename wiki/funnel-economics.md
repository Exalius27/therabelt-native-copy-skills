## Core Thesis

Scaling is not about finding one magic ad or one perfect landing page. It is about knowing four numbers (CPA, AOV, COGS, LTV) and systematically pulling five levers. The business that understands these levers and compounds small improvements across them will outscale the business that only optimizes ads.

---

## The Four Numbers You Must Know

**1. CPA (Cost Per Acquisition):** What it costs on average to acquire one customer — whether through ads, affiliates, or other channels.

**2. AOV (Average Order Value):** Gross revenue per customer on day zero — including upsells, bumps, and package selections.

**3. COGS (Cost of Goods Sold):** Fixed costs to fulfill one customer's order — product, shipping, processing fees, commissions.

**4. Gross Profit:** AOV minus CPA minus COGS. This is day-zero economics before LTV.

**Day-zero formula:** Gross Profit = AOV - CPA - COGS

If gross profit is positive, you can scale immediately. If negative, you need either lever optimization or strong LTV to justify the loss.

## The Five Levers

Every optimization you can make to a funnel falls into one of five categories. They compound — a 15% improvement on three levers simultaneously creates a much larger total gain than any single lever at 45%.

### Lever 1: Lower CPA

**At the ad level:**
- Better ads (higher CTR, lower CPM) = more visitors for same spend
- Example: Same $3,000 ad spend, but doubling visitors from 1,000 to 2,000/day cuts CPA in half ($150 → $75)

**At the conversion level:**
- Better landing pages, CRO testing, friction reduction
- Example: Improving conversion rate from 1% to 2.5% on same traffic cuts CPA proportionally

**Alternate traffic sources:**
- New ad platforms, better media buyers, affiliate recruitment at lower CPA
- Branded keywords on Google (high buyer intent, often $20 CPA vs. $150 on cold Meta)

**Recovery channels:**
- Abandoned cart sequences (email + SMS + call center)
- Decline recovery
- These have zero or low CPA relative to cold acquisition

### Lever 2: Lower COGS

- Negotiate with manufacturer ($5/bottle → $3/bottle = $2 saved × volume)
- Change fulfillment partners for lower shipping costs
- Lower merchant processing fees (5% → 2.5% per transaction)
- At 1,000 sales/day, saving $2.50/transaction = $75,000/month

### Lever 3: Increase AOV

- **Money close copy:** Repeatedly direct customers to largest package as "most popular"
- **Add packages:** Going from 1-bottle + 3-bottle to 1 + 3 + 6 immediately increases AOV
- **Offer bumps on checkout:** "Add this for only $20 more" with near-zero COGS. **But test carefully — bumps can cannibalize upsell AOV.** Example: A $27 bump at 40% take rate adds $10.80 to AOV. But if that bump reduces your $97 upsell take rate from 20% ($19.40) to 7% ($6.79), total AOV drops by $1.81. The buyer feels "I already bought something extra" and declines the upsell. Always test bump present vs. absent on *total funnel AOV*, not just bump conversion rate alone.
- **Better upsells:** Improving upsell 1 take rate from 20% to 40% can add $15-25 to bottom line per customer
- **Split test pricing:** Often the highest-impact, least-tested lever

### Lever 4: Increase Conversions

- Landing page copy and design testing
- CRO: buttons, page elements, testimonial placement, above-the-fold variations
- Customer journey optimization (quiz funnels, friction reduction)
- Heat maps and session recordings to find drop-off points
- A/B testing is king — every element is testable

### Lever 5: Increase LTV

- **Subscription/continuity:** Monthly rebills are the single most powerful profit multiplier
- **Email list monetization:** Warm promotions, affiliate offers, bundles to existing buyers
- **Phone sales:** Call center selling additional packages, upsells, cross-sells
- **Stick rate optimization:** Keeping subscribers longer (better onboarding, results tracking, engagement)

## Pricing Split Tests — The Most Underused Lever

Most brands never test pricing. Two real examples:

**Example 1 (Skincare):** Single bottle price increased from $59 to $79. Other prices unchanged. Result: AOV increased, conversion rate stayed exactly the same. Pure margin gain.

**Example 2 (Blood sugar supplement):** Pricing dropped from $69/$59/$49 to approximately $39/$34/$29. Result: Conversions doubled or tripled. Lower AOV per unit but dramatically higher volume enabled scaling.

Key insight: People always want to go lower, but you must test going higher. Customers do not know your COGS. See also [[rmbc-ii-funnel-structure]] (CRO section) for related pricing test data.

### Premium Pricing as Value Creation (Hormozi)

Higher prices don't just increase revenue — they increase the value the customer experiences. In blind wine tastings, identical wine labeled as "expensive" was rated significantly better than the same wine labeled "cheap." Price literally changes perceived quality.

Three strategic implications:
1. **Investment drives compliance.** The more someone pays, the harder they work. A $50 gym membership gets ignored. A $5,000 coaching program gets used. Higher-paying customers get better results because their financial commitment compels action.
2. **Category-of-one positioning.** The goal is not to be slightly above market — it's to be so far above that the customer thinks "this must be something entirely different." At that price gap, direct comparison stops and you operate as a monopoly.
3. **Customer quality filter.** Premium pricing attracts committed buyers and repels bargain hunters. Hormozi's Gym Launch competitor served the same market with the same sales volume but made 70x less profit — because they accepted anyone while Hormozi accepted only high-value customers at premium prices.

The math: Same number of total sales, same vertical. Competitor LTV: $6-8K. Hormozi LTV: $45K+. The margins compound because better customers also churn less, require less support, and refer more.

See also [[offer-design]] (Grand Slam Offer) for the Value Equation framework, and [[money-models]] for the offer structures that execute premium pricing.

## The LTV Compounding Effect

This is where subscription businesses become exponentially profitable. The math:

**Setup:** $166/month average rebill profit per customer (after accounting for churn). No additional ad cost on rebills.

**At 1,000 sales/day (30,000 customers/month):**
- Month 1: Day-zero gross profit only
- Month 2: Day-zero profit + 30,000 × $166 = $4.98M additional
- Month 3: Day-zero profit + 60,000 × $166 = $9.96M additional

Even at 100 sales/day, month 2 adds $498K and month 3 adds $996K in rebill profit.

**Why this changes the CPA conversation:** If LTV is $2,000 over 6 months with 50% margins ($1,000 net), you can afford to lose $146 per customer on day zero and still build a wildly profitable business. The willingness to go negative on day zero is a competitive advantage — it lets you outspend competitors for traffic and dominate the market.

**Agora model:** Famously acquires customers at a loss for months, knowing LTV justifies it. Requires either upfront capital or enough accumulated rebills to fund ongoing acquisition.

## Real Case Study: Peptide Company ($300 CPA → $97)

**Starting position:** CPA $300, AOV $299, COGS $145. Losing $146 per customer.

**Three changes made:**
1. **Simplified customer journey** (friction reduction in medical questionnaire flow): Conversion rate doubled from ~1% to ~2%, CPA dropped from $300 to $150
2. **Better landing page variants:** Conversion rate improved to 2.5%, CPA dropped to $125
3. **Abandoned cart recovery** (call center at $125/conversion + email/SMS sequences recovering 15% of 300 daily abandons): Effective CPA dropped to $97.82

**Result:** From -$146/customer to +$56.18/customer. At 1,000 sales/day = $56K daily gross profit.

**Still untouched levers:** Better ad creatives, new ad platforms, affiliate traffic, branded Google keywords, COGS negotiation, upsells, pricing tests, LTV optimization. Each would compound further.

## Business Goal Determines Strategy

The levers you prioritize depend on your goal:

| Goal | Primary Focus |
|------|--------------|
| **Maximize net profit** | All five levers, prioritized by impact |
| **Maximize revenue** | CPA reduction + volume (revenue is a vanity metric without profit) |
| **Maximize new customers** | CPA reduction + willingness to go negative (lead gen / acquisition mode) |
| **Lifestyle business** | High margin, low complexity — optimize AOV and COGS, keep volume manageable |

## Diagnostic: Finding the Bottleneck

When a funnel underperforms, diagnose systematically:

1. **Start with the goal:** What CPA/ROAS do I need to be profitable?
2. **Get benchmarks:** Ask people running similar offers what their metrics look like (masterminds, peers, not internet gurus)
3. **Check ad metrics:** Are CPM and CPC in line with benchmarks? If wildly high, ad creative or targeting is the issue.
4. **Check funnel metrics:** What % of landing page visitors reach checkout? What % of checkout visitors convert? Use Google Analytics, heat maps, session recordings.
5. **Find the break:** If ad metrics are fine but funnel converts poorly, the funnel is the bottleneck. If funnel converts well but CPC is too high, ads are the bottleneck. If both are fine but AOV is too low, the offer is the bottleneck.
6. **Test the highest-impact lever first:** Unoptimized funnels can often double conversion rates with one structural change. Mature funnels need incremental improvements that compound.
