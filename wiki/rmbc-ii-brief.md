# RMBC II: The Brief 2.0

A 25-point strategic document that bridges research and copy generation. The brief 2.0 is designed for AI language models (primarily Claude) but remains useful for human copywriters. It consolidates product positioning, customer psychology, offer structure, and copy strategy into one reference document that instructs both LLMs and copy chiefs.

## Why Brief 2.0 vs. Brief 1.0?

**Brief 1.0** (from RMBC I) was a document you'd hand to a human copywriter. It asked human questions: "Who's your audience? What are their pain points? What's the unique mechanism?"

**Brief 2.0** adds specificity for LLM generation while remaining structured for human thinking:
- More detailed mechanism description (so LLMs don't struggle)
- Explicit credibility elements (studies, endorsements, industry recognition)
- Specific future-pacing language expectations
- Hook and objection inventory ready to test
- Multi-option pricing clearly mapped
- Story framework templates

You don't fill the entire brief from scratch. You pull from your **unified research document**, then add product-specific details, pricing, guarantee, and offers.

## The 25-Point Brief Template

### SECTION 1: AUDIENCE & PSYCHOLOGY

**1. Who is the target audience?**
- Primary demographic (age, life stage, income, location if relevant)
- Psychological profile (worldview, aspirations, fears, identity they want)
- Platform context (where they encounter ads, what they're doing when they see them)

Example: "Woman, 45-55, empty nester or managing household + aging dog. Believes she's been invisible for years. Wants proof that she's still capable and youthful. Scrolling Instagram/Facebook mid-morning while coffee cools."

**2. What are their primary pain points?** (short-term & long-term)
- Short-term: Immediate frustration (slow energy, visible aging, product failure)
- Long-term: Existential fear (aging invisibility, becoming irrelevant, watching capability decline)

Use verbatim language from research when possible.

**3. What does it look like when their pain points are solved?** (physical, emotional, social, psychological)
- Physical transformation: "Visibly younger appearance, more energy, movements feel easier"
- Emotional: "Confident, hopeful, proud of how she looks and feels"
- Social: "Friends notice and comment; feels seen again"
- Psychological: "Belief that she's not past her prime shifts"

**4. What solutions already exist for this problem?** (and why aren't they working?)
- Existing solutions: Competitors, DIY attempts, supplements, procedures
- Why they fail: Cost, side effects, inconvenience, time, inadequate results
- Psychological block: Buyer believes solution category itself is flawed ("Supplements don't work")

---

### SECTION 2: MECHANISM & UNIQUE ANGLE

**5. What is the unique mechanism?** (detailed enough for LLM clarity)
- The HOW and WHY it works
- What makes it different from competitors
- Ground it in research or science, not invention

Example: "Clinically-proven compound that penetrates the dermal layer within 6 minutes, triggering collagen remodeling at the cellular level. Competitors use surface-active ingredients; this one works systemically."

**6. Is there a nickname or shorthand for the mechanism?**
- Creates hook potential
- Makes mechanism memorable and testable
- Examples: "The 7-Second Bowel-Releasing Ritual," "The 30-Second Breakfast Hack," "The Collagen Remodeling Protocol"

**7. Who is the main speaker/hero in this story?**
- Quick bio: What's their credential and relatability?
- Why should the customer trust them?
- Tired mom who discovered the solution? Doctor who sees patients suffering? Customer with breakthrough results?

**8. Is there a secondary expert/speaker?**
- Doctor endorsing the solution?
- Co-founder doing deep dive on mechanism?
- Customer testimonial with expertise?

---

### SECTION 3: PRODUCT DETAILS & PROOF

**9. What are the main ingredients/features?** (if applicable)
- Primary active ingredients
- Supporting ingredients
- Key differentiators vs. competitors

**10. What 2-3 studies prove efficacy?** (if applicable)
- Study name, institution, key finding
- Relevance to customer's pain point
- Quotable results (e.g., "73% saw visible results in 2 weeks")

Use Perplexity to pull citations and studies if not provided.

**11. What are bold product claims you can safely make?**
- Results people can expect
- Timeframe for results
- Specificity matters ("More energy" is vague; "Feel 40% more energetic by day 3" is testable)

**12. What are emotional/transformation claims?**
- Beyond product features
- Identity shift: "Go from invisible to unforgettable"
- Belief shift: "Prove to yourself you're not past your prime"

---

### SECTION 4: OFFER STRUCTURE & PRICING

**13. What is the normal retail price?**
- The anchor price (even if fictional, it sets expectation)
- Used to justify the discount

**14. What is the actual price prospect pays?**
- Lisps the tension (discrepancy creates urgency perception)

**15. Are there multiple options?** (and pricing for each)
- One-time purchase vs. subscription
- Bundle pricing (1 bottle, 3-bottle, 6-bottle)
- Tier options (basic vs. premium vs. platinum)

Example structure:
- 1 bottle: $49
- 3 bottles: $39/each ($117 total, $12 savings)
- 6 bottles: $29/each ($174 total, $120 savings)

**16. What's the guarantee?**
- Money-back period (30, 60, 90, 180 days?)
- No-hassle terms
- Builds confidence and removes risk

**17. Are there bonuses?** (if any)
- List each bonus and its value
- Make bonuses real (not inflated prices)
- Should amplify the core promise

Example: "Bonus 1: The 7-Day Protocol PDF ($47 value) — Day-by-day guide for maximum results."

**18. What scarcity elements exist?** (if any)
- Limited-time discount (expires at midnight)
- Limited supply (only 100 bottles this week)
- Limited-time offer structure (first-time buyers only)

*Caution:* Fake scarcity damages trust. Only use real scarcity.

---

### SECTION 5: CREDIBILITY & SOCIAL PROOF

**19. What credibility builders can open the sale?**
- Science-backed: "Harvard researchers uncover..."
- Celebrity: "Even Tony Robbins uses this protocol..."
- Media: "As featured in Forbes, Good Morning America..."
- Certification: "FDA-tested," "Dermatologist-approved..."

**20. What testimonials/results are available?**
- Real customer transformations (names, before/afters if possible)
- Specific results ("Lost 23 pounds and haven't gained it back in 2 years")
- Emotional shifts ("I feel like myself again")

**21. What are industry recognition points?**
- Awards, certifications, recognitions
- Endorsements from relevant authorities
- Track record (years in business, customers served)

---

### SECTION 6: STORY & NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK

**22. What's the background story?** (hero's journey outline)
- The problem (person like customer was suffering)
- The search (they looked for solutions; nothing worked)
- The breakthrough (they discovered/created the mechanism)
- The partnership (they brought it to market via your brand)
- The proof (now thousands benefit)

*Format:* 2-3 sentences per story beat for LLM clarity. These become the VSL narrative structure.

---

### SECTION 7: COPY STRATEGY & OBJECTIONS

**23. What are common objections prospects will have?**
- "I've tried supplements before; they don't work"
- "Isn't this just marketing hype?"
- "Will it work for me specifically?"
- "What if I don't see results?"

Pair each objection with answer strategy (not full copy—just strategy).

**24. What specific hooks or big ideas should be tested in the lead?**
- If you have hook ideas from research, inventory them here
- Provides LLM with specific directions vs. free-form generation
- Examples:
  - "Lead with the age reveal (she looks 10 years younger)"
  - "Lead with the science angle (Harvard researchers find...)"
  - "Lead with customer frustration (I wasted $10,000 before I found this)"

---

### SECTION 8: FUTURE PACING & RESULTS LANGUAGE

**25. What specific results should be described in future-pacing?**
- Week 1: What should they expect? ("You'll notice your clothes fit differently")
- Week 2-4: Sustained benefit or new benefit? ("Friends ask what you're doing different")
- Month 2+: Long-term transformation? ("This feels like the new normal")

Specific > vague. "Feel amazing" is marketing speak. "Wake up without grogginess for the first time in 3 years" is specific.

## The Brief Generator: Claude Project Setup

You don't fill the brief manually. You set up a Claude project that generates it from your research documents.

### Project Setup

**Project instructions:**
> Your job is to create new briefs for copywriting projects. These are things that I can give to Claude projects or other LLMs to have them write copy. We'll mostly be focused on VSLs here, but I may end up asking you to create a brief for an e-commerce page in the future as well. I've included two documents in your knowledge base for now. The first is an in-depth read titled "The New Brief." The second is an outline of the classic video sales letter structure I've used for a long time. That one is mostly for reference. Here's how this will work: I will use your chat feature to provide documents that can assist you in writing the brief for a specific product. You'll then do that. For anything where you don't have the information, you can use placeholders to start.

**Knowledge base uploads:**
1. The New Brief template (blank — all 25 questions)
2. A classic VSL outline (for structural reference)

### Generating a Brief

**Input prompt:**
```
I want your help generating a new brief for our brand's [product name]. Here is the current product page where you can pull some inputs: [URL]

Additionally, I'm attaching:
- A UMP/UMS document
- Market research (unified research document)
- Ingredient research with studies

Please go ahead and get started now.
```

**What to upload:**
1. Unified research document (from the three research streams)
2. UMP/UMS document (from mechanism ideation/evaluation)
3. Ingredient research with studies (from ingredient deep research)
4. Link to product page (if one exists — Claude can browse it)

**What you get:** A complete 25-point brief auto-filled with research-backed content. Pain points, promises, mechanism explanation, ingredient claims, objection handling, pricing, story framework — all populated.

### After the Brief: Immediate Copy Generation

The brief feeds directly into copy generation projects. From one brief, you can generate:

**Medium VSL (12-18 min):** "Here's a comprehensive brief for a new VSL. Please review and give me an outline along with your estimated length. Let's aim for 18 minutes."

**Short VSL ads (2-3 min):** "Please review and then give me 5-10 short VSL concepts you think would be worth testing." Then pick the best hook and write.

**Long VSL (25-30 min):** "Write a long-form VSL. Please provide an outline, tell me the estimated length. Shoot for about 28 minutes."

**ADHD hook ads:** Multiple hook concepts from the brief, then write 2-3 minute scripts for each.

### Iteration After Generation

After generating, provide specific feedback:
- "This was great overall. Here are a few quick pieces of information for revisions: [pricing details, spokesperson details, story adjustments, remove bonuses]"
- "I want to move away from the DEJ angle. Rewrite the mechanism without it."
- "This objection answer feels weak. Research shows customers are skeptical of results timeframe."
- Claude revises in place and you copy the updated version.

### The Speed Insight

The entire pipeline — unified research, mechanism, brief generation, VSL draft — can happen faster than ordering lunch. The brief is the bottleneck reducer: once it exists, every copy format becomes a single prompt away.

## Common Brief 2.0 Mistakes

**Skipping research.** Brief without research = generic copy. Fill brief FROM your research, not from assumption.

**Vague product claims.** "Helps with energy" loses to "Feel 40% more energetic by day 3." LLMs amplify vagueness into mediocrity.

**Inconsistent mechanism description.** LLM needs 2-3 detailed sentences about mechanism—why it works, what makes it different. Vague descriptions produce vague copy.

**Fake scarcity/fake bonuses.** Transparency builds trust. Fake claims destroy it.

**Over-determining copy direction.** Brief provides strategy, not script. Say "Lead with the age reveal," not "Start by saying 'I looked in the mirror and screamed.'" LLMs do better with direction than dictation.

**Not using research verbatim.** One best practice: If research shows customers say "I felt invisible," use that language in pain points. LLMs will weave it into copy naturally.
