# Lead Types — Cheat Sheet for Newsletter and Long-Form Leads

> Sources: [[raw/2026-04-07-agora-s-big-black-book-1-06-part-6.md]], [[raw/2026-04-07-agora-s-big-black-book-1-07-part-7.md]]
> Related: [[copy-structure]], [[vicious-hooks]], [[story-craft]], [[direct-response-copywriting]], [[four-legged-stool]]
> Last updated: 2026-04-07

A taxonomy of lead types developed at Agora (primarily by Brian Hunt). When stuck on a lead, run through this list and ask which type fits your material. Being formulaic beats staring at a blank page — fake it till you make it.

---

## The Lead Types (in order of preference)

### 1. Boots-on-the-Ground Lead

First and best option. "I'm here... I'm watching this... I'm experiencing this."

The writer visited a port, a mine, a drill rig, a research facility, a farm, a construction project. The key words are YOU and VISIT. Get your boots on the ground. The leads will flow.

If you can't travel, call a trusted contact and detail THEIR boots-on-the-ground experience. Almost as good.

**Why it works:** Personal experience = authority + story + specific detail. Nobody can argue with what you saw.

### 2. History Lead

Second-favorite option. Tell a historical story that ties into your current idea.

**Requirements:**
- Must have a solid connection to the present-day idea
- The historical story must be engaging and entertaining on its own
- You have to actually know history — this requires reading lots of it

**Why it works:** History stories are inherently interesting. They establish the writer as knowledgeable. And the pattern-match from past to present makes the current recommendation feel inevitable.

### 3. Strong Claim Lead

Come out swinging. Hit the reader in the face with a bold claim.

**Example:** "This is the single best opportunity I've ever found in my entire career as an equity analyst. I truly expect to make 21 times my money on this single stock."

**Why it works:** Pure confidence + specificity. The reader can't look away from someone staking their reputation on a specific, measurable claim.

**Risk:** The claim must be genuine. If the writer is an entertaining character, readers will forgive a bad outcome. If not, credibility is destroyed.

### 4. Timeless Wealth Idea Lead

Teaches a valuable lesson in an entertaining way. Hard to describe — you know it when you see it.

**Example:** "The key to investment success is a simple, powerful core belief... Shopping trumps politics." Then proves that consumer spending matters more than political events to markets and daily life.

**Key:** Keep reminding the reader WHY the idea is so valuable. This compels attention.

### 5. Brilliant Investor Lead

Tell readers about a brilliant, successful person and how they can follow the same approach.

Structure: briefly provide credentials and track record, then tell the reader they can invest with the guru or at least in the guru's favorite idea.

### 6. What's-My-Reader-Most-Worried-About Lead

Ask: what is my reader most worried about right now? Zero in on that concern, make them feel better, and offer the solution.

**Why it works:** Immediate relevance. Meets the reader where they already are emotionally.

### 7. One-Big-Awesome-Project Lead

Describe one superlative project or asset in vivid detail. Paint a picture. Tell a story. Use superlatives — biggest, richest, most promising.

**Key:** The power comes from SUPERLATIVES and specific scale details.

### 8. Personal Experience Lead

Tell a story from your own experience that relates to the current idea. War stories from a market crash, an oil rally, a gold bottom. People want to hear from practitioners who've been through it.

**When it works:** The experience must connect meaningfully to what's happening now. A tenuous link makes it feel like an excuse to talk about yourself.

---

## What NOT to Do

- **Boring personal story.** Taking a mundane life event (kids, golf, ski vacation, getting the flu) and making a flimsy connection to your idea. They all sound forced.
- **Boring company origin story.** "On April 23rd, 1973, Sally Benson opened a small flower store..." Flat and boring.
- **Describing your research process.** "This week, I ran 37 different stock screens. It took me 23 hours..." Self-indulgent. Save it for later in the piece.

---

## How to Choose

Run through these questions in order. Go with the first "yes":

1. Did I travel to get the information? → Boots-on-the-ground
2. Did I talk to someone with local/insider insight? → "This guy is there" variant
3. Is there an interesting historical story that connects? → History lead
4. Can I give a valuable, unconventional, timeless lesson? → Timeless Wealth Idea
5. Is a super-wealth creator investing in this? → Brilliant Investor
6. Did I live through a related market experience? → Personal Experience
7. Can I make a strong, specific claim? → Strong Claim
8. Are past ideas producing big profits for readers? → Remind and redirect
9. Is the company involved in something huge? → Big Awesome Project
10. What is the reader most worried about right now? → Reader Worries

Above all, shoot for telling stories. Every lead type is stronger when it's a story.
