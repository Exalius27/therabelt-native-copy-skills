## The Core Idea: Vicious

Most people start their ad 3-4 sentences in. There's filler, throat-clearing, warm-up. A vicious hook goes right for the gut in the absolute first line. No breathing room.

Vicious means: it must grab something the reader already feels — a fear, a shame, an unspoken dread — and squeeze it so hard they physically cannot scroll past.

A polite hook is a dead hook. A comfortable hook is a dead hook. A hook that doesn't make someone flinch, gasp, or feel personally called out is a dead hook.

The vicious version is always the most specific, most embodied, most uncomfortable, and most charged version of the hook. It replaces abstraction with sensation. It replaces vague descriptions with details that make you feel something.

Spend 70-80% of writing time on the hook. If you can't get someone to open, the ad is dead on arrival.

## The 8 Principles

### 1. Relevant in the First Line

Must be relevant to the reader in the first 10-20 words. No exceptions.

Three categories of relevance:

- **Pain/condition callout.** Call out the pain they have or a mechanism they already understand is bad. "Most people have no idea that the water they're drinking is feeding the bacteria and inflammation slowly killing them." Works because they already believe inflammation is bad.
- **Belief callout.** Take a stance for or against something the market believes. Challenge myths, sacred cows, things you're never supposed to question. "They told you red meat and butter would clog your arteries."
- **General openness.** Soap-opera hook. Pure gossip, intrigue, novelty. No product signal at all. Hard to write, high risk, but grand-slam potential when they hit. "My husband came out as gay after 26 years of marriage, and it nearly killed me."

Key: if your mechanism is unknown to them (e.g., "electrical imbalance"), they'll skip past it. The hook must connect to something they already care about.

### 2. Emotionally Charged Language

Use visceral, concrete words — typically Germanic-root words over Latin/French.

| Weak (Latin/French) | Strong (Germanic) |
|---|---|
| perspire | sweat |
| fabrics | cloth |
| excessive | too much |
| insomnia | sleeplessness |
| severe | bad |
| synthetic | man-made |
| persuade | sweat |
| harmless quirk | harmless thing |

Words with raw emotional charge: sexless, stink, panties, lying, die, naked, killing, terrifying, mortal, beg.

You don't need every word to be charged. One or two in the hook creates the "whoa" effect. Germanic words also produce a lower reading grade level, which reduces friction.

### 3. Natural Intrigue and Open Loops

Don't give away too much in the hook. If they can make an educated guess about what you're going to say, they have no reason to read the ad.

Bad open loop: "Don't do this one thing with AI." (They can guess.)
Good open loop: "When I cleaned out my father's guest bedroom, I realized I inherited his sexless marriage." (They cannot predict where this goes.)

The loop must be strong enough that curiosity pulls them through the copy before you reveal.

### 4. Stakes and Rubbernecking

There must be something heavy at stake — implicit or explicit. Something bad that could happen, something good that could be lost.

People want to look at tragedy. If someone's life is falling apart, there's a reason to pay attention. If there are no consequences, there's no urgency to read.

### 5. Specificity and Tangibility

Use like a spice — season appropriately, don't overdo it. Adding specific details makes the hook feel more real and concrete.

Specific > vague. "26 years of marriage" hits harder than "decades of marriage."

### 6. Doesn't Read Like an Ad

It should read like information promising new insight, a story, a confession, or something forbidden. The moment it reads like an ad, the reader's guard goes up.

### 7. Caveman Language

Super simple. Rooted. Earthy. Third-grade reading level. Short words, short sentences. This reinforces the Germanic word principle — write like you're talking to someone, not lecturing them.

### 8. Consequence First, Mechanism Later

Lead with what they already care about (the consequence), not your unique mechanism.

Bad: "Silver releases ions that neutralize bacteria in stored water." (They don't care about ions.)
Good: "This weird morning habit spikes cortisol." (They already know cortisol is bad.)

If the mechanism isn't commonly known, they won't click. The relevance has to come first. Introduce your mechanism after you've hooked them with something they already believe matters.

## Vicious Hook Examples

- "I went to sleep as a black woman. I woke up as a white woman."
- "My doctor accused me of lying."
- "My husband came out as gay after 26 years of marriage, and it nearly killed me."
- "When I cleaned out my father's guest bedroom, I realized I inherited his sexless marriage."

What makes these work: open loop + emotionally charged words + stakes + can't predict the product + feels transgressive/unsafe to read.

## The Checklist (Apply to Every Hook)

1. Is it relevant in the first 10-20 words?
2. Does it use at least one emotionally charged word?
3. Is there an open loop they can't predict the answer to?
4. Are there real stakes?
5. Is there a specific, tangible detail?
6. Does it NOT read like an ad?
7. Is the language simple enough for a child?
8. Does it lead with consequence, not mechanism?

## Video Hook Anatomy — The Three Ingredients

A video ad hook is not just copy. It's three components working together, with very different weight:

| Component | Weight | What It Is |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| **Copy** | ~80% | The first 1-2 sentences. This is where the punch lives. |
| **Scroll stopper** | ~15% | The visual — the first frame or clip. Must provoke "What the fuck did I just watch?" |
| **Audio** | ~5% | Voiceover, music, or sound effect. Smallest needle mover. |

**The scroll stopper test:** Show the visual to someone with no context. If their reaction isn't visceral confusion or intrigue ("What IS that?"), it won't stop a scroll. See [[contrast-principle]] — the visual must break the pattern of everything else in the feed.

**Clarity > Cleverness:** The biggest hook mistake is trying to be clever. Clever hooks impress other marketers. Clear hooks make money. Prioritize immediate comprehension over stylistic sophistication. Fifth-grade reading level is ideal; never exceed eighth grade. If a hook requires a second read to understand, it's dead.

This is a step in the chiefing process: after writing, check every hook for crystal clarity. Can someone reading at fifth-grade level understand this in one pass? If not, simplify.

**Connection:** "Ugly ads = pretty profits" ([[contrast-principle]]). The most profitable ads often look unpolished, but the copy is unmistakably clear. Aesthetics serve the marketer's ego. Clarity serves the customer's comprehension.

## Hook Patterns from Real Winning Ads (RMBC II Swipe File)

Analysis of hooks across 23 real winning ads reveals three dominant hook architectures used in health/beauty/supplement DR advertising. These are distinct from the story-native hooks above (which are for Facebook story ads). These patterns appear in native advertising, advertorials, and VSL scripts.

### Pattern 1: The Myth-Bust Rapid Fire (Video Ads)

Format: "[Common belief], right? Nope. [Another common belief]? Nope. [Real cause]."

**Examples from swipes:**
- "Eating too much salt causes hypertension, right? Nope. What about bacon? Nope. It's actually calcium buildup that turns arteries to stone."
- "Moisturizer helps your skin, right? Nope. What about collagen? Nope. Do this to erase wrinkles fast."
- "Lemon water detoxes your liver, right? Nope. What about apple cider vinegar? Nope. Do this to flush out liver toxins fast."

**Why it works:** Violates the reader's existing beliefs in rapid succession (Principle 2: belief callout). Each "Nope" creates a micro-open-loop. By the third beat, the reader MUST know what the real answer is. Extremely effective for 3-7 minute video scripts where you have 5 seconds to hook.

**Checklist score:** Relevant (pain callout) + emotionally charged ("Nope" is dismissive) + open loop (what IS the cause?) + stakes (health) + specific + doesn't read like ad + simple language + consequence first. Hits all 8.

### Pattern 2: The Specific-Claim Headline (Written Advertorials)

Format: "[Number] + [Specific result] + [Time frame or qualifier]"

**Examples from swipes:**
- "1 Trick That Can Eliminate ED in Less than Half Hour!"
- "7-Minute Eye Bag Fix A-List Stars Swear By"
- "We Tested 17 Anti-Aging Creams — Only 1 Delivered Jaw-Dropping Results"
- "This $99 'miracle hearing aid' is shocking experts"

**Why it works:** Hopkins' specificity principle in action. The number forces the brain to process it as a claim rather than a slogan. "7-Minute" is a claim. "Quick" is noise. The specificity implies testing, which implies credibility. These headlines function as selectors (Hopkins) — they identify the exact person who has this problem.

**Checklist score:** Relevant + specific + consequence first. But: often reads like an ad (fails #6), low emotional charge (fails #2), and no real open loop (reader CAN guess what comes next, weakening #3). These hooks trade viciousness for relevance and specificity. They work in native ad placements where the editorial framing suppresses the ad-guard that #6 is designed to defeat.

### Pattern 3: The Expert-Discovery Open (Long Advertorials + VSLs)

Format: "I'm [Expert Name], [credentials]. And I'm going to reveal [specific thing]."

**Examples from swipes:**
- "Hi, I'm Dr. Morgan, a board-certified dermatologist... When patients come to me complaining about short, thin, weak lashes, they always say the same thing: 'I've tried EVERYTHING — and nothing works.'"
- "Hi, my name is Dr. John Martin and I'm an otolaryngologist from Chicago. I have 10 years of clinical experience... But it wasn't until earlier this year that I truly unraveled the code."
- "Does your skin pass the pinch test? Let's try an experiment." (Beverly Hills MD — opens with a physical action before introducing the expert)

**Why it works:** Establishes credibility and creates the frame for a discovery story. The reader's implicit question shifts from "why should I read this ad?" to "what did this expert discover?" The Beverly Hills MD variation (opening with a physical test) is the strongest version because it creates immediate physical engagement before any selling begins.

**Note:** This pattern deliberately violates Principle 6 ("doesn't read like an ad") and gets away with it because the expert frame replaces the editorial frame. The reader knows it's selling something but accepts it because the expert has earned the right to recommend.

### Pattern 4: Video Hook Clip Types (Evolve Framework)

The visual hook clip (first 1-3 seconds of video) pairs with the text hook. Four clip types, each suited to different awareness stages:

| Clip Type | Shows | Best For |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| **Ideal outcome** | The result the viewer wants | Unaware, solution-aware |
| **Problem** | The pain point in action | Problem-aware |
| **Product** | The product being used | Product-aware, most-aware |
| **Weird/ASMR** | Something unexpected or satisfying | Unaware (attention-first) |

**Pairing with awareness stages:**
- Unaware → outcome clip or problem clip + story/secret/infotainment text
- Problem-aware → problem clip or solution clip + pain point text
- Solution-aware → outcome clip or product clip + benefit text
- Product-aware → product clip + proof/comparison text

**Enhancers:**
- **Reverse the clip.** Throw product down, reverse it flying back up. Creates visual novelty.
- **Third-person perspective.** Instead of "I lost weight," use "I can't believe how good you look." People care more about what others think of them. For every category: frame the benefit from someone else's reaction, not the user's self-report.

### What the Swipe File Proves About Hook Theory

The vicious hook principles (above) are the gold standard for story-native and Facebook feed ads. But winning DR ads in native placements use a modified playbook:

1. **Specificity and relevance matter more than emotional charge in native ads.** The editorial framing does the work that emotional charge does in feed ads. The reader already clicked through from a news site — her guard is lower.
2. **Myth-bust hooks are the fastest-performing pattern for video scripts.** Three rapid violations in under 10 seconds creates an obligation to watch.
3. **Expert credibility can replace open loops.** When the expert is credible enough, the reader stays to learn what the expert knows — even without a mystery to solve.
