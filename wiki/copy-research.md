## Core Thesis

The failure of most sales copy is not a writing problem — it's a research problem. If you understand your prospect deeply enough (their words, their pain, their failed attempts, their language patterns), the writing becomes mechanical. Research is what separates copy that connects from copy that performs well in testing and fails in the real world. Do research first. Every time. Without exception.

The RMBC method makes this explicit: R (Research) is not preparation for the real work. It IS the real work. The M, B, and C stages are downstream consequences of how deeply you researched.

## Principles

### 1. Research Has Two Jobs

Most copywriters think of research as "understanding the product." That's only half.

Research answers two separate questions:

**Job 1: Get inside the prospect's mind.** Learn how they think, talk, feel. Learn their specific fears, their language, their past failures. Mentally BECOME the prospect while writing. If you don't, the copy will never feel like it was written for them — it'll feel like it was written AT them.

**Job 2: Understand the competitive landscape.** What solutions has the prospect already tried? What did they like about those solutions? What failed? This is the only way to differentiate your product in a way that's real to the prospect, not just real in a marketing document.

Why both matter: Job 1 creates resonance. Job 2 creates differentiation. Without resonance, the copy doesn't feel relevant. Without differentiation, the product doesn't feel necessary. Great copy requires both.

### 2. Forums Are the Primary Research Source

Forums are where prospects talk to each other without any marketing agenda. No one is performing for an audience. No one is being polished. They're sharing real struggles, real hopes, real failures, and real language — which is exactly what you need.

What to look for in forums:
- Stories of failed attempts (this becomes your mechanism setup)
- Language patterns — the exact words people use to describe their problem
- Specific fears and specific hopes (not general; very specific)
- The emotional texture of the experience: what does it feel like to live with this problem every day?
- Recurring complaints about existing solutions

The best forum research finds a thread where someone is documenting their experience in real time — a journal of their struggle. This gives you the full arc: what they tried, how they felt, what failed, what they're hoping for.

**For TheraBelt:** Dog owner communities, aging pet subreddits, and pet care forums. Look for posts where owners describe noticing their dog slowing down, the specific moments that broke their heart, what they tried, what the vet said, what changed. The voice-of-customer language from these threads should flow directly into story-native copy — not as fabricated testimonials, but as authentic emotional texture.

### 3. Amazon Reviews Are the Competitive Intelligence Source

Amazon reviews for products in the same category reveal exactly what the prospect likes and hates about existing solutions. This is not available anywhere else at this density and specificity.

**5-star reviews:** What are they praising? These are the benefits your prospect values most. Any overlap with your product = message confirmation.

**1-star reviews:** What are they complaining about? These are the reasons your product exists. When you address these specific complaints in your mechanism (without naming competitors), you're answering the real objection before it's raised.

How to use: Go through the 5-star and 1-star reviews for the 3-4 most popular competing products. Extract specific phrases, specific complaints, specific praises. The patterns that repeat across multiple products and multiple reviewers are the most important.

**Connection:** This is the research input for the [[insight-vectors]] taxonomy. A pattern of 1-star complaints around the same failure point is a control point or hidden constraint vector waiting to be built.

### 4. The 22 Research Questions (Georgi's Framework)

Georgi developed 22 questions to answer before writing. Not all 22 are equally important for every product or situation. The most operationally valuable cluster:

**Understanding the prospect:**
- What does the prospect's daily life look like? What does the problem feel like day-to-day, not in general?
- What specific moment do they most want to change? (Not "I want to lose weight" — "I want to fit into my daughter's wedding without everyone pitying me.")
- What language do they use to describe the problem? (Not your category language — their language.)
- What are they most embarrassed about or most afraid of?
- What do they believe is the CAUSE of their problem right now? (This tells you where your mechanism needs to insert itself.)

**Understanding failed solutions:**
- What have they tried before? (Specific products, specific approaches)
- What did they like about those attempts? (What almost worked)
- What failed, and why did it fail in their mind? (The reason they tell themselves, not the real reason)
- What did trying and failing cost them — emotionally, financially, practically?

**Understanding what they want:**
- What does success look like to them in concrete, observable terms?
- What happens in their life if they solve this problem? (Downstream consequences)
- Who else benefits when they solve the problem? (The husband who gets his wife back, the owner whose dog can play again)

**For the mechanism:**
- What do they currently believe explains why they can't succeed? (This is the false assumption your mechanism will overturn)
- What would they find surprising, relieving, or empowering to learn about why they failed?

### 5. Research Answers Before the Brief, Not During Copy

The fatal mistake: starting to write before the research is finished, then going back to research when you get stuck. This produces fragmented, disconnected copy.

The discipline: Complete research. Then complete the mechanism. Then write the brief (which answers 12 structural questions about the copy). Then write. Each stage is complete before the next starts.

Why this matters operationally: When you write the brief, you answer questions about the copy structure (big promise, background story, mechanism explanation). If you didn't finish research, you'll make things up. Made-up things feel made up in copy — the specific textures and language that come from real research are impossible to fabricate.

## Frameworks

### Voice of Customer Mining (Forum Research Process)

Step 1: Identify 3-5 forums or communities where your prospect gathers. Reddit works for almost every category. Niche forums are often better. For pet care: Reddit, Facebook groups for specific breeds, Quora, vet forums.

Step 2: Search for threads where people describe their EXPERIENCE, not just their question. "My dog is slowing down — here's what's happening" is what you want. Not "what supplement should I give my aging dog?"

Step 3: Read the full thread, including replies. Extract:
- Direct quotes that capture an emotion (mark these — they may become direct copy)
- Recurring complaints about failed solutions
- The language they use (especially terminology, metaphors, and how they describe the problem)
- Specific fears named (death, loss of mobility, guilt, expense)
- The moment that triggered them to act or post

Step 4: Group what you find into 4 categories: (a) language patterns, (b) failed solutions and why, (c) specific fears and desires, (d) moments that matter.

**Pain points vs. day-to-day struggles:** These are two different things. Pain points are emotional states — "I cry every day," "it's destroying my mental health," "I feel so alone." Day-to-day struggles are recurring experiences caused by the problem — "every time I wash my hair, clumps come out," "I'm afraid to go outside," "I see hairs all over my pillow every morning." Both are valuable but serve different roles: pain points fuel the emotional hook and connection. Day-to-day struggles create specific, relatable scenes in the story body. Don't conflate them during extraction — tag each one correctly so you use it in the right place.

Step 5: Run the results through the [[insight-vectors]] taxonomy. Where do the recurring complaints and false assumptions fall? What vector type fits?

Step 6: The language patterns from Step 3 go directly into the brief and eventually into copy. Don't paraphrase. Don't clean up. Use their words.

**The Commonality Filter:** Never build creative around something only one person mentioned in one thread. A single post about "family heartbreak" doesn't mean that experience is common. Wait until you see the same emotion, struggle, or desire repeated across multiple posts and multiple sources before treating it as an ad angle. One story is not a pattern. If it's not repeating, don't assume it's widespread — you'll write ads that feel relatable to no one. Patterns across sources = safe to use. Single anecdotes = note it but don't build on it.

**The 100% / 50% Rule:** What customers say directly = 100% reliable data. Underlying themes you infer from what they said = 50% reliable. In early stages (before you have surveys or enough volume to confirm patterns), only pursue 100% data. If they say "I don't feel feminine" — that's identity loss, explicitly stated. If you suspect stress is a factor because of how they describe their routine — that's 50%, an inference. The danger: if you build copy on a 50% idea and it flops, you can't tell whether the angle failed or the insight never existed. With 100% data, when something fails, you know the angle is real — the execution needs fixing, not the strategy. This eliminates the most expensive confusion in early-stage testing. To move a 50% idea to 100%: find enough raw calls/posts confirming it explicitly, or run a survey targeting that specific insight.

### Kennedy's 10 Diagnostic Questions + Theater In Your Mind

A pre-writing ritual focused on emotional identification with the prospect rather than data collection. This complements Georgi's 22 research questions (which are product/mechanism-focused) with a visualization protocol.

**The 10 questions (Kennedy's exact list):**

1. What keeps them awake at night, indigestion boiling up their esophagus, eyes open, staring at the ceiling?
2. What are they afraid of?
3. What are they angry about? Who are they angry at?
4. What are their top three daily frustrations?
5. What trends are occurring and will occur in their businesses or lives?
6. What do they secretly, ardently desire most?
7. Is there a built-in bias to the way they make decisions?
8. Do they have their own language?
9. Who else is selling something similar to them, and how?
10. Who else has tried selling them something similar, and how has that effort failed?

These questions are less about demographic fact-gathering and more about emotional and situational reality. Questions 1-6 feed directly into the emotional texture of story-native copy. Questions 7-8 govern tone and language register (see the Responsive Chord principle in [[direct-response-copywriting]]). Questions 9-10 map the competitive landscape and failed solutions — overlap with Georgi's Amazon research.

**Theater In Your Mind (Kennedy's visualization technique):**

After research, close your eyes and visualize the letter recipient as a living, breathing person. Not a demographic. A specific human in a specific moment.

The exercise: trace their entire day. When do they get their mail? Where do they stand when sorting it? What else are they preoccupied with at that moment? What do they worry about, complain about, secretly wish for, enjoy?

This is the transition from research data to writing empathy. You're not extracting information anymore — you're *becoming* the prospect so you can write from inside their experience.

Kennedy's story: when writing for dentists (a profession he'd never been part of), he read years of trade journals, attended their convention, took a dentist to lunch. Not to gather facts — to be able to visualize himself AS a dentist so he could write in their language.

**How to use:** Run the 10 diagnostic questions from research data. Then do the Theater In Your Mind exercise before writing the first line. The questions feed the intellect. The visualization feeds the intuition. Both are required.

**Connection:** The 10 diagnostic questions overlap with and complement [[direct-response-copywriting]]'s mass desire framework (question 6 = the desire; questions 1-4 = the pain side). They're a faster, more visceral diagnostic tool designed for empathy, not architecture.

### Amazon Competitor Review Analysis

Step 1: Search Amazon for the 3-4 top-selling products that address the same pain point.

Step 2: For each product, read the most helpful 5-star reviews and the most helpful 1-star reviews (usually the first 10-15 of each).

Step 3: In 5-star reviews: What are they specifically praising? Make a list of benefits named. Do not generalize — keep the specifics.

Step 4: In 1-star reviews: What specifically failed? Make a list of failure modes named. Note recurring themes.

Step 5: Cross-reference with your product. Does your product address the 1-star failures? Does it deliver on the 5-star wins? This is your differentiation map.

Step 6: The most common 1-star failure that your product genuinely solves is the foundation of your mechanism of the problem. ("Every other solution fails because of [X]. [Product] works because it addresses [X] directly.")

### AI-Assisted Bulk Review Analysis

When there are hundreds or thousands of reviews across multiple competing products, manual reading doesn't scale. Export reviews (using browser extensions like Amazon Review Export or tools like Shoelacer) and process them through an LLM with a structured prompt.

**The prompt framework** (adapt niche-specific details to your category):

> Act as a world-class direct response strategist with experience in [your niche]. You will be analyzing raw customer reviews from products very similar to ours. Extract insights by answering:
>
> 1. **What do they love about these products?** Focus only on the product itself -- not outcomes or results. Look for: materials/ingredients they trust, usability features, design/form factor, packaging and delivery experience, safety/certification/trust signals, convenience factors (dosing, size, portability, daily routine fit), anything that makes the product feel premium or thoughtful. Use exact customer phrases.
>
> 2. **What do they hate or complain about?** Side effects, dosage/price concerns, packaging/taste/trust issues, texture problems. Use exact customer phrases to capture objections.
>
> 3. **What desires and expectations are these products failing to meet?** What did buyers want that the product didn't deliver -- beyond the core outcome? (e.g., sugar-free, vegetarian, no GMO, specific certifications)
>
> 4. **What messaging angles can we use to stand out?** Based on loves and hates, what positioning would differentiate us?
>
> 5. **What objections should we permanently handle?** The highest-frequency objections that we must eliminate in ads, landing pages, and product pages.

**For health/supplement niches, add a sixth question:**

> 6. **What health-specific concerns emerge?** FDA approval, GMO status, additive suspicion, side effect transparency, allergen interactions, chemical mistrust. These are distinct from product-level complaints -- they are category-level trust barriers.

**Process:**
1. Export reviews from 3-5 competing products (prioritize products with 500+ reviews)
2. Run the prompt on each product's reviews separately first
3. Then consolidate findings across all products into one document
4. Cross-reference: patterns that repeat across multiple products and multiple reviewers are the highest-priority objections to address

**Why this matters:** The objections that block purchase are often invisible to the product creator. They come from the prospect's prior experience with the category, not with your specific product. Someone who has been burned by sugar-laden gummies or suspicious additives brings those objections to every new supplement -- including yours. You handle them or you lose the sale.


### Alternative Solution Research — The Bridge Framework

A distinct research stream from Amazon reviews: instead of analyzing product reviews, you analyze your avatar's direct experience with competing solutions. The goal is not product comparison — it's understanding the avatar's emotional journey through alternatives so you can position your product as the obvious next step.

**Why it matters:** When your avatar has already tried other solutions, they arrive at your product with specific frustrations, specific things they liked, and specific fears about trying something new. If you don't know these, your messaging speaks generically. If you do know them, you can build a "bridge" from their current experience to your product.

**The five research questions:**

1. What alternative solutions or products has the avatar already tried?
2. What positive experiences or benefits has the avatar mentioned about these alternatives?
3. What specific frustrations, disappointments, or complaints has the avatar expressed?
4. Which solution does the avatar currently perceive as the best option, and why?
5. How can we strategically position our solution as the clearly superior option based on these insights?

**The bridge concept:** If your avatar is already using a common solution (and many are), you don't need to convince them they have a problem — they already know. You need to convince them their current solution is insufficient. The "bridge" connects their existing frustration with a competing solution to your product's specific advantage.

Example: PCOS hair loss avatars commonly use minoxidil. Research reveals: they get some results but hate the dependency (hair falls out when they stop), the scalp irritation, and the fact that it only treats surface-level symptoms without addressing the hormonal root cause. The bridge: "You already know minoxidil works on the surface. Here's why it can't fix what's actually causing your hair loss."

**How research becomes ad angles:**
- Each frustration with a competing solution = a potential ad angle
- "Debunking" content that explains why a specific alternative fails for the avatar's specific situation
- Positioning that highlights what your product does that alternatives structurally cannot

**Connection to Amazon review analysis (Principle 3 above):** Amazon reviews are product-centric — what people say about a product. Alternative solution research is avatar-centric — what the avatar experienced across all solutions she tried. Both feed positioning, but alternative solution research generates more emotionally resonant ad angles because it captures the full journey of trying and failing, not just a product rating.

**Connection to [[avatar-segmentation]]:** The more specific your sub-avatar, the more specific their alternative solution history. "Women with hair loss" tried everything. "Women with PCOS hair loss" tried a specific set of solutions and have a specific set of frustrations. Niching the avatar also niches the competitive landscape.

## Connections

The research phase feeds every downstream stage:

- Research → [[insight-vectors]]: Voice of customer data reveals the mental model gaps that become mechanism vectors.
- Research → Mechanism: What the prospect believes is why they failed becomes the Mechanism of the Problem. What your product uniquely does becomes the Mechanism of the Solution.
- Research → [[persuasion]]: The specific language and emotional texture from forum research is the raw material for belief chains and emotional resonance.
- Research → [[story-native-format]]: The real stories, real moments, and real language from forum research become the story beats. The character's voice should sound like the forum threads, not like copy.
- Research → [[avatar-1]], [[avatar-2]]: Avatar profiles become alive (not theoretical) when filled with real language from research. The avatar's inner monologue should be written in voice-of-customer language.

## Emotional Trigger Ranking — From Research to Creative Priority

After completing avatar research, you'll have dozens of emotional states, pain points, desires, and fears. Not all are equally powerful. The emotional trigger ranking determines which emotions to lead with in creative development.

**The process:**

Step 1: Extract all emotional triggers from forum research — the specific feelings your avatar expresses (identity loss, shame, hopelessness, frustration, fear, grief, insecurity, exhaustion, envy, etc.).

Step 2: Rank by two factors: (a) frequency — how many different people express this emotion, and (b) intensity — how deeply the emotion is felt based on the language used.

Step 3: Start creative development with the highest-ranked trigger. Your first ads, first landing page concepts, first VSL hooks should all revolve around the dominant emotional trigger.

Step 4: Work down the ranking for creative diversity. Each emotional trigger becomes a different ad angle — same avatar, same product, different entry point.

**Why this matters:** Starting with the dominant emotional trigger maximizes the probability that your first creative resonates. If identity loss dominates your avatar's emotional landscape, an ad built around identity loss will outperform one built around envy or exhaustion — even though all are real emotions. You can test secondary triggers later, but lead with the strongest.

**Connection to [[avatar-segmentation]]:** Different sub-avatars within the same audience may have different dominant triggers. Single women with PCOS hair loss may rank insecurity highest; married women may rank identity loss. Segmenting the avatar also segments the emotional trigger priority.

## Two-Layer Objection Framework — Problem vs. Product

Objections come in two distinct layers, and each requires different handling:

**Layer 1: Problem-level objections** — "I've tried everything and nothing works." "Nothing can fix my hair." "I'm broken."

These objections aren't about your product — they're about the avatar's belief that any solution can work. She's projecting past failures onto every new option. Handle these through root cause education and unique mechanism explanation: "The reason nothing worked is that every solution you tried only addressed the surface. Here's what's actually causing the problem."

**Layer 2: Product-level objections** — "Just another supplement." "Side effects." "I don't want to be dependent forever." "How do I know this isn't a scam?"

These objections are about your specific product category or offering. Handle these through product transparency, ingredient credibility, lifestyle framing ("daily armor, not a crutch"), and social proof.

**The operational rule:** Not every creative asset needs to handle both layers. Image ads and short-form hooks handle neither — their job is emotional trigger + click. Landing pages and long-form VSLs must handle both. The ad lab should track which objections each asset addresses so you can identify gaps in the funnel.

**Connection to [[direct-response-copywriting]]:** Problem-level objections map to the "justify failure before making claims" principle from Albuquerque. Product-level objections map to Kennedy's Damaging Admission technique and Sugarman's Satisfaction Conviction.

## Halbert's Behavioral Research Method (The Boron Letters, 1984)

Halbert adds a third research source alongside forums and Amazon reviews: **observing what people actually buy** — not what they say they buy.

The core principle: truth is determined by wallets, not words. People systematically misrepresent their preferences when surveyed — not from lying, but from answering what they think they should prefer (the Bible, premium beer, live theater) rather than what they actually choose (the National Enquirer, regular beer, movies). Surveys reveal aspirational identity. Purchase behavior reveals desire.

**The SRDS Direct Mail List Book** as market research tool:

The SRDS (Standard Rate and Data Service) is a directory of mailing lists — every list of buyers available to rent. Reading the SRDS reveals not what people say they want, but what they have demonstrably paid for. Each list entry shows:
- What category of product these buyers purchased
- Average unit of sale (how much they spent)
- Quantity of buyers (market size)
- Recency of purchase (hotline buyer availability)

Reading SRDS to understand a market before building a product is the direct-mail equivalent of forum research. It answers: what has this specific type of person already paid real money for? The bigger the list and the higher the average unit of sale, the stronger the evidence of genuine, demonstrated desire.

**Repeat advertising as market signal:** Track which ads run month after month and year after year in mail-order publications, and which mailing lists keep growing. Consistent repetition = someone is making money. That's the market signal. Don't guess which categories are hot — observe what keeps running.

**Behavioral research complements VOC research:** Forums (Principle 2 above) reveal the language and emotional texture of desire. Behavioral data (SRDS, repeat advertising, Amazon bestseller lists) reveals where desire is strong enough that people are already paying. The two used together — what they say and what they do — form a complete picture.

For TheraBelt: lists of pet supplement buyers, dog product buyers with high average unit of sale, and repeat buyers of natural pet care products are more valuable as targeting signals than any survey of "how important is your dog's health to you?" Every dog owner will say their dog's health is very important. Behavioral purchase data shows who actually acts on it.

## Awareness Levels Are Dynamic — Not Static Buckets

The standard model treats Schwartz's five awareness levels as separate groups of people: problem-aware people in one bucket, solution-aware in another. The reality found through manual research is that awareness levels are dynamic mind-states, not demographic segments. A single person can be problem-aware AND solution-aware AND product-aware simultaneously.

**Why this matters operationally:** If you write a mechanism that explains why the problem exists and why your product category is the solution, but your audience has already tried your product category and watched it fail, your mechanism lands on deaf ears. They already know about your category. They tried it. It didn't work.

### The Purify Case Study

Cat water fountain. Initial mechanism: "Cats avoid still water because they're biologically hardwired to seek running water — you need a fountain."

Research revealed: forum posts like "Before you say she has a fountain, I tried giving water in different bowls in different places" and "I've tried everything. Water fountain, faucet, different locations, different types of bowls. Nothing works."

These people are problem-aware (cat won't drink), solution-aware (running water helps), AND product-aware (they already own a fountain). The original mechanism spoke to solution-aware only. Result: $2-3k/day.

New mechanism: invalidated other fountains in the category specifically — explained why standard fountains fail even though the running-water principle is correct. Result: $10k/day. Same product. Same market. Different mechanism targeting the correct awareness state.

### How to Research for Dynamic Awareness

Step 1: Go into forums where people discuss the problem your product solves.

Step 2: Read posts specifically looking for what solutions people have ALREADY tried. Not just "have they tried X?" but "have they tried a product in MY category?"

Step 3: Look for multiple instances before giving weight. One person mentioning they tried a fountain is anecdotal. Three or four people across different threads saying the same thing is a pattern.

Step 4: Classify what you find:
- Pure problem-aware (knows the problem, hasn't explored solutions) = mechanism explains why the problem happens + introduces your category
- Problem + solution-aware (knows the problem, has tried other approaches but not your category) = mechanism explains why other approaches fail + why your category is different
- Problem + solution + product-aware (has tried your product category and it failed) = mechanism explains why other products in your category fail + what makes yours structurally different

Step 5: Write your mechanism to MEET THEM WHERE THEY ARE. If a large portion of your research shows product-category awareness, your mechanism must invalidate competing products in your category, not just competing solution types.

**The AI research gap:** AI research prompts tend to assign people to clean awareness buckets. Manual research reveals the overlap. This is one of the primary reasons manual research outperforms AI-only research — AI skips the nuance, and the nuance is where the mechanism lives.

**Connection to [[unique-mechanisms]]:** The mechanism type you need (UMP vs. UMS, problem vs. solution vs. product-level) is determined by where your audience actually sits on this dynamic spectrum, not where a framework assigns them.

**Connection to [[avatar-system]] Factor 4 (Awareness):** The 7-factor framework lists awareness as one factor. This section operationalizes it: awareness is not a single setting — it's a range you discover through research, and your mechanism must address the highest awareness level present in significant numbers.

## Unaware Audience Research — Symptom-First Methodology

In Stage 5 markets (hyper-saturated), competitors dominate product-aware and most-aware. Problem-aware is increasingly saturated by dropshippers. Unaware audiences are larger, deliver cheaper CPMs, and face less competition — but require a fundamentally different research and writing approach.

### The Key Distinction: Symptoms vs. Problems

Your product solves a PROBLEM. But unaware people don't know they have the problem. What they DO experience are SYMPTOMS — physical or emotional states that are caused by the problem but aren't recognized as connected to it.

- Product: anti-snoring mouthguard
- Problem: snoring / sleep apnea
- Symptoms: exhaustion, brain fog, inability to focus at work, car accidents from fatigue, feeling like "a piece of garbage," mental health deterioration

The unaware audience doesn't think "I have a snoring problem." They think "I'm so tired I can't function." The symptom is their lived reality. The problem is the hidden cause you'll reveal.

### The Entry Mechanism: Identification

Because unaware people don't know they have a problem, you can't lead with the problem. You enter through IDENTIFICATION — emotional or physical states they recognize as their own.

**Emotional identification:** Hidden fears, hidden dreams, hidden resentments (per Schwartz). States of being the person would nod at: "I'm not just tired. I'm non-thinking and non-functioning."

**Physical identification:** Observable symptoms like a doctor would catalog. If a dog constantly licks its paws — that's a physical symptom of yeast infection. If a cat uses the bathroom outside the litter box — that's a physical symptom of chronic dehydration.

### Research Process for Unaware Audiences

Step 1: Start with the PROBLEM your product solves (e.g., snoring).

Step 2: Pick a specific demographic/avatar to explore (e.g., menopausal women).

Step 3: Ask: if this person has this problem, what symptoms would they experience day-to-day? Follow the causal chain: snoring → disrupted sleep → exhaustion → brain fog → poor work performance → car accidents → mental health deterioration.

Step 4: Search forums for those SYMPTOMS, not the problem. Search "menopause tired all day reddit" or "menopause bad sleep reddit" — never "menopause snoring."

Step 5: Collect symptom descriptions in the prospect's own language. These become the raw material for your lead. Examples from actual research:
- "I'm maybe getting 3-4 broken hours a night. I've been running on fumes for months."
- "My work is really suffering, and I've had two minor car accidents."
- "I just want to say, I'm not just tired. I'm not just sleepy. I am non-thinking and non-functioning."

Step 6: Build the lead from symptoms → problem revelation. The copy follows this structure:
1. Open with the most attention-grabbing symptom (the car accident)
2. Layer in more symptoms the reader identifies with (exhaustion, fog, inability to think)
3. Gradualize into the problem: "The reason you feel this way isn't menopause alone — it's that you may have underlying sleep apnea you've never been tested for"
4. Introduce the product as the solution to the now-revealed problem

### Why This Creates Competitive Advantage

- Unaware audiences are LARGER (everyone who has the problem but doesn't know it yet)
- CPMs are LOWER (less advertiser competition for these audiences)
- No one is marketing to them with this angle (competitors target problem-aware and product-aware)
- The copy feels helpful, not salesy (you're educating them about something they didn't know)

**Proof:** Problem-aware ads in the same account showed significantly higher CPMs than unaware-targeted ads because problem-aware audiences are smaller and more competitive.

**Connection to [[avatar-segmentation]]:** Finding unaware audiences is a form of horizontal scaling — you're not competing for the same problem-aware buyers as everyone else. You're reaching fundamentally different people who have the problem but don't know its name yet.

**Connection to [[landing-page-architecture]]:** The 12-section LP structure works differently for unaware traffic. The "Root Cause + Villain" section does heavier lifting because you must bridge from symptom to problem before the mechanism makes sense.

## Limiting Belief Research and Gradualization

The primary objective of copy is to make the prospect believe the ONE BELIEF: your product is the only way to achieve their desire, and it's only possible through your mechanism (from 16-Word Sales Letter). Existing limiting beliefs are what prevent the one belief from landing. Finding and dismantling these beliefs through gradualization is the core skill of converting research into copy.

### How to Find Limiting Beliefs in Research

People rarely state their beliefs directly. They don't write "I believe wet food is sufficient for cat hydration." They write "just feed your cat wet food — that's all they need." You must read between the lines and infer the belief from what they're saying.

Step 1: Go to forums where people discuss the problem your product solves.

Step 2: Read posts and comments looking for what people RECOMMEND to each other. Their recommendations reveal their beliefs about what works.

Step 3: Look for consensus. When multiple people in a thread unanimously agree on an approach ("everybody seems to agree wet food is the key"), that consensus IS the limiting belief you need to address.

Step 4: Write down the inferred beliefs explicitly:
- "If you just feed your cat lots of wet food, your cat won't get kidney disease"
- "Cats get adequate hydration from their wet food"

Step 5: Test each belief against your one belief. If someone believes this, CAN they believe your one belief? If not, this is a limiting belief that must be gradualized.

### The Gradualization Process

You cannot confront a limiting belief head-on. If someone believes wet food is the answer and you say "wet food doesn't work — buy a fountain," they'll reject you because your message contradicts their existing belief. Schwartz called this the process of gradualization — stringing together a series of micro-acceptances that incrementally shift belief.

The four-step structure:

**Step 1 — Start with what they already believe.** "You're actually doing the right thing feeding wet food. Every vet recommends it for hydration." The prospect reads this and thinks "yes, exactly." First micro-acceptance.

**Step 2 — Introduce an observable contradiction.** Something they've experienced themselves but don't want to admit. "But here's something strange — have you noticed how your cat's litter box clumps are getting smaller and darker over time?" Observable contradictions work because they're undeniable — the prospect has SEEN this. They can't argue with their own experience.

**Step 3 — Lead with questions.** "Even with wet food twice a day, when was the last time you actually saw your cat drink water from their bowl? This morning? Yesterday? Can you even remember?" Questions let the prospect discover the gap themselves. A conclusion they reach on their own is more persuasive than one you hand them (see [[sugarman-triggers]] — Mental Engagement principle).

**Step 4 — Let them realize the gap, then introduce the problem.** "Most owners can't remember because cats drinking from bowls is actually rare. Wet food alone only covers about 70% of daily water needs. Even with wet food, your cat is spending most of their day in mild dehydration." Now the limiting belief has been shifted — not by confrontation, but by leading them through their own experience.

### Why AI Fails at This

AI can generate plausible-sounding mechanisms and gradualization sequences. But the OBSERVABLE CONTRADICTIONS — the things the prospect has actually experienced and can't deny — only come from manual research. AI invents contradictions that sound logical but aren't grounded in real behavior. The prospect reads them and thinks "that's not true for my cat" and the whole chain breaks.

**The comments test:** If your ad comments are full of people saying "just feed your cat wet food" or "this is bullshit," you failed to gradualize. You confronted their belief instead of leading them through it.

**Connection to [[direct-response-copywriting]] Belief Chains:** Gradualization is the method for building the belief chain. Each micro-acceptance is one link. The chain must start from where the prospect currently is (their limiting belief) and end at the one belief. Without knowing the starting point (from research), you can't build the chain.

**Connection to [[persuasion]]:** Observable contradictions are a specific application of the reason-why principle — you're giving the prospect a reason to question their own belief, grounded in their own experience rather than your claims.

## Intuition as the Output of Manual Research

The ultimate goal of manual research is not a document, a spreadsheet, or a list of quotes. It is INTUITION — the ability to intuitively know what your avatar needs to hear, the same way you intuitively know how to persuade your mother, your father, or your best friend.

### The Family Member Test

Think of someone you love deeply. Imagine a problem they face. Now visualize:
- How their expression changes when they encounter the problem
- Whether they cry, hide their tears, or smile through it
- How they act 3 hours later around other people — do they put on a strong guard? Drag their feet? Slump their shoulders?
- What tone they use when they talk about it
- What they would NEVER say out loud

Now think about your avatar and the mass desire you're targeting. Can you see them with the same clarity? If not, you haven't done enough research.

### What Intuition Produces

When you develop intuitive understanding of your avatar:
- You intuitively know which desires to focus on
- You intuitively know which angles work before testing
- You intuitively know how your avatar wants to be treated in copy
- You intuitively know what your avatar desperately wants to hear
- You intuitively know what's stopping them from buying

**Proof:** After 2 weeks of 3-4 hours daily manual research, one practitioner re-read their own copy and felt angry at it. They had agitated the problem excessively in the lead without getting to the point. After consuming hundreds of Reddit posts and forum discussions, they intuitively knew their avatar was already blaming themselves intensely for the problem. More agitation was the WRONG move — the avatar needed reassurance and hope via the unique mechanism, fast. Result: CPC dropped from $2.23 to $0.91 and the product became profitable.

### How to Build Intuition

This is not a rigid process. It is immersion.

1. **Collect raw quotes** from Facebook groups, Reddit, forums, niche-specific clinic reviews, YouTube comments, TikTok. Be creative about WHERE your avatar consumes content.

2. **Organize by day** in a Google Doc or similar. One tab per research session.

3. **Write your own hypotheses** about what you're seeing. This is critical — don't just collect, interpret. What beliefs are forming? What patterns emerge?

4. **Run through Claude** for perspectives you haven't considered. AI is useful HERE — not for the initial research, but for challenging your interpretations.

5. **Go beyond passive research.** Post in Facebook groups and analyze comments. Find people's Instagram profiles from these groups. Check which accounts they follow, what humor and references are relevant to them.

6. **Don't overthink it.** Building intuition is a passive process that happens by putting yourself in your avatar's shoes consistently over time.

### Frameworks Are the Chassis, Not the Engine

A common trap: reading Breakthrough Advertising, Great Leads, 16-Word Sales Letter, Cashvertising, and trying to mechanically apply every principle simultaneously. The result is forced, rigid copy that follows rules but doesn't resonate.

The reframe: use frameworks as mental models for LEARNING MORE ABOUT YOUR CUSTOMER, not as rigid rules to apply. Understanding your avatar is the engine. Frameworks are the chassis the engine sits inside. Without the engine, the chassis goes nowhere.

**The proof:** "What's the point about reading awareness levels, the 7 techniques, the rule of one, the one belief and trying to stuff ALL the principles into one copy IF YOU DON'T KNOW WHO YOU'RE EVEN WRITING TO?"

**Connection to [[avatar-system]]:** The 7-factor framework is a structure for organizing what you discover through research. Intuition is what fills that structure with truth instead of guesses.

## Nuances and Exceptions

### When time is short
Minimum viable research: At least 1 hour on forums and 30 minutes on Amazon reviews before writing anything. Skipping entirely produces generic copy. Reduced research produces generic-sounding copy with occasional flashes of truth.

### Research is not the same as knowing the product
You can know TheraBelt perfectly and still not know your prospect. The product knowledge and the prospect knowledge are separate. Research is about the prospect, not the product.

### Do not fabricate voice-of-customer language
If you couldn't find a forum quote for a specific emotion or claim, don't write it as if it came from research. Either find the real quote, or write the line as authorial copy — not as implied VOC. This is the source-attribution rule applied to research. See [[direct-response-copywriting]] and the system rule: never fabricate quotes, metrics, or customer language.

### Secondary avatars (gift angle)
Forum research sometimes surfaces posts from relatives of the sufferer — spouses, siblings, parents posting about someone else's problem. These represent secondary avatars who might buy the product as a gift or solution for their loved one. The desire magnitude is lower than self-purchasing (they don't have the problem themselves), but it expands the pool of potential buyers. Use for creative volume expansion, not as the primary angle.

### Forum demographic skew
Forums skew toward older demographics (50+). Younger audiences tend to gather on Reddit, Facebook groups, TikTok comments, and social platforms instead. For niches targeting under-40 prospects, forums may yield sparse emotional data. Adjust your primary research source accordingly.

## AI-Assisted Avatar Discovery (Bulk Review Mining)

A method for discovering untapped avatar segments by feeding large volumes of Amazon reviews into ChatGPT with a structured prompt. This extends the Amazon Competitor Review Analysis framework (above) from competitive intelligence into avatar discovery.

### When Amazon Beats Reddit for Avatar Discovery

Reddit is best for understanding avatars you already know exist — they self-select into communities and discuss their experiences in depth. But Reddit communities tend toward beginner-level discussion, and people with niche conditions (e.g., hair loss from PCOS vs. hair loss from menopause vs. hair loss from postpartum) are scattered across their own subreddits, not gathered in one place.

Amazon reviews are better for discovering avatars you didn't know existed. A single product (e.g., a hair loss shampoo) serves multiple customer types. 55,000 reviews contain postpartum women, menopausal women, PCOS sufferers, cancer survivors, stress-related hair loss — all buying the same product for different reasons. Bulk analysis surfaces these segments automatically.

Use Reddit to go deep on a known avatar. Use Amazon to discover which avatars exist in the first place.

### The Bulk Review Mining Process

Step 1: Find 4-6 top-selling Amazon products in the category with high review counts (2,000+ each). Download reviews using a review extraction tool.

Step 2: Feed all reviews into ChatGPT with a prompt that instructs:
- Act as a top-tier marketer skilled in copywriting, human psychology, and [[halbert-market-first]] principles
- Find specific, scalable avatar segments (large enough to support $1M/month in ad spend)
- Avoid generic categories ("busy professionals," "health enthusiasts," "self-conscious professionals") — these are useless for targeting or research
- Filter for sentences directly linked to the core problem being solved
- Identify unique avatars that haven't been overused in the market
- Focus on individuals with deep burning pain (high desire magnitude = higher willingness to act)
- Structure each avatar with: name, desire, awareness level, how the problem affects their life, likely objections

Step 3: Review AI output critically. ChatGPT defaults to generic segments. Filter for avatars where you can (a) find a distinct community to research further, (b) identify specific emotional drivers different from the broad market, and (c) verify market size.

### Avatar Selection Matrix

After generating candidate avatars, evaluate each on a simple matrix:

| Avatar | Desire Magnitude | Competition Level | Decision |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| [Name] | High/Med/Low | High/Med/Low | Yes/No |

**Desire magnitude** = how deeply the problem impacts their specific life context. Not just "they want a solution" but "the problem is destroying something they care about." A menopausal woman losing hair while already dealing with anxiety, depression, and body changes has higher desire magnitude than a generic "person with thinning hair" — the hair loss is the last straw on top of everything else.

**Competition level** = who is actively advertising to this specific avatar? Check Facebook Ad Library by searching for the condition + product type. Low ad count for a high-desire segment = opportunity signal.

**Decision rule:** High desire + low competition = go. High desire + high competition = possible but harder (need superior creative or product). Low desire = skip regardless of competition.

### Facebook Ad Library as Competitive Validation

After identifying candidate avatars, scan Facebook Ad Library to validate the competitive landscape:

Step 1: Search for the avatar's condition/problem + product category. Filter by country.

Step 2: Count how many brands are actively running ads specifically targeting this avatar (not just the broad category).

Step 3: Analyze what angles they use. Are they speaking to the avatar's specific situation, or running generic ads? Generic competitors are weaker than avatar-specific competitors.

Step 4: Check competitor website traffic (SimilarWeb or similar) to estimate if they're succeeding. High traffic + persistent ads = proven market. Low traffic + few ads = either untapped or unviable.

**The blue ocean principle:** Instead of competing in a massive, saturated category (e.g., "hair loss"), find the specific avatar segment where competition is low but desire is high. Start with that niche, scale to $1-3M, then expand to adjacent avatar segments. You can afford to outspend and out-create competitors when you're the only one speaking directly to a specific group's pain.

**Connection:** This extends [[halbert-market-first]]'s starving crowd principle from "find hungry markets" to "find hungry segments within crowded markets." The starving crowd might be hiding inside a competitive space, just not being served specifically.

### AI Deep Research for Avatar Deep Dives

Beyond discovering avatars (above), AI tools with deep research capabilities (ChatGPT deep research, Claude) can automate the Reddit/forum mining process itself — browsing real URLs, reading actual threads, extracting real quotes, and producing document-quality avatar deep dives.

**The prompt structure that produces useful output:**

1. **Role**: Top creative strategist for a $100M+ direct response brand (specifying "direct response" prevents generic brand-marketing output)
2. **Task**: Conduct a deep dive on [specific avatar] to fill out the Avatar Deep Dive Research template
3. **Template**: Provide the actual template with categories (pain points, day-to-day struggles, victories, failures, goals, desires, beliefs, objections, awareness level, hopes) and explain what each category means with examples
4. **Example**: Upload a completed avatar deep dive from previous manual research as a quality calibration document
5. **Guidelines**:
   - Go deep. No surface-level answers. No short replies or shallow takes.
   - Use customer language. Include exact quotes from Reddit users.
   - Prioritize patterns over outliers. Find big shared beliefs, struggles, and desires — not niche opinions.
   - Keep context intact. Include real-world conversation, post titles, back-and-forth discussion.
   - Explore beyond given subreddits. Follow rabbit holes that align with the avatar's world.
6. **Subreddits**: List specific subreddits to start with, but allow exploration beyond them

**Critical quality control:**
- AI output should be read manually before use. It becomes input for your thinking, not a replacement for it.
- The AI deep dive is a starting point, not the endpoint. Continue manual research daily to surface new angles.
- Generic AI output = generic ads. The prompt quality determines the output quality. Without the role, template, example document, and guidelines, ChatGPT defaults to surface-level summaries.

**Connection:** This is the AI-assisted equivalent of the manual forum research process (Voice of Customer Mining above). Use manual research first to build intuition about the avatar, then use AI deep research to accelerate and expand. The manual research teaches you what to look for; the AI research helps you find more of it faster.

### Ad Comment Mining — Ongoing VOC From Your Own Ads

> Source: GTE Copywriting Sauce community posts

Your own ad comments are a live, constantly updated VOC source. Not the "tag someone" comments — the ones with emotional depth:

- Specific failed solutions they've tried
- Exact emotions they're experiencing
- Identity shifts ("I used to be the person who...")
- Fears about what happens if nothing changes

**The process:**

1. **Collect weekly.** Go through ad comments. Copy every comment with genuine emotional depth into a running document. Look for length, specificity, and raw honesty.
2. **Feed into AI.** Paste the document and ask: "What are their biggest fears? What have they already tried? What emotions show up most? What identity shifts are they experiencing?"
3. **Use the analysis in prompts.** When writing new copy, paste the emotional analysis directly into the copy prompt. "Use these exact emotions. Reference the solutions they've already tried. Use their language, not marketing language."

This produces angles rooted in real emotional data rather than guesswork. One practitioner reports 90% profitable angle hit rate since starting this systematically.

**The engagement campaign hack:** When you can't find enough VOC data (no Facebook groups, target demographic not on Reddit), run a $30 engagement campaign with a native-looking post that touches on the problem your product solves — no product mention, no store link, no solution. Write it as a "cry for help" from a distressed person, emphasizing helplessness and failed solutions. End by inviting people to share their experience. Within days, the comment section fills with stories: failed solutions, frustrations, fears, specific language. These comments become the raw material for angle creation.

**Connection:** This extends the forum research methodology above with a generate-your-own-data approach. Especially valuable for niches where existing VOC data is thin. See [[creative-testing]] for how these angles feed into hypothesis-driven testing.
