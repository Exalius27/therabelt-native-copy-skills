## Core Thesis

Offers are not just "what you sell." They are engineered structures that determine how customers enter, how they pay, how they stay, and how they spend more. The right offer structure can transform the same product into dramatically different revenue outcomes. Hormozi splits all offers into two jobs: **Attraction Offers** (get customers in the door) and **Monetization Offers** (maximize revenue once they're in).

---

## Attraction Offers — Getting Customers In

Attraction offers exist to lower the barrier to first purchase. They trade short-term revenue for long-term customer value. The best ones create a feeling of "I'd be stupid not to try this."

### Win Your Money Back

The customer pays full price. If they hit a defined result or complete defined actions, they get their money back (or store credit). This is Hormozi's highest-performing offer across gym businesses.

Why it works: The customer is fully invested (they paid), but perceives zero risk (they can earn it back). Investment drives compliance — they do the work because they have skin in the game. Most customers who hit the result stay anyway because they've built habits. Those who don't hit the result still paid.

Two modes:
- **Results-based:** Customer gets money back if they achieve the outcome (lose X lbs, make $X/month). They bet on their own ability.
- **Action-based:** Customer gets money back if they complete every required action (attend all sessions, log all meals). Results don't matter — compliance does.

Action-based is safer for the business because you control the criteria. Results-based creates stronger stories but higher payout risk.

### Pay Less Now or Pay More Later

Give the customer a choice: pay a discounted price today, or pay full price after experiencing the result. Example: "$97 now, or $297 tomorrow after the training — cancel before then if you don't love it."

Why it works: Customers self-select into commitment levels. Those who pay now are more invested. Those who try first still convert at high rates because experiencing value before paying creates conviction. The business wins either way — cash now from confident buyers, deferred revenue from skeptics who convert after proof.

### Free Trial with Accountability Fees

A free trial where the customer puts a card on file and gets charged small fees only if they miss commitments (skip sessions, don't log progress). If they complete everything, the trial is truly free.

Why it works: The customer perceives the offer as free, but the accountability fee structure ensures compliance. Compliant customers get results. Customers who get results convert to paid. The fees themselves offset some cost of delivery.

Key execution detail: Get the card BEFORE explaining the fee structure. Frame it as "that's just how we've always done it." If you explain fees first, resistance spikes. After card is down, frame fees as being on the customer's side — "we charge to keep you on track."

### Buy X Get Y Free

Bundle a paid product with a genuinely valuable free addition. The free component must be something the customer would want independently.

Works best when: The free item has high perceived value but low marginal cost to deliver (digital products, group access, recordings). The paid item covers costs; the free item creates the "no-brainer" feeling.

## Monetization Offers — Maximizing Revenue Per Customer

Once a customer is in, monetization offers extract more value from each transaction and each relationship.

### The Economist Play (Decoy Pricing)

Present three options where the middle option makes the bundle obvious. The Economist offered: (A) Digital only — $59, (B) Print only — $125, (C) Print + Digital — $125. Nobody picks B because C is the same price with more. B exists only to make C look like a steal.

Principle: When you want people to buy both options, add a decoy priced identically to the more expensive single option. The decoy makes the bundle look irrational to refuse.

### A/B Upsells (Binary Choice)

Instead of asking "do you want this?" (yes/no), ask "which one do you want?" (A or B). Binary choices assume the sale and let the customer choose HOW to buy rather than WHETHER to buy.

Applicable to: Flavors, sizes, colors, time slots, start dates, delivery speeds, personnel. Nearly anything can become an A/B choice.

Add a nudge: "This is my favorite" or "most people love this" or "Tuesday's sessions are a little smaller." Nudges move sales for customers with limited experience.

### Continuity Discount Offers

Incentivize longer commitments with progressively deeper discounts. Month-to-month costs most, annual costs least per month. The customer saves money; the business locks in predictable revenue.

Cancellation architecture matters: Customers who cancel early pay back the discount they received (returning to month-to-month rate). This means harsh terms = fewer sign-ups but fewer cancellations. Light terms = more sign-ups but more churn. Optimize based on your economics.

Always allow cancellation and always offer an exit interview (waive the cancellation fee as incentive). Exit interviews save customers or reveal what to fix.

### Waived Fee Offers

Charge a setup/onboarding fee for month-to-month customers. Waive it for those who commit to a longer term. The fee itself is a real cost of onboarding — waiving it for committed customers is justified because their LTV covers the cost.

## Pricing Philosophy — Why Higher Prices Create Better Outcomes

Hormozi's central pricing argument: higher prices don't just increase revenue — they increase the value the customer actually receives.

**The wine experiment:** Researchers gave tasters the same wine three times labeled as cheap, medium, and expensive. Tasters rated the "expensive" wine highest. Price literally changed perceived quality. Implication: raising your price raises the customer's experience of your product.

**Investment drives compliance:** The more someone pays, the more they do the work. A $50 gym membership gets ignored. A $5,000 coaching program gets full attention. The customer who pays more gets better results not because the product is different, but because their investment compels them to use it.

**The goal is category-of-one pricing:** Not 10% above competitors. So much higher that the customer thinks "this must be something entirely different." That perception creates a monopoly position where price comparison stops.

**The person who needs the exchange less has the upper hand.** When you don't need the customer's money, you can price freely and select only ideal customers. Premium pricing is both a revenue strategy and a customer quality filter.
