## Core Thesis

A landing page is a belief-shifting journey, not a sales pitch. It guides a cold visitor through sequential psychological stages — emotion first (System 1), then logic (System 2) — so the buying decision feels self-driven. The architecture below is designed for Meta ads traffic (cold → high-intent buyer in one page).

**The dual-system sequence:** Open with System 1 (emotion, visuals, stories, gut feeling) to earn attention and engagement. Transition to System 2 (logic, mechanism, proof, comparison) to justify the decision. Good LPs use both in order — emotion opens the door, logic closes the sale. (Connects to [[persuasion]] Principle 3: Elaboration Likelihood Model — same principle, different application.)

## The 12-Section Blueprint

Each section has a specific psychological job. The order matters — each section builds on the belief state created by the one before it.

### 1. Hero Section — Emotional Hook

**Job:** Capture instant emotional attention (System 1). Make the visitor feel "That's me. I need this." in under 3 seconds.

**Must-haves:**
- **Emotional headline** — Triggers desire by promising a benefit that solves a pain
- **Clarifying subheadline** — Introduces unique mechanism or credibility (why this is different)
- **Visual** — Avatar-facing, not generic product. Before/after, close-up of avatar's face, or UGC-style product-in-use
- **CTA** — Simple, clear, friction-free ("Start Now" not "Buy Now")
- **Trust touch** — Tiny badge, star reviews, or "As seen in..." for instant credibility

**Visual rules:** Person in image looks toward CTA or product (guides eye flow). No white-background product shots alone. No stock photos. No logo dominating the top.

**Connection:** Hero section = the hook. See [[vicious-hooks]] for hook psychology. The headline here serves the same function as the first line of a story-native ad — if System 1 says "Meh," they bounce regardless of what follows.

### 2. Background Story — Narrative Transportation

**Job:** Build emotional connection through a relatable personal story that mirrors the reader's internal struggles. Prime them for deeper belief-shifting.

**Structure formula:**
1. Open with a relatable moment ("I didn't even notice at first...")
2. Describe the progression of pain + confusion
3. Share the emotional toll
4. Tease the turning point — but delay the full reveal
5. Hint at the result, then pull back (leave tension)
6. Bridge smoothly into Problem Amplification

**Why it works:** Narrative transportation makes readers forget they're being sold to (see [[story-craft]]). Identity theory makes them project themselves onto the storyteller. Stories activate emotion before logic kicks in.

**Connection:** This is the LP equivalent of the lead section in a story-native ad (see [[story-native-format]]). Same principle — the story IS the selling.

### 3. Problem Amplification — Pain Agitation

**Job:** Deepen urgency by making the pain feel real, relatable, and pressing. Move the reader from "I relate" to "I need to fix this NOW."

**Structure formula:**
1. Open with emotional fatigue ("You do everything right... and still feel stuck")
2. List failed solutions / lived struggles (checklists or bullets work)
3. Highlight invisible costs — time, confidence, relationships, self-worth
4. Introduce disbelief and urgency ("How can this still be happening?")

**Connection:** This is Kennedy's Agitation step (P-A-S) applied to LP format. See [[direct-response-copywriting]] — agitation is the step most copywriters skip. Maximum emotional pressure between identifying the problem and presenting the solution.

### 4. Root Cause + Villain Framing

**Job:** Give the reader a clear explanation for WHY nothing has worked — then externalize the blame so they stop feeling broken and start feeling motivated.

**Structure formula:**
1. Start with confusion and emotional fatigue
2. Introduce the real root cause (System 2 clarity)
3. Use a metaphor or logic-based analogy
4. Describe what happens when this isn't fixed
5. Reveal the villain: the system, industry, or structure that kept them misinformed
6. Remove shame: "It's not your fault"
7. Bridge to solution: "That's exactly what this approach was made for"

**Why villain framing is critical:** People who blame themselves freeze and don't buy. A villain creates relief ("it's not me"), clarity ("now I understand"), and motivation ("I can fight back"). The reader becomes the hero fighting against the thing that held them down. Without a villain, people feel lost — and that kills action.

**Connection:** Maps to [[rmbc-ii-vsl-architecture]]'s "three failed solutions" structural unit — same function (eliminate competitors, build fear, prime for mechanism), different format.

### 5. Unique Mechanism — Solution Reveal

**Job:** Position the product as the inevitable discovery — the natural fix that addresses the real root cause. Should feel exciting, science-backed, and trustworthy — not hypey.

**Structure formula:**
1. Frame the product as a breakthrough discovery
2. Name the unique mechanism (branded or "science-y" name)
3. Explain how it directly targets the root cause
4. Use a metaphor or simple science explanation
5. Mention proof — studies, experts, tech, third-party credibility
6. Tease upcoming benefits

**Connection:** This is where the mechanism becomes the hero (see [[rmbc-ii-vsl-architecture]] — "The Mechanism is the Hero"). Everything before mechanism = tension-building. Everything after = proof. The reveal is the moment skepticism shifts to belief. See also [[insight-vectors]] for how to generate mechanisms.

### 6. Product Reveal + Features

**Job:** Introduce the product as the physical embodiment of the mechanism. Not "what it is" — "what it activates."

**Structure formula:**
1. Reveal the product name + visual (in-use, not sterile)
2. Reconnect it to the unique mechanism
3. List 3-5 key features — each tied to a specific benefit
4. Explain how features work in harmony to address root cause
5. Use a visual metaphor or real-world comparison
6. Lead into social proof

**Connection:** Features-to-benefits mapping = [[four-legged-stool]] deeper benefit drill (feature → benefit → deeper benefit → expressed indirectly).

### 7. Social Proof — Round 1

**Job:** Show that real people with the same pain trusted the process and got results. Turn belief into evidence.

**Structure formula:**
1. 1 strong transformation quote (before → after narrative)
2. 2-3 short "snackable" UGC lines or review pulls
3. Pain-to-relief framing — highlight what changed
4. Avatar cues: "Mom of 2, 42 yrs", "Struggled with [pain] for 6 years"
5. Optional: video clip or before/after for higher believability

**Rule:** Results, not praise. "It works!" is not good proof. Specific transformation is.

### 8. Offer Stack

**Job:** Present an irresistible deal that makes the price feel small compared to the value.

**Structure formula:**
1. Anchor full price first → then show discount (anchoring effect)
2. Highlight bonus items or added value
3. Include urgency element (limited time, low stock)
4. Bold, clear CTA above the fold for both options
5. Subscription feels like the smart, obvious choice

**Connection:** See [[offer-design]] for the 12 price resistance techniques (Bob Bly), AAC formula, and Rule of 100. Price anchor ceiling: max 20x actual price (see [[direct-response-copywriting]]).

### 9. Transformation Timeline — Future Pacing

**Job:** Help the reader visualize the journey from pain → relief → empowerment. Make the result feel real, measurable, and inevitable.

**How it works:** Show believable progress (Week 1 → Week 4 → Beyond). Include both emotional and physical benefits. Each week builds momentum. Uses mirror neurons — they imagine it happening to them.

**Connection:** Future pacing shifts focus from fear to hope. Activates System 1 (emotion) + System 2 (timeline logic). NOTE: For TheraBelt copy, timestamped transformation is banned — show change through scenes, never week/month numbers.

### 10. Final CTA

**Job:** Restate the emotional core of the offer and make the next step feel natural.

**Must-haves:**
- Restates emotional benefit ("Start feeling like you again")
- CTA button is clear and matched to awareness level
- Trust boosters visible (money-back guarantee, shipping info)
- Visually distinct from rest of page (different background)

**Connection:** See Milligan's Crossroads Close — present two paths, let them choose. Choice = autonomy. People buy when they feel they decided, not when they feel sold. (See [[direct-response-copywriting]].)

### 11. Objection Handling / FAQs

**Job:** Eliminate doubt. This is where fence-sitters either bounce or convert.

**Structure formula:**
1. Reassuring intro line
2. Dropdowns/accordions for mobile UX
3. Focus on real buying blockers (safety, delivery, expectations, trust) — not generic stuff
4. Short, confident, helpful answers
5. Mention guarantee again

**Rule:** FAQ is not filler — it's sales psychology. See [[rmbc-ii-vsl-architecture]]'s finding: FAQ sections in long-form VSLs function as a complete second sales pitch (1,500-3,000 words, each answer re-selling the product).

### 12. Social Proof — Round 2

**Job:** Reinforce belief with fresh proof right when they're almost ready to buy.

**Must-haves:**
- New testimonials (not repeats) — recency bias
- Both emotional AND result-driven outcomes
- Diversity of avatars for relatability
- Soft re-CTA or emotional close

**Why two rounds:** Repetition bias — seeing positive stories again builds familiarity and reduces fear. The bandwagon effect intensifies ("So many people are getting results... why not me?").

## Three Key Psychological Mechanisms Across the LP

These three shape perceived value and drive action across every section:

1. **Processing Fluency** — If the page feels easy to read and understand, people trust it more and decide faster. Big text, clear headlines, short sentences, clean design. If they can skim it and still get it — you win.

2. **Anticipated Regret** — Before people act, they imagine how they'll feel if they DON'T act. "Most people regret waiting." This creates emotional urgency without countdown timers. More powerful than deadline scarcity (see [[persuasion]] — loss aversion).

3. **Anchoring** — People judge value by comparing to the first number they see. Show original price before discount. Compare to expensive alternatives. Show full bonus stack before price. See [[offer-design]] for detailed techniques.

## Villain Framing — Deep Principle

This deserves its own section because it's the most operationally distinct principle in this source.

**The mechanism:** People naturally try to understand why they're stuck. Without a clear external reason, they blame themselves. Shame freezes action. A villain creates:
- **Relief** — "It's not me"
- **Clarity** — "Now I understand why nothing worked"
- **Motivation** — "I can fight back — with this product"

**Common villain types:**
- Hormones / biology (PCOS, genetics)
- The broken system (Big Pharma, fake industry, sugar industry)
- Past habits ("you were never taught the right way")
- Environment (stress, toxins, hidden causes)

**Connection:** Maps to [[rmbc-ii-vsl-architecture]]'s "three failed solutions" — both externalize blame, but villain framing does it through a named enemy rather than through listing what didn't work. The two techniques work together: failed solutions show WHAT didn't work, villain framing explains WHY.

**For TheraBelt context:** Villain framing must be handled carefully — TheraBelt uses elevation framing, not problem-solving framing. The villain (if used) would be time/aging itself, never a medical condition. See [[company-overview]] and [[positioning]].
