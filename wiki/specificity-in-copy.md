## Core Thesis

Vague copy is invisible. The reader's brain has been trained to auto-filter generic phrases ("game-changing," "life saver," "worth every penny") the same way it filters banner ads. If your copy could describe a competitor's product, it's doing nothing.

Specificity is not a nice-to-have. It is the competitive moat between brands that scale and brands that stall. And it produces a paradox: the more painfully specific you get about one person's experience, the more violently a wider audience relates.

**The Competitor Swap Test (litmus test for all copy):** Before publishing any line, ask: "Could this sentence describe a competitor's product?" Yes = kill it. No = ship it.

---

## Principle 1: Labels vs. Evidence

Two types of copy exist.

**Labels** = telling someone the CATEGORY of result. "Changed my life." "Game changer." "So good." Labels are a closed door. The reader's brain has seen them 47 times before lunch. They're wallpaper.

**Evidence** = showing someone the FOOTAGE of that result. "I stopped canceling dates because I was ashamed of my jawline acne." Evidence kicks the door open and drags you inside.

The distinction: labels describe what happened in the broadest possible terms. Evidence recreates the scene so the reader's brain makes a movie. One gets scrolled past. The other gets screenshotted and sent to a friend at 11pm.

**Why this matters beyond conversion:** Vague copy attracts vague buyers (chargebacks, 1-star reviews, subscription ghosts). Specific copy attracts specific buyers who saw their actual life described in your ad. Those people are locked in. Specificity doesn't just convert better — it converts better people.

**Connection:** This is the operational application of Hopkins' specificity principle ([[direct-response-copywriting]]). Hopkins proved that specific claims carry weight because they imply testing. This source extends it: specific *stories* carry weight because they imply lived experience.

## Principle 2: The Door Framework

Every vague phrase is a locked door hiding a room full of sellable details.

Your only job: kick the door in and describe what's inside.

"Game changing" is a door. Behind it:
- What does their morning actually look like now?
- What did they stop doing?
- What did they start doing?
- Who noticed?
- What moment made them realize it was working?

The room behind the door is the ad. The door itself is garbage.

**The crowbar question:** "What does that look like?" Ask it repeatedly until you hit emotion + behavior simultaneously.

Example chain:
1. "This product is amazing." → What does amazing look like?
2. "My skin is clearer." → What does clearer look like?
3. "I don't have breakouts." → What does not having breakouts look like?
4. "I stopped canceling dates because I was humiliated by my jawline." → THAT'S the copy.

Four questions deep. Most people stop at one. This is the same depth-extraction principle as the [[copy-research]] interview technique, applied to every single line of copy.

## Principle 3: The 5 Ingredients of Specific Copy

Every strong specific line contains the same DNA. Miss one and the copy limps. Hit all five and it's a weapon.

**1. TIME** — Real numbers. "15 minutes." "Day 3." "3 weeks." "2am." Never "quickly" or "fast." The number.

**2. PEOPLE** — Other humans and their reactions. Husband. Boss. Mom. Boyfriend. Kids. Their reactions are social proof baked into the story. (Connection: this is why [[story-craft]] requires real dialogue and named characters — the people ARE the proof.)

**3. CONTRAST** — Before AND after. "Used to... now I..." Without the before, there's no gap. Without the gap, there's no desire. Without desire, there's no sale.

**4. BEHAVIOR CHANGE** — What they literally DO differently. Not how they "feel" — what they DO. Stopped wearing foundation. Stopped ordering DoorDash. Played with their kids. (Connection: this maps to [[sugarman-triggers]] — involvement through concrete action, not abstract sentiment.)

**5. UNEXPECTED DETAILS** — The stuff nobody would make up. Grinding teeth. Shower drain. Cereal over the sink. Pillow creases. You can't fake these. Your audience knows it. (Connection: this is why [[copy-research]] mines forums for raw language — the unexpected details live there, not in a copywriter's imagination.)

## Principle 4: The 4-Level Depth Model

Most copywriters stop too early. The first "decent" line is worthless.

| Level | Example (sleep product) | Status |
|-------|------------------------|--------|
| 1 — Surface | "I sleep better." | Useless. Delete. |
| 2 — Clearer | "I sleep through the night now." | Better. Still generic. |
| 3 — Visual | "I don't wake up at 3am staring at the ceiling anymore." | Getting warm. There's an image. |
| 4 — Emotional + Behavioral | "I used to dread getting into bed because I knew I'd be up for hours bargaining with my own brain. Now I actually look forward to it. I didn't know adults were allowed to look forward to bedtime." | THAT'S the copy. Stop here. |

**The rule:** Keep pushing until you hit emotion AND behavior in the same line. A feeling AND an action. That's where conversions live.

Level 1 gets you a 1.2 ROAS. Level 4 gets you a 4x. Same product. Different words.

---

## Principle 5: The Specificity Paradox

The more specific you get, the MORE people relate. This is counterintuitive.

- "Tired of feeling tired?" → speaks to everyone, lands with nobody.
- "I used to set 6 alarms and still show up to work with pillow creases on my face." → every exhausted human just felt SEEN.

Broad language bounces off. Specific language triggers a jolt of recognition: "How do they know? Are they watching me?"

That jolt IS the sale.

**Why it works:** When you describe one person's exact experience, everyone who shares a version of it maps their own life onto the details. The specific scene becomes a projection surface ([[story-craft]] calls this "the reader thinks about her own dog"). Generic language gives the brain nothing to project onto.

---

## Application: The "Without It" Story

The "without it" story does more selling than the "with it" story ever could. Loss aversion does all the work.

Ask your best customers: "What would happen if you couldn't use this product anymore?"

The fear of going back is always more specific than the joy of moving forward:
- "I'd be back to waking up exhausted and mean."
- "I'd have to go back to full-coverage foundation every single day."
- "I'd probably blow $300 a month on DoorDash again."

This is a research source ([[copy-research]]) and a copy structure simultaneously.

## Quick-Reference: Rewrite Patterns

| Vague label | What it's hiding | Rewrite direction |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| "Game changing" | Before/after footage | What does the morning look like now? |
| "Life saver" | The thing that was destroying them | What was so bad it needed "saving"? |
| "Can't live without it" | What collapse looks like | What happened when they ran out? |
| "Worth every penny" | The money they burned before | What were they spending on things that didn't work? |
| "Best ever" | The comparison stack | What specifically failed before this? |
| "Highly recommend" | The moment of conviction | What moment made them realize it was working? |
