# RMBC II: Funnel Structure & the 15 Decision Factors

> Sources: [[raw/2026-04-07-1-2-checkouts-walkthrough-part-2-mp4.md]]
> Related: [[meta-ads-strategy]], [[funnel-economics]], [[rmbc-ii-advertorials-and-pdps]], [[rmbc-ii-email-sequences-retention]], [[offer-design]]
> Last updated: 2026-04-08

The strategic framework for deciding which copy types go where in your funnel. Rather than guessing or copying what competitors do, RMBC II provides a systematic checklist (15 factors) that predicts the optimal funnel shape for any product/market combination. For platform-specific application of these principles to Meta campaigns, see [[meta-ads-strategy]].

## Why Funnel Structure Matters

**The old paradigm:** Ad → Long VSL on landing page → Checkout. Always this shape. Still works sometimes, but increasingly rare.

**The 2025 reality:** Ad → [Pre-sale page varies] → Checkout. The pre-sale page could be VSL, advertorial, product page, or none at all. It depends on 15 factors.

A product that requires heavy education needs 20 minutes of mechanism explanation. A product with high market awareness needs 2 minutes to checkout. Same product type, different markets, opposite funnel shapes.

**The 15-factor checklist** helps you decide before you write a single word.

## The Two-Stage Customer Journey

### Stage 1: Prospect to Purchaser (A → B)
**Funnel shape:** Ad → Pre-sale page(s) → Checkout

**Variations:**
- Ad → VSL → Checkout (classic)
- Ad → Advertorial → Checkout (editorial approach)
- Ad → Product page → Checkout (no pre-sale)
- Ad → Advertorial → VSL → Checkout (maximum persuasion)
- Ad → VSL ad → Advertorial → Product page → Checkout (complex, high-ticket)

### Stage 2: Customer to Repeat (B → C → D → E)
**Funnel element:** Upsell, email sequences, reorder triggers

## The 15 Decision Factors

### Factor Group 1: Product Type & Category

**1. Is it physical, digital, or service?**
- Physical product (supplement, device, apparel) → Can show, often needs testimonials
- Digital product (course, software, template) → Needs demonstration, often higher perceived price resistance
- Service (coaching, consulting) → Highest trust barrier, longest education needed

**Decision:** Physical products can close faster (show it, prove it works, sell). Digital and services need more trust-building.

**2. Price point**
- Under $50 → Low risk, can close on minimal proof
- $50-300 → Moderate risk, needs solid proof + mechanism
- $300-2000 → High risk, needs extensive education + guarantee
- $2000+ → Very high risk, needs highest trust, longest sales process

**Decision:** Price point directly correlates to education length. Higher price = longer funnel.

**3. Competitive intensity**
- Uncontested market (new product category) → Can be shorter funnel, you're teaching the problem
- Moderate competition (3-5 strong competitors) → Medium funnel, need differentiation
- High competition (10+ competitors, known category) → Need longer funnel, stronger mechanism

**Decision:** New categories can close faster (you define the solution). Crowded categories need deep differentiation.

**4. Uniqueness of mechanism**
- Completely novel mechanism → Longer funnel needed to explain
- Similar to existing products → Shorter funnel, just prove superiority
- Me-too product → Very short funnel, compete on price/convenience

**Decision:** Novel mechanisms need mechanism-education time. Me-too products need speed.

---

### Factor Group 2: Market Awareness Level

**5. What's the market awareness level?**

This is THE critical factor. It overrides almost everything else.

**Unaware:** Doesn't know the problem exists
- Example: Woman with dark circles doesn't think of it as treatable; thinks it's genetic
- Funnel need: Teach problem exists, teach it's solvable, teach YOUR solution
- Time needed: Longest
- Funnel type: Ad → Long advertorial OR VSL → Checkout OR Product page
- Example structure: Native ad (looks like news) → Advertorial (teaches problem) → VSL (mechanism reveal) → Checkout

**Problem-aware:** Knows problem exists, wants to solve it, shopping for solutions
- Example: Woman sees dark circles as a problem, searching for solutions
- Funnel need: Show WHY your solution works differently than others
- Time needed: Medium
- Funnel type: Ad → VSL OR Short advertorial → Checkout OR Product page
- Example structure: Meta ad → VSL (mechanism reveal) → Checkout

**Solution-aware:** Shopping between known solutions, knows category exists
- Example: Woman knows about retinol, comparing retinol products
- Funnel need: Prove you're the better choice (price, quality, guarantee)
- Time needed: Short
- Funnel type: Ad → Product page → Checkout
- Example structure: Meta ad → Direct to PDP with comparison table → Checkout

**Product-aware:** Customer from before OR knows your brand
- Example: Previous buyer ordering again; or brand enthusiast
- Funnel need: Minimal. Removed friction is the goal.
- Time needed: Shortest
- Funnel type: Ad → Checkout (or email reminder → Checkout)
- Example structure: Email → Direct checkout (maybe minimal product page)

**Decision:** This factor alone can flip your entire funnel. A supplement for unaware market needs 2x the funnel length vs. solution-aware market. Same product, different awareness = completely different strategy.

---

### Factor Group 3: Customer Psychology & Objections

**6. What's the primary psychological barrier?**
- Trust in category (people don't believe supplements work) → Need mechanism education + proof
- Trust in brand (people don't know your brand) → Need credibility builders, guarantees
- Fear of side effects → Need safety education, testimonials from skeptical people
- Fear of wasting money → Need strong guarantee, risk reversal
- Identity concerns (embarrassment, shame) → Need relatability, privacy assurance

**Decision:** Different barriers require different copy angles. Trust barrier = mechanism focus. Price fear = guarantee focus.

**7. Is there fear or shame around the problem?**
- High shame (baldness, erectile dysfunction, incontinence) → Need very relatable storytelling, not just mechanism
- Low shame (supplement for energy) → Can be more direct mechanism-first approach

**Decision:** Shame-related problems need longer lead-in to build trust. Problem-solution can come later.

---

### Factor Group 4: Market Dynamics

**8. Is there time urgency?** (Real urgency, not manufactured)
- Seasonal (winter skincare needs, holiday gifting) → Can emphasize timely relevance
- Constantly urgent (pain relief, energy) → Ongoing relevance
- No natural urgency → Must create via scarcity/limited offer

**Decision:** Real urgency can shorten funnel. Fake urgency damages trust.

**9. Is the audience actively searching?** (Intent level)
- High intent (searching for solutions) → Shorter funnel, they're ready
- Medium intent (aware of problem, not actively searching) → Medium funnel, need motivation
- Low intent (unaware of problem) → Longest funnel, must educate first

**Decision:** Search volume (and intent) determines how aggressively you can close.

---

### Factor Group 5: Offer & Packaging

**10. Is this a one-time purchase or subscription/repeat?**
- One-time purchase → Can afford longer, more expensive funnel (higher LTV per customer)
- Subscription or repeat → Lower acquisition cost acceptable (will repeat)
- High-ticket one-time → Can afford even longer, more expensive funnel

**Decision:** Repeat business models justify longer, more expensive funnels.

**11. Are there natural upsells?** (Products customers will want after)
- Yes (supplement → higher tier, accessories, complementary products) → Can take lower margin on first sale
- No → Must close sale at higher margin immediately

**Decision:** If upsells exist, you can invest in longer acquisition funnel.

**12. What's the perceived value gap?** (Normal price vs. your price)
- Large gap (normally $200, you're selling $50) → Short funnel works (good deal speaks for itself)
- Small gap (normal $50, you're selling $45) → Need longer funnel (why is it different/better?)
- Negative gap (you're more expensive) → Longest funnel (must justify premium)

**Decision:** The bigger the value gap in your favor, the shorter the funnel can be.

---

### Factor Group 6: Platform & Distribution

**13. What's the primary platform?** (Where customers see ads)
- Meta (Facebook/Instagram) → Short-form video ads, often VSL or advertorial pre-sale
- Native advertising → Natural fit for advertorial-to-VSL flow
- YouTube → Educational format works; can go longer
- TikTok → Must be short, hook-heavy; long VSLs won't work
- Google/Search → Intent is high; can be shorter funnel
- Email → Can be longer form; audience is warm

**Decision:** Platform determines ad format and pre-sale page type.

**14. What's the expected CTR?** (How many people click ads)
- Low CTR (cold audience, low awareness) → Need longer funnel to close higher % of visitors
- High CTR (warm audience, high intent) → Shorter funnel acceptable

**Decision:** Lower CTR compensates with higher conversion rate through longer funnel.

---

### Factor Group 7: Competitive & Business Context

**15. What are your margins/budget?** (How much can you spend to acquire a customer)
- Slim margins, limited budget → Must be efficient, shorter funnel
- Healthy margins, robust budget → Can spend more on longer, more expensive funnels (video production, etc.)

**Decision:** Budget determines funnel complexity and copy production scope.

---

## The Decision Matrix

Once you've evaluated all 15 factors, you're ready to decide funnel shape. Here's how factors stack:

### Short Funnel Indicators
- High market awareness (solution or product-aware)
- High intent audience
- Large value gap in your favor
- Low price point
- High CTR/warm audience
- Good margins
- Platform favors brevity (TikTok, Google shopping)

**Typical funnel:** Ad → Product page → Checkout (2-5 min sales time)

### Medium Funnel Indicators
- Problem-aware market
- Moderate competition
- Moderate price point ($50-500)
- Repeat business model
- Healthy margins
- Platform supports video (Meta, YouTube)

**Typical funnel:** Ad → Advertorial or medium VSL → Checkout (8-15 min sales time)

### Long Funnel Indicators
- Unaware market
- High competition
- High price point ($500+)
- Complex mechanism
- Shame/trust barriers
- Healthy margins
- One-time high-ticket purchase

**Typical funnel:** Ad → Long advertorial + VSL + Product page → Checkout (20-40 min sales time)

## Real Examples from RMBC II Case Studies

### Dr. Clean (Cleaning Spray)
- Product type: Physical, low price ($20-50)
- Market awareness: Solution-aware (people know about cleaning sprays)
- Competition: Extreme (Lysol, Clorox, dollar store options)
- Value gap: Small or negative (premium positioning)
- Analysis: Needs longer funnel despite low price, because of competition

**Chosen funnel:** Ad → Long advertorial + VSL → Product page → Checkout

### Emma (Digestive Supplement)
- Product type: Physical supplement, moderate price ($30-40)
- Market awareness: Problem-aware (people know about digestive issues, may not know Emma)
- Competition: High
- Value gap: Small (many supplements exist)
- Repeat: Yes (subscription model)

**Chosen funnel:** Ad → VSL → Product page + upsell → Email sequences (welcome, promotional, reorder)

### Happy Mammoth (Supplement)
- Product type: Physical supplement, moderate price
- Market awareness: Problem-aware
- Mechanism: Novel (unique formulation)
- Margins: Healthy (can afford longer funnel)

**Chosen funnel:** Ad → VSL → Product page → Upsell (at checkout) → Email sequences

### Capital Syndicate (High-ticket coaching)
- Product type: Service (coaching), very high price ($5k+)
- Market awareness: Problem-aware (people know they want to build businesses)
- Competition: Extreme
- Trust barrier: Highest
- Psychology: Success identity

**Chosen funnel:** Ad → VSL → Product page + FAQ + Objection handling → Checkout + webinar/discovery call

## Practitioner Case Study: Systematic Funnel Diagnosis (0.5% → 3% CVR)

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course)

A step-by-step example of diagnosing a funnel as a system rather than guessing. The store was stuck at 0.5-0.8% CVR with solid ad metrics (CPCs under $1, normal CPMs). The operator's instinct was to blame traffic — but the ads were performing fine.

**Step 1: Tighten targeting.** Cut to one gender, focused on 40+. Result: +0.1% CVR. Helpful but not the fix.

**Step 2: Map the funnel as a system.** Ad → Advertorial → Sales Page. Found: 20-27% of advertorial readers clicked to sales page, but only 1-2% of those converted. The break was at the sales page, not the ads or advertorial.

**Step 3: Diagnose the sales page.** Multiple AI tools gave the same critique: "You're treating the sales page like the customer hasn't already been educated." The advertorial had already built belief and emotional buy-in, but the sales page was still teaching instead of closing. Fix: cut educational content, rewrote headline for core demo, refocused on buying. Result: +0.2% CVR, now at ~1%.

**Step 4: Track value per visitor, not just CVR.** Revenue was ~$1/visitor. After a 1,500-word ad and 3,200-word advertorial, visitors were deeply qualified. $1/visitor for that level of education = the offer is wrong, not the persuasion.

**Step 5: Simplify the offer.** Original bundles were logical (pay more → better results) but added friction. Fix: stripped to core product only, moved extras to cart upsells. Result: $1/visitor → $7/visitor (6-7x increase). Final CVR: ~3%.

**Key principles confirmed:**
- Don't blame ads first — identify WHERE in the funnel the break actually is
- Value per visitor exposes offer problems that CVR alone misses (connects to Earnings Per Session in the CRO section below)
- After heavy pre-sale education, the sales page's job is to CLOSE, not re-educate
- Simpler offers with upsells can dramatically outperform logical bundle structures (see also [[offer-design]])

## Quiz Funnels as Alternative Entry Point

> Source: raw/2026-04-08-13-funnel.md (GTE e-commerce course)

Quiz funnels replace or supplement the advertorial step. Instead of Ad → Advertorial → PDP, the flow is Ad → Quiz → PDP.

**How it works:** A series of questions gradually leads the prospect to realize the severity of their problem. Each question makes them more aware and more qualified. By the time they reach the product page, they've self-diagnosed.

**Structure:**
1. Generic/demographic questions (low commitment)
2. Awareness-building questions (reveal the problem's severity)
3. "Analyzing your answers" page with progress bar + social proof (builds anticipation during load delay)
4. Redirect to product page

**Early results from practitioner:** Quiz funnel converting at 1.24-2.76% vs. 0.66% on the advertorial, with only one ad set and minimal optimization. Early data, but directionally strong.

**Tools:** Octane AI (Shopify app, $50/month plan). Quiz builders auto-generate initial quizzes that can be customized.

**When to test:** When advertorial performance plateaus, or when the product has a natural diagnostic angle (health, skincare, pet care — anything where "do you have this problem?" can be explored through questions).

## Common Mistakes

**Copying competitor's funnel without analyzing context.** Your competitor might be succeeding despite their funnel, not because of it. Analyze the 15 factors for YOUR situation.

**Assuming one funnel works for all markets.** Different customer segments have different awareness levels. May need different funnels.

**Making funnel longer than audience awareness allows.** If market is solution-aware, a 20-minute VSL will turn them away. They're ready to buy; you're educating.

**Making funnel shorter than psychological barriers require.** High-shame product with low trust in category needs longer education, even if price is low.

**Confusing platform constraints with market strategy.** TikTok *requires* short videos, but that's format, not strategy. You can still drive to longer-form page if needed.

## Conversion Revenue Optimization — The CRO Layer (Ethan Bence, 4080 Marketing)

CRO is the systematic layer that optimizes funnel performance after the funnel shape is decided. Not button colors — structural changes that move earnings per session. Bence's team generates $10M+/year in incremental revenue from split testing across e-commerce, info products, and call funnels. RMBC II Bonus Training.

### The Golden Metric: Earnings Per Session

**Earnings per session = Conversion Rate × Average Order Value.** This is the number you optimize for, not conversion rate alone. Increasing conversion rate at the expense of AOV (e.g., 99% off sale) is pointless. CRO means increasing the revenue that each visitor generates.

**Connection to ROAS:** Earnings per session ÷ cost per click = ROAS. If your EPS is $2 and your CPC is $1, your ROAS is 2x. Move EPS up and everything downstream improves: revenue, customer count, LTV base, ad spend headroom, exit multiples.

### Inside-Out CRO Framework

Test from the bottom of the funnel up, not the top down. Like airport security — the bottleneck determines system throughput regardless of how good check-in is.

**Priority order:**
1. **Post-purchase upsells** — Can't lose conversion rate (already purchased). Pure upside.
2. **Checkout** — Every buyer passes through. Adding social proof to checkout often yields 10-20% lifts. See checkout optimization checklist below.
3. **Cart** — Another universal bottleneck.
4. **Sales page / landing page** — Above the fold first (everyone sees it; only 12% reach midpage).
5. **Product / collection pages** — Design and information architecture.
6. **Navigation / menu** — Eliminate exits back to the battlefield (social media).

### Checkout Optimization Checklist

From real brand checkout audits (Emma, Happy Mammoth, Capital Syndicate, David):

1. **"No subscription" reassurance.** If selling one-time, say it explicitly: "No subscription. No hidden fees. This is a one-time purchase." Put it near the CTA. Subscription anxiety is a real conversion killer — consumers are burned out on surprise subscriptions.

2. **CTA button copy matters.** "Claim Your Supply Now" outperforms generic "Buy Now" or "Add to Cart." The language on the button is not trivial — brands like Emma test this heavily. Match the button to the energy of the offer (claiming, getting, starting — not just transacting).

3. **Testimonials on checkout.** Push hesitant buyers over the edge. Place 2-3 testimonials near the payment fields. Keep dates current — testimonials from 2+ years ago signal a dead or stale brand. If using dates, update regularly. If you can't maintain freshness, omit dates entirely (they last longer but lose recency signal).

4. **Phone number on checkout (own traffic only).** If you run your own ads (not affiliates), add a phone number. Fence-sitters who would otherwise abandon will call and convert. Affiliates don't want phone numbers because call center sales don't attribute to them — but on your own traffic, it's free conversions. Even major brands like Emma are missing this.

5. **Quantity toggling on checkout.** Show 1/3/6 options with visible per-unit savings directly on the checkout page. If someone lands on a single-bottle checkout and can see that 3 or 6 bottles saves them money, some will upgrade. Potential AOV lift with zero additional traffic.

6. **Mobile: fields above the fold.** On mobile checkout, the information fields (email, shipping) should be immediately visible. Social proof, offer stack, and testimonials go below. Don't make mobile buyers scroll to start entering information.

7. **Offer bumps: test total funnel AOV.** Bumps look great in isolation (40% take rate) but can cannibalize downstream upsells. Always test bump present vs. absent on total funnel AOV, not just bump conversion. See [[funnel-economics]] for the math.

### Testing Philosophy

**Bigger swings beat micro-optimizations.** Changing button colors takes the same traffic to reach significance as changing the entire above-the-fold section — but the button test yields 2% gain while the ATF test yields 20%. Losers are losers for one week; winners are winners for months.

**You are not the customer.** Internal opinions about which version "looks better" are wrong 50% of the time. Even with years of experience, Bence reports being right only half the time. Divorce ego from testing.

**Don't assume competitor success.** Just because a competitor runs a page doesn't mean it's working. They might be succeeding despite their funnel, not because of it. They're looking at YOUR page too.

**Get paid while learning.** An 80% probability winner at unknown causation beats a precisely understood loser. Implement the winner, then figure out why it works.

### High-Impact Test Types

1. **Price tests** — The easiest test. Most brands never test pricing. Customers don't know your COGS. Test 10-15% higher; often no conversion rate change with pure margin gain. Info products may actually convert BETTER at higher prices ($39→$49, $199→$299).

2. **Avatar/demographic tests** — Testing different hero images for different customer segments. A demographic image swap (younger vs. older model) yielded 34% increase. Heat maps and review mining reveal which segments are actually buying.

3. **Above-the-fold tests** — Headlines, sub-headlines, hero images. Run 10-12 variations in a couple months. Once you find a winner, immediately retest — odds of finding the best version on the first win are low.

4. **Social proof placement** — Hammer reviews, testimonial counts, and customer numbers across menu, cart, checkout. Subconscious trust-building throughout reduces checkout anxiety.

5. **Offer structure** — When stuck at a scaling wall, flip the offer. Test 3-4 different offer structures simultaneously (bundles, free add-ons, different pricing tiers). One always booms.

### CRO Infrastructure

- **Split testing software:** Convert.com, VWO, IntelliGems, Optimizely — any is fine. Tech-stack agnostic.
- **Heat mapping:** Hotjar, Lucky Orange, heatmap.com — attribute revenue to clicks. Reveals where people engage vs. where they convert.
- **Lifetime value tagging:** Tag variants in CRM to measure not just immediate conversion but 3/6/9-month LTV. A variant with lower day-one EPS might have higher LTV.
- **Incremental revenue tracking:** Track monthly incremental revenue per test, not just percentage lifts.

### Statistical Significance (Practitioner Rules)

- **Minimum test duration:** 1 week before calling a loser, 14 days before calling a winner
- **Cover a full business cycle** — don't miss weekends, paydays, or seasonal patterns
- **Bayesian 80-85% probability to win** is acceptable for business decisions — this isn't pharmaceutical research
- **Representative sample required** — don't cherry-pick traffic segments or time periods

### Key Insight: Talk to Your Customers

The single highest-impact CRO activity is calling customers on the phone. Not scraping reviews into ChatGPT — actual phone calls. 15 calls will yield headlines, sub-headlines, and offer ideas that no amount of competitor analysis will produce. Customers are the best copywriters in the world. Listen for feelings and emotions, not stated preferences (what they say they want vs. what actually motivated them are different things). Customer service teams and salespeople are the second-best source.

## E-Commerce Distribution Channels (Eddie Maalouf, RMBC II Bonus)

Multi-channel distribution tactics from an agency spending $40-50M/month on Meta, managing ~$2B/year in e-commerce sales. RMBC II Bonus Training.

### Amazon as Additive Revenue (Not Cannibalization)

**Core insight:** Amazon buyers and direct-to-consumer buyers are largely different populations. 9 out of 10 people who open the Amazon app transact — they don't shop, they buy. Most Amazon buyers ONLY shop on Amazon. Adding Amazon typically adds 50%+ of DTC revenue without diluting existing channels. Average revenue growth for new Amazon brands: 153%.

**Key tactics:**
- **Amazon SEO over PPC.** Organic ranking = zero acquisition cost beyond Amazon fees. Some keywords have $30-40M/month in transaction volume. PPC bids can be cut to half the suggested bid and still get volume at strong ROAS (11-35x reported).
- **Brand defense is mandatory.** If you're running traffic and not on Amazon, competitors bid on your brand keywords for pennies and steal your transactions.
- **Custom referral links** reduce Amazon fees from ~25% to ~8% when you drive external traffic. Amazon incentivizes external traffic with reduced commission.
- **Amazon + Meta catalog integration** (upcoming) — Amazon fulfillment with two-day delivery through Meta ads. Early movers with high Amazon rankings will benefit most.
- **Customer data extraction.** Amazon is legally required to provide buyer physical addresses and names for tax purposes. Cross-match with email-finding software to build customer lists.
- **LTV tracking exists on Amazon.** Repeat customer rates, LTV-to-CAC ratios trackable. Reported 6.3x LTV:CAC without VSLs or direct response.

### TikTok Shop as Creative Farm

**Core insight:** TikTok Shop is less about direct sales and more about farming a massive creative pool. Most successful Meta ad accounts at scale are powered by TikTok Shop-sourced creatives.

**The flywheel:**
1. Get on TikTok Shop (even with zero volume initially)
2. Run promotions to email list to build initial volume (creators check your numbers before partnering)
3. Use automated outreach bots to filter and contact creators at scale
4. 100 creators make content → ~5 produce winners
5. Put those 5 on retainer ($1,500-2,000/month for ~10 videos/week)
6. Take winning TikTok creatives and run them as Meta ads (often become top performers)
7. Increased GMV lets you reach bigger creators → cycle compounds

**TikTok incentives (time-limited):** Co-funding where TikTok covers 10-20% of promotional discounts. Some cases: TikTok fronts the entire discount and reimburses the brand.

**Trend intelligence:** Tools like FastMoss and CaloData provide transparent visibility into trending TikTok products. TikTok is the trend predictor — products blow up on TikTok 1-2 months before everywhere else.

### Meta Tactics at Scale

**Listicle landing pages.** "6 Reasons Why [Product] Is Your Key to [Result]" format. Third-party editorial feel builds trust vs. product page self-promotion. CPA drops 20-50% vs. standard product pages, especially in health and cosmetics.

**Page whitelisting.** Run ads from different Facebook pages (not your brand page). Same targeting, same audience, but CPMs can drop by 2/3. A brand page at $21 CPM vs. a generic "Healthy Finds" page at $8.80 CPM with the same ad. VAs can build content-filled pages with AI in an hour.

**Test every campaign type.** CBO, ABO, Advantage Plus, cost caps, bid caps — what works varies by account. Don't swear by one approach. Let the algorithm do the audience/placement work (it's better than human media buyers now); focus human energy on creatives, hooks, and offers.

**Visitor identity resolution.** Software that identifies anonymous website visitors (60+ seconds on site, viewed a product) and pulls their email, address, name from a 280M-person database. Reported results: $1.5M additional revenue in 30 days, 145K new emails, 52% open rate on cold emails, $11.5K/month software cost. Feed resolved identities back to Meta as lookalike or direct targeting audiences.

## The Scoring System

Rate each of the 15 factors from 1 (needs most copy) to 5 (needs least copy). Higher total score = shorter, simpler funnel. Lower total score = longer, heavier funnel.

**Score tiers and recommended structures:**

| Total Score Range | Funnel Weight | Recommended Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **46+** (high) | Lightest | Static or short ad → Product page → Cart |
| **31-45** (mid) | Medium | 3-10 min video ad or short ad → Quiz or advertorial → PDP → Cart |
| **15-30** (low) | Heaviest | Ad → Advertorial → 25-45 min VSL or webinar → Checkout (possibly application/ascension) |

**Example — GetThin (GLP-1 telemedicine):** Scores ~5 on market awareness (everyone knows GLP-1s exist), ~5 on demonstrable proof (prescribed medication, zero doubt it works), ~5 on urgency. Total score high → minimal funnel: static ad → simple landing page → medical questionnaire. Did $20M+ with near-zero long-form copy.

**Counterexample — ClickBank weight loss supplement:** Low awareness (unknown brand), low trust, crowded market, no demonstrable proof, moderate price perceived as high vs. Amazon alternatives. Score low across most factors → longest funnel with full VSL needed.

**Key rule:** Even if 14 factors score high, one critical low-scoring factor (e.g., market completely unaware) can override the total and require a longer funnel. The scoring is a guide, not a formula.

### Quick Diagnostic: AOV × Conversion Rate

Agency heuristic (unverified but directionally useful): If AOV × conversion rate < 350, CRO is the bottleneck. Example: $200 AOV × 1.75% CVR = 350. Breaks down at low AOV (would require unrealistic conversion rates). More useful at $100+ AOV ranges. Not a bible metric, but a quick sanity check.

## How to Use This in Your Work

1. **Before writing a single word,** run through the 15 factors
2. **Score each factor 1-5** (1 = needs most copy/longest funnel, 5 = needs least)
3. **Sum the scores.** Use the tier table above as a starting point.
4. **Check for overrides.** If 12 factors score high but market awareness is unaware, the funnel may still need to be longer.
5. **Decide on specific copy types** based on the funnel shape decision
6. **Brief creation** happens with full funnel understanding
