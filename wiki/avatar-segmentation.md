## Core Thesis

When you don't have a superior product, you compete by understanding a specific sub-avatar better than anyone else. The general audience (everyone with the problem) has already been saturated with ads from bigger brands with bigger budgets. Sub-avatars — narrower slices of that audience defined by demographics, situation, or deeper desire — give you a leverage point that generic competitors can't match.

This is the avatar-level application of [[halbert-market-first]]: starving crowd selection applied not at the market level, but at the audience segmentation level within a market.

---

## Principle 1: The General Avatar Is a Red Ocean

Every competitor in a niche targets the obvious audience. Hair loss → everyone who wants hair back. Dog aging → every dog owner. The general avatar's surface desires are known to everyone: confidence, looking better, living longer. These desires are real but they're also what every other brand is communicating.

When you sell to the general avatar, you need one of two things:
1. **A superior product** — a genuinely new mechanism or demonstrably better formulation that makes the comparison about the product itself, not the messaging.
2. **A massive ad budget** — enough spend to outbid competitors who are communicating the same desires to the same people.

Most brands have neither. Without product superiority or budget superiority, selling to the general avatar means fighting for scraps left by established brands.

---

## Principle 2: Sub-Avatars Have Deeper Desires

The general avatar's desire stops at the surface: "I want my hair back." A sub-avatar's desire goes deeper because their specific situation gives the problem a more specific meaning.

The "so what" chain reveals the deeper desire:

- General: I want a beard.
- So what? I want to look better.
- So what? I want to attract women.
- So what? I'm single, I'm in the dating game, and I believe my patchy beard is the reason women reject me.

That final level — the real desire — is only visible when you define a specific sub-avatar (single men in the dating game who believe their beard affects their attractiveness). The general audience "men who want a beard" includes men who are already married, already happy, already have a girlfriend. Those men don't share the same deep desire, so messaging that speaks to the deepest level won't resonate with all of them — but it will resonate powerfully with the sub-avatar who lives there.

**Connection:** This is the research methodology from [[copy-research]] applied in reverse. Forum research (Principle 2 in copy-research) reveals these deeper desires. The avatar segmentation decision determines WHICH forums to mine and WHICH threads matter.

## Principle 3: Impact-Based Avatar Selection

Not all sub-avatars are equal. The selection criteria:

**1. Magnitude of impact.** How severe is the consequence of the problem for this avatar? The more devastating the impact of not solving, the stronger the desire, and the more willing they are to buy.

**The turning point principle:** Desire intensity isn't binary (has problem / doesn't have problem). It's a progressive scale. Someone with mild occasional back pain can live with it. Someone whose pain prevents them from sitting at work for more than 15 minutes is at a TURNING POINT — the moment the problem's impact on their life becomes unbearable. The turning-point avatar is already looking for solutions, already willing to pay. Target avatars AT the turning point, not before it. People below the turning point require far more persuasion because human beings don't think beyond a week of consequences — they say "I'll figure it out later" until it's too urgent to ignore.

**Loved-ones amplifier:** People are more willing to act for loved ones than for themselves. Mothers for children is the strongest version — willing to sacrifice anything. Pets as family members trigger similar response (see [[avatar-1]], [[avatar-2]]). When the problem affects a loved one (child's health, pet's aging, spouse's wellbeing), the desire intensity is amplified beyond what the same problem would produce for the person themselves.

Examples from the source:
- Single male (20-30) with hair loss → high impact (dating, self-image, social life is at stake)
- Married male (45+) with hair loss → lower impact (already has family, career; appearance matters less)
- Female with hair loss → highest impact (society judges bald women far more harshly than bald men; identity and femininity at stake)
- Mother with back pain → amplified impact (it's not about pain — it's about not being able to hold her kids, being a bad mom)

**2. Competition density.** Has this avatar already been targeted by major brands? If yes, you're back in the red ocean. If no, you have first-mover messaging advantage.

**3. Audience size.** Don't niche so far down that you have 1,000 people. Sub-avatars should still represent millions. "30+ males with hair loss" = huge. "31-year-old white married women with alopecia in a specific city" = too small.

**The sweet spot:** An avatar with high-magnitude desire, low competition, and sufficient audience size.

## Principle 4: Segmentation Enables Superior Communication

When you know the specific sub-avatar, every marketing asset improves:
- **Ads** speak to their specific desire, not the generic one
- **Landing pages** address their specific objections and situation
- **UGCs** feature people who look and sound like them
- **Offers** are framed around their specific outcome
- **Copy** uses their language, references their daily life, mirrors their internal monologue

This is the operational payoff of [[copy-research]]: the research becomes sharper because you're researching a specific person, not "anyone with the problem." Forum mining becomes targeted. Amazon review analysis focuses on what this avatar specifically complains about.

## Case Study: Beard Club

A brand selling derma rollers and beard growth products — a commodity product with no product superiority. Instead of selling to "everyone who wants a bigger beard," they identified a specific sub-avatar: **single young men who believe their patchy beard is preventing them from attracting women.**

How they executed:
- Ads feature women playing with beards, kissing bearded men, women as authority figures recommending the product
- Hook: "How to go from broke boy beard to daddy beard" — speaks directly to dating/attraction desire
- Visual proof: women reacting positively to beards
- The desire communicated isn't "grow a beard" — it's "get the girl"

Result: ~500 active ads, estimated ~$1M/month revenue, ~400K monthly traffic. All built on a commodity product (derma roller) that has massive competition when sold generically.

**What they didn't do:** Try to sell to every man who wants a beard. Some men want a beard for professional appearance. Some want it for personal aesthetics. Those are different sub-avatars with different desires. Beard Club picked one and went deep.

## Data-Driven Avatar Selection — The Vista PE Method (Hormozi)

When you already have customers, stop guessing which avatar is best. Use data. This is the method Vista Equity Partners uses to grow acquired companies, applied by Hormozi at Gym Launch (result: 70x more profit than a competitor serving the same market with the same number of total sales).

**Step 1: Survey existing customers.** Four categories of questions:
- Demographics: age, gender, location, marital status, business type
- Business/situation stats: revenue, tenure, pricing, customer count (before and current)
- Aspirations: what goal drove them to buy? What problem were they solving?
- Buying process: biggest single reason they bought, trigger events, content consumed before purchase, time from first contact to purchase, referral source

**Step 2: Score by value.** Sort customers by who stayed longest, spent the most, and caused the least problems. Focus on the top 20%. Ignore the bottom 80%.

**Step 3: Find the common qualifiers.** Read through answers and identify the fewest characteristics the top 20% share. Usually 3-5 qualifiers. This requires thinking, which is why most competitors skip it.

**Step 4a: Speak to the new avatar.** Make all advertising address these specific qualifiers. Spell out requirements in ads and pages. Talk only about THEIR problems and aspirations, not generic ones. This repels bad customers and attracts good ones.

**Step 4b: Reverse-engineer the buying process.** Analyze how the best customers bought — what content they consumed, what sequence they followed, what triggered the decision. Then recreate that exact journey for all new leads.

Hormozi's finding: 78% of top customers had consumed at least two pieces of long-form content before buying. His team then injected two high-value content pieces into every lead's journey, replicating the path that produced the best buyers.

**The math:** Same market, same number of sales. The competitor who accepted anyone had $6-8K LTV. Hormozi, who only sold to qualified top-20% avatars, had $45K+ LTV. Same effort, 6-8x the revenue per customer, and vastly higher margins because servicing was easier (better customers need less support, complain less, refer more).

**Key insight:** Every time Hormozi removed qualification steps to increase volume, lead count went up but revenue went down. More leads does not mean more money. Quality of customer is the dominant variable.
