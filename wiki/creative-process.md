# Creative Process — Ideation, Influence, and the Work Itself

Principles for how to generate ideas, study influences, and maintain creative output. Extracted from *Steal Like an Artist* (Austin Kleon) and filtered for what's operationally useful for a copywriter and creative strategist.

---

## Steal the Thinking, Not the Style

The most common way to misuse a reference ad is to copy what it looks like. The move that actually produces results is understanding why it works — then applying that logic in an entirely different execution.

"You don't want to look like your heroes, you want to see like your heroes."

This is the difference between structural analysis and surface mimicry. When you study the Luhxe ads, you aren't trying to write a story about luggage. You're trying to understand the emotional architecture: diminishment → proof → public vindication. That architecture transfers to dogs, to supplements, to relationships. The luggage is incidental.

When you study a reference for copy, your job is to reverse-engineer the logic:
1. What emotional state is the reader in before reading?
2. What belief does the ad need to install or confirm?
3. What moment or scene creates that belief shift?
4. How does the product enter the story — as proof, as instrument, as discovery?

Once you have the logic, the reference has given you everything it has. The surface (the characters, the setting, the product) is raw material. The logic is what you steal.

**If you steal from one reference, you sound derivative. Steal from ten, and no one can trace it.** The writer Wilson Mizner: "If you copy from one author, it's plagiarism, but if you copy from many, it's research." The implication for creative strategy: build a wide swipe file, not a deep one. A copywriter who has absorbed 50 emotional architectures has 50 logics to combine. A copywriter who has deeply studied one has one.

## Imitation → Emulation → Voice (The Gap Is the Point)

The path to finding your own voice runs through copying your models — and failing to copy them perfectly.

Kobe Bryant stole moves from everyone. But his body type meant he couldn't execute them the way his models did. He had to adapt. The adaptation became the move. Johnny Carson tried to be Jack Benny, fell short, and became Johnny Carson. Conan tried to be Letterman and became Conan.

"It is our failure to become our perceived ideal that ultimately defines us."

For copy work: when you try to replicate a winning emotional architecture and it doesn't work for TheraBelt, the gap tells you something. The adaptation required to make it work is the insight. Don't force the fit — examine what had to change and why. That's where your system's specific logic lives.

**Steps for intentional imitation:**
1. Pick the ad or structure you want to learn from
2. Try to replicate the logic exactly, in the TheraBelt context
3. Note specifically where it breaks — what can't transfer and why
4. The answer to "why it breaks" is the actual insight. Write that down.
5. Build the adaptation. That adapted version is stronger than either the original model or a direct copy.

## Analog Before Digital — Two-Desk System

The computer is optimized for finishing, not generating. The moment something goes onto a screen, it starts moving toward being done. The pressure to polish kills early ideas before they can breathe.

"Once the computer is involved, things are on an inevitable path to being finished. Whereas in my sketchbook the possibilities are endless."

**The practical version for copywriting:**
- Analog phase: fragments, word clusters, emotional beats, scene fragments — written by hand or on paper. No judgment. The goal is volume and connection, not coherence.
- Digital phase: select, sequence, refine. This is where the best material gets developed into actual copy.

Going hands → screen → hands → screen (not just screen throughout) keeps the material alive longer before it locks down. The key is staying analog until you've found the real idea. Too many writers go digital immediately and end up editing before they've generated.

For concept work specifically: write concepts on index cards or scraps. Move them around. Pair them with other fragments. The physical act of rearranging — not scrolling through a doc — creates connections that linear thinking misses.

## Constraints as Liberators

Unlimited possibilities produce paralysis. Tight constraints produce better work.

Dr. Seuss wrote *The Cat in the Hat* with 236 words. His editor bet him he couldn't do it in 50. He came back with *Green Eggs and Ham* — one of the bestselling children's books of all time.

"What we respond to in any work of art is the artist's struggle against his or her limitations."

For copy, constraints aren't obstacles — they're the forcing function. The copy-style rules (third-grade reading level, no em dashes, no dialogue tags, no rule-of-three) aren't restrictions on creativity. They're the constraint set that makes the writing better by removing the easy moves.

When you can't use em dashes, you find better sentence breaks. When you can't write "She said," you write better dialogue. When you can't write timestamped transformation, you find better scene architecture. The constraint forces a solution the unconstrained writer never would have found.

**More importantly: what you leave out defines the work.** The story that includes every detail is a weaker story than the one that made hard cuts. Every scene, line, and beat that isn't earning its place is diluting the scenes that are. The instinct to add ("I should include this detail about the vet visit") should be treated with suspicion. The instinct to cut is almost always right.

## Write What You Want to Read

The best move when stuck on creative direction: stop thinking about what the reader wants, and think about what you would genuinely want to read.

"The best advice is not to write what you know, it's to write what you like. Write the kind of story you like best — write the story you want to read."

This sounds like a craft platitude but it's actually a diagnostic tool. When you're forcing a story that doesn't feel interesting, it's usually because you've optimized for what you think you should write, not for what would actually grab you. A story the writer finds boring will be a story the reader finds boring.

For concept development: before finalizing a concept, ask — would you stop scrolling for this? Not "is this technically correct" or "does this hit all the framework checkboxes." Would you actually stop?

If the answer is no, the concept isn't done.

This is a filter, not a license. The story still has to do the work of the ad — projection, product placement, belief shift. But within those constraints, write the version you'd actually want to read. That's the one that converts.

## Climb the Tree — Building Influence Depth

The swipe file instruction "steal from many" is right but incomplete. Random breadth produces a shallow pool. The genealogy method builds structured depth.

Pick one copywriter, ad, or creative whose work generates the "steal this" instinct. Study everything they made. Then find the three influences they named or drew from — the people who shaped how they think. Study those. Find their influences. Repeat.

"If you try to devour the history of your discipline all at once, you'll choke. Instead, chew on one thinker you really love. Study everything there is to know about that thinker. Then find three people that thinker loved, and find out everything about them. Repeat this as many times as you can. Climb up the tree as far as you can go. Once you build your tree, it's time to start your own branch."

Applied to this system: instead of randomly adding swipe ads, trace the lineage. If a Luhxe ad works, find the format it drew from. If a direct-response letter works, find the copywriter's acknowledged teachers. The goal isn't more references — it's a connected network of principles that compounds.

**Steps:**
1. Pick one reference that generates a strong "steal this" response
2. Identify what it's clearly drawing from — predecessor ads, formats, structural moves
3. Go study those originals. What did they do first?
4. Identify what the original drew from, one level deeper
5. After 2-3 levels of climbing, you have a genealogy, not a swipe pile — a map of why things work

## Routine Over Inspiration

Output quality over time is less about talent and more about staying in the work. Inertia kills creativity. The creative process runs on schedule, not on feeling ready.

"Establishing and keeping a routine can be even more important than having a lot of time. Inertia is the death of creativity. You have to stay in the groove. When you get out of the groove, you start to dread the work, because you know it's going to suck for a while — it's going to suck until you get back into the flow."

The Seinfeld chain method: break work into daily chunks. Each day you complete the work, mark the box. Your only job is to not break the chain. Don't wait for a concept to feel ready. Don't wait for inspiration. Sit down, do the work, mark the box.

"Work gets done in the time available." Parkinson's Law runs both directions. A session will expand or contract to fill whatever time is allocated. The solution is a fixed daily commitment, not a flexible intention.

For concept and copy work: consistent daily output (even rough, even wrong) keeps the system sharp. A copywriter who writes every day stays in the groove. One who writes in bursts spends half of each session getting back into flow — wasting the most valuable creative resource.

## Connection to This System

These principles chain together in one operational sequence:

1. Build a wide swipe file of diverse emotional architectures — not just ads, whatever generates the "steal this" instinct
2. Trace the lineage of what works: climb the tree, find the originals, understand the genealogy
3. When you need a concept, steal the logic of the most promising reference — not the surface
4. Try to apply that logic to TheraBelt. Note where it breaks.
5. The gap between model and execution is the insight. Build from the gap.
6. Use analog tools to generate, digital to develop and refine
7. Apply copy constraints as the forcing function — they aren't restrictions, they're the frame
8. Before locking a concept: would you stop scrolling for this?
9. Work on a routine schedule regardless of whether inspiration is present

## Pre-Writing Ritual — The Advertorial Writing Process

A specific sequence for producing advertorial copy. The order matters because each step primes a different mode of thinking. Source: Ecom Masterclass.

### The Sequence

1. **Clear your mind.** Meditation (5-10 minutes) or journaling to dump unrelated thoughts. The brain can't access creative depth if it's noisy with stress, notifications, or unrelated tasks. No phone in the room. No distractions.

2. **Enter buyer mode.** Pull up all avatar research documents. Read through them — not to extract, just to absorb. Read emotional quotes out loud to make them hit. Let the data transform you into the avatar. You're not researching anymore; you're becoming the person you're writing for. Schwartz: "Great copy is not invented. It's assembled from deep understanding of the market."

3. **Define the one emotional mission.** Before writing a single line, get clear on the one shift the copy must create. From what feeling → to what feeling? What's the one core belief the reader should walk away with? Without a clear goal, the copy will meander. Example: from identity loss → to feeling feminine again.

4. **Sketch the skeleton.** Don't write — outline. Use a napkin, a whiteboard, or a voice memo. Map the flow: how does the story open, what's the emotional arc, where does the root cause get explained, where does the product enter? Keep it rough. The goal is the big picture, not polished sentences.

5. **Walk with the story.** Step away from the desk with only the sketch in your head. Walk, shower, do something physical. Let the idea breathe. Ask yourself: What will make them stop scrolling? Where will they start thinking "this isn't for me" — and how do I fix that? When ideas come, capture them on your phone. Come back and improve the sketch.

6. **Write fast, no editing.** Block time. Zero distractions. Expand the sketch into a full draft. No editing, no second-guessing, no polishing. Let the brain dump everything it's been building. The goal is volume and raw creativity, not perfection. Don't use AI to generate — this is where your internalized avatar understanding produces the actual copy.

7. **Step away completely.** Don't edit the same session you wrote. Walk, eat, sleep on it. The brain needs to switch from creator mode to editor mode. In creator mode, you're emotionally attached to what you produced. Everything feels good. You need distance to see it clearly.

8. **Edit like a killer.** Now switch to System Two. Cut anything that drags. Simplify every sentence. Check flow — are transitions smooth, or did the creative brain jump between ideas without connecting them? Add tension, emotional flavoring, knowledge gaps. Test every line: "Would I keep reading if I didn't write this?" If the answer is no, cut or fix it.

9. **Iterate.** The first edit is not the final version. Spend 3-4 days refining. Each pass, ask: Is this still hitting emotionally? Is this simple enough? Is there a knowledge gap pulling the reader forward? Does every line earn its place?

### Key Insight

The ritual is designed to separate generating from judging. Most bad copy comes from writers who edit while they generate — killing ideas before they develop. Or writers who never properly edit — shipping the first draft because they're emotionally attached to it. The sequence enforces the separation: steps 1-6 are pure generation, steps 7-9 are pure evaluation.
