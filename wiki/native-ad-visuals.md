## Core Thesis

A native ad visual has exactly one job: interrupt the scroll. It is not the selling mechanism — copy and the lander do that. The visual stops the thumb so the copy gets read. "The first job of any ad is to be seen. Everything else is secondary." (David Ogilvy)

The creative is 10-20% of the ad. Copy does the heavy lifting. But if the visual fails to stop the scroll, nobody reads the copy. If you are killer at copy, you can make any native ad work. If you are decent, the visual and copy have to amplify each other.

## Principles

### 1. Every visual must hit at least one of the 4 scroll-stopping triggers

There are exactly four mechanisms that interrupt a scroll. A native ad image must use at least one. They work because the brain cannot ignore unresolved pattern breaks.

Why it matters: Without a trigger, the image blends into the feed and gets scrolled past. The ad is never seen, so the copy never gets read.

### 2. One angle per image

Each image targets exactly one emotional angle. Trying to hit multiple angles in a single visual dilutes the pattern interruption and confuses the viewer.

Why it matters: People have one second to look at your ad and decide if they scroll past. A single clear angle makes that decision instant. Multiple angles create noise.

### 3. Visual-copy-lander congruence

The image, the ad copy, and the lander must tell the same story from the same angle. A walking-stick image with gardening copy and a sleep lander kills the ad.

Why it matters: Mismatch between creative, copy, and lander breaks trust and confuses the reader. They clicked because the visual+copy spoke to their problem — the lander must continue that thread. See also [[copy-structure]] on hook-lead congruence.

### 4. Specificity from research beats generic concepts

Image concepts should include specific details drawn from actual customer research — real brands they use, real objects in their environment, real situations they describe.

Why it matters: Specificity creates recognition. A generic "person in pain" gets scrolled past. A specific "three different pharmacy boxes lined up on a bathroom shelf" makes someone think "that's my shelf."

## Frameworks

### The 4 Rules — Scroll-Stop Evaluation

Derived from: Principle 1

For every image concept, check which rule(s) it hits. If it hits zero, rework it.

**Rule 1: Controversial**
The image creates a slight feeling of discomfort or disagreement. Something that makes the brain pause because it doesn't match expectations.

Step 1: Look at the image concept. Does it contain something the viewer would disagree with, feel uncomfortable about, or find "wrong"?
Step 2: Check that the discomfort is SLIGHT — enough to pause, not enough to repulse or offend.
Step 3: Verify the controversy is relevant to the angle (not random shock value).

Example: A before photo that looks better than the after.

**Rule 2: Colour**
One dominant colour that is completely out of place in the feed. The feed is grey and beige — a single bright block stops the scroll.

Step 1: Identify the dominant colour in your image concept.
Step 2: Ask: does this colour exist in a typical Meta feed? Grey, beige, muted tones blend in. Bright, saturated colours stand out.
Step 3: If the image is muted, add one dominant bright element (clothing item, object, background).
Step 4: Yellow is the strongest option on Meta — it's the anti-colour of blue. Use yellow jackets, yellow t-shirts, yellow cups, yellow tables.

Example: Electric yellow on a white background when every other ad is dark.

**Rule 3: Weird Object in the Background**
Something that should not be there creates involuntary curiosity. The brain cannot scroll past an unresolved visual question.

Step 1: Look at the scene in your image concept. What objects belong there?
Step 2: Add one object that does NOT belong — something that makes the viewer think "wait, what?"
Step 3: The object should be noticeable but not the main focus. It sits in the background or periphery.

Examples: A product shot with a rubber duck on the shelf behind it. Piles of clothing with stains. A coffee mug that fell on the carpet.

**Rule 4: Recognisable**
A face, a logo, a format they have seen before — pattern recognition stops the thumb. Familiarity creates safety. Safety creates the click.

Step 1: Can you use a familiar FORMAT for the image? (screenshot, meme structure, news article layout, text message, social media post)
Step 2: If not format, can you include a recognisable OBJECT that the target audience encounters daily?
Step 3: The recognition should feel organic, not forced or staged.

Example: A screenshot format. A meme structure. A news article layout.

### Image Concept Development Process

Derived from: Principles 2, 3, 4

Step 1: Start with one emotional angle (from your angle research).
Step 2: Write the image concept in one clear sentence. If you can't, the concept isn't clear enough.
Step 3: Check which of the 4 Rules the concept hits. If zero, rework.
Step 4: Add specific details from customer research (real brands, real objects, real environments).
Step 5: Create 3 variations of the same angle — same emotional core, different visual execution.
Step 6: For each variation, verify congruence: does this image match the ad copy angle and lander angle?

When to use: Every time you create image concepts from angles.

## Proven Examples

### "Three different pharmacy medication boxes lined up on a bathroom shelf, all different. Half-shot from eye level with a slightly shaky hand."
Source: Camperman video — arthritis angle | Result: Used as example of specificity + camera-roll authenticity

What it shows: Principle 4 (specificity from research) + Rule 4 (recognisable — everyone has a medicine shelf). The shaky hand adds realism. The three different boxes tell a story of trial and failure without words.

Pattern: [Specific objects from target's daily life] + [authentic camera angle] + [implicit narrative through arrangement]

### "Dusty trainers next to a walking stick"
Source: Camperman video — arthritis/mobility angle | Result: Used as example of implied story

What it shows: Rule 1 (controversial — trainers imply someone who was active, walking stick implies they're not anymore). Two objects that shouldn't belong together create an emotional gap the viewer fills in themselves.

Pattern: [Object from 'before' life] placed next to [object from 'after' life] — the contrast tells the story.

### 5. Pain visuals must carry hope

Never show pain without a signal of hope somewhere in the ad. Pain alone pushes people away — nobody wants to read more about suffering with no promise of resolution. Even if the visual and headline are entirely pain-focused, the subconscious must be triggered with hope through a subheadline, CTA text, or secondary visual element.

Why it matters: Pain creates recognition ("that's me"). Hope creates the click ("maybe there's a way out"). Pain without hope creates depression and scroll-away. The combination — recognition + possibility — is what drives the click.

How to apply: If your image shows clumps of hair on a brush (pain), the headline can acknowledge the pain but the subheadline or CTA must whisper hope: "Here's what I did to stop it" or "Learn how 10,000 women reversed this." The hope doesn't need to be the dominant element — just present enough that the viewer feels clicking will lead somewhere better.

### 6. Match the awareness level

The image ad's job changes based on the audience's awareness level. Product-feature ads (easy to swallow, FDA approved, non-GMO, 30% off) fail when shown to problem-aware or solution-aware audiences because those viewers don't know or care about the product yet.

**Problem-aware audiences:** Show their pain, their daily experience, the moments they recognize. No product. No features. The ad's job is to make them feel seen and curious enough to click. The landing page does the education.

**Solution-aware audiences:** They know solutions exist and may have tried alternatives. The ad can reference the solution category but should focus on emotional outcomes, not product details. "Still using minoxidil?" works. "FDA-approved capsules" doesn't.

**Product-aware audiences:** Now you can show product features, offers, discounts, social proof. They already know what the product category is — they're comparing options.

The mistake: Running product-aware creative to a problem-aware audience. Features and offers bounce off people who don't yet understand why they need your category of product. See [[rmbc-ii-funnel-structure]] for the full awareness-level framework.

## Nuances and Exceptions

### Don't use celebrities or copyrighted elements
Situation: Temptation to use recognisable famous faces or branded content for Rule 4.
Why the rule doesn't apply: Copyright infringement risk. Not worth it.
What to do instead: Use recognisable FORMATS (screenshot, meme, news layout) or recognisable everyday objects — not famous people.

### The 10-20% rule — when visuals matter MORE
Situation: The source says creatives are 10-20% of the ad, copy does the heavy lifting.
When this shifts: If your copy skills are average (not "killer"), the visual and copy must amplify each other. The weaker your copy, the more the visual has to carry. For strong copywriters, almost any decent image works. For everyone else, visual-copy synergy is critical.

## Image Research Methodology

### 7. Source images from top-performing organic content

The best native ad images come from content that already performs organically. Organic reach is harder to earn than paid reach — you can force impressions with money, but organic posts must earn attention through natural magnetism. A post that gets hundreds of likes, comments, and shares in a Facebook group or subreddit has proven it resonates with that audience. That same visual format will likely perform well as paid creative.

Why it matters: An organic post with 1,000+ likes and 700 comments in a niche group has been validated by the exact audience you're targeting. Using that visual format (not the image itself — recreate it) in your ad means you're running content Facebook's algorithm already knows people engage with. This directly lowers CPMs because Facebook rewards engaging content in its auction. See [[rmbc-ii-funnel-structure]] for awareness-level targeting.

### Image Sourcing Process

Derived from: Principle 7

**Method 1: Facebook Groups**

Step 1: Find public Facebook groups in your niche. Search your problem keyword (e.g., "grief and loss of a pet," "bloating," "knee pain") and filter by Groups.
Step 2: Look for large, active communities (3k+ members with regular posting).
Step 3: Sort or scroll through posts and identify top performers by interaction count (likes + comments + shares).
Step 4: Study the image format, not just the image. What TYPE of visual is it? Selfie with pet? Before/after? Text post? Meme? That format is what you recreate.
Step 5: Save reference images. Recreate them in Higgsfield, Gemini, or ChatGPT (never use the original — copyright).

**Method 2: Reddit**

Step 1: Find the subreddit for your market's pain point or interest.
Step 2: Filter by "Top posts" (all time, or past year).
Step 3: Study what formats get upvotes. Some niches are image-heavy (pets, fitness). Others are text-heavy (IBS, health conditions). If text posts dominate, that's a signal — text-based ad images may outperform photos.
Step 4: Look for story hooks in post titles. "A letter to the woman currently in the TJ Maxx bathroom with me" is both a native image concept (text format) and a hook.

**Method 3: Google Blog Search (2009-2012)**

Step 1: In Google Images, search your keyword + "blog."
Step 2: Set the date range to 2009-2012.
Step 3: Results return pre-smartphone-era photos that nail the native, impromptu, authentic look — before everything became overproduced and filtered.

**Method 4: Viral Organic Pages**

Step 1: Find Facebook pages with large followings that earn attention purely through content (no ads). Examples: Spirit Science, Ranker Graveyard Shift, niche-specific pages.
Step 2: Study their image formats. These pages survive by grabbing attention organically — they are masters at click-worthy visuals.
Step 3: Adapt their formats for your niche. The format is the asset, not the specific image.

**Method 5: Competitor Ad Libraries**

Step 1: Search Meta Ad Library for larger brands in or adjacent to your niche (e.g., Clarifion).
Step 2: Identify which image formats they run consistently — consistency signals those formats are working.
Step 3: Recreate with your own angle and visual execution. See also `site:facebook.com + [problem keyword]` in Google to surface relevant Facebook images.

### 8. FB auction mechanics reward native content

Facebook is a pay-per-impression platform. CPM (cost per thousand impressions) is not fixed — it's determined by Facebook's estimated total value of your ad, which factors in: estimated click-through rate, estimated click-to-conversion rate, and user engagement signals (likes, comments, shares, x-outs).

When your ad image is content people naturally engage with, Facebook estimates higher CTR and engagement → assigns higher total value → charges you less per impression. This is why native images directly reduce CPMs and CPCs.

The inverse is also true: stock-photo-looking images, AI slop, crying women with pills — Facebook recognizes these get low engagement, assigns low total value, and charges you MORE to show them.

Why it matters: The image is the #1 lever for CPM and CPC. Copy drives conversions, but the image determines how much you pay for each eyeball. High CPMs ($60+) and CPCs ($2-3+) in desire testing usually signal an image problem, not a copy problem. Fix the image first.

## AI Image Generation

### 9. Creative brief method produces more accurate AI images

Instead of prompting an AI model directly with your image concept, use a two-step process: have the AI analyze a reference image, create a detailed creative brief, then generate from the brief. The AI instructs itself detail by detail, producing more precise and less AI-looking outputs.

Why it matters: Direct prompting often produces skewed details, wrong compositions, or obviously AI-generated outputs. The creative brief method forces the model to plan the image systematically before generating, resulting in outputs that match the reference style more closely.

### Creative Brief Workflow (Gemini / Any Model)

Derived from: Principle 9

Step 1: Find a reference image from Google, Facebook, or your research (Method 1-5 above).
Step 2: Open a new chat. Set constraints: "You are my expert graphic designer and image generator. We'll be making all images 1:1 aspect ratio and keep image resolution 720p." (720p prevents the overly clean AI/CGI look.)
Step 3: Upload the reference image and ask: "Can you identify the type of photography used in this image?"
Step 4: After the analysis, ask: "Now make a creative brief of this image but with [your changes]." Specify avatar changes, scenery, clothing, facial expressions, objects to add or remove.
Step 5: Review the brief. If you need changes, update the brief BEFORE generating: "Change the avatar to male and update the creative brief before we proceed."
Step 6: Say "Run the image."
Step 7: Iterate on outputs by updating the brief, not by re-prompting from scratch.

Important: When Gemini starts degrading in quality, open a new chat and restart the process. This resets the model's context.

Why the brief works: By asking the LLM to write the creative brief first, you're making it instruct itself detail by detail. Without briefing, details get skewed or lost. This works across all models (Gemini, ChatGPT, Sora, Higgsfield).

## Proven MSL Image Formats

### Format 1: Article-Style (Text Overlay on Native Image)

The top-performing format across multiple GTE practitioners. Image + headline text overlay that looks like a clickable article or news post.

Step 1: Find a native image using the research methods above.
Step 2: Recreate it in Higgsfield/Gemini at 1:1 aspect ratio.
Step 3: Write text overlays using Claude. Format: "Most [audience] don't realize..." or "Most [audience] don't know..." — keep clickbaity and vague to encourage the click to your advertorial.
Step 4: In Canva, upload image onto 1:1 canvas. Crop to leave white space on the top half. Add headline text.
Step 5: Create 4-5 variations (different images, same format).

Result: One practitioner reports this format cut CPC in half AND took majority of spend at target CPA. $8k/day from a single image in this format. See [[rmbc-ii-advertorials-and-pdps]] for advertorial structure.

### Format 2: Snapchat-Style (AI Person + Bold Text)

Step 1: Take a face image matching your advertorial's "author" persona.
Step 2: Generate a scene with Sora — the person in an authority position (solved their problem, enjoying life) OR in a pain state.
Step 3: Add compelling text overlay. Proven performers:
- "The real reason sleep doctors push CPAP machines..."
- "My doctor said I'd die without CPAP. He was wrong..."
- "Been hiding this from my apnea doctor for 2 months now"
Step 4: The text creates the hook; the image creates the native feel.

### Format 3: Text-Based (White Text on Black Background)

Step 1: Write 4 short paragraphs in first person. Structure: issue → cause → solution (your product).
Step 2: Color-code: issue in yellow, cause in red, solution in green.
Step 3: Keep it raw and personal-sounding. Must read as an authentic person sharing their experience.

### Format 4: Review-Based (5-Star Review + Product)

Step 1: Write a 5-star review describing a personal experience with a problem and how the product solved it.
Step 2: The review MUST sound authentic and human — not polished or corporate.
Step 3: Show the product below the review text.

### Format 5: Before/After

Step 1: Open with an introductory question or statement identifying the avatar's main concern.
Step 2: Follow with a reassuring "Here's the solution" statement.
Step 3: Show before/after images with the product either being actively used in the "after" portion or displayed below.

### Text Overlay Targeting Hypothesis

Early data (unconfirmed, needs corroboration): Adding text to images that includes target audience identifiers may help Facebook's algorithm learn who to show the ad to faster. One practitioner reports CPM and CPA cut in half by doing this. Rationale: Facebook uses the image itself (not just copy) to determine ad targeting.
