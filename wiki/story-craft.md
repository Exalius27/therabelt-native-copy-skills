# Story Craft — What Makes a Story Work

> Sources: Internal creative testing and iteration
> Related: [[story-native-format]], [[vicious-hooks]], [[copy-structure]], [[direct-response-copywriting]], [[avatar-1]], [[avatar-2]], [[avatar-3]], [[creative-process]], [[sugarman-triggers]], [[specificity-in-copy]]
> Last updated: 2026-04-07

This file covers the storytelling principles that make [[story-native-format]] ads feel like real stories people want to read, share, and engage with. These principles are about the story itself — not the ad structure, not the product placement, not the CTA. The story.

A story-native ad should generate engagement on its own. If you removed the product link entirely, people should still be reading, commenting, sharing. They should not realize it's an ad. That's the bar.

---

## Concept Is Everything

The concept of the story is the single most important factor. If the concept isn't compelling, no amount of good writing saves it. Nobody is going to read a boring situation no matter how well it's written.

A concept is the situation itself — what happened, who it happened to, and why someone scrolling would stop and want to know more. It has to create a question the reader needs answered or a situation they need to see play out.

The concept is what stops the doom scroll. Before a single word of the story is written, the concept has to be strong enough that someone would stop scrolling to read it.

A weak concept: "I love my dog and she's getting older."
That's a mood. Nobody stops scrolling for a mood.

A strong concept is a situation that demands attention. Something happened, something is happening, and the reader has to know how it plays out.

When building creative strategy, the concept comes first. If the concept doesn't pull, nothing else matters — don't move forward until it does.

---

## You Are With the Character the Whole Time

The reader is never watching the story from outside. They are inside the narrator's head, living it moment to moment. This is the most important craft principle.

When it's done right, the reader forgets they're reading. They're in the apartment at 2am. They're in the parking lot. They're on the walk. They feel what the narrator feels because they're experiencing it in real time with them, not being told about it afterward.

This means the narrator doesn't summarize what happened. They walk the reader through it as it unfolds. The reader sees what the narrator sees, thinks what the narrator thinks, feels what the narrator feels — at the same pace, in the same order.

The whole story is this. From the first line to the last line, the reader is with the character. Not just the opening, not just the emotional parts — the entire thing. Even when the product enters the story, the reader is still with the character. The story never stops being a story.

---

## The Reader Thinks About Her Own Dog

Being with the character is necessary but not sufficient. The reader must also think about HER dog, HER life, HER situation — not just feel for the character's.

Empathy without projection is engagement without conversion. A reader who cries for Owen's dog and then scrolls is not a buyer. A reader who thinks about her own dog the entire time — comparing, mapping, wondering — is.

Three structural mechanisms create projection:

**Comparison triggers.** When the narrator compares one dog to another — specific ages, breeds, behaviors — the reader compares to hers. A "10-year-old golden" makes the reader with a 9-year-old lab think about her own dog immediately.

**Universal specific moments.** Moments the target [[avatar-2]] has her own version of. Not generic emotions — specific moments she recognizes from her own life. What those moments are depends on who you're writing for. The moment must be specific enough to feel real, universal enough to the target audience that the reader has lived it.

**Real-time discovery.** The narrator discovers alongside the reader, not retelling a finished story. She doesn't know the answer yet. The reader goes through the realization at the same pace. When the narrator is ahead of the reader (already bought, already seen results), the reader is passive audience to a completed story. When the narrator discovers in real time, the reader discovers with her.

This is structural, not second person. Never address the reader directly. The projection comes from the story's architecture — what the narrator compares to, what she doesn't know yet, what details trigger the reader's own memories.

## The Reader Wants to Be That Owner

The story must give the reader an identity she wants to claim.

Every dog owner who loves her dog already believes "I'm the owner who does the best for my dog." That's already her identity. She doesn't always live up to it, but that's who she believes she is.

The product doesn't give her a new identity. It gives her dog the proof that she was right about herself all along. The dog looking incredible at its age is evidence that she IS the owner she always believed she was.

The story shows someone whose dog is walking, visible proof of who that owner is. The reader watches and wants to be that. Not because the story tells her to — because she sees what that type of owner looks like, and she already believes she's that person.

This must come through the story naturally. Never stated. Never labeled. The reader sees the identity and claims it herself.

## Scenes You Can See

The story is built from specific moments the reader can picture. Not summaries, not generalizations — moments with time, place, and sensory detail.

A summary: "After a few weeks, I started noticing changes."
A scene: the specific morning she walked into the kitchen and the dog was already standing at the door, tail going, something it hadn't done in over a year.

A summary: "The vet was impressed."
A scene: the vet going quiet, looking at the numbers, looking at the dog, looking back at the numbers. "How old did you say she is?"

Scenes are what make a story feel real. They're proof that this happened to a real person, because only someone who lived it would remember those details. The specific time of day. The exact thing someone said. The small physical detail nobody would think to make up.

Every story needs at least two or three scenes the reader can fully see and feel. These are the moments that stay with people after they stop reading.

## The Narrator Thinks Out Loud

The reader doesn't just see what happens — they hear the narrator's thoughts in real time. Doubt, skepticism, confusion, hope, fear. Not cleaned up, not reflected on with wisdom after the fact. Raw, in the moment.

"I kept thinking, what am I even doing?"
"I almost didn't go back."
"Part of me thought she was full of it."

This is what makes the reader feel like they're inside the narrator's head instead of being told a story by someone who already knows how it ends. The narrator is processing as they go, and the reader processes with them.

When the narrator shares doubt or skepticism, it also builds trust. A narrator who questions things, who doesn't immediately believe, who resists — that's a real person. A narrator who accepts everything and never doubts sounds like a script.

---

## The Whole Thing Is a Story

There is no point where the story stops and the ad begins. From the first word to the last word, it's a story. The product exists inside the story, characters talk about it inside the story, results happen inside the story — but the story never breaks. The reader never gets pulled out.

If the reader at any point feels the shift from "I'm reading a story" to "I'm being sold something," the spell is broken. That means the product, the proof, the urgency, the close — all of it has to live inside the narrative. Characters say it, scenes show it, the narrator experiences it. Nothing exists outside the story.

The measure of success: if people are engaging with it — commenting, sharing, reacting — as if it were a real post from a real person, and they don't realize it's an ad, that's the standard. That's what we're building toward.

---

## Authenticity Over Craft

The writing doesn't need to be expert level. It needs to feel like a real person telling you what happened. Simple language. Short sentences. Fragments. The way someone actually talks.

Not overdramatic. Not literary. Not polished to the point where it feels performed. The best stories feel like someone sat down and typed it while they were still feeling it. That raw, unfinished energy is what makes it feel true.

We will write better than that baseline — that's a bonus. But the priority is always authenticity first. A technically perfect story that feels written will always lose to a messy story that feels real.

Punctuation can be loose. Caps can be used for emphasis. Sentences can be incomplete. Dialogue should sound like how people actually talk, not how writers think people talk. The writing should never call attention to itself. The reader should be so deep in the story that they don't notice the writing at all.

**No stage directions between dialogue.** Real people telling stories on Reddit don't write "She paused." "She looked at me." "Dana kind of smiled." They just put the dialogue and tell you what happened. The reader infers the pauses and expressions. Stage directions between dialogue lines are a writer's habit, not a storyteller's. Cut them. Let the dialogue carry itself.

---

## Engagement Is Necessary, Not Sufficient

Engagement matters. Are people reading the whole thing? Are they commenting? Are they sharing it? Are they tagging friends? Are they telling their own stories in the comments? If the story isn't generating that, the concept isn't strong enough or the story doesn't feel real enough.

But engagement without conversion means the story is a movie, not a mirror. A beautiful story people love to read and share but that generates zero sales is a story that creates empathy without projection. The reader felt for the character's dog but never thought about her own.

Engagement is proof the story works as a story. Conversion is proof it works as an ad. Both must be present.

When both are working, the engagement itself becomes social proof. The comments become part of the sales mechanism. Real people reacting to a real-feeling story is more convincing than any testimonial or review. But engagement alone is not the goal — it's the foundation that conversion is built on.

If a story generates engagement but no sales, the first question is: did the reader think about her own dog, or did she just feel for the character's? Fix the projection first.
