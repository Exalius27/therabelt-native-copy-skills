## The 5-Step Creative Strategy Framework

A systematic process for creating winning ads. Each step feeds the next. Skip a step and the system breaks.

### 1. Research

The brand that understands their customers the most wins.

**Methods:**
- **Amazon review scraping** (tools like Shoelix): Breaks down competitor product reviews into pros, cons, use cases. Competitor cons become your ad's selling points.
- **Customer reviews and ad comments:** What are YOUR customers saying? What do they love? What do they complain about? This is overlooked constantly.
- **TikTok/YouTube content:** Watch what your target audience watches. Read the comments. Comments reveal desires and pain points that reviews miss.
- **Ad spy tools** (Atria, Foreplay): Search competitor brands, sort by longest running. Longest-running ads = proven winners. Take inspiration from the marketing logic, not the creative execution. Direct copying has never worked.

**The rule:** If you can't scale right now, it's because you don't understand your customer well enough.

### 2. Planning

Before launching any ad, document:
- **Market awareness stage** you're targeting (unaware → most aware)
- **Desire** you're channeling
- **Persona** you're speaking to

Track these in a growth guide/sheet for every ad concept. This is what makes post-test learning possible. Without it, you can't explain WHY an ad worked or failed — only that it did.

**Why metrics alone don't work:** CTR, CPM, hook rate are easily manipulated. High CTR with zero conversions = bad ad. High CPM with strong ROAS = good ad. Break down ads by marketing variables (awareness stage, desire), not platform metrics.

### 3. Execution

Make it look good without being a designer.
- **Static ads:** Use template libraries (Creative OS, Creative Donut) — plug in your headline and image
- **Video ads:** Use ad spy tools for structural inspiration, AI voiceovers for production

### 4. Learnings

After every test, document:
- Winning or losing?
- WHY? (Not "bad hook" — what specifically about the hook, the awareness level, the desire?)
- What to replicate or avoid next time?

This feeds directly into the [[creative-testing]] feedback loop system. Most 8-figure brands cannot answer "why did your best ad this month win?" That's why they plateau.

### 5. Iterations

Never reinvent the wheel when you have a winner. Build from it.

## Iteration Methodology

### The Testing Sequence

For any ad concept, test three variations. Change ONE variable at a time:

**Test 1: Image/Hook (highest impact)**
- Same headline, same copy
- Three different images (statics) or three different hooks (video)
- Find which visual/hook wins

**Test 2: Headlines**
- Winning image/hook from Test 1
- Three different headlines targeting different desires
- Find which headline wins

**Test 3: Benefits/Body**
- Winning image + winning headline
- Three different benefit sets or body copy approaches
- Find which messaging wins

**Test 4: Persona (later stage)**
- Everything else locked in
- Change the person (different age, ethnicity, gender)
- Hits different audience pockets

### From Iteration to Breakthrough: Live Example

A humidifier brand's progression from one insight to a $10K/day winner:

**Batch 1: Big swing — superlative statics.** Tested 6 different claims ("world's cleanest," "first doctor-designed," "world's first stainless steel"). The one nobody expected to win — stainless steel — took majority spend at 1.9x ROAS.

Most people would have killed it at 1.9x. Instead: dig deeper.

**Batch 2: Why stainless steel matters.** Iterated on the stainless steel angle: "Your family deserves better than moldy mist," "Hate constant cleaning? You'll love this," "99.99% sterilizable." Became a new top spender at scale.

**Batch 3: Deep research → video.** Went to Reddit, searched "stainless steel humidifier." Found threads of people who didn't even know non-plastic humidifiers existed. Built an AI-voiceover video with a skeptical, relatable tone: "Someone finally made one out of stainless steel... At first I was like, okay, but why though?"

Result: Day 1 = $64. Day 2 = $100. Day 3 = $5,000. Doubled account spend in two weeks.

**The chain:** Research reveals what people care about → First test identifies which angle gets spend → Research explains WHY that angle gets spend → Next iteration goes deeper into that "why" → Breakthrough.

## 19-Point Ad Failure Checklist

Before launching or when reviewing a failed ad, check each:

1. **Editing** — Too disruptive? Doesn't match audience expectations?
2. **Brand alignment** — Does the visual match the brand positioning?
3. **Length** — Too long, too short, too much filler?
4. **Product focus** — Is the product actually featured? Or random content?
5. **Benefits** — Relevant benefits or irrelevant ones?
6. **Text length** — Overwhelming amount of text on image/video?
7. **Visual quality** — Would this actually stop a scroll?
8. **Headline** — Does it intrigue the right person?
9. **Belief** — Does the ad create belief that the claim is real?
10. **Avatar resonance** — Does it speak to a specific person's identity?
11. **Desire** — Is a real desire being addressed?
12. **Market awareness mismatch** — Going after unaware when product-aware would scale better? Or vice versa?
13. **Objection handling** — Does it feel skeptical/unbelievable without addressing that?
14. **Mechanism clarity** — Is the product's mechanism explained or just assumed?
15. **Importance** — Are you communicating why this matters to the user?
16. **Value justification** — Does the ad justify the price point?
17. **Complexity** — Is the messaging confusing or jargon-heavy?
18. **Visual fatigue** — Same visuals people have seen before?
19. **Messaging differentiation** — Same messaging competitors use?

## 14 Questions for Video Ad Review

1. Does the first 1-2 seconds hook the right user AND keep them hooked?
2. Is this a video my potential customer would want to see on their feed?
3. Does the copywriting create a clear visual in the user's mind?
4. Does this video feel native to the platform (not TV/Netflix quality)?
5. Does the ad contain a label the viewer identifies with? (Identity, not demographics)
6. Is this video relatable?
7. Would this video make someone smile or laugh?
8. Does it educate the user in a creative way on how to achieve their core desire?
9. Does this video actually look like an ad? (Best ads are camouflaged)
10. What learnings from previous winners have been applied?
11. Does the ad educate creatively on achieving the core desire?
12. Does this video tell a story the ideal customer would find interesting?
13. Have you applied escalation to exaggerate claims in a clear, relatable way?
14. Does this ad separate your brand from the competition?

### Escalation Principle

Take your product's claim and exaggerate it to the highest extent in a playful, relatable way. "Our humidifier is easy to clean" → "It's like Mr. Clean invented a humidifier." The exaggeration makes the benefit memorable and shareable. Harmon Brothers built their entire ad model on this.

## Key Metrics for Ad Review

| Metric | What It Tells You | Benchmark |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Hook rate** | % who watch past 3 seconds | 25% = weak, 45%+ = strong |
| **Hold rate** | % who keep watching after hook | 5% = weak, 14%+ = strong |
| **Average watch time** | How far people get | 4 sec = dead on hook, 11+ sec = engaged |
| **CTR** | Click-through rate | 1.15% = low, 4%+ = strong |
| **CPC** | Cost per click | Context-dependent, compare to winners |
| **Frequency** | How often same person sees ad | 1.2 = prospecting, 3.8 = retargeting |

**If hook rate is low:** The ad's problem is in the first 3 seconds. Don't bother analyzing the rest — nobody saw it. Spend all review time on the hook.

**If hook rate is fine but hold rate is low:** Hook works but the body loses them. Review the transition from hook to body.

**If both are fine but no conversions:** The ad is entertaining but not persuading. Check desire alignment, mechanism clarity, and CTA strength.

## Video Ad Scriptwriting Process

Writing a video ad script is fundamentally an exercise in walking the viewer down the awareness stages — from wherever you enter (unaware, problem-aware, etc.) to most-aware (CTA/offer). Each section of the script corresponds to one awareness level transition.

### The 5-Step Scriptwriting Sequence

**1. Draft — Expand Awareness Stages**

Write hooks for every awareness stage first, even if you only plan to use one entry point. This reveals the full landscape of what's possible:
- Unaware hook: "Why do 90% of X fail?" (curiosity/education)
- Problem-aware hook: "[Problem] is the #1 reason [bad outcome]" (dig into pain)
- Solution-aware hook: "If you're tired of [problem], here's what's working" (introduce category)
- Product-aware hook: "How do you know if [solution] is doing a good job?" (comparison)
- Most-aware hook: CTA/offer

Then stitch these sections together as a natural flow from your chosen entry point down to CTA. Each section is really just expanding upon the awareness stage it represents.

**2. Reinforce — Deepen the Desire**

Go back through and make each section hit harder:
- Replace neutral language with emotional language ("unstable revenue" → "you could risk everything with one wrong move")
- Add time pressure and consequence framing
- Paint heaven and hell — describe the desired outcome so it feels like paradise, describe the problem so it feels hellish
- Add your new mechanism to the solution section if applicable

**3. Refine — Make It Human**

Read the script out loud. If it sounds robotic or unnatural, rewrite it.
- Remove words that don't add selling power (pure fluff)
- Use language your persona actually uses. If your avatar says "get snatched" instead of "lose weight," use their words.
- Every line should sound like something a real person would say.

**4. Visualize — Pair Script with Clips**

For each script section, determine what to SHOW:
- Exaggerate the desire — show outcomes at their best, problems at their worst
- Use the persona's native content style (if they watch TikTok UGC, make UGC — not Netflix ads)
- Attention-grabbing visuals for the hook (the "Mark Zuckerberg stealing your money" principle — make people stop)
- Demonstration clips for the product section

**5. Reflect — Execution Quality Check**

Before launching, ask:
1. Does the hook call out who I want AND showcase the benefit/problem?
2. Can my target persona actually read/see the text? (Font size matters for 65+ audiences)
3. Does this look like something my persona sees natively on their platform?
4. Is the language natural and relatable for this specific avatar?
