## Part 1: Hook Techniques

### The Nightmare Compare

Open with two things that shouldn't be true at the same time. The cognitive dissonance is the hook.

**ProDentim:** "This is a pair of 2,500 year old teeth from one of our ancestors... Not even a small cavity or crack, even though toothpaste, brushes or dentists were inexistent. Now these are the teeth of a 45-year-old patient who brushes every day." Two images. One comparison. Instant "wait, what?"

**Why it works:** The prospect can't resolve the contradiction without watching more. It also pre-frames the mechanism (modern dental care is the problem).

### The Physical Demonstration

Show something visual before making any claims. Proof-first structure.

**Dr Marty:** Opens with a yeast + sugar experiment. Balloons inflate proportional to sugar content. "Do you see how excessive carbs can lead to gut imbalances in dogs?" The prospect has already SEEN the proof before any selling starts.

**Why it works:** Demonstration bypasses skepticism entirely. You're not asking them to believe — you're asking them to watch.

### The Side-View Mirror

A specific physical moment of self-recognition that every viewer has experienced.

**Steve Harvey:** "I was in the mirror one day and I turned to the side, and I thought somebody had come into the bathroom. It was actually me." Then: "I didn't even know I looked like that."

**Why it works:** Universal experience (the unexpected photo/mirror angle), delivered by someone who shouldn't have this problem (a celebrity). If Steve Harvey can feel this way, the viewer's shame is validated.

### The Near-Death / Can't Save Family

Maximum emotional stakes immediately. Life-or-death framing within the first 60 seconds.

**Meticore:** Son falls and screams for help. Narrator runs upstairs, body can't keep up. Collapses. Wakes up in hospital with wife crying and son frightened. "Your heart cannot cope anymore."

**Why it works:** Bypasses all rational resistance. The viewer is in fight-or-flight empathy mode before the pitch even starts. Only works when the narrator earns it through specific, concrete detail.

### The Open Loop Stack

Pile multiple curiosity gaps before beginning the actual story. Each loop promises a different type of revelation.

**Meticore stacks 7 open loops in the first 3 minutes:**
1. Painful story reveals (emotional)
2. Breakthrough morning trigger (the mechanism)
3. Nothing to do with diet/exercise (contrarian)
4. Found on exotic island (origin story)
5. Still eating all favorite foods (unbelievable)
6. It's not your fault — I'll tell you why (absolution)
7. The simplicity will surprise you (ease)

**Why it works:** Each loop appeals to a different motivation. Even if one doesn't hook the viewer, another will. Creates a sense of massive value ahead.

### The Educational Open

Skip all story. Open with the question the viewer is already asking, then immediately reframe it.

**Miami MD 9min:** "What causes dark spots and how to fix them? Well, the first secret is that these unflattering blotches aren't caused by the sun, your liver, or even your age." Zero preamble. Mechanism IS the hook.

**Gundry MD:** "What does gluten do to the body?" Then immediately pivots: "33% buy gluten-free products but only 1% have celiac. The other 32% may feel better because they are actually avoiding a different plant protein."

**Why it works:** Matches the viewer's search intent directly. Most effective for viewers who already know they have the problem and are actively looking for solutions. Works best in shorter VSLs (under 20 min).

## Part 2: Credibility Choreography

These moves appear in a consistent order across all VSLs. Not every VSL uses all of them, but the order is stable.

### Move 1: The Credential Stack

Appears within the first 2-5 minutes. Purpose: establish "this person has the right to tell me this."

**Gundry MD (maximum version):**
- "Cardiothoracic surgeon"
- "Graduated with honors from Yale"
- "Medical doctorate from Medical College of Georgia"
- "16 years professor of surgery and pediatrics"
- "Chairman of cardiothoracic surgery at Loma Linda"
- "More infant heart transplants than any other doctor in the world"
- "Inventor of the Gundry retrograde cardiplegia cannula"
- "One of the original 20 surgeons who tested the first artificial heart"
- "Personal physician for Tony Robbins"

**Dr Marty (celebrity version):**
- "Celebrity vet"
- "Forbes dubbed him 'The Miracle Worker'"
- "Treated dogs of Oprah, Martha Stewart, Jerry Seinfeld, John Travolta"
- "Featured on Good Morning America, Martha Stewart Show"
- "45 years practicing veterinarian"
- "Graduated Cornell"

**Pattern:** Stack 5-8 credentials. Mix institutional (university, board certification) with social proof (celebrity clients, media appearances, patient count). The stack should feel overwhelming — the viewer should think "okay, this person knows their stuff" before the mechanism section begins.

### Move 2: The Disillusionment Story

The expert explains why they LEFT conventional practice. Positions them as a rebel who discovered the truth.

**Gundry:** "After 16 years as chief of heart surgery, I left my position at Loma Linda. Why? I realized that much of what I had learned, much of what we were telling patients, was flat out wrong."

**ProDentim:** "My frustration grew by the day, especially when I saw colleagues blindly prescribing the same chemical medications for different people. So I decided I couldn't continue like that anymore."

**Dr Marty:** "I graduated Cornell number two in my class, and I was taught very little about pet nutrition. We weren't taught a single thing about how to prevent dogs from having health problems."

**Why it works:** Transforms the expert from "another doctor" into "the one doctor brave enough to question the system." Creates an us-vs-them frame where the viewer and the expert are on the same side against the establishment.

### Move 3: The Conspiracy/Suppression Frame

The reason the viewer hasn't heard this before — powerful forces are hiding it.

**Gundry (the light bulb story):** A light bulb in a fire station has been running since 1901. Making 100-year filaments is easy. "But big corporations don't want us to have it. They want us to literally stay in the dark. And I think the same goes for your gut."

**Meticore:** "The weight loss industry is worth $80 billion a year. They do not want you to see this video."

**ProDentim:** "Tens of studies have proved that big oral care brands contain hidden chemicals, but they have been ignored or worse, buried by corporate lawyers."

**Gundry (over-the-counter pills):** "There is no profit in fixing someone's problem. There is a lot of profit in making sure it never goes away."

**Why it works:** Explains the viewer's past failure (it wasn't your fault — they were hiding the answer). Also creates urgency (this video might get taken down). Also validates any skepticism (of course you're skeptical — that's what they want).

### Move 4: The Difficult Case Study

A specific patient whose situation mirrors the viewer but worse. The expert makes a personal promise to help them.

**ProDentim — Samuel Berman:** 54 years old, husband and grandfather. Life of the party who stopped laughing. Bad breath so severe his wife only kissed his forehead. Loose teeth. Afraid to eat. Brain fog. "He was the ninth specialist I was seeing." The expert promises: "I would do absolutely everything in my power to help him."

**Meticore — Todd's son Aaron:** Falls and screams for help. Todd can't make it up the stairs. Collapses. Wife tells friend: "I just don't find him attractive anymore."

**Why it works:** The case study is the viewer in microcosm. They see themselves. The expert's promise to the patient becomes a promise to the viewer. When the case study gets results, it's proof the viewer can too.

### Move 5: The Foreign/Exotic Discovery

The solution comes from an unexpected place — not a lab, but a remote culture or accidental discovery.

**Meticore:** Dr. Tabera from Madagascar, during a hospital transfer. Writes down a recipe with exotic plants on a piece of paper.

**Gundry:** Blue Zones research — Sardinia, Okinawa, Costa Rica, Greek islands. People living past 100 with gut health of 30-year-olds.

**ProDentim:** Patient Sam brings a newspaper photo of 2,500-year-old teeth. "Look, Doc, they look way better than mine."

**Activated You:** Bitter melon from Southeast Asia, celebrated for centuries.

**Why it works:** Positions the solution as natural, time-tested, and outside the system that failed the viewer. The exotic origin also makes the ingredient story more interesting and the scarcity frame more believable.

## Part 3: Closing Architecture

Every VSL in this collection uses the same 5-section closing sequence. The order is invariant.

### Section 1: Price Descent Choreography

Anchor absurdly high, then descend through multiple steps.

**ProDentim's descent:**
- "How much would you pay? $300? $500? $700?"
- "Retail value: $175"
- "But you won't pay $170, nor $150, nor $120"
- "$99 for one bottle — but not today"
- Reveal: "$69"
- Multi-bottle discount: even less per unit

**Dr Marty's descent:**
- "You could spend several hundred dollars a month on ingredients"
- "You're not going to pay $100 a bag"
- "Not even $70"
- Reveal: "$30.95"
- Multi-pack: deeper discounts

**Pattern:** The descent always has at least 3 price points above the reveal. The "wholesale" or "cut out the middleman" justification explains why the price is lower than expected. The multi-bottle discount creates a second descent within the first.

### Section 2: Multi-Bottle Justification

Explain why the viewer should buy 3-6 months, not 1. This isn't just upselling — it's framed as medical necessity.

**ProDentim:** "I recommend at least 60 days to make 100% sure there's not a single trace of chemicals left. If you stop, it might lead to immediate death of all the good guys. You could lose all the progress."

**Meticore:** "A single capsule allows for all-day release and absorption" (implies daily consistency matters).

**Gundry:** Extended education about butyrate building over time — longer use = more repair.

**Why it works:** Creates a medical rationale for the bulk purchase. The viewer isn't buying more product — they're committing to a treatment protocol.

### Section 3: The Guarantee

Always 60-90 days. Always "return empty bottles." This language is near-universal.

**ProDentim:** "You can even send back the empty bottles and we'll still refund your money."

**Dr Marty:** "The first ever 'your dog is healthier or it's free' guarantee. You can even send us back the empty bag."

**Activated You:** "Returning empty jars is not only allowed, it's encouraged."

**Why it works:** "Empty bottles" language eliminates the objection "but what if I use it and it doesn't work?" It signals supreme confidence and makes the risk genuinely feel zero.

### Section 4: The Crossroads Close

Paint two futures. Make the "without" path viscerally painful.

**ProDentim:** "You might decide to spend more time and money looking for various options. In the meantime, the health of your gums might get worse until the point where they are too damaged and weak to even hold implants."

**Meticore:** Combines the emotional stakes (wife doesn't find him attractive, can't save son) with health statistics (62% more likely heart attack, 81% more likely coronary disease).

**Pattern:** The "without" path always escalates — it doesn't just stay the same, it gets WORSE. The "with" path is described in present tense as if already happening.

### Section 5: FAQ as Second Sales Letter

The FAQ section isn't housekeeping — it's a compressed re-pitch.

**ProDentim's FAQ structure:**
1. How and why does it work? (Restates mechanism in 200 words)
2. Any side effects? (Safety reassurance + "150,000 customers")
3. How should I take it? (Reinforces daily habit)
4. What if it doesn't work? (Restates guarantee)
5. How do I get started? (Final CTA)

**Pattern:** Each FAQ answer ends with a micro-close. The FAQ functions as a complete mini-VSL for viewers who scrolled or skipped to the bottom.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

### The "Not Your Fault" Thread
Every VSL explicitly absolves the viewer of blame for their problem. Meticore: "They want you to believe your weight gain is your fault. It's not." Gundry: "If you've suffered from digestive issues... it is not your fault." This thread runs from hook through mechanism through close.

### The "Simple/Easy" Promise
Complexity of the mechanism section contrasts with simplicity of the solution. The science is deep; the action is one thing. "Let one candy melt in your mouth." "Add one scoop to water." "Sprinkle a layer over the top of your dog's bowl." The cognitive load is in understanding, not in doing.

### Celebrity vs. Expert vs. Everyman Narrators
- **Expert narrator** (Gundry, Dr Marty, Dr J): Credential-first. Works for brands where the doctor IS the brand.
- **Celebrity narrator** (Steve Harvey, Maggie Q): Personal struggle first. Expert is introduced as the person who helped them.
- **Everyman narrator** (Meticore's Todd Pittman): "I'm no doctor. I'm a 46-year-old civil engineer." Maximum relatability. Foreign doctor provides the expertise.

All three work. The choice depends on who the target audience trusts most. See [[attractive-character]] for narrator persona types.
